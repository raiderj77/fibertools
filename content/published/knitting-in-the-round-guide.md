# How to Knit and Crochet in the Round

Working in the round changes everything. No seams. No sewing pieces together at the end. Hats, socks, cowls, mittens, and seamless sweaters all start with the same skill. You join your work into a circle and spiral upward.

If you have only worked flat, circular knitting and crochet can feel awkward at first. Once it clicks, you will wonder why you ever worked flat.

## What Does "Working in the Round" Mean?

Instead of turning your work at the end of each row, you join the first and last stitches into a circle. Then you work continuously in one direction. Each "row" is now a "round" that spirals upward like a tube.

**For knitting:** You work only knit stitches to make stockinette. No purl rows needed. The right side always faces you. You use circular needles, double-pointed needles (DPNs), or the magic loop method.

**For crochet:** You work in continuous spirals or joined rounds. The right side usually faces you. You use a standard crochet hook with no special equipment.

The result is a seamless tube. It can become a hat, sock, mitten, sweater body, bag, or amigurumi piece.

## How Do You Knit in the Round?

### Method 1: Circular Needles

Circular needles have two short needle tips connected by a flexible cable. You cast on your stitches and spread them around the cable without twisting. Then you join by knitting the first cast-on stitch with the working yarn from the last cast-on stitch.

**Best for:** Projects with many stitches, like sweater bodies, blankets worked circularly, and cowls. You need enough stitches to stretch comfortably around the cable. A 16-inch circular works for hats with 80 to 120 stitches. A 32-inch circular works for sweater bodies with 200 or more stitches.

**How to join without twisting:**
1. Cast on all stitches. Lay the needle flat on a table.
2. Make sure all cast-on stitches face inward. The bumps point down and the smooth edge faces up. There should be no twists in the cable.
3. Place a stitch marker on the right needle tip.
4. Knit the first stitch on the left needle. Pull firmly to close the gap between the last and first cast-on stitches.

### Method 2: Double-Pointed Needles (DPNs)

A set of 4 or 5 short needles with points on both ends. You spread stitches across 3 or 4 needles and knit with the one empty needle.

**Best for:** Small projects with few stitches, like sock toes, hat crowns, mittens, and fingers of gloves. DPNs handle 20 to 60 stitches comfortably.

**How to set up:**
1. Cast on all stitches onto one DPN.
2. Spread them evenly across 3 needles for a 4-needle set, or across 4 needles for a 5-needle set.
3. Arrange them into a triangle or square. Place a marker at the beginning of the round.
4. Knit with the empty needle. When you finish one needle's stitches, that needle becomes the new working needle.

### Method 3: Magic Loop

You use one long circular needle, 40 inches or longer, for any size project, even very small ones. The cable creates two loops that divide your stitches into front and back halves.

**Best for:** Small projects if you do not like DPNs. Works well for socks, mittens, hat crowns, and sleeves. Also great for knitting two socks at once using the two-at-a-time method.

## How Do You Crochet in the Round?

### Continuous Spiral (Most Common)

You work the first stitch of each new round right into the last stitch of the previous round. You do not join. The rounds spiral upward like a spring. There is no visible join line.

**How to do it:**
1. Make a magic ring or chain and join with a slip stitch.
2. Work the required stitches into the ring.
3. Place a locking stitch marker in the first stitch of the round.
4. Keep crocheting into the next stitch without joining. When you reach the marker, move it to the new first stitch.

**Best for:** Amigurumi, hats worked from the top down, and any project where you do not want a visible seam line.

### Joined Rounds

You work each round separately. At the end of the round, you slip stitch into the first stitch to join. Then you chain up for the next round's height and work the next round.

**How to do it:**
1. Make a foundation ring. Chain the height for your stitch. That is ch 1 for single crochet, ch 2 for half double crochet, or ch 3 for double crochet.
2. Work all stitches of the round.
3. Slip stitch into the top of the turning chain, or the first stitch, to close the round.
4. Chain up for the next round. Repeat.

**Best for:** Granny squares, projects where you need clean round edges, and colorwork in the round.

## What Are the Key Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Check for twists before joining.** A twisted join makes a Mobius strip instead of a tube. You cannot fix it without frogging. Lay your cast-on flat and check that no stitches twist around the needle before you join.

**The first round gap.** A small gap can form where you join the round. You can fix it by casting on one extra stitch, then slipping it to the other needle and knitting it together with the first stitch. Or just sew the gap closed with a yarn tail when you finish.

**Track your rounds.** Without tracking, rounds blur together. Use the [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter), or place a physical marker at the beginning of every round. For crochet spirals, move a locking marker every round. Without tracking, you will overshoot or undershoot your target length.

**Gauge changes between flat and circular.** Most knitters purl at a different tension than they knit. Since circular stockinette uses only knit stitches, your circular gauge may differ from your flat gauge by 0.5 to 1 stitch per inch. Always swatch in the round if your project is worked in the round.

**DPN ladder lines.** Loose stitches can form at the spot where you switch from one DPN to the next. This creates visible vertical lines called ladders. Fix it by pulling the first 2 stitches on each needle extra tight. You can also shift the needle boundaries every few rounds so the transitions do not stack up.

**Common mistakes:**
- Joining with a twist, which cannot be fixed without frogging
- Forgetting to place a beginning-of-round marker
- Not swatching in the round, since gauge differs from flat
- Pulling the magic loop cable too tight and scrunching stitches
- In a crochet spiral, losing track of the round start and crocheting too far

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What size circular needle do I need for knitting in the round?

Match the cable length to your project's circumference. A 16-inch circular fits hats and necklines with 80 to 120 stitches in worsted weight. A 24-inch fits child sweater bodies. A 32-inch fits adult sweater bodies. A 40-inch is needed for the magic loop method. Your stitches must stretch comfortably around the cable without bunching.

### Can I use circular needles for flat knitting too?

Yes. Circular needles work just like straight needles when you turn your work at the end of each row instead of joining. Many knitters prefer circulars for all their knitting. The cable holds the weight of the project and reduces wrist strain. Long circulars work especially well for wide flat projects like blankets.

### How do I avoid the jog in crochet joined rounds?

The jog is the visible step where one round ends and the next begins. To minimize it, try the invisible join method. Cut the yarn after the last stitch, pull it through, and use a tapestry needle to duplicate the first stitch of the round. For striped projects, the jogless jog method shifts the round start by one stitch with each color change.

### Is circular gauge really different from flat gauge?

Yes, for most knitters. Flat stockinette alternates knit and purl rows. Circular stockinette is all knit stitches. If you purl tighter or looser than you knit, your circular gauge will be different. The difference is usually 0.5 to 1 stitch per inch. Always swatch in the round for circular projects to avoid sizing surprises.
