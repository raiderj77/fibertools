# Arm Knitting and Finger Knitting Guide

Arm knitting gives you fast results. You do not need needles or hooks. You do not need weeks of work. Just use your arms, some chunky yarn, and about 45 minutes. You can finish a whole blanket. The big, fluffy fabric has become very popular on Pinterest and Instagram. It looks fancy. It costs much less than store-bought blankets. And it really does take under an hour.

Finger knitting is simpler than arm knitting. It works great for narrow projects like scarves, garlands, and decorative ropes. If you have never worked with yarn before, finger knitting is the fastest way to finish something.

## What Is Arm Knitting?

Arm knitting uses your forearms instead of needles. You start by making slip knots and loops over your wrist. Then you move stitches from one arm to the other. It works just like regular knitting, but much bigger.

The fabric has very large stitches. Each stitch is 2 to 3 inches wide. The texture is loose and open. It is not thick or warm like regular knitting. It is a decorative fabric. It drapes nicely over furniture or works as a light throw.

**Best for:** Chunky throw blankets, decorative pillows, infinity scarves, pet beds, photo props, and home decor.

**Not ideal for:** Fitted clothing, winter accessories that need warmth, baby items (the holes are too large), and anything that needs to hold its shape.

## How Do You Arm Knit Step by Step?

### Cast On

1. Make a slip knot with a long tail. Leave about 6 feet of tail for every 10 stitches you plan to cast on.
2. Place the slip knot on your right wrist.
3. Use the long-tail cast-on method, the same one used in regular knitting. Loop each new stitch onto your right arm.
4. For a throw blanket, cast on 15 to 25 stitches. Fewer stitches means a narrower blanket.

### Knit

1. Reach through the first stitch on your right arm with your left hand.
2. Grab the working yarn and pull it through the stitch. This makes a new loop.
3. Slide the new loop onto your left arm. Slide the old stitch off your right arm.
4. Repeat this across the whole row. All stitches will now be on your left arm.
5. Switch directions. Knit from your left arm to your right arm for the next row.

### Bind Off

1. Knit 2 stitches onto the receiving arm.
2. Pull the first stitch over the second stitch and off your arm.
3. Knit 1 more stitch. Pull the stitch before it over. Keep repeating until 1 loop is left.
4. Cut the yarn. Pull the tail through the last loop and pull it tight.

### How Long Does It Take?

A throw blanket (30x50 inches) takes 30 to 45 minutes. An infinity scarf takes 15 to 20 minutes. A small pillow cover takes 10 to 15 minutes. Arm knitting is fast because the stitches are so large. Each row covers several inches at once.

## How Much Yarn Do Arm Knitting Projects Need?

Arm knitting uses a lot of yarn. Each stitch is 2 to 3 inches wide. The yarn is usually Super Bulky (CYC 6) or Jumbo (CYC 7) weight.

## How Does Finger Knitting Work?

Finger knitting makes a narrow knitted cord or strip. You use just your fingers as needles.

**Basic finger knitting:**
1. Loop yarn over your index finger, under your middle finger, over your ring finger, and under your pinky.
2. Wrap back the other way. Go over your pinky, under your ring finger, over your middle finger, and under your index finger.
3. You now have 2 rows of loops on 4 fingers.
4. Start with your pinky. Pull the bottom loop over the top loop and off your finger. Do the same for each finger.
5. Push the remaining loops down. Wrap another row. Pull the bottom loops over again.

The result is a knitted tube about 1 inch wide. You can connect multiple tubes side by side to make wider projects. Or use a single tube as a garland, scarf, or decorative rope.

**Finger knitting is best for:** Kids' first fiber arts projects, garlands, scarf ropes, headbands, and decorative trim. A 6-year-old can learn finger knitting in about 10 minutes.

## What Are the Best Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Keep consistent tension.** Arm knitting tension can change a lot. Your arms are not a uniform tool. Try to pull each stitch to about the same looseness. Too tight means stiff fabric that does not drape. Too loose means floppy fabric with huge gaps.

**Use a table surface.** Lay your stitches across a table as you work. Do not hold everything on your arms the whole time. This reduces arm fatigue. It also helps you keep a consistent stitch size.

**Do not leave the project on your arms overnight.** You cannot put arm knitting down and come back to it like needle knitting. The stitches will slide off or tighten up. Finish in one session. Or carefully slide the stitches onto a very large circular needle or a broomstick to hold them.

**Wash gently or not at all.** Arm-knit fabric is loose and stretches easily. Machine washing can warp the stitches. Spot-clean or hand wash very gently. Lay it flat to dry. Merino roving yarn felts very quickly in water. This can be a good thing (felting tightens the loose stitches) or a bad thing, depending on what you want.

**Common mistakes:**
- Not buying enough yarn. Arm knitting uses yarn fast because the stitches are so big.
- Casting on too many stitches. More than 20 stitches makes a blanket wider than most arms can handle.
- Pulling stitches too tight. The fabric becomes stiff and loses its soft, fluffy drape.
- Using regular worsted weight yarn. The stitches will look empty and sparse. You need Jumbo or Super Bulky.
- Trying to make a fitted garment. Arm-knit fabric has no structure for wearable clothing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much yarn do I need for an arm-knit blanket?

A throw-sized arm-knit blanket (30x50 inches) needs 200 to 275 yards of Super Bulky or Jumbo weight yarn. A larger throw (40x60 inches) needs 300 to 400 yards. You can also buy by weight. Plan on about 3 to 5 pounds of chunky yarn for a throw. Use the Yarn Calculator with a gauge of 1.5 stitches per inch to get an exact number.

### Is arm knitting durable?

Arm-knit fabric is loose and decorative. It is not structural. It works well as a throw blanket draped over furniture. It will not hold up to heavy daily use. The large stitches stretch and shift over time. Chenille and tube yarns hold up better than unspun merino roving. If you want a durable chunky blanket, use regular needles (US size 50) instead of your arms.

### Can kids do arm knitting?

Kids ages 8 and up can arm knit with supervision. Their shorter arms make a narrower fabric. This works well for scarves and small throws. Finger knitting is better for younger kids ages 5 and up. It needs less arm span. It also makes a narrow cord quickly, which feels satisfying.

### What yarn should I use for arm knitting?

You need Jumbo or Super Bulky weight yarn (CYC 6 to 7). Look for chenille tube yarn, roving-style yarn, or bulky acrylic. Do not use regular worsted or DK weight. The stitches will look too open and empty. Chenille yarn costs about $5 to $10 per 30-yard skein. It makes a soft, velvety fabric. Merino roving looks beautiful but sheds and felts easily.
