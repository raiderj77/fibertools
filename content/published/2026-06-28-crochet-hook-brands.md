# Crochet Hook Brands: Which Ones Are Actually Worth Your Money?

The short answer: Clover Amour and Tulip Etimo are the two brands most working crocheters reach for, and for good reason. Both have ergonomic handles, consistent sizing, and smooth heads that move through yarn without snagging. Budget hooks from Boye or Susan Bates are fine starting points, but your hand can get tired fast after a long session.

---

> **My honest confession:** I changed hooks halfway through a project and wondered why the size changed. The hook size really does matter. Check it against the [hook and needle guide](/needle-guide).

## Does the brand of crochet hook actually matter?

Yes, but not in every situation. Hook material and head shape affect how yarn slides, how your hand feels after two hours, and whether your tension stays consistent. For a quick 30-minute project, almost any hook will do. For a 200-hour blanket, the hook you're holding matters a lot.

Three things separate a mediocre hook from a great one. Those are head geometry (inline vs. tapered), handle comfort, and sizing accuracy. Cheap hooks are sometimes mislabeled by as much as 0.25mm. That is enough to throw off your gauge on a fitted garment. If you are ever getting weird results, measure your hook with a needle gauge tool before blaming your tension.

---

## What is the difference between inline and tapered hooks?

Inline hooks have a head that sits in line with the shaft. Tapered hooks have a head that flares out slightly before narrowing back to the shaft. Susan Bates uses an inline style. Boye uses a tapered style. Neither is better than the other. It comes down to how you hold your hook and what your tension naturally does.

Pencil-grip crocheters often prefer inline hooks. The consistent shaft diameter gives them more control. Knife-grip crocheters sometimes find tapered heads easier to insert into tight stitches. If you have crocheted for years and always had tension problems, try switching head styles before you assume the issue is your technique.

## Which brands are best for beginners?

Susan Bates and Boye are the most widely available starter hooks in the US. You can find them at most craft stores and online for about $3 to 6 per hook or $10 to 15 for a basic set. They work. They are not going to ruin your project. The aluminum shafts slide reasonably well through acrylic and wool. The sizing is close enough for practice swatches.

The main complaint with both brands is the handle. The straight, thin aluminum handle gets uncomfortable after a long session. This is especially true if you have any hand tension or joint issues. If you crochet more than an hour at a stretch, this matters.

One underrated beginner option is the [Furls Streamline](https://furlscrochet.com/) line. The handles are larger and more ergonomic than Bates or Boye. The price is in the $8 to 12 range per hook, which is still affordable for someone just starting out.

---

## What makes Clover Amour hooks so popular?

The Clover Amour has a soft rubber grip handle. It also has a tapered head with a slightly pointed tip that slides into stitches easily. The grip is solid without being too bulky. The rubber does not get slippery when your hands get warm. A single hook costs about $8 to 10. A full set of nine hooks (B through N) usually costs around $55 to 65.

[Clover is a Japanese company](https://www.clover-mfg.com/) with a long history making sewing and fiber arts tools. Their quality control is consistent. The sizing on Amour hooks is accurate and matches the labeled size reliably. For single crochet and double crochet work in medium-weight yarn, this is the hook most experienced crocheters keep in their bag.

One thing to know: the rubber grip is glued onto the aluminum shaft. After years of heavy use, some crocheters report the grip loosening. It does not happen to everyone, but it does happen.

---

## How does the Tulip Etimo compare to the Clover Amour?

The Tulip Etimo is the other top pick in the ergonomic hook category. It is also made in Japan. The Etimo has a slightly different grip texture, a gold-colored shaft, and a head shape that many crocheters find even smoother than the Amour. Single hooks cost $10 to 13. The [Tulip Etimo set](https://tulipco.com/) with a case costs around $60 to 80 depending on the size range.

The Etimo head has a very smooth taper and a rounded throat. Some yarn types move through it more cleanly than through the Amour. This is especially true for splitty or loosely plied yarns. If you work a lot with linen, bamboo, or other plant-based fibers that tend to split, the Etimo is worth trying.

The grip on the Etimo is a soft rubber-foam hybrid. It feels slightly more cushioned than the Amour. For crocheters with arthritis or repetitive strain issues, this can make a real difference over a long session.

## Are there good options for very fine or very large hooks?

For thread crochet (steel hooks, sizes 00 through 14), [Clover's steel hook line](https://www.clover-mfg.com/) and Boye's steel hooks are both solid choices. The Clover steel hooks have a small comfort grip. That grip makes a noticeable difference when you are working size 10 crochet thread for hours.

For large hooks (P/15mm and up), the options thin out. Furls makes some larger sizes in their resin and wood lines. The added weight of a resin hook can actually help with tension control on bulky yarn. Knitter's Pride also makes jumbo hooks with decent ergonomics in the $10 to 15 range.

For Tunisian crochet, you need a hook with a stopper or a cable extension. [Knitter's Pride Dreamz](https://www.knitterspride.com/) makes a Tunisian set with interchangeable cables. It works well and costs around $30 to 40 for a starter set.

---

## Should you buy a set or individual hooks?

Buy individual hooks first until you know what brand and style works for your hands. A full set of a brand you end up hating is money wasted. Start with a 5mm (H) hook in two or three different brands. Work a swatch with each one. Notice how your hand feels after 20 minutes. That tells you more than any review.

Once you know your preferred brand, a set makes sense and saves money. Most sets cover B through N (2.25mm to 9mm). That range handles most worsted and bulky weight projects. Measure your gauge with every new hook you pick up. Even within the same brand, there can be minor variation between hook sizes.

## Frequently asked questions

### What are the best crochet hook brands for beginners?
Clover Amour and Susan Bates are widely considered the best crochet hook brands for beginners. Clover Amour hooks have a soft, ergonomic rubber grip that reduces hand fatigue during long sessions. Susan Bates offers affordable aluminum hooks with a smooth inline head. Both brands are widely available and sold in starter sets. That makes it easy for new crocheters to try different sizes without spending a lot upfront.

### What is the difference between inline and tapered crochet hooks?
Inline hooks have a head that sits flush with the shaft. Tapered hooks have a more pronounced throat that flares outward. Brands like Susan Bates make inline hooks. Boye is known for its tapered design. The style you prefer often depends on your tension habits. Inline hooks suit crocheters with tighter tension, while tapered hooks work well for those with a looser grip. Trying both styles helps you find your ideal match.

### Are expensive crochet hook brands worth the money?
Higher-end brands like Furls and Clover Amour are often worth the investment for frequent crocheters. These brands use premium materials such as resin, wood, or cushioned rubber. Those materials can greatly reduce hand and wrist strain over long crafting sessions. Budget hooks from brands like Boye or Susan Bates perform well for occasional use. But crocheters who work daily often find that ergonomic hooks improve both comfort and stitch consistency.

### Which crochet hook brands are best for people with arthritis or hand pain?
Furls, Clover Amour, and Tulip Etimo are the top brands recommended for crocheters with arthritis or hand pain. These brands focus on ergonomic handle design. They offer wide, cushioned grips that spread pressure evenly across the hand. The reduced strain makes repetitive motions more comfortable over long periods. Many crafters with chronic pain specifically recommend Clover Amour and Tulip Etimo for their balanced weight and gentle grip material.

### What crochet hook brand is best for working with fine or lace-weight yarn?
Tulip and Addi are considered top brands for working with fine or lace-weight yarn. These brands make hooks with precisely tapered, smooth tips. Those tips glide easily through tight stitches without splitting delicate fibers. Tulip's steel hooks are especially praised for their polished finish and accuracy at smaller sizes. When working with lace-weight or thread crochet, hook tip quality matters most. Both Tulip and Addi consistently deliver the precision needed for detailed projects.
