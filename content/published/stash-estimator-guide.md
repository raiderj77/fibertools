# How to Estimate Yarn Yardage in Partial Skeins: Weigh, Calculate and Plan

Most fiber artists have a stash. Somewhere in that stash are half-used skeins with no ball band, no label, and no yardage info.

The question is always the same: is there enough yarn here to make something? You do not need to rewind and measure every inch. A kitchen scale and one simple formula will tell you exactly how many yards you have.

## When You Need to Estimate Partial Skein Yardage

This comes up more often than most crafters expect:

- You finished a project and want to know what is left over
- You are combining partial skeins for a scrappy project
- The ball band fell off, or was never saved
- You are deciding whether you have enough yarn to finish without buying more
- You want to catalog your stash for project planning

The [Stash Estimator](/stash-estimator) takes the guesswork out of all these situations. Weigh your partial skein, enter the original skein specs, and get your remaining yardage right away.

## The Core Formula

The math is simple:

**Remaining yardage = (remaining weight divided by full skein weight) times full skein yardage**

That is it. One multiplication and one division.

### Example Calculation

You have a partial skein of worsted weight yarn. The original skein was 3.5 oz (100g) with 220 yards. Your partial skein weighs 1.4 oz (40g) on your kitchen scale.

**Remaining yardage = (1.4 divided by 3.5) times 220 = 0.4 times 220 = 88 yards**

You have 88 yards to work with. That is enough for a hat in worsted weight. It is not quite enough for a pair of mittens.

## Yardage Reference by CYC Yarn Weight

When you do not have the original ball band, this table shows typical yardage ranges for a standard 100g (3.5 oz) skein. Fiber content affects these numbers. Cotton and wool are heavier per yard than acrylic and nylon.

| CYC Weight | Name | Typical Yardage per 100g | Typical Yardage per 50g |
|------------|------|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| 0 | Lace | 800 to 1,000 yards | 400 to 500 yards |
| 1 | Fingering / Sock | 400 to 450 yards | 200 to 225 yards |
| 2 | Sport | 275 to 325 yards | 138 to 163 yards |
| 3 | DK | 225 to 275 yards | 113 to 138 yards |
| 4 | Worsted | 200 to 230 yards | 100 to 115 yards |
| 5 | Bulky | 125 to 150 yards | 63 to 75 yards |
| 6 | Super Bulky | 75 to 100 yards | 38 to 50 yards |
| 7 | Jumbo | 40 to 55 yards | 20 to 28 yards |

Use the middle of these ranges if you are not sure. For superwash merino in fingering weight, 425 yards per 100g is a safe default. For 100% cotton in worsted weight, use the lower end, around 200 yards per 100g.

## Kitchen Scale Tips for Accurate Results

Your yardage estimate is only as good as your scale. Here is how to get reliable numbers:

**Use a digital kitchen scale.** Analog scales are not precise enough. You need accuracy to at least 1 gram (0.1 oz). Scales with 0.1g precision are even better, especially for small leftover pieces.

**Weigh without the swift or ball winder.** Place the yarn right on the scale. If you need to hold it in something, put a lightweight bowl on the scale first. Tare, or zero, it out before you add the yarn.

**Account for yarn labels.** Paper ball bands weigh 2 to 5 grams. If the original skein weight on the label was measured without the band, remove it before weighing your partial skein. If you are not sure, it probably does not matter for skeins over 50g. The error is under 5%.

**Check your scale's calibration.** Weigh a known object to verify your scale is reading correctly. A US nickel weighs exactly 5 grams.

**Weigh twice.** Pick up the yarn, put it back, and weigh again. If you get a different number, your scale may need a flat surface or new batteries.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

### Tips for Stash Organization

- **Weigh and label everything** before it goes into storage. A piece of masking tape with the yarn name, weight, and estimated yardage saves future headaches.
- **Keep ball bands** in a binder or envelope, organized by project.
- **Take a photo** of every ball band before you throw it away. Your phone's camera roll becomes a searchable yarn record.
- **Group partial skeins by weight category,** such as fingering, DK, and worsted, so you can quickly find yarn for scrap projects.

### Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Using the wrong original weight.** Some brands list skein weight including the label. Others do not. A 5-gram difference on a 50g skein throws your estimate off by 10%.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting fiber content matters.** Cotton yarn is denser than wool. A 100g skein of cotton has fewer yards than a 100g skein of merino at the same weight category. Check the specific brand when you can, instead of relying on general ranges.

**Mistake 3: Ignoring moisture.** Yarn absorbs humidity. A skein stored in a damp basement will weigh more than the same skein kept in a dry room. This is a small effect, around 1 to 3%, but it adds up with large amounts of yarn.

**Mistake 4: Rounding too aggressively.** If your scale says 37g and you round to 40g, that is an 8% error. On a 400-yard skein, that is a 32-yard difference. That could affect whether you can finish a project.

## Real Project Examples

### Example 1: Do I Have Enough for a Hat?

You have a partial skein of CYC weight 5 bulky yarn. The ball band says 136 yards per 100g skein. Your scale reads 62g.

**Remaining yardage = (62 divided by 100) times 136 = 84.3 yards**

A basic adult hat in bulky weight takes about 80 to 100 yards. You are right on the edge. You could make a shorter brim hat. A long slouchy beanie would come up short.

### Example 2: Planning a Scrap Blanket

You have seven partial skeins of worsted weight acrylic. They are all from the same brand, at 200 yards per 100g. You weigh each one:

### Example 3: Mystery Yarn Identification

You found a skein with no label. It feels like DK weight and looks like wool. You weigh it at 78g.

Using the DK midpoint of 250 yards per 100g: (78 divided by 100) times 250 = 195 yards. That is enough for a pair of fingerless mitts, a cowl, or a baby hat.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How accurate is the weight-based yardage formula?

It is very accurate, within 2 to 5%, if your scale is reliable and you use the correct original skein weight and yardage. The main sources of error are inaccurate scales, moisture in the yarn, and using general yardage estimates instead of the actual ball band numbers. Always use brand-specific data when you can.

### Can I use a bathroom scale instead of a kitchen scale?

Bathroom scales are not precise enough for yarn. Most bathroom scales are accurate only to the nearest half-pound, which is about 225 grams. That means your whole partial skein could be smaller than the scale's margin of error. A digital kitchen scale with gram accuracy costs under $15 and is essential for stash estimation.

### What if I do not know what yarn weight category my mystery yarn is?

Wrap the yarn around a ruler for one inch and count the wraps. Lace yarn gives 30 or more wraps per inch. Fingering gives 14 to 20. Sport gives 12 to 14. DK gives 11 to 12. Worsted gives 8 to 10. Bulky gives 6 to 7. Super bulky gives 5 or fewer. This wraps-per-inch method works well for identifying yarn weight even without a label.

### Should I weigh yarn on a cone differently?

Yes. Cones and tubes have significant weight. Weigh the cone with yarn and note the number. Use the yarn, then weigh again. The difference is the weight of yarn you used. If you need the empty cone weight, wind off a known length, such as 100 yards, and weigh it. Use that as your yards-per-gram reference for future calculations.
