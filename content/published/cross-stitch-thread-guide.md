# How to Estimate Thread for Cross Stitch Projects

You found the perfect cross stitch pattern. Maybe it is a beautiful landscape, a funny quote, or a detailed portrait. The pattern lists 47 DMC colors. Now you are asking the same question every stitcher asks: how many skeins of each color do I actually need?

Most patterns include a thread estimate, but not all of them. And if you are designing your own pattern or converting an image to cross stitch, you are starting from scratch. Here is how to calculate thread amounts so you never run out in the middle of a project.

## How Much Thread Does Each Cross Stitch Use?

A single cross stitch on 14-count Aida uses about 1 inch (2.5 cm) of 6-strand embroidery floss. That is when you stitch with 2 strands. That is the baseline number for every thread calculation.

But a few things can change that baseline:

**Fabric count matters.** Higher count fabric, like 18-count or 28-count linen, has smaller holes and shorter stitch distances. Lower count fabric, like 11-count, has longer stitch distances. Thread per stitch ranges from about 0.8 inches on 18-count to 1.4 inches on 11-count.

| Fabric Count | Thread Per Stitch | Strands Used |
|---|---|---|
| 11-count Aida | ~1.3 to 1.4 inches | 3 strands |
| 14-count Aida | ~1.0 inch | 2 strands |
| 16-count Aida | ~0.9 inches | 2 strands |
| 18-count Aida | ~0.8 inches | 2 strands (or 1) |
| 28-count linen (over 2) | ~1.0 inch | 2 strands |

**The number of strands changes how much thread you use.** Stitching with 3 strands on 11-count uses 50% more thread per stitch than using 2 strands on 14-count. Always match your strand count to your fabric count. This gives you the right coverage.

## How Do You Calculate Thread for a Full Project?

### Step 1: Count Stitches Per Color

Go through your pattern and count how many stitches each color needs. Most pattern software, like Pattern Keeper, WinStitch, or PCStitch, counts stitches for you automatically. For patterns you count by hand, tally each symbol on the chart.

### Step 2: Calculate Thread Length Per Color

Multiply the stitch count by the thread per stitch for your fabric count.

Example: Color DMC 310 (black) has 2,400 stitches on 14-count Aida. Thread per stitch is 1 inch. Total thread needed: 2,400 inches, which equals 200 feet, which equals 66.7 yards.

### Step 3: Convert to Skeins

A standard DMC embroidery floss skein contains 8.7 yards (8 meters) of 6-strand floss. If you stitch with 2 strands, you get 3 usable lengths from each skein. You split the 6 strands into 3 pairs of 2.

Usable thread per skein: 8.7 yards times 3 pairs equals 26.1 yards.

From Step 2: 66.7 yards divided by 26.1 yards per skein equals 2.56 skeins. Round up to 3 skeins.

### Step 4: Add a Buffer

Add 15 to 20% for thread waste. Waste comes from starting and ending lengths, re-threading, and mistakes. 3 skeins times 1.15 equals 3.45. Round up to 4 skeins of DMC 310.

## What About Specialty Stitches and Backstitching?

Not everything in cross stitch is a simple X. Here is how to estimate thread for common extras:

**Backstitching** outlines and defines the design. Each backstitch uses roughly 0.5 to 0.8 inches of thread per stitch on 14-count. Backstitching usually adds 5 to 10% to your total thread usage. Most patterns list backstitch as a separate color count.

**French knots** use about 2 inches of thread each. The wrapping uses up extra thread. If your pattern has 50 French knots in one color, add 100 inches (2.8 yards) to that color's total.

**Half stitches and quarter stitches** use half and one quarter of the thread a full cross stitch uses. Some patterns, especially Dimensions kits, use a lot of half stitches to create blending effects.

**Long stitches** that span multiple fabric squares use more thread. A stitch that spans 3 squares uses roughly 3 times the thread of a single-square stitch.

## What Are the Best Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Organize before you stitch.** Wind each color onto a bobbin or card and label it with the DMC number. A project with 40 or more colors gets messy fast without organization. Bobbin boxes cost $5 to $8 and hold 30 to 50 wound bobbins.

**Cut 18-inch working lengths.** Longer threads tangle, fray, and twist. Shorter threads mean you have to re-thread more often. 18 inches works best for most stitchers. Some stitchers prefer 12 inches for metallic threads because those fray more quickly.

**Stitch from the center outward.** Start your first stitch at the center of the fabric and the center of the pattern. This keeps your design centered and gives you equal margins on all sides for framing.

**Do not knot your thread.** Knots create bumps you can see from the front. They can also pull through the fabric. Instead, use a loop start. Fold one strand in half, thread both cut ends through the needle, and catch the loop on the back with your first stitch. Or just weave the tail under existing stitches.

**Common mistakes:**
- Not buying enough of the main background color, which always has the highest stitch count
- Buying more thread after starting and getting a slightly different dye lot
- Stitching with more strands than the fabric count needs, which creates puffy and overstuffed stitches
- Forgetting to count backstitch thread in your estimates, which can add 5 to 10% to your total thread

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many skeins of DMC floss do I need for a typical project?

A small project under 10,000 stitches uses 5 to 15 skeins total. A medium project with 10,000 to 30,000 stitches uses 20 to 50 skeins. A large full-coverage project with 50,000 or more stitches can use 80 to 150 or more skeins. Each standard DMC skein contains 8.7 yards of 6-strand floss. That gives you about 26 usable yards when you stitch with 2 strands.

### Can I substitute thread brands in a cross stitch pattern?

Yes, thread brands are interchangeable. Use the Thread Converter tool to find matches between DMC, Anchor, Cosmo, and Sulky. Colors are close but not always identical. Compare the actual threads side by side before you commit to a full project. Most stitchers stick with one brand per project to keep colors consistent.

### How do I estimate thread for a pattern without stitch counts?

Count the chart squares yourself, or use the chart dimensions. A 200x150 chart has a maximum of 30,000 total stitches. Some squares will be blank. Count or estimate the percentage that is blank. If 30% is blank, you have about 21,000 stitches. Then use the per-stitch thread amounts for your fabric count to calculate total yardage.

### What if I run out of thread mid-project?

Buy the same DMC number. DMC floss has very consistent dye lots. Most stitchers cannot tell the difference between skeins bought years apart. Anchor and other brands vary more. If you use hand-dyed or specialty threads, buy extra upfront. Small-batch threads may not be restocked in the same color.

## Calculate Your Thread Before You Start

Running out of your main color 500 stitches from the finish is avoidable. Count your stitches, multiply by thread per stitch, divide into skeins, and add your buffer. Five minutes of math saves a frustrating trip to the craft store.
