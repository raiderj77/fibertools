# How Many Cross Stitches Per Skein? A Real-World Guide

**The short answer:** A standard 8.7-yard (8m) skein of DMC 6-strand floss covers roughly 150 to 200 cross stitches on 14-count Aida when you stitch with 2 strands. Fabric count, strand count, and your personal tension all shift that number. Use the formula below to calculate for your specific project.

---

## What actually determines [how much floss I need](/blog/accurately-calculating-your-yarn-yardage-with-a-customizable/)?

Your stitch count is only one variable. The fabric count and the number of strands you pull from the skein matter just as much. These two things can double or cut your yardage needs in half.

Each cross stitch on 14-count Aida uses about 1.5 inches of floss per stitch when worked with 2 strands. This comes from [DMC's own yardage guidance](https://www.dmc.com/us/). Move up to 18-count evenweave and each stitch is smaller. You get more stitches per yard. Drop down to 11-count and each stitch is bigger. You use up floss faster. Add a third strand and you use 50% more thread per stitch. These things add up fast.

A practical starting point:

- **11-count Aida, 3 strands:** about 80 to 100 stitches per 8.7-yard skein
- **14-count Aida, 2 strands:** about 150 to 200 stitches per 8.7-yard skein
- **18-count evenweave, 1 strand:** about 300 to 350 stitches per 8.7-yard skein
- **28-count linen over 2 threads, 2 strands:** about 175 to 225 stitches per 8.7-yard skein

These are working estimates, not guarantees. Your tension, how you start and end threads, and whether you do fractional stitches all affect the real number.

## How do I [calculate floss for a specific project](/blog/creating-a-realistic-yarn-yardage-estimate-incorporating-fib/)?

Count your stitches in each color. Then divide by your stitches-per-skein estimate. Round up and add a buffer skein for any color you use a lot.

Here is the formula most stitchers use:

> **(Total stitches in that color) / (stitches per skein for your fabric and strand count) = skeins needed**

Example: You have 1,200 stitches in DMC 321 (red) on 14-count Aida with 2 strands. Use 175 stitches per skein as a middle estimate:

1,200 / 175 = 6.85 skeins

Round up to 7. Then buy 8 if you are worried about matching the color later. Dye lots on hand-dyed threads can vary. Even DMC can look slightly different if you buy months apart.

For large projects, the [Stitch Fiddle calculator](https://www.stitchfiddle.com) and [DMC's pattern software](https://www.dmc.com/us/create-a-pattern/) both let you import an image. They give you a stitch count for each color. That makes this math much faster.

## Does the type of floss change the math?

Yes, the thread type changes everything. Standard DMC and Anchor cotton both come on 8.7-yard skeins, so swapping between them is simple. Silk floss, pearl cotton, and specialty threads use completely different skein lengths. You need to recalculate from scratch using the actual yardage on the label.

DMC and Anchor standard 6-strand cotton floss come in 8.7-yard (8m) skeins. That is where the similarity ends.

[Anchor skeins](https://www.anchorcrafts.com) are also 8.7 yards. The math works the same if you swap colors between the two brands. Silk floss, pearl cotton, and specialty threads are a different story:

- **DMC Perle/Pearl Cotton #8:** Comes in 95-yard balls. It is a twisted thread that you cannot split. You use the whole strand. You get many more stitches per ball, roughly 1,000 or more on 14-count. But you cannot split it for finer work.
- **Silk floss (e.g., Kreinik, hand-dyed silks):** Often sold in shorter skeins, sometimes as little as 5 yards. The price per yard is higher. Calculating carefully matters more here.
- **Hand-dyed overdyed floss (e.g., Weeks Dye Works, The Gentle Art):** Usually sold in 5-yard skeins. That is nearly half the yardage of DMC. Your skein count will be about double. Always check the skein length before you buy.

If your pattern lists DMC skein counts and you want to use Weeks Dye Works instead, multiply the pattern's skein count by about 1.75. That accounts for the shorter skein length.

## What's the best way to track floss usage on a large project?

Keep a running tally with color number, total stitches, skeins bought, and skeins used. Update it as you stitch. On a project that takes months, memory is not reliable. A phone note or small notebook costs nothing. It prevents that frustrating moment when you run short of a color that has been discontinued.

Keep a simple tally sheet with color number, total stitches, skeins bought, and skeins used. Update it as you go.

For a sampler or large heirloom piece that takes months, it is easy to forget how many skeins of a color you started with. A small notebook or a note in your phone with each color's running count saves money and frustration. Some stitchers use the [Stitchbow or bobbins system](https://www.123stitch.com) to label each wound bobbin with the color number and [how many skeins they started with](/blog/the-physics-of-yarn-yardage-why-fiber-properties-matter-in-y/).

If you run out mid-project, try to buy from the same retailer batch if possible. For important background colors, buy more than you think you need upfront. A skein of DMC 3865 (white) bought six months from now may look slightly different under your project lighting than the one you started with.

## Can I use a leftover partial skein, and how do I measure what's left?

Yes, use your partials. Measuring them is easier than it sounds. Wrap the thread around a six-inch ruler and count the wraps. Each wrap equals twelve inches. Twenty wraps gives you roughly 6.6 yards. That is about 115 stitches on 14-count with two strands. That is plenty for small accent areas.

Store partials by color family in small zip bags or on labeled bobbins. That way you can find and use them instead of buying duplicates of colors you already own.

## The bottom line

Buy a little more than your calculation says. This is especially true for background colors and anything you cannot easily reorder in the same dye lot. The formula works. But thread is cheap compared to a half-finished project sitting in a drawer because you ran short by one skein.

## Frequently asked questions

### How many cross stitches can I get from one skein of embroidery floss?
A standard skein of embroidery floss (such as DMC or Anchor) contains about 8 meters (about 8.7 yards). It can make roughly 150 to 200 cross stitches on 14-count Aida cloth using two strands. The exact count depends on your fabric count, number of strands, and stitch tension. Higher fabric counts like 18 or 28 need less thread per stitch. So you get more stitches per skein.

### Does fabric count affect how many stitches I get per skein?
Yes, fabric count has a big effect on your stitch yield per skein. Higher-count fabrics like 18 or 28-count have smaller individual stitches. Each stitch uses less thread, so one skein goes further. On 14-count Aida you might get around 175 stitches with two strands. On 18-count you could get 250 or more. Always factor in your specific fabric count when estimating how many skeins a project needs.

### How many strands of floss should I use for cross stitch, and how does it affect skein usage?
Most cross stitch projects use two strands of floss. This balances coverage and thread use well. Using more strands, such as three or four, makes bolder and fuller stitches. But it uses thread much faster and lowers your stitch count per skein. Using a single strand stretches your skein further but may give lighter coverage. Always match your strand count to your fabric count for the best results.

### How do I calculate how many skeins I need for a cross stitch project?
Start by counting the total stitches in each color on your pattern. Then divide by your estimated stitches per skein for your fabric count and strand count. Add a 10 to 15% buffer for waste, mistakes, and finishing knots. Many cross stitch pattern software tools and online calculators can do this for you. When in doubt, buying one extra skein of each color is a safe and common practice.

### Does thread brand affect how many cross stitches I get per skein?
Thread brand can slightly affect stitch yield because skein lengths vary between makers. DMC skeins contain 8 meters of floss. Anchor skeins also offer about 8 meters. That makes them roughly equal. But some specialty or hand-dyed threads come in shorter skeins of 5 to 6 meters. That noticeably reduces your stitch count. Always check the skein length on the label or product page before buying thread for a large project.
