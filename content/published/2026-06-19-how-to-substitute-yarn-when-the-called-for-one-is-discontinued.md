# How to Substitute Yarn When the Called-For One Is Discontinued

When a yarn is discontinued, find a replacement by matching three things in order: fiber content, weight (yards per 100g is more reliable than the label weight), and ply structure. Swatch before you commit to yardage. Most substitutions fail because the knitter matched weight category but ignored fiber behavior or twist.

---

## Why does fiber content matter more than the label says?

Fiber content drives how a yarn behaves on the needle, how it blocks, and how it wears. A 100% merino and a 100% cotton labeled the same weight will knit up differently, block differently, and feel different against your skin. Matching "worsted" to "worsted" without checking fiber is the fastest way to end up with a sweater that disappoints you.

Wool has memory and bounce. Cotton has drape and weight. Acrylic has neither the drape of cotton nor the stretch of wool. When you substitute, ask yourself what the original yarn was doing in the pattern. A lace shawl in a silk-merino blend gets its drape from the silk. Swap that for a superwash wool and the shawl will block out but spring back. Not wrong, just different. If the pattern calls for a plant fiber, stay with plant fibers. If it calls for a wool-silk blend, a wool-silk or wool-linen blend will behave closer to the original than a 100% wool.

A useful tool: [Ravelry's yarn search](https://www.ravelry.com/yarns) lets you filter by fiber content, weight, and yards per 100g. It is the fastest way to find yarns that are actually comparable.

---

## How do I actually compare yarn weights?

Skip the weight category name and look at yards per 100g. Weight category names like "DK" or "worsted" are not standardized across brands. They mean almost nothing on their own. Yards per 100g is a real number you can compare.

The [Craft Yarn Council's standard yarn weight system](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) gives ranges. A DK weight runs roughly 200 to 250 yards per 100g. A worsted runs roughly 190 to 240 yards per 100g. Those ranges overlap. That is exactly why you cannot rely on the label alone. If the called-for yarn ran 220 yards per 100g and your substitute runs 190 yards per 100g, that is a real difference in thickness. You will need to swatch and possibly change your needle size.

Here is what to do: Write down the called-for yarn's yardage per 100g. Then search for substitutes within 10 to 15 yards of that number. If you are outside that range, you are already fighting the pattern.

## What is ply structure and do I really need to care about it?

Ply structure affects stitch definition and how yarn behaves under tension. Yes, it matters. It matters especially for cables and colorwork. A single-ply yarn and a 4-ply yarn at the same weight will look different in a cable pattern even if your gauge is right.

Single-ply yarns are soft and have a nice halo. But they split on the needle and blur stitch definition. A tightly plied 4-ply or 5-ply yarn will show cables sharply. It also holds up when you frog without falling apart. If the original yarn was a plied wool and you substitute a single, the texture of the finished fabric will look different. That is not always bad. A single-ply substitute in a stockinette sweater is usually fine. In a heavily cabled Aran sweater, you will notice the difference.

## How much yardage should I buy?

Buy at least as many yards as the pattern calls for, then add 10%. Substituting yarn almost always means your gauge swatch is close but not identical. A looser gauge uses more yarn. Running out mid-project is worse than having a leftover skein.

To figure out how much to buy: Take the total yards the pattern calls for. Multiply by 1.1. Then divide by the yards per skein of your substitute. Round up to the next whole skein. If a pattern calls for 1,200 yards and your substitute comes in 200-yard skeins, you need at least 1,320 yards. That is 6.6 skeins. Buy 7.

If you are buying online and cannot return the yarn, check whether the retailer sells partial skeins. Also check whether the yarn is easy to reorder. Dye lots matter. Buy from the same dye lot when you can.

## Where do I actually find good substitutes?

Ravelry is the most useful tool for this. Search the discontinued yarn and go to its page. Look for the "similar yarns" section. You can also browse the "used in projects" tab to see what other knitters have already swapped in. Someone has probably already done this substitution and noted whether it worked.

[Yarnsub.com](https://yarnsub.com) is a tool made just for finding substitutes. It compares fiber, weight, and yardage and gives you a match score. It is not perfect but it is a fast first step.

Your local yarn shop is also a great resource. If you can bring the pattern or a sample of the original yarn, a good shop employee can feel the yarn and give you a real opinion. That hands-on check is something no website can do.

---

## What if I can't find a close match?

Swatch a lot and measure as you go. If you are 10% off on yardage per 100g, try going down a needle size. If the fiber is different, block your swatch the same way you will block the finished piece. See how it behaves. A swatch that has been blocked and left for a day tells you more than a fresh-off-the-needle swatch.

The honest answer is that some substitutions are compromises. You might get a fabric that is close but not identical. That is okay. Patterns are instructions, not contracts. The goal is a finished object you are happy wearing.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I find a substitute for a discontinued yarn?
Start by writing down your original yarn's key details: weight category, fiber content, gauge, and yardage per skein. Compare those details against other yarns instead of relying on brand recommendations alone. Tools like the yarn substitution feature on FiberTools.app let you filter by all of these at once. Always swatch with the substitute before you commit. Even yarns with the same specs can behave differently because of twist, ply structure, and fiber blend.

### Does fiber content matter when substituting yarn?
Yes. Fiber content affects drape, stretch, stitch definition, and how you wash the finished piece. Swapping a wool yarn for cotton can change how a finished garment fits and feels. Cotton has no memory and is much heavier than wool. Try to match fiber families when you can. Use wool for wool and plant fiber for plant fiber, unless you want to change the fabric on purpose. Always check care instructions so you can wash the finished project the same way.

### What does it mean to match gauge when substituting yarn?
Matching gauge means your substitute yarn makes the same number of stitches and rows per inch as the original. This makes sure your finished piece comes out the right size. Gauge depends on yarn weight, fiber, twist, and your own tension. Even if a yarn is labeled the same weight as the original, always knit or crochet a swatch and measure it before you start. Adjust your needle or hook size as needed to hit the right numbers.

### How do I calculate yardage when substituting a different skein size?
Divide the total yardage the project needs by the yardage in your substitute skein. That tells you how many skeins to buy. For example, if a pattern calls for 1,000 yards and your new yarn comes in 200-yard skeins, you need at least 5 skeins. Always round up by one extra skein to allow for swatching and small changes in your tension. FiberTools.app can calculate this for you when you enter both yarns.

### Can I substitute a different yarn weight if I adjust my needle size?
This is generally not a good idea. Changing yarn weight changes more than just gauge. It changes drape, fabric density, stitch definition, and how much yarn you need. Going up or down a full weight category usually means you have to recalculate the whole pattern. Small adjustments within the same weight class, like a light DK versus a heavy sport, can sometimes work with needle size changes and careful swatching. But always check that your row and stitch gauge matches the pattern before you move forward.
