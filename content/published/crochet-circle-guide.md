# How to Crochet a Perfect Flat Circle: Increase Formula & Pattern Generator

A flat circle sounds easy. You work in the round and increase evenly. But anyone who has crocheted a few rounds knows the frustration. The circle cups up like a bowl, or it ruffles like a potato chip. The fix is not guesswork. It is math.

You might be making a hat crown, a round rug, an amigurumi base, or a coaster. No matter what you make, the number of increases per round depends on which stitch you use. Get that number wrong and you will fight your fabric the whole way.

> **A mistake I made:** My circles curled into bowls and my bowls flattened into plates. A flat circle needs the right number of increases each round. The [circle calculator](/circle-calculator) gives you the counts, and the [amigurumi shapes guide](/amigurumi-shapes) helps with toys.

## Why the Increase Number Matters

Every crochet stitch has a different height compared to its width. A taller stitch takes up more vertical space per row. So you need more increases per round to keep the fabric flat. Here is the breakdown:

| Stitch Type | Abbreviation | Increases Per Round |
|---|---|---|
| Single crochet | sc | 6 |
| Half double crochet | hdc | 8 |
| Double crochet | dc | 12 |
| Treble crochet | tr | 16 |

A single crochet circle starts with 6 stitches in your magic ring. It adds 6 stitches every round. By round 5, you will have 30 stitches total. A double crochet circle starts with 12 stitches and adds 12 per round. By round 5, that is 60 stitches.

These numbers are not random. They come from the geometry of a circle. Each round adds one stitch-width of circumference. The ratio of stitch height to width tells you how many new stitches fill that space.

## Round-by-Round Example: Single Crochet Flat Circle

Here is what a standard sc flat circle looks like for the first 8 rounds using staggered increases:

- **Round 1:** 6 sc in magic ring, *6 stitches*
- **Round 2:** 2 sc in each st around, *12 stitches*
- **Round 3:** (sc, 2 sc in next st) x 6, *18 stitches*
- **Round 4:** (sc in next 2 sts, 2 sc in next st) x 6, *24 stitches*
- **Round 5:** (sc in next 3 sts, 2 sc in next st) x 6, *30 stitches*
- **Round 6:** (sc in next 4 sts, 2 sc in next st) x 6, *36 stitches*
- **Round 7:** (sc in next 5 sts, 2 sc in next st) x 6, *42 stitches*
- **Round 8:** (sc in next 6 sts, 2 sc in next st) x 6, *48 stitches*

The pattern is steady. Each round, the number of plain stitches between increases goes up by one. At a typical gauge of 4 sc per inch, round 8 gives you a circle about 3.8 inches across.

## Staggered vs. Aligned Increases

This is where many crocheters run into trouble, or miss a good option.

**Aligned increases** place the increase stitch right above the increase from the round before. This creates visible "spokes" that go out from the center. Some people like this look for decorative pieces.

**Staggered increases** shift the increase position each round so the increases do not stack. This makes a rounder, smoother circle without a hexagon shape.

To stagger, shift where your increase falls within the repeat:

- **Round 3 (aligned):** (sc, inc) x 6
- **Round 3 (staggered):** (inc, sc) x 6

Then on round 4:

- **Round 4 (aligned):** (sc, sc, inc) x 6
- **Round 4 (staggered):** (sc, inc, sc) x 6

For rugs, coasters, and amigurumi bases, staggered increases almost always look better. For decorative mandalas or when you want a geometric look, aligned increases work well.

## Fixing Cupping and Ruffling

Two problems. Two causes. Two fixes.

**Cupping** (the circle bowls up like a hat):
- You do not have enough increases per round
- Your tension is too tight
- Fix: add 1 to 2 extra increases per round, or go up a hook size

**Ruffling** (the edges wave and will not lay flat):
- You have too many increases per round
- Your tension is too loose
- Fix: remove 1 to 2 increases per round, or go down a hook size

A common mistake is using the dc increase count (12) with single crochet stitches. That will give you a ruffle by round 3. Always match the increase count to your stitch type.

Another common problem is uneven tension between rounds. If your first few rounds are tight and the later rounds loosen up, you will get a circle that cups in the center and ruffles at the edge. Blocking can help, but steady tension is the real fix.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why does my crochet circle turn into a hexagon?
That hexagon shape happens when you place increases in the same spot every round. Those are called aligned increases. The six increase points create straight edges between them. Switch to staggered increases. Offset the increase position within each repeat and the circle rounds out. Most pattern generators, including ours, offer a staggered option.

### Can I use the flat circle formula for hdc or dc too?
Yes, but the increase count changes. For half double crochet, increase 8 stitches per round and start with 8 in the magic ring. For double crochet, increase 12 per round. For treble, it is 16. The formula grows with stitch height. Taller stitches need more increases because they cover more vertical space per round.

### How do I start a circle without a magic ring?
Chain 2 and work your starting stitches into the first chain. For single crochet, that is 6 sc into the chain. Pull the tail tight to close the small hole. This method works fine, but the center hole is usually a little larger than a magic ring. Some crocheters prefer to chain 4, slip stitch to join, then work into the ring.

### How many rounds do I need for a specific diameter?
Divide your target diameter by 2 to get the radius. Then multiply the radius by your row gauge, which is rounds per inch. For example, a 10-inch circle at 4 rounds per inch needs 20 rounds.
