# How to Read a Knitting Pattern: A Beginner's Guide

You found a great pattern. Maybe it is a cozy cabled hat, a delicate lace shawl, or your very first scarf. You download it, open the PDF, and it looks like another language. Asterisks, brackets, and abbreviations like "ssk" and "yo" can look like a secret code. The good news? Once you crack that code, every pattern in the world opens up to you.

This guide walks through every section of a knitting pattern. It explains the most common abbreviations and shows how repeats work. It also shows you how the [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) and [Row Counter](/row-counter) keep you on track so you never lose your place.

## What a Knitting Pattern Actually Contains

A well-written knitting pattern has the same sections every time. Learning each one before you pick up your needles saves a lot of confusion later.

**Materials list.** This tells you the yarn, needle size, and other supplies you need. The yarn info includes weight, fiber, and yardage. Pay attention to the yarn weight number, such as Worsted/4 or DK/3. Also check the total yardage, not just the number of skeins.

**Finished measurements.** These are the dimensions of the finished project. They are not your body measurements. The difference between your body measurement and the finished measurement is called ease.

**Gauge.** This might look like "20 stitches and 26 rows = 4 inches in stockinette stitch." If your gauge does not match the pattern, your finished piece will not match the listed measurements.

**Abbreviations key.** Most patterns list every abbreviation they use. Always read this section first. The same abbreviation can mean different things in different designers' patterns.

**Instructions.** These are the row-by-row or round-by-round directions for your project.

## Common Knitting Abbreviations You Need to Know

Here are the abbreviations you will see in almost every pattern:

| Abbreviation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| k / p | knit / purl |
| st(s) | stitch(es) |
| CO / BO | cast on / bind off |
| RS / WS | right side / wrong side |
| yo | yarn over (creates a hole/increase) |
| k2tog | knit two together (right-leaning decrease) |
| ssk | slip, slip, knit (left-leaning decrease) |
| sl | slip a stitch without working it |
| pm / sm | place marker / slip marker |
| rep | repeat |
| MC / CC | main color / contrast color |
| dpn | double-pointed needles |
| rnd | round (for circular knitting) |

Keep this table nearby when you sit down with a new pattern. Over time, these abbreviations will feel natural.

## How to Read Repeats: Asterisks, Brackets, and Parentheses

Repeats are where most beginners get confused. Patterns use shorthand so they do not have to write out every stitch across a 200-stitch row.

**Asterisks** mark a section you repeat: `*K2, p2; rep from * to end of row.` This means knit 2, purl 2, then go back to the asterisk and repeat until the end of the row.

**Brackets or parentheses** group stitches that are worked together or repeated a set number of times:

- `[K1, yo, k1] in next stitch` means all three actions happen in one stitch.
- `(K3, p1) 4 times` means you work that sequence exactly four times.

**Multiple sizes** also use parentheses: `CO 80 (88, 96, 104, 112) sts.` If you are making the medium, follow the second number in every set of parentheses throughout the whole pattern.

## Reading Charts vs. Written Instructions

Many patterns include both a chart and written instructions. Charts are visual grids where each square stands for one stitch. Symbols inside the squares tell you what to do.

**Key chart-reading rules:**

- Read RS (right-side) rows from right to left. That is the same direction you are knitting.
- Read WS (wrong-side) rows from left to right.
- For circular knitting, read every row from right to left. You are always on the RS.
- Bold outlines usually mark the pattern repeat section.
- A symbol key, also called a legend, is always provided. Never guess what a symbol means.

Charts are very helpful for lace and colorwork. You can see the overall design at a glance. Written instructions for a 24-row lace repeat can look like a jumble of letters, but the chart shows you the pattern's shape right away. Neither format is better than the other. Use whichever one works best for you.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

**Read the entire pattern first.** Do not just jump in at row 1. Skim the whole thing so you know what is coming, including increases, shaping, and finishing steps. You will spot confusing sections before your needles are full of stitches.

**Make a gauge swatch.** A 5x5 inch swatch tells you if you need to change your needle size. A half-stitch-per-inch difference can mean a sweater that is 4 inches too wide.

**Highlight your size.** For patterns with multiple sizes, go through every instruction and highlight the number that applies to you. This keeps you from switching sizes by accident partway through.

**Check stitch counts.** Many patterns include a count at the end of a row, like "(96 sts)." This is where the [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) really helps. Catching an error on row 5 is much easier than catching it on row 50.

**Watch for errata.** Many designers publish corrections after a pattern is released. Check their website or Ravelry page for errata before you start.

## Real Project Examples

**Example 1: A simple ribbed scarf.** The pattern says: `CO 42 sts. Row 1 (RS): *K2, p2; rep from * to last 2 sts, k2. Row 2 (WS): *P2, k2; rep from * to last 2 sts, p2. Rep Rows 1 to 2 until piece measures 60 inches. BO in pattern.`

Here is what that means. You cast on 42 stitches. On the right side, you alternate knit 2 and purl 2, ending with knit 2. On the wrong side, you reverse it. You repeat those two rows for the whole scarf. "BO in pattern" means you knit the knits and purl the purls as you bind off.

**Example 2: Following a multi-size hat.** The pattern says: `CO 80 (88, 96) sts. Join to work in the rnd, being careful not to twist. Rnd 1: *K2, p2; rep from * around.`

You are making the medium, so you cast on 88 stitches. You join them in a circle on circular needles. Then you work k2, p2 ribbing around and around. The [Row Counter](/row-counter) tracks how many rounds that takes. That way you can copy it if you make the hat again.

**Example 3: A lace pattern row.** `Row 1 (RS): K3, *yo, ssk, k4; rep from * to last 5 sts, yo, ssk, k3. (48 sts)`

You knit 3. Then you repeat the yarn-over, slip-slip-knit, knit-4 sequence until 5 stitches are left. The "(48 sts)" at the end confirms your count. Check it with the Stitch Counter.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between RS and WS rows?

The right side (RS) is the side people see when you wear or display the finished piece. The wrong side (WS) faces inward. Patterns label rows so you know which side you are looking at. This matters because many stitches look different from each side.

### Why do patterns use asterisks instead of writing out every stitch?

Asterisks create a short way to show repeated sequences. Writing "knit 2, purl 2" fifty times across a 200-stitch row would be very hard to read. The asterisk system shrinks a full row down to one short line. That makes instructions much easier to follow.

### Do I have to make a gauge swatch for every project?

For anything that needs to fit, like sweaters, hats, socks, and mittens, yes. A half-stitch difference per inch can change a sweater's width by several inches. For scarves, blankets, and dishcloths where exact size does not matter much, you can usually skip the swatch and adjust as you go.

### How do I keep track of which row I'm on in a long pattern?

Use the [Row Counter](/row-counter) to log each finished row. Some knitters also use a sticky note placed below the current row on a printed pattern. They move it down as they go. For complex stitch patterns, using the Stitch Counter for each repeat section helps prevent miscounts.

## Start Reading Patterns with Confidence

Knitting patterns are not a foreign language. They are a shorthand system built to save space and keep instructions clear. Once you learn the abbreviations, understand how repeats work, and know how to find your size, every pattern becomes easier to tackle.

Start with a simple scarf or dishcloth pattern to practice reading the format. Use the [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) and [Row Counter](/row-counter) to track your progress. That way you can focus on understanding the instructions instead of recounting stitches. Before long, you will be reading lace charts and cable panels without breaking a sweat.
