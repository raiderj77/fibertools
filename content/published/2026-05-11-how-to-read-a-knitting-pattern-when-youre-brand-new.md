# How to Read a Knitting Pattern When You're Brand New

[Reading a knitting pattern is mostly about learning a compact shorthand](/blog/how-to-read-a-crochet-pattern-for-beginners/). Patterns use abbreviations, asterisks, and brackets to avoid printing the same instruction forty times. Once you know that **k** means knit, **p** means purl, and brackets mean "repeat this chunk," about 80% of any beginner pattern unlocks itself.

---

## What do all those abbreviations actually mean?

Every abbreviation stands for a specific stitch or action. You do not need to memorize them before you start. Just keep a reference list open beside you until they stick.

The most common ones you will see in a first project are:

- **k** -- knit
- **p** -- purl
- **co** -- cast on
- **bo** or **bind off** -- bind off (sometimes written as "cast off")
- **st / sts** -- stitch / stitches
- **rep** -- repeat
- **RS / WS** -- right side / wrong side
- **yo** -- yarn over
- **k2tog** -- knit two stitches together (a decrease)
- **sl** -- slip a stitch without knitting it

The [Craft Yarn Council maintains a standardized abbreviation list](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/knit-abbreviations) that most US patterns follow. When a pattern uses something unusual, it will define it in a "special stitches" or "abbreviations" section near the top. Always read that section first, even before you look at the actual instructions.

---

## What do the asterisks and brackets mean?

Asterisks and brackets are repeat markers. They tell you to work a set of stitches more than once. The pattern does not have to write it out every single time.

Here is what that looks like:

> *k3, p1; rep from * to end

That means: knit 3, purl 1, then keep repeating that all the way to the end of the row. The asterisk marks where the repeat starts. You keep cycling through that chunk until you run out of stitches.

Brackets work the same way. They usually tell you how many times to repeat a smaller section within a row:

> k2, [k1, p1] 4 times, k2

Work k2, then work (k1, p1) exactly 4 times, then work k2. The number after the bracket tells you the count.

If you lose your place, use a row counter or mark a printed copy with a pencil. Trying to hold it all in your head is where new knitters get into trouble.

---

## What does the pattern mean by "gauge" and can I skip it?

Gauge is [the number of stitches and rows that fit into a 4-inch square](/blog/when-gauge-is-no-gauge-how-yarn-weight-impacts-crochet-fabri/) using [a specific yarn and needle size](/blog/demystifying-yarn-weights-for-crochet-using-the-yarn-weight-/). Skipping gauge is the fastest way to end up with a hat that fits a cantaloupe or a sweater that is way too big.

A pattern might say: **20 sts x 28 rows = 4 inches in stockinette on US 7 needles.** That is the designer's gauge. If your swatch gives you 22 stitches over 4 inches, your stitches are smaller than the designer's. Your finished piece will come out narrower than planned. Go up a needle size and swatch again.

[The Craft Yarn Council recommends knitting a swatch at least 6 inches square](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/gauge), then measuring the center 4 inches. This avoids edge distortion. Wash and block the swatch the same way you will wash the finished item. Yarn can shift a full stitch per inch after washing. Measure as you go on the actual project too, not just at the start.

---

## What does "multiple of X stitches" mean in the cast-on instructions?

It means the stitch pattern only works if your stitch count is divisible by that number. The pattern is telling you the math it needs to come out even.

For example, "cast on a multiple of 4 sts" means 12, 16, 20, or 24 stitches. Any number divisible by 4 will work. If the pattern says "multiple of 4 sts, plus 2," then add 2 to any of those: 14, 18, 22, or 26.

This matters most when you adjust a pattern to a different size. If your stitch count is off by even one, the repeat will not line up at the end of the row. You will be staring at your needles wondering what went wrong.

---

## How do I figure out which size to make?

Look at the finished measurements, not the size label. Pattern sizes labeled S, M, L, or 1, 2, 3 vary a lot between designers.

Find the finished chest, length, or circumference measurement in the schematic. The schematic is a small diagram, usually at the end of the pattern. Compare that number to something you already own and wear. Most patterns are written with a few inches of ease. Ease means extra room built in. A fitted sweater might have 1 to 2 inches of ease. An oversized pullover might have 6 or more. The pattern notes will usually tell you what ease was intended.

If there is no schematic, measure a similar item you own. Then pick the pattern size whose finished measurements come closest.

---

## What should I actually do before I cast on?

Read the whole pattern once before you pick up your needles. It takes about five minutes. It saves you from hitting a surprise technique mid-row with no idea what is coming.

One full read-through helps you catch things like "at the same time" instructions. Those are spots where two things happen at once. You might also spot an unusual construction method or a technique you have never tried. Look those things up before you are in the middle of a row. Note any abbreviations you do not know and look them up right away.

Print the pattern if you can. Or use an app that lets you highlight and add notes. Keep a pencil, a row counter, and a few stitch markers within reach. A cluttered workspace is where mistakes happen.

Pattern reading gets faster every time. The first pattern feels like a foreign language. By the fifth one, you will just scan for the parts that are actually new.

## Frequently asked questions

### What do all the abbreviations in a knitting pattern mean?
Knitting abbreviations are short codes for specific stitches or actions. For example, "k" means knit and "p" means purl. Most patterns include a key at the beginning that defines every abbreviation used. If yours does not, search for "standard knitting abbreviations" to find the most common ones. Fibertools.app also offers resources to help you decode unfamiliar terms as you work through your first pattern.

### How do I know what size needles and yarn to use for a knitting pattern?
Every knitting pattern lists a recommended needle size and yarn weight in the materials section, usually near the top. These are based on getting the correct gauge, which affects the finished size of your project. Using a different yarn weight or needle size can change how your finished piece looks and fits. Always check this section before buying supplies so your project turns out as planned.

### What is gauge and do I really need to swatch before starting a knitting pattern?
Gauge is the number of stitches and rows that fit into a set measurement, usually 4 inches. Yes, swatching is worth it. Knitting a small test swatch lets you check whether your tension matches the pattern's gauge. If your swatch is off, you can adjust your needle size before starting the full project. Skipping this step is one of the most common reasons finished knitting projects end up the wrong size.

### What does it mean when a knitting pattern says to repeat a section?
A repeat instruction tells you to work the same set of stitches more than once across a row or round. Repeats are usually shown inside asterisks, brackets, or parentheses, like "repeat from * to end." Count your stitches carefully before starting so you have the right number for the repeat to work out evenly. Placing stitch markers at the start of each repeat can help you stay on track and catch mistakes early.

### How do I follow a knitting pattern that has instructions for multiple sizes?
Multi-size patterns list different numbers separated by parentheses, like "cast on 80 (90, 100, 110) sts." Each number matches a different size. Before you begin, find your size and circle or highlight every number that applies to you throughout the whole pattern. Using the wrong set of numbers is an easy mistake to make mid-project. Taking five minutes to mark your size on a printed or digital copy will save a lot of frustration later.
