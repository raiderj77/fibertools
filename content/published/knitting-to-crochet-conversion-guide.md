# How to Convert Knitting Patterns to Crochet (and Vice Versa)

You found the perfect sweater pattern. It has gorgeous cables, a flattering shape, and a great neckline. But there is one problem. It is a knitting pattern, and you crochet. Or maybe the reverse. Perhaps you are a lifelong knitter who spotted a crochet blanket pattern that would look great on your couch.

Converting between knitting and crochet is not a direct stitch-for-stitch swap. But it is absolutely doable. The two crafts make different fabrics. They use yarn at different rates. They also have different gauges. Once you understand those differences, you can adapt most simple to moderate patterns from one craft to the other.

## What Changes When You Convert Between Crafts?

Knitting and crochet make fabrics that are built differently. Knit stockinette is thin, smooth, and drapey. Crochet single crochet is thicker, stiffer, and about 30% heavier per square inch. That means you cannot just replace "knit" with "single crochet" and expect the same result.

**Fabric thickness.** A crochet fabric is roughly 1.5 to 2 times as thick as a knit fabric made with the same yarn. Crochet garments have more structure. Knit garments have more drape.

**Stitch height.** Knit rows are short. In worsted stockinette, each row is about 0.15 inches tall. Crochet rows are taller. Single crochet rows are about 0.3 inches tall. Half double crochet rows are about 0.5 inches tall. Double crochet rows are about 0.75 inches tall.

**Yarn consumption.** Crochet uses about 25 to 35% more yarn than knitting for the same finished size. A knit sweater that needs 1,200 yards will need 1,500 to 1,600 yards in crochet. Plan your yarn budget for this.

**Stitch count.** Knitting usually makes more stitches per inch than crochet in the same yarn weight. Worsted stockinette runs about 18 stitches per 4 inches. Worsted single crochet runs about 14 to 16 stitches per 4 inches.

## How Do You Convert a Pattern Step by Step?

### Step 1: Swatch in Both Crafts

Make a 6-inch gauge swatch in the original craft's stitch pattern. Then make another 6-inch swatch in the crochet or knit stitch you plan to use instead. Block both swatches. Measure the stitches per inch and rows per inch for each one.

Example: Your knitting swatch measures 4.5 stitches per inch in stockinette. Your crochet swatch measures 3.5 stitches per inch in single crochet. That gives you a conversion ratio of 3.5 divided by 4.5, which equals 0.78.

### Step 2: Calculate the Stitch Conversion Ratio

Divide your new craft's gauge by the original pattern's gauge.

Say the pattern tells you to cast on 80 stitches for a 20-inch-wide front panel at 4 stitches per inch. Your crochet gauge is 3.5 stitches per inch. Your new chain count is: 20 inches times 3.5 stitches, which equals 70 stitches.

### Step 3: Calculate the Row Conversion Ratio

Do the same thing with row gauge. Say the pattern says to work 120 rows for 18 inches at 6.7 rows per inch. Your crochet makes 4 rows per inch. Your new row count is: 18 inches times 4 rows, which equals 72 rows.

### Step 4: Adjust Shaping

Recalculate any increases, decreases, or armhole shaping based on your new stitch and row counts. This is where the [Gauge Calculator](/gauge-calculator) becomes essential. Enter both gauges and the tool shows you how stitch counts translate.

### Step 5: Recalculate Yarn

Multiply the original yarn estimate by 1.25 to 1.35 if you are converting from knit to crochet. Divide by 1.25 to 1.35 if you are going the other direction.

## What Are the Stitch Equivalencies?

Not all stitch substitutions work equally well. Here is a guide for matching knit stitch patterns to crochet equivalents:

| Knit Stitch | Best Crochet Substitute | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Stockinette | Half double crochet (hdc) | Closest in drape and thickness |
| Garter stitch | Single crochet (sc) | Both produce a dense, textured fabric |
| Ribbing (k1p1) | Front post / back post dc | Creates elastic, ribbed texture |
| Seed stitch | Linen stitch or moss stitch | Textured, reversible |
| Cables | Crochet cables (FP stitches) | Possible but uses much more yarn |
| Lace | Crochet mesh or filet | Different aesthetic but similar openness |

**Half double crochet is the best general-purpose substitute for stockinette.** It makes a fabric with similar weight and drape. Its gauge is also closer to knit stockinette than single crochet, which is denser, or double crochet, which is more open.

Worsted weight stockinette at 18 stitches per 4 inches converts roughly to half double crochet at 14 to 15 stitches per 4 inches. Always swatch to confirm your own numbers.

## What Are the Common Mistakes and Limitations?

**Not all patterns convert well.** Complex colorwork like Fair Isle uses stranded yarn carried behind the fabric. That technique does not translate to crochet. Crochet colorwork, such as tapestry crochet, exists. But it makes a much thicker and stiffer fabric.

**Cables convert but get bulky.** Crochet cables made with front post stitches are thicker than knit cables. They also use roughly 40% more yarn. They look beautiful but feel heavy. Think about using DK weight yarn for a crochet cable project when the knit pattern calls for worsted.

**Do not forget about edge differences.** Crochet edges are naturally cleaner than knit edges. If the knit pattern includes picked-up stitches for a collar or border, you may be able to skip that step in crochet. You can simply work directly along the edge instead.

**Yarn weight matters more than you think.** Going down one yarn weight in crochet, such as using DK instead of worsted, can make a fabric that feels closer to the original knit. Swatch both and compare how they feel before starting a full project.

**Row gauge is harder to match than stitch gauge.** Most conversion problems show up in the length, not the width. If your row gauge does not match, adjust by working to measurements instead of row counts. Work until the piece measures 14 inches rather than working 96 rows.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does crochet really use 30% more yarn than knitting?

Yes, roughly 25 to 35% more depending on the stitch. Crochet stitches wrap the yarn around the hook more times than knitting wraps it around the needle. Single crochet uses about 25% more yarn than stockinette. Double crochet uses about 35% more. Half double crochet falls in between at about 30%.

### Can I convert any knitting pattern to crochet?

Simple patterns, like scarves, blankets, and basic sweater shapes, convert well. Patterns with complex techniques like short rows, stranded colorwork, or intricate lace are much harder. The basic shape and proportions convert. But specific construction techniques do not always have crochet equivalents.

### Should I use the same yarn weight when converting?

Not always. Crochet fabric is thicker than knit fabric in the same yarn. Going down one weight, such as DK instead of worsted, can make a fabric that feels more similar to the original knit. Swatch in both the original weight and one step lighter. Then pick the fabric that has the drape you want.

### How do I convert shaping instructions?

Recalculate all increases and decreases based on your new gauge. Say the pattern tells you to decrease 1 stitch every 6 rows. Figure out how many inches that covers in the original gauge. Then calculate how many rows that equals in your new gauge. The [Gauge Calculator](/gauge-calculator) handles this conversion math for you.
