# How to Calculate Yarn for Shawls and Wraps

Shawls can fool you. The size is flexible, so they seem easy. But the triangle or crescent shape makes it hard to guess how much yarn you need. A triangular shawl does not use half the yarn of a rectangle with the same wingspan. It uses much more than you would expect. Each row gets longer than the one before it.

Running out of yarn on the last 10 rows of a shawl is very common. Here is how to calculate yardage so that does not happen to you.

## Why Are Shawls Hard to Estimate?

Most projects are rectangles. You multiply width by length, factor in gauge, and you have your yardage. Shawls break that formula because their shapes grow in uneven ways.

**Triangular shawls** start with just a few stitches and grow every row. Row 1 might have 3 stitches. Row 100 might have 203 stitches. The last 20 rows of a triangular shawl use more yarn than the first 60 rows combined. This fast growth surprises a lot of crafters.

**Crescent shawls** curve along the top edge using short rows or uneven increases. The shaping makes them hard to estimate because row lengths change in unpredictable ways.

**Rectangular wraps** are the easiest to estimate. They are just very wide scarves. Width times length times gauge equals yardage.

The key to a good estimate is to know your shawl's shape. Calculate the total stitch count, not just the size. Then add a safety buffer.

## How Do You Calculate Shawl Yardage Step by Step?

### Method 1: The Gauge Swatch Method (Most Accurate)

1. **Knit or crochet a 6-inch gauge swatch** in your shawl's stitch pattern.
2. **Measure the yarn used.** Before you swatch, mark your yarn at 1-yard intervals with small ties. Count how many yards the swatch used.
3. **Calculate yards per square inch.** If your 6x6 swatch (36 square inches) used 22 yards, that is 0.61 yards per square inch.
4. **Calculate your shawl's area.** For a triangle: (wingspan times depth) divided by 2. A shawl with a 60-inch wingspan and 30-inch depth equals 900 square inches. For a rectangle: wingspan times depth.
5. **Multiply.** 900 square inches times 0.61 yards equals 549 yards. Add a 15% buffer: 631 yards.

## What Factors Change Shawl Yardage?

**Stitch pattern matters more than you think.** Lace patterns use less yarn than stockinette. The yarn overs create holes, and holes use no yarn. A lace shawl in fingering weight might use 600 yards. A stockinette shawl the same size might need 800 yards. Textured patterns like cables or bobbles use even more yarn.

**Yarn weight has an opposite relationship with yardage.** Fingering weight shawls need more yards but less weight. Worsted weight shawls need fewer yards but more weight. A 650-yard fingering skein might weigh 100 grams. A 200-yard worsted skein also weighs 100 grams. You will use 650 yards of fingering versus 450 yards of worsted for a similar-sized shawl.

**Blocking changes everything.** Lace shawls grow 15 to 30% during blocking. A shawl that measures 48 inches off the needles might stretch to 58 inches after a good wet blocking. If you want specific finished dimensions, measure after blocking and factor in the stretch.

**Crochet shawls use more yarn.** A crochet shawl uses about 25 to 30% more yarn than a knit shawl of the same size. A knit triangular shawl that needs 600 yards in fingering weight means the crochet version needs 750 to 780 yards.

## What Are Common Mistakes and Tips?

**The biggest mistake: underestimating the last section.** In a top-down triangular shawl, the final 25% of rows holds about 40 to 50% of the total stitches. If you have 100 yards left and think you can get 15 more rows, you might only get 8. Weigh your remaining yarn and calculate carefully.

**Weigh your yarn, do not guess.** A kitchen scale accurate to 1 gram costs about $10. It will save you from yarn chicken forever. Weigh your full skein before you start. Weigh what is left from time to time. If your 100-gram skein has 400 yards and you have used 60 grams, you have about 160 yards remaining.

**Plan your bind-off yarn.** The bind-off row is the longest row in a shawl. It needs to be stretchy. Set aside at least 3 times the wingspan in yarn for the bind-off. For a 60-inch shawl: 180 inches equals 5 yards minimum. For a decorative picot bind-off, set aside 6 to 8 times the wingspan.

**Buy one extra skein.** Shawl yarn is often hand-dyed or indie-dyed in small batches. If you run out, the same colorway may not be available again. One extra 400-yard skein of fingering costs $15 to $25 and gives you peace of mind. Return it unopened if you do not need it.

## What Do Real Shawl Projects Look Like?

**The one-skein wonder.** A knitter planned a small triangular shawl from a single 400-yard skein of fingering weight merino. She used a lace pattern and calculated she needed about 350 yards for a 48x20 inch shawl. She finished with 40 yards left. That was enough for a stretchy bind-off and a little safety margin.

**The oversized wrap.** A crocheter made a rectangular wrap in DK weight. It was 70 inches wide and 24 inches deep, worked in half double crochet. Her calculation: 70 times 24 equals 1,680 square inches, times 0.5 yards per square inch equals 840 yards, plus 15% equals 966 yards. She bought 1,050 yards (6 skeins) and finished with 80 yards to spare.

**The yarn chicken loss.** A knitter working a top-down triangular shawl in worsted weight reached row 88 of 95 and ran out. She had estimated 500 yards but needed 580. The last 7 rows alone would have used 80 yards. She frogged 12 rows and worked a shorter shawl, finishing the bind-off at row 82. The smaller shawl still measured a solid 52 inches across.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much yarn do I need for a triangular shawl?

A medium triangular shawl (56-inch wingspan, 26-inch depth) uses 550 to 700 yards in fingering weight, 450 to 600 in sport, or 350 to 450 in worsted. The final quarter of the rows uses almost half the total yarn because each row is longer than the last. Always add 15% to your estimate for safety.

### Why do shawl patterns often call for fingering weight yarn?

Fingering weight creates a light, drapey fabric that wraps nicely around your shoulders. It also shows lace patterns more clearly than heavier yarns do. A fingering-weight shawl weighs 100 to 150 grams (3.5 to 5.3 ounces) even at large sizes, so it is comfortable to wear. Heavier weights make warmer but bulkier shawls.

### Should I buy extra yarn for a shawl?

Yes, always. Buy one extra skein beyond what you calculated. Shawl yarn, especially hand-dyed or indie-dyed, comes in small batches that may not be restocked. If you do not use the extra skein, most shops accept returns within 30 to 60 days. Running out 10 rows from the finish is much worse than having a little left over.

### How do I adjust a shawl pattern if I have limited yarn?

Work the pattern until you have used half your yarn, then measure your shawl depth. You will know about how many more rows you can work. If you are behind your target size, switch to a needle or hook that is 1 to 2 sizes larger. That will stretch your remaining yarn over more area. The fabric will be a little looser, but shawls handle gauge changes well.
