# The Four Basic Amigurumi Shapes: Spheres, Cones, Cylinders, and Ovals

Every amigurumi project breaks down into four basic shapes. Those shapes are spheres, cones, cylinders, and ovals. It does not matter if you are making a tiny cat, a chunky dinosaur, or a stack of fruit. Learn the math behind these shapes and you can build almost anything without a pattern.

This guide covers the stitch counts, increase and decrease formulas, and construction steps for each shape. You will also learn how to use the [Amigurumi Shapes Calculator](/amigurumi-shapes) to get custom stitch counts for any size you need.

## What Are the Basic Amigurumi Shapes?

Amigurumi uses single crochet (sc) worked in continuous spiral rounds. This creates a dense fabric that holds stuffing well. The shape of each piece depends on where you place your increases and decreases.

Here is what each shape is used for:

- **Sphere**, Heads, bodies, round fruits, balls
- **Cone**, Horns, beaks, carrots, Christmas trees, limbs that taper
- **Cylinder**, Arms, legs, fingers, tails, straight body sections
- **Oval**, Shoe soles, animal bodies, elongated heads, muzzles

Once you know the stitch math for these four shapes, you can change the proportions to make any 3D shape you want.

## Sphere: Round-by-Round Construction

The sphere is the most basic shape. Here is the formula that makes it work.

**The Rule of Six**: Increase by 6 stitches every round during the top half. Then decrease by 6 stitches every round during the bottom half.

### Standard Sphere Pattern (6-stitch start)

| Round | Stitch Count | Instructions |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| 1 | 6 | 6 sc in magic ring |
| 2 | 12 | (inc) x 6 |
| 3 | 18 | (sc, inc) x 6 |
| 4 | 24 | (2 sc, inc) x 6 |
| 5 | 30 | (3 sc, inc) x 6 |
| 6 | 36 | (4 sc, inc) x 6 |
| 7 to 9 | 36 | sc in each st around (3 rounds even) |
| 10 | 30 | (3 sc, dec) x 6 |
| 11 | 24 | (2 sc, dec) x 6 |
| 12 | 18 | (sc, dec) x 6 |
| 13 | 12 | (dec) x 6 |
| 14 | 6 | (dec) x 3, fasten off |

The number of even rounds at the widest point changes how the sphere looks. Three even rounds makes a nearly perfect ball. One even round makes a flatter disc shape. Five or more even rounds starts to look like a cylinder with rounded caps.

**Sizing tip**: You can figure out the diameter of your sphere using the max stitch count, your gauge, and pi. Or you can just enter your numbers into the calculator.

### Magic Ring Start

The magic ring is also called an adjustable ring or magic loop. It gives you a tight center with no gap. Pull the tail tight after your first round of 6 sc. This closes the hole completely and keeps stuffing from poking through.

## Cone: Taper Rates and Construction

A cone starts the same way as a sphere. You use a magic ring and 6 sc. But you space the increase rounds differently. This creates a gradual taper instead of a curved surface.

### Slow Taper (Tall, Narrow Cone)

Increase every other round. This works well for unicorn horns, long carrots, and elongated beaks.

- Round 1: 6 sc in magic ring
- Round 2: 6 sc (even)
- Round 3: 12 sc (inc round)
- Round 4: 12 sc (even)
- Round 5: 18 sc (inc round)
- Keep alternating until you reach the width you want

### Fast Taper (Short, Wide Cone)

Increase every round, just like the top half of a sphere. This gives you a wide, shallow cone. It works well for hats, mushroom caps, and wide tree shapes.

### Medium Taper

Increase every third round. This is the most common choice for limbs and moderate cone shapes. A cone that goes from 6 sc to 36 sc with increases every third round will be about 15 rounds tall.

The key thing to control is the **increase interval**. That is how many even rounds come between each increase round. You can set this directly in the calculator.

## Cylinder: Straight Sides Made Simple

Cylinders are the easiest shape to make. You work the increase section like the top of a sphere. Then you work even rounds for as long as you need. After that, you can leave the bottom open or close it with decreases.

### Standard Cylinder Formula

1. Work increase rounds up to the circumference you want. For example, go from 6 to 12 to 18 to 24 stitches for a 24-stitch cylinder.
2. Work sc in each stitch around for as many rounds as you need to reach your target height.
3. Stuff as you go. It is hard to stuff evenly after the piece is closed.
4. Close with decrease rounds or leave open for sewing.

For a cylinder that is 1.5 inches wide using worsted weight at 5 sc per inch, increase to about 24 stitches. Here is why: the circumference is 1.5 x pi, which is about 4.7 inches. Multiply 4.7 by 5 sc per inch and you get about 24 sc. Round to the nearest multiple of 6.

### Keeping Cylinders Straight

Spiral rounds can create a visible seam line that spirals up the piece. This does not change the shape. But if it bothers you, move your stitch marker a few stitches every few rounds. Some crocheters use joined rounds instead. This creates a slightly different texture.

## Oval: Elongated Shapes

Ovals start with a foundation chain instead of a magic ring. You work around both sides of the chain. Then you increase at each end to form the rounded tips.

### Basic Oval Formula

1. Chain this many stitches: (desired length minus desired width) plus 1. Then add 1 turning chain.
2. Sc in the 2nd chain from the hook. Sc across to the last chain.
3. Work 3 sc in the last chain. This turns the corner.
4. Sc along the other side of the chain.
5. Work 2 sc in the last stitch. Then join or continue in a spiral.
6. Keep increasing at each end for the next rounds. Add 3 stitches at each tip per round.

For a 3-inch by 1.5-inch oval at 5 sc per inch: chain (3 minus 1.5) plus 1, which equals 8, plus 1 turning chain. After 4 to 5 rounds of increases at the tips, you will reach your target size.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

**Stitch count drift** is the most common problem. Count your stitches at the end of every round. Use a stitch marker to track the first stitch of each round. In spiral crochet, rounds blend together. It is easy to miss a stitch or add an extra one.

**Stuffing timing matters.** Stuff spheres and cones before the opening gets too small. For cylinders, stuff in sections as you go. Polyester fiberfill should feel firm but should not stretch the fabric.

**Hook size affects density.** For amigurumi, use a hook that is 1 to 2 sizes smaller than what the yarn label says. Worsted weight (CYC 4) yarn that calls for a 5mm hook should be crocheted with a 3.5mm hook for amigurumi. The tight fabric keeps stuffing from showing through.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Forgetting to close the magic ring before Round 2
- Placing increases directly above each other every round. This causes hexagonal shapes in spheres. Stagger them instead.
- Mixing up US and UK terms. In US patterns, "dc" means double crochet. That is not the same as UK double crochet, which is US single crochet.
- Skipping stitch counts and ending up with lopsided shapes

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many stitches should I start with in a magic ring for amigurumi?

Start with 6 single crochet for most amigurumi shapes. This gives you clean 6-stitch increase steps that produce smooth, round results. Some patterns use 8 sc for a rounder look. But 6 is the standard starting point for spheres, cones, and cylinders.

### Why does my amigurumi sphere look hexagonal instead of round?

You are stacking increases directly above each other every round. The fix is to offset your increases. Some patterns do this by shifting the start of each round. You still use 6 increases per round. But staggering their placement round by round smooths out the corners.

### What yarn weight works best for amigurumi?

Worsted weight (CYC 4) with a 3.5mm hook is the most popular choice. It works up quickly. The fabric is dense enough to hide stuffing. And the finished pieces end up a practical size. DK weight (CYC 3) with a 3.0mm hook gives finer detail for smaller projects.

### How do I calculate yarn yardage for an amigurumi project?

Count the total stitches across all rounds. Then multiply by your average stitch length. For worsted weight sc, that is about 0.5 inches per stitch. A 36-round sphere with an average of 24 stitches per round uses about 864 stitches. That is roughly 36 yards.
