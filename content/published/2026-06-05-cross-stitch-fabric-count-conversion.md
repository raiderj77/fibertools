# Cross Stitch Fabric Count Conversion: What the Numbers Actually Mean

**The short answer:** Fabric count tells you [how many stitches fit in one inch](/blog/cross-stitch-count/). To convert between counts, divide the count number into the total stitch width or height of your design. A 100-stitch-wide pattern on 14-count aida covers about 7.1 inches. The same pattern on 28-count evenweave stitched over two threads also covers 7.1 inches. Count and thread-over determine everything.

---

## What does "fabric count" actually mean?

Fabric count is the [number of holes (or threads) per inch in your ground fabric](/blog/cross-stitch-fabric-count/). On 14-count aida, you get 14 stitches per inch. On 18-count, you get 18 stitches per inch. That one number controls how large or small your finished piece will be.

This is not a rough guess. The [Zweigart fabric specifications](https://www.zweigart.de/en/) for their standard 14-count aida list exactly 14 holes per inch. Their 18-count lists 18. When makers say "count," they mean a steady, measurable thread density. Your job is to use that number as a conversion factor.

Think of count the way a knitter thinks about gauge. It is stitches per inch. If you know your stitch count and your fabric count, you can always find the finished size.

---

## How do I calculate finished size from stitch count?

Divide your total stitch count by the fabric count. That gives you inches. Do it for width and height separately.

If your pattern is 140 stitches wide by 200 stitches tall, and you are working on 16-count aida:

- Width: 140 / 16 = 8.75 inches
- Height: 200 / 16 = 12.5 inches

Add at least 3 inches on each side for a hoop margin or framing allowance. That means your fabric needs to be at least 14.75 x 18.5 inches. Most stitchers round up to the nearest inch and add a little more. For anything going to a framer, add 4 inches per side.

---

## How do I convert a pattern from one count to another?

Keep your stitch count exactly the same. Just swap the fabric count number in the formula. The design does not change. Only the finished size changes. Moving that same 140 x 200 pattern from 14-count to 18-count shrinks the finished piece by about two inches in each direction.

Same 140 x 200 stitch pattern:

| Fabric | Width | Height |
|---|---|---|
| 11-count aida | 12.7 in | 18.2 in |
| 14-count aida | 10.0 in | 14.3 in |
| 16-count aida | 8.75 in | 12.5 in |
| 18-count aida | 7.8 in | 11.1 in |
| 28-count evenweave (over 2) | 10.0 in | 14.3 in |

Notice that 14-count aida and 28-count evenweave stitched over two threads give you the same finished size. That pattern holds across all evenweave counts. [28-count over 2 equals 14-count](https://www.dmc.com/us/learn-how-to-cross-stitch.html). 32-count over 2 equals 16-count. 36-count over 2 equals 18-count.

---

## What is the difference between aida and evenweave counts?

Aida is woven in blocks. Each "count" unit is one block with one clear hole. Evenweave is woven in single threads. You stitch over two threads to make a stitch the same size as one aida block.

That is why you will see patterns say "28-count evenweave, stitched over two." If you stitch over just one thread on 28-count, your stitches will be tiny. Your finished piece will be half the size you expected. [The Needlework Tips and Techniques guide from DMC](https://www.dmc.com/us/learn-how-to-cross-stitch.html) explains this difference. It is worth saving.

Linen works the same way as evenweave. 32-count linen stitched over two threads gives you a 16-count result. The thread count in linen can vary a little within a piece because linen is a natural fiber. If your project needs to be exact, measure your actual fabric before you start.

## Can I use this conversion to resize a design?

Yes, but keep one thing in mind. You are resizing the physical dimensions, not the stitch count. The number of stitches stays the same. Only the fabric you choose changes how large or small those stitches look on the cloth.

Say you have a portrait that works out to 10 x 14 inches on 14-count. You need it to fit in an 8 x 10 frame. You need a higher count fabric. Work backwards:

- The frame opening is about 7.5 x 9.5 inches (standard mat opening for 8 x 10)
- 140 / 7.5 = 18.7, so you need about 18-count fabric
- 200 / 9.5 = 21.1, so you actually need closer to 22-count

When the width and height do not land on the same count, you have to pick one count. Then accept that the design will be a little taller or wider than the frame opening. You can adjust your mat or crop your margin. This is a real trade-off, not a math error.

## Quick reference: common count conversions

Every aida count has a matching evenweave count stitched over two threads. 14-count aida matches 28-count evenweave. 16-count matches 32-count. The stitch density stays the same. Your finished size formula works the same way no matter which fabric type you choose.

| Aida Count | Evenweave Equivalent (over 2) | Stitches per inch |
|---|---|---|
| 11-count | 22-count | 11 |
| 14-count | 28-count | 14 |
| 16-count | 32-count | 16 |
| 18-count | 36-count | 18 |
| 22-count | 44-count | 22 |
| 28-count | 56-count | 28 |

The formula never changes: **[finished inches = stitch count / fabric count](/blog/cross-stitch-size-chart/)**. Everything else in cross stitch sizing is just this equation used in different ways.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is fabric count in cross stitch?
Fabric count is the number of threads or holes per inch in cross stitch fabric. A higher count means more threads packed into each inch. That gives you smaller, finer stitches. Common counts include 14-count aida, 18-count aida, and 28-count evenweave. The count you choose affects the finished size of your project and how much detail you can show in your design.

### How do I convert a cross stitch pattern from 14-count to 18-count fabric?
Divide the original count by the new count. Then multiply your pattern dimensions by that number. For example, a design that measures 5 inches on 14-count aida will measure about 3.9 inches on 18-count fabric. The stitch count of the pattern stays the same. Only the finished size changes based on the fabric you pick.

### What is the difference between aida and evenweave fabric counts?
Aida is woven in blocks. Each stitch crosses one block. Evenweave has single threads, and you usually stitch over two of them. A 28-count evenweave gives you the same stitch size as 14-count aida because you cross two threads per stitch. When you convert patterns between these fabric types, always check whether you are stitching over one or two threads on the evenweave.

### How does fabric count affect the finished size of a cross stitch project?
Fabric count decides how large or small your finished piece will be. Divide the total stitch count of a design by the fabric count to get the finished size in inches. A 140-stitch-wide design on 14-count fabric gives you a 10-inch width. The same design on 28-count fabric gives you only 5 inches. Always figure out your finished size before you buy fabric. That way you know you have enough material.

### Can I use a cross stitch fabric count conversion calculator for any fabric type?
Yes. A fabric count conversion calculator works for aida, evenweave, linen, and other counted fabric types. Enter your current fabric count, the new count you want, and your original design size. The calculator gives you the new finished size. Tools like the converter on [fibertools.app](https://fibertools.app) make it easy to plan projects across different fabric types. You can see finished sizes before you buy materials or start stitching.
