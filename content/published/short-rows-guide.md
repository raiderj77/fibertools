# How to Work Short Rows in Knitting and Crochet

Short rows are partial rows. Instead of going all the way across, you stop partway, turn your work, and go back. This adds extra fabric in one spot without adding fabric to the rest of the row. The result is curved shaping inside flat fabric.

Short rows are how sock heels curve around the foot. They are how sweater shoulders slope smoothly instead of stair-stepping. They are also how bust darts add room in the chest without making the whole garment wider. If patterns that say "work short rows" have scared you off, here is how they work.

## What Are Short Rows and When Do You Use Them?

A short row is any row where you turn before reaching the end. By working extra rows in one spot, that spot grows taller than the fabric around it. The extra height makes a curve, slope, or wedge shape.

**Common uses:**

- **Sock heels:** Short rows across the center back make a cup that wraps around the heel bone.
- **Shoulder shaping:** Instead of binding off in stair-steps, short rows make a smooth diagonal slope.
- **Bust darts:** Extra rows in the chest area add length for a better fit without making the garment wider.
- **Curved hems:** A slightly longer back than front, or the other way around, on a sweater.
- **Wedge-shaped shawls:** Short rows create a crescent or wedge shape from a flat piece.
- **Hat crowns:** Short rows can shape the crown instead of using decreases.

## How Do You Work Short Rows in Knitting?

### Method 1: Wrap and Turn (W&T)

This is the most traditional method. When you reach the turning point, you wrap the yarn around the next stitch. This prevents a hole. Then you turn.

**To wrap and turn on a knit row:**
1. Knit to the turning point.
2. Slip the next stitch purlwise to the right needle.
3. Bring the yarn to the front, between the needles.
4. Slip the stitch back to the left needle.
5. Turn the work. The yarn is now ready to purl back.

**To wrap and turn on a purl row:**
1. Purl to the turning point.
2. Slip the next stitch purlwise.
3. Bring the yarn to the back.
4. Slip the stitch back.
5. Turn the work.

**Picking up wraps:** When you later work across the wrapped stitches, pick up the wrap. Knit or purl it together with the wrapped stitch. This hides the wrap and closes any gap.

### Method 2: German Short Rows (Easiest)

No wrapping needed. When you turn, pull the working yarn firmly over the needle. This makes the first stitch of the previous row into a "double stitch." Both legs of the stitch sit on the needle.

**How to do it:**
1. Knit or purl to the turning point. Turn the work.
2. Pull the working yarn firmly over the needle from front to back.
3. The first stitch on the left needle pulls up. Both legs sit on the needle. This is the double stitch.
4. Keep working the row.
5. When you reach a double stitch on a later row, work both legs together as one stitch.

German short rows are the most beginner-friendly method. No wrapping, no picking up. The double stitch is easy to see and easy to work.

### Method 3: Japanese Short Rows

Place a removable stitch marker on the yarn between the last worked stitch and the turning stitch. On later rows, use the marker to find where to pick up and close the gap. This method makes the most invisible short row. But it takes careful marker management.

## How Do You Work Short Rows in Crochet?

Crochet short rows are simpler than knitting short rows. Crochet stitches are independent. You do not need wraps or double stitches to prevent holes.

**Method:**
1. Work to the turning point. Stop.
2. Chain the turning chain height for your stitch. That is ch 1 for single crochet, ch 2 for half double crochet, ch 3 for double crochet.
3. Turn the work and crochet back to the other end, or to another turning point.

**To hide the turning gap:** When you work the full-length row that passes over the short row section, work the stitch at the turning point through both loops plus the top of the turning chain. This closes the small gap that forms at the turn.

In crochet, short rows are most often used for garment shaping like bust darts and shoulder slopes. They are also used in sculptural amigurumi to create 3D curves without increases and decreases.

## What Are Common Tips and Mistakes?

**Practice on a swatch first.** Cast on 20 stitches. Practice turning at stitch 15, then 10, then 5. This creates a wedge. It teaches you the turn, the wrap or double stitch, and the pick-up without risking a real project.

**Close every gap.** Wraps, double stitches, and Japanese markers all exist to close the hole that forms at the turning point. If you skip this step, your fabric will have visible holes at every turn.

**Work short rows in pairs.** Most shaping needs short rows on both the right side and wrong side. A shoulder slope needs turns on both knit and purl rows to make a smooth diagonal on both edges.

**Count your turning points.** Mark each turning point with a stitch marker. That way you can find the wrap or double stitch when you work the pickup row.

**Short rows add yarn.** Extra rows in one section means extra yarn. Budget 5 to 10% more yarn for projects with a lot of short row shaping, like sock heels, bust darts, and shaped shoulders.

**Common mistakes:**
- Forgetting to pick up wraps, which leaves visible holes at every turn
- Turning at the wrong stitch, so the shaping lands in the wrong place
- Not counting short row pairs, which causes uneven shaping
- Pulling too tight at the turn, which causes a pucker instead of a smooth curve
- Working short rows in the wrong direction, so the curve goes the wrong way

**The German short row sock heel.** A knitter shaped a heel with German short rows on a top-down sock. She knit across 32 heel stitches. She turned 1 stitch earlier on each side until 10 stitches remained in the center. Then she worked back outward, turning 1 stitch later each time until all 32 stitches were active again. The double stitches were easy to spot and work. She made 22 turns total. The heel fit perfectly with no holes.

**The bust-dart sweater.** A knitter added bust darts to a raglan pullover using wrap-and-turn short rows. She worked 8 short rows, which is 4 pairs, across the front chest. Each turn was 3 stitches further from center. The extra 8 rows added 1.3 inches of length in the bust area without making the sweater wider. The wraps were invisible after picking up.

**The crochet wedge shawl.** A crocheter made a crescent-shaped shawl using short rows. She worked half double crochet rows that turned progressively further from the center. This created a wedge that curved into a crescent. Each group of 6 short rows added a new wedge. After 12 wedges, the shawl measured 60 inches across the top edge with a gentle crescent curve.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the easiest short row method for beginners?

German short rows are the easiest. When you turn, pull the yarn firmly over the needle to create a double stitch. When you reach the double stitch later, work both legs together. No wrapping, no picking up, no markers. The double stitch is visible and hard to miss, so you are unlikely to create holes.

### Do short rows use more yarn?

Yes, but only a little. Short rows add extra rows to one section, which uses extra yarn. For sock heels, the short rows add about 5% more yarn than a heel-flap construction. For bust darts with 8 to 12 short rows, the extra yarn is very small. Budget 5 to 10% extra for heavily shaped projects.

### Can I convert a pattern's short row method to a different method?

Yes. Wrap-and-turn, German, and Japanese short rows are interchangeable. The turning points stay the same. Only the technique at the turn changes. If a pattern calls for wrap-and-turn and you prefer German short rows, work the same turning sequence but use the double-stitch method instead of wrapping.

### Why do I get holes at my short row turns?

Holes form when you do not close the gap between the last worked stitch and the turning stitch. With wrap-and-turn, you must pick up the wrap and knit it together with the wrapped stitch. With German short rows, you must work both legs of the double stitch together. Skipping either step causes a hole. Tension at the turn also matters. Do not pull too tight.

## Start Using Short Rows

Short rows turn flat fabric into 3D shapes. Sock heels, shoulder slopes, and bust darts all use the same basic idea. Turn early, work back, and close the gap. Practice on a swatch first, then try your first short row heel.
