# How to Add Borders to Knit and Crochet Projects

A border is what turns "I finished knitting" into "I finished a blanket." The right border stops curling and hides uneven edges. It frames your work and makes it look polished. It turns a rectangle of stitches into something you are proud to give as a gift.

You might add a simple single crochet edge to a knit blanket. Or you might work a shell border on a crochet afghan. Either way, you start the same way: picking up stitches evenly around the edge. Here is how to do it right.

## Why Should You Add a Border?

Borders solve three problems at once.

**They prevent curling.** Stockinette knitting curls at the edges. Every knitter knows this. A few rounds of garter stitch, seed stitch, or a crocheted border stops the curl and keeps the fabric flat. Even crochet projects can flare or pull in at the edges. A border fixes that.

**They create a finished edge.** Raw cast-on and bind-off edges look rough. Side edges can be uneven, especially on blankets worked over many weeks. Your tension may shift slightly over time. A border covers all of that and creates a clean, even frame.

**They add structure.** A border makes the edge a little stiffer. This helps blankets drape evenly. It keeps lightweight shawls from stretching out of shape. On garments, a border around the neckline or hem adds a small amount of stability.

## How Do You Pick Up Stitches for a Border?

This is where most crafters run into trouble. The edges of your work do not have the same stitch-per-inch ratio as the front. So you cannot just work one border stitch into every edge stitch.

### For Crochet Borders on Crochet Projects

Work directly into the edge stitches. Along the top and bottom edges, you have a clear stitch to work into. Use one border stitch per existing stitch.

Along the side edges, space your stitches evenly based on the height of the original rows:
- **Single crochet rows:** 1 border stitch per row
- **Half double crochet rows:** 1 border stitch per row, plus 1 extra stitch every 3 to 4 rows
- **Double crochet rows:** 2 border stitches per row

### For Crochet Borders on Knit Projects

This is the most common combination. It is a crocheted edge on a knit blanket. Along the cast-on and bind-off edges, work 1 single crochet into each stitch. Along the side edges, the standard ratio is 3 single crochet stitches for every 4 rows of stockinette. For garter stitch edges, work 1 stitch per garter ridge, which is every 2 rows.

### For Knit Borders

Pick up stitches with a knitting needle along the edge. The standard ratio for stockinette side edges is 3 stitches for every 4 rows. Pick up 3, skip 1, pick up 3, skip 1. Along cast-on and bind-off edges, pick up 1 stitch per stitch.

Use the [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) to keep track of your count as you work around the edge. It is easy to lose count on a long blanket side. A throw blanket edge can have 200 or more stitches.

## How Do You Handle Corners?

Corners can make or break a border. Too few stitches and the corner pulls in. Too many and it ruffles.

**For crochet borders:** Work 3 stitches into the corner stitch. This works for single crochet borders. For half double crochet borders, try 3 to 4 stitches in the corner. For double crochet, try 5 stitches. If the corner looks tight after the first round, add 1 more stitch on the next round.

**For multi-round borders:** Increase at each corner on every round. The standard is to add 2 stitches per corner per round. Work 3 stitches into the center corner stitch each round. This adds 2 net stitches since one was already there.

**For knit borders:** Work a yarn over at each corner. Or place a marker and work a make-one increase on either side of the corner stitch every round.

**Square corners vs. rounded corners:** 3 stitches in the corner stitch makes a crisp 90-degree angle. 5 stitches makes a slightly rounded corner. For blankets, sharp corners with 3 stitches look best. For garment hems, slightly rounded corners with 4 to 5 stitches drape better.

## What Are the Most Popular Border Styles?

### Single Crochet Border (Easiest)

Work one round of single crochet in a contrasting or matching color. Work 1 sc in each stitch across the top and bottom. Use the pickup ratios above for the sides. Work 3 sc in each corner stitch. This is the go-to choice for a clean, simple finish. It takes about 30 to 60 minutes on a throw blanket.

### Shell Border

Work this repeat around the entire edge: skip 2 stitches, work 5 double crochet in the next stitch, skip 2 stitches, slip stitch in the next stitch. This creates a scalloped edge. It requires a stitch count that is divisible by 6 plus 1. It works best over a foundation round of single crochet.

### Picot Edge

Work this repeat around the edge: single crochet, chain 3, slip stitch into the first chain, single crochet in the next 2 stitches. This creates small decorative bumps. It looks delicate and is popular on baby blankets and shawls. It uses about 15% more yarn than a plain single crochet border.

### Garter Stitch Border (Knit)

Pick up stitches around the edge and knit every row for 6 to 10 rows. Garter stitch lies flat and counteracts stockinette curl. It creates a textured frame. This is simple and works well on any knit blanket.

### I-Cord Bind-Off (Knit)

Cast on 3 stitches. Then work an attached i-cord along the edge. Knit 3, slip them back to the left needle, pick up 1 stitch from the edge, and pass the picked-up stitch over. This creates a round, rope-like border. It looks beautiful on blankets and shawl edges. It is slower to work, but the result is worth it.

### Reverse Single Crochet (Crab Stitch)

Work single crochet from left to right instead of right to left. This is for right-handed crocheters. It creates a twisted, rope-like edge. It is quick and adds a decorative detail to any crochet project. It works best as the final round on top of a plain single crochet round.

## How Do You Estimate Yarn for a Border?

Calculate border yarn in three steps:

**Step 1: Measure the perimeter.** For a 50 x 60 inch throw: (50 + 60) x 2 = 220 inches, which is about 18.3 feet.

**Step 2: Calculate stitches per round.** At 4 single crochet stitches per inch: 220 x 4 = 880 stitches per round, plus 8 corner stitches (2 extra at each of 4 corners) = 888 stitches per round.

**Step 3: Estimate yardage per round.** In worsted weight single crochet, each stitch uses roughly 0.5 inches of yarn. So 888 stitches x 0.5 inches = 444 inches, which is about 37 feet, or 12.3 yards per round.

For a 3-round border: 12.3 x 3 = about 37 yards. For a shell border, the stitches are taller and use more yarn. Plan for roughly double that, around 70 to 80 yards.

**Buy 50 to 100 extra yards** beyond your estimate. Running out of border yarn one corner from the finish is the worst kind of yarn chicken.

## What Are the Most Common Border Mistakes?

**Not working a foundation round first.** If you jump straight into a fancy border, the uneven edge underneath shows through. Work one round of single crochet first to create a clean, even base. Then start your decorative border on round 2.

**Wrong corner stitch count.** Too few corner stitches pull the blanket into a trapezoid shape. Too many create ruffled corners. Start with 3 stitches in each corner for single crochet and adjust on the next rounds.

**Inconsistent tension.** If your border tension is tighter than your project tension, the border pulls the edges in. If it is looser, the border flares out. Try to match the tension of the main fabric.

**Not counting.** If the two long sides of a blanket have different stitch counts, the border will look lopsided. Count your stitches on each side and adjust to match.

## What Do Real Border Projects Look Like?

**The curling stockinette blanket.** A knitter finished a stockinette throw that curled badly at all four edges. She added 2 rounds of single crochet in a matching color. The first round used 48 yards. The second used 50 yards. Total border time: 2 hours. The blanket lay perfectly flat.

**The granny square border.** A crocheter joined 42 granny squares into a baby blanket, but the edges looked jagged. She worked 1 round of sc to even the edge. Then she worked 2 rounds of a shell border in white. Total border yarn: 95 yards. The shells hid the join lines and gave the blanket a polished finish.

**The i-cord masterpiece.** A knitter added a 3-stitch i-cord border to a lace shawl. The shawl edge had 380 stitches around the perimeter. The i-cord took 4.5 hours and 65 yards of yarn. But the rope-like edge turned a nice shawl into a stunning one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many rounds of border should I add?

One round of single crochet is the minimum for a clean edge. Two to three rounds add a visible frame and more stability. Decorative borders like shells or picots work best as 1 round on top of a foundation round of single crochet. For blankets, 2 to 4 total rounds is the sweet spot between finished and bulky.

### Can I add a crochet border to a knitting project?

Yes, and it is one of the most popular finishing techniques. Use a crochet hook one size smaller than your knitting needles. Pick up through the edge stitches just as you would for a knit border. The standard pickup ratio is 3 single crochet stitches for every 4 stockinette rows along the side edges.

### How do I keep my border from ruffling?

Ruffling means you have too many stitches. The border has more stitches per inch than the project edge. Remove stitches evenly by skipping 1 stitch every 8 to 10 stitches until the border lies flat. If the border pulls in instead, you have too few stitches. Add 1 stitch every 8 to 10 stitches.

### What color should my border be?

A matching color creates a subtle, polished frame. A contrasting color makes a bold statement. For multi-color projects like granny square blankets, a neutral border in white, cream, gray, or black ties everything together without competing. When in doubt, hold the border yarn against the project and step back 6 feet to judge.

Every project deserves a finished edge. Whether you go simple with single crochet or fancy with a shell border, the key is the same. Pick up stitches evenly, get the corner math right, and have enough yarn.
