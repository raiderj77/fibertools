# Corner-to-Corner Crochet: Block Counts, Rows, and Yardage Explained

Corner-to-corner crochet builds fabric on the diagonal. You start with one block in one corner. You add blocks until you reach the widest point. Then you decrease back down to the opposite corner. This is one of the most popular techniques for blankets and graphghans. But figuring out how many blocks, rows, and yards of each color you need takes more math than most people expect.

This guide explains how C2C is built, how to count blocks, and how to use the [C2C Calculator](/c2c-calculator) to plan your project from start to finish.

## What Is C2C Crochet and When Should You Use It?

C2C stands for corner-to-corner. It creates a grid of small blocks. Each block is made from a cluster of double crochets. The blocks form on a diagonal. Each row adds or removes one block.

People love this technique for a few reasons:

- The block grid makes it great for pixel-style colorwork and graphghans
- The diagonal construction keeps things interesting. You never crochet 200 stitches in one row
- It often works up faster than row-by-row colorwork
- The texture looks the same across the whole piece

C2C works best for blankets, wall hangings, pillow covers, and any project that follows a pixel chart. It is not the best choice for garments. The diagonal construction does not drape the same way as regular fabric.

## How C2C Construction Works

Each C2C block is made the same way. You chain 3 (this counts as a turning chain or space). Then you make 3 double crochet (dc) into the chain space below. Every block follows this same pattern.

### The Two Phases

**Increase phase (Rows 1 through the diagonal):** You start with 1 block. You add 1 block every row. Row 1 has 1 block. Row 2 has 2 blocks. Row 3 has 3 blocks. This keeps going until you reach the widest point.

**Decrease phase (after the diagonal to the end):** You remove 1 block every row. This continues until you are back down to 1 block.

### Block Count Math

For a rectangular piece:

- **Blocks wide** = finished width divided by block width
- **Blocks tall** = finished height divided by block height
- **Total diagonal rows** = blocks wide plus blocks tall minus 1

If your blanket is 40 blocks wide and 50 blocks tall, you will crochet 89 diagonal rows total.

The increase phase runs for as many rows as the shorter dimension. The decrease phase runs for the same number. The middle section, where rows stay at their longest, covers the difference.

| Phase | Row Numbers | Blocks per Row |
|-------|------------|----------------|
| Increase | 1 to 40 | 1 to 40 |
| Full-width | 41 to 50 | 40 each |
| Decrease | 51 to 89 | 39 down to 1 |

For a square piece, say 40 by 40 blocks, there is no full-width phase. You increase for 40 rows. You hit the peak row at 40 blocks. Then you decrease for 39 rows. The total is 79 diagonal rows.

## Step-by-Step: Planning a C2C Blanket

Here is a real example. You want a baby blanket that is 30 by 36 inches. You are using worsted weight yarn with a block gauge of 1 by 1 inch.

**Step 1: Calculate blocks.**
- Width: 30 divided by 1 = 30 blocks
- Height: 36 divided by 1 = 36 blocks
- Total blocks: 30 times 36 = 1,080 blocks

**Step 2: Calculate diagonal rows.**
- 30 plus 36 minus 1 = 65 diagonal rows
- Increase phase: Rows 1 to 30 (adding 1 block per row)
- Full-width phase: Rows 31 to 36 (30 blocks per row, 6 rows)
- Decrease phase: Rows 37 to 65 (29 blocks down to 1)

**Step 3: Estimate yardage.**
Each C2C block uses about 2.5 yards of worsted weight yarn. This includes the chain spaces and the 3 dc. For 1,080 blocks: 1,080 times 2.5 = 2,700 yards. A more accurate estimate for a 30 by 36 inch solid-color C2C blanket in worsted weight is roughly 2,200 to 2,500 yards.

**Step 4: Add border yarn.** One round of single crochet border on a 30 by 36 inch blanket uses roughly 140 yards. Two rounds use about 290 yards.

## Yardage Per Color for Graphghans

Graphghans are blankets that follow a pixel chart to create pictures. C2C is perfect for this. But you need to know how much of each color to buy.

Here is the process:

1. Create or find a pixel chart. Each pixel equals 1 C2C block.
2. Count the number of blocks in each color.
3. Multiply each color's block count by your per-block yardage. That is about 2.5 yards for worsted weight.
4. Add 10 to 15 percent extra for tails and color changes.

**Example:** A 40 by 50 block graphghan with a simple 3-color design:
- Background (cream): 1,400 blocks times 2.5 = 3,500 yards, plus 15 percent = 4,025 yards
- Main image (navy): 450 blocks times 2.5 = 1,125 yards, plus 15 percent = 1,294 yards
- Accent (gold): 150 blocks times 2.5 = 375 yards, plus 15 percent = 431 yards

That is roughly 8 skeins of cream, 3 skeins of navy, and 1 skein of gold. This is based on a typical 364-yard worsted weight skein, like a standard 7 oz acrylic skein.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

**Tension consistency is critical.** C2C blocks stack on a diagonal. Uneven tension shows up as wavy edges or blocks that do not line up. Keep your chain-3 spaces the same height throughout.

**Weaving in ends with colorwork.** Graphghans create a lot of yarn tails from color changes. Carry colors across the back when switching within 3 to 4 blocks. For longer stretches, cut and rejoin. Carrying yarn across 10 or more blocks makes the back stiff and bulky.

**C2C does not have to use double crochet.** You can make mini C2C blocks with half double crochet (hdc). Use chain 2 plus 3 hdc instead of chain 3 plus 3 dc. This makes smaller blocks, about 0.7 inches in worsted weight. Smaller blocks give finer detail for graphghans, but you will need more blocks and more time.

**Common mistakes:**

- Miscounting blocks during the increase phase. Always count at the end of each row
- Forgetting that C2C fabric stretches a little on the diagonal. Your finished piece may relax 5 to 10 percent wider than your blocked gauge
- Skipping the gauge swatch. Block size varies a lot between crocheters
- Buying yarn based on total block count without adding extra for color-change tails

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I calculate the number of C2C blocks I need?

Divide your target width and height by your block gauge. A 40 by 50 inch blanket at 1-inch block gauge needs 40 times 50 = 2,000 blocks. The total diagonal rows equal width blocks plus height blocks minus one. In this case, that is 89 rows.

### How much yarn does a C2C blanket use?

Each C2C block in worsted weight (CYC 4) uses about 2.5 yards of yarn. A 30 by 36 inch blanket at 1-inch gauge needs roughly 2,200 to 2,500 yards total. For graphghans, add 10 to 15 percent extra per color to cover tails from color changes.

### Can I make a C2C blanket that isn't square?

Yes. Rectangular C2C blankets are common. The increase phase goes until you reach the shorter dimension's block count. Then you work straight rows at the full width until you cover the difference. Then you decrease. The calculator handles all this math for you.

### What's the best yarn for C2C blankets?

Worsted weight (CYC 4) acrylic is the most popular choice for C2C blankets. It is affordable for large projects, easy to care for, and makes consistent 1-inch blocks with a 5.0mm hook. For graphghans with fine detail, DK weight (CYC 3) gives smaller blocks and sharper image resolution.
