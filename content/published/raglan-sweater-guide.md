# How to Calculate a Top-Down Raglan Sweater: Stitch Math and Construction Guide

A top-down raglan is one of the most satisfying things you can knit or crochet. You start at the neck, increase outward along four diagonal lines, and watch a sweater take shape as you go. The tricky part is getting the stitch math right from the very first row.

Whether you are designing from scratch or changing an existing pattern, understanding raglan stitch math is what makes the difference between a sweater that fits and one that does not. The [Raglan Calculator](/raglan-calculator) handles the hard math, but knowing why the numbers work makes you a better maker.

## What Is a Top-Down Raglan and When Do You Need the Math?

A raglan sweater has four diagonal lines running from the neckline down to the underarms. These lines separate four sections: the front, the back, and two sleeves. Every increase round adds 8 stitches total. That is 2 stitches at each raglan line. The yoke grows evenly until you reach the underarms.

You need raglan math whenever you are:

- Designing a sweater without a pattern
- Adjusting a pattern to fit a different size
- Converting a flat pattern to top-down construction
- Substituting yarn at a different gauge
- Adding length to the yoke for a deeper neckline

The great thing about top-down raglan construction is that you can try it on as you go. But that only works if your starting numbers are right.

## Step-by-Step Raglan Stitch Distribution

### Step 1: Determine Your Neck Circumference

Typical neck circumferences by size:

| Size | Neck Circumference | Typical Cast-On (Worsted, 5 st/in) |
|------|-------------------|--------------------------------------|
| XS (28 to 30" chest) | 13" | 64 stitches |
| S (32 to 34" chest) | 14" | 72 stitches |
| M (36 to 38" chest) | 15" | 76 stitches |
| L (40 to 42" chest) | 16" | 80 stitches |
| XL (44 to 46" chest) | 17" | 84 stitches |
| 2XL (48 to 50" chest) | 18" | 88 stitches |

These starting numbers are for a crew neck. For a V-neck or scoop neck, you will cast on fewer stitches. You will also use short rows to shape the front neckline.

### Step 2: Subtract Raglan Seam Stitches

Most raglan sweaters use 1 to 2 stitches per seam line. With 4 seam lines at 2 stitches each, that is 8 stitches set aside for the raglan lines.

**Working cast-on for stitch distribution = total cast-on minus raglan seam stitches**

For a Medium with 76 cast-on stitches: 76 minus 8 = 68 stitches to distribute.

### Step 3: Distribute Stitches Using the Percentage Method

The standard raglan split for those remaining stitches is:

- **Front**: 33% (about one-third)
- **Back**: 33% (about one-third)
- **Each sleeve**: 17% (about one-sixth each)

For 68 distributable stitches:

- Front: 68 x 0.33 = 22 stitches
- Back: 68 x 0.33 = 22 stitches
- Left sleeve: 68 x 0.17 = 12 stitches
- Right sleeve: 68 x 0.17 = 12 stitches
- **Check**: 22 + 22 + 12 + 12 = 68 stitches

If the numbers do not divide evenly, add the extra stitches to the front and back. Do not add them to the sleeves. Sleeves handle small changes better.

### Step 4: Calculate Increase Rounds

Each increase round adds 8 stitches, which is 2 per raglan line. To find out how many increase rounds you need:

**Increase rounds = (target chest stitches minus cast-on stitches) divided by 8**

For a 38" chest at 5 stitches per inch with 2" of ease: the target is 40" x 5 = 200 stitches. Starting from 76 stitches: (200 minus 76) divided by 8 = 15.5, rounded up to 16 increase rounds.

In most raglan construction, you increase every other round, which means every 2nd round. So 16 increase rounds means 32 total yoke rounds. At 7 rows per inch, that is about 4.5 inches of yoke depth. That is right in the normal range.

### Step 5: Split for Body and Sleeves

After all increase rounds are done, you place the sleeve stitches on waste yarn and join the body to work in the round. At this point:

- Each sleeve section has grown: 12 + (16 x 2) = 44 stitches per sleeve
- Front has grown: 22 + (16 x 2) = 54 stitches
- Back has grown: 22 + (16 x 2) = 54 stitches

Place the sleeve stitches on holders. Cast on 4 to 8 stitches at each underarm for ease of movement. Then keep working the body in the round.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

**Adjusting for different body types:** If you need a wider chest but standard shoulders, work extra increase rounds. Then add short rows across the front and back only. This adds width without making the sleeves too big.

**Common mistake: forgetting ease.** Your target chest measurement should include 2 to 4 inches of positive ease for a standard fit, or 4 to 8 inches for an oversized look. Negative ease works for socks, not sweaters.

**Common mistake: ignoring row gauge.** Stitch gauge controls width, but row gauge controls how deep your yoke ends up. If your row gauge is tighter than expected, you will run out of yoke depth before you hit your target chest size.

**Tip for crocheters:** Crochet stitches are taller than knit stitches. Your yoke will reach the underarm in fewer rounds. You may need to increase every round instead of every other round. This helps you get enough width before the yoke gets too deep.

**Variation: modified raglan.** Some designers move a few stitches from the sleeves to the front and back. For example, 35% front, 35% back, 15% each sleeve. This gives broader shoulders and slimmer sleeves. It works well for drop-shoulder styles.

## Real Project Examples

### Example 1: Child's Raglan in Worsted Weight

- **Size**: Child 6 (26" chest)
- **Gauge**: 5 stitches and 7 rows per inch in stockinette
- **Neck circumference**: 11.5"
- **Cast-on**: 56 stitches (minus 8 for seams = 48 to distribute)
- **Distribution**: Front 16, Back 16, each sleeve 8
- **Target chest**: 28" (with 2" ease) = 140 stitches
- **Increase rounds**: (140 minus 56) divided by 8 = 10.5, rounded to 11
- **Yoke depth**: 22 rounds divided by 7 rows per inch = 3.1 inches

### Example 2: Adult Raglan in DK Weight

- **Size**: Large (42" chest)
- **Gauge**: 5.5 stitches and 8 rows per inch
- **Neck circumference**: 16"
- **Cast-on**: 88 stitches (minus 8 = 80 to distribute)
- **Distribution**: Front 26, Back 26, each sleeve 14
- **Target chest**: 44" (with 2" ease) = 242 stitches
- **Increase rounds**: (242 minus 88) divided by 8 = 19.25, rounded to 19
- **Yoke depth**: 38 rounds divided by 8 rows per inch = 4.75 inches

Both examples land in the expected yoke depth range. That range is 3 to 5 inches for children and 4 to 7 inches for adults. If your numbers fall outside that range, check your gauge swatch again.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many stitches should I cast on for a raglan sweater?

Your cast-on depends on neck circumference and gauge. Multiply your neck measurement in inches by your stitch gauge. For an adult medium at 5 stitches per inch with a 15-inch neck, that is 76 stitches. Always round to a number that divides evenly by 4. This keeps the raglan lines clean.

### Can I use raglan construction for crochet sweaters?

Yes, raglan construction works for both knitting and crochet. The main difference is how often you increase. Crochet rows are taller, so you will often increase every round instead of every other round. Your row gauge tells you whether to use single-round or alternating-round increases to get the right yoke depth.

### How do I fix a raglan yoke that's too wide before reaching the underarm?

If your yoke hits the target chest width but is not deep enough, stop increasing. Work even rounds until the yoke reaches the right depth. You can also try increasing every 3rd round for the last inch or two. This adds depth without adding as much width.

### What's the difference between a raglan and a yoke sweater?

A raglan has four distinct diagonal increase lines. These lines separate the front, back, and sleeve sections. A circular yoke spreads increases more evenly around the whole circumference. Circular yokes often use decorative patterns. Raglans are easier to calculate and adjust. Circular yokes offer more design options but need more complex math.
