# How Much Yarn Do You Need for a Blanket?

**Quick answer:** Most knit or crochet blankets use between 1,000 and 4,000 yards of yarn. The exact amount depends on size, stitch pattern, and yarn weight. A typical throw (50x60 inches) in worsted weight needs around 2,000 to 3,000 yards. Always buy 10 to 15% extra and check your gauge before buying a full cone or lot.

---

## What size blanket are you actually making?

Your finished size is the first number you need. Nothing else matters until you know it. A baby blanket is usually around 30 by 36 inches. It needs roughly 800 to 1,500 yards depending on yarn weight. A throw is about 50 by 60 inches. A king bed blanket can need 4,000 yards or more.

Size is the biggest factor. A baby blanket uses a fraction of what a king-size blanket needs. Nail down your dimensions before you buy anything.

The table below shows common blanket sizes and realistic yardage ranges. Use these as starting points. They are estimates based on average stitch patterns and yarn weights. Your gauge will change everything.

| Blanket Type | Approximate Size | Worsted Weight Yardage |
|---|---|---|
| Baby blanket | 30x36 in | 700-1,200 yds |
| Lap blanket | 36x48 in | 1,200-1,800 yds |
| Throw | 50x60 in | 2,000-3,000 yds |
| Full/Queen bed | 60x80 in | 3,500-5,000 yds |
| King bed | 108x90 in | 6,000-9,000 yds |

These numbers assume a basic stockinette or single crochet fabric. Lace uses less yarn because it is more open. Dense cables and textured stitches use more. More on that below.

## How does yarn weight change the math?

Yarn weight changes your yardage estimate a lot. Sometimes by thousands of yards for the same blanket size. Bulky yarn works up fast, but each skein covers less area than you might think. Fingering weight needs far more yards per project. But each skein holds a lot of it, so the cost per skein often evens out.

Heavier yarn covers ground faster, but each yard covers less area. Lighter yarn needs more yards, but each skein holds more of them.

Super bulky or bulky yarn on a size 13 to 15 needle or a 10mm to 12mm crochet hook uses up yardage fast. You need more physical fiber to fill the same space. A fingering weight on a size 2 needle works the opposite way. Here is roughly how the math looks for a 50x60 inch throw:

- **Super bulky (size 6):** 500 to 1,200 yards (thick yarn, fewer yards per ounce)
- **Bulky (size 5):** 1,200 to 1,800 yards
- **Worsted (size 4):** 2,000 to 3,000 yards
- **DK (size 3):** 2,500 to 3,500 yards
- **Fingering (size 1):** 3,500 to 5,500 yards

Super bulky yarn looks like fewer yards because each yard of yarn is physically thick. You are not doing less work. You are just using a different unit. Check the yardage on your skein label. Do not just look at the weight in ounces.

## Does stitch pattern really affect yardage that much?

Yes, and underestimating this is one of the most common reasons knitters run short. Cables can use 30 to 40 percent more yarn than stockinette at the same size. Even seed stitch adds a noticeable amount. Always swatch your actual pattern before figuring out how many skeins to buy. Do not just swatch a plain square.

Here is why it matters. Cables cross stitches over each other. That pulls the fabric in and creates extra layers of fiber. Textured stitches like seed stitch or moss stitch also use a bit more than stockinette. The constant knit and purl alternation adds up. On the crochet side, a double crochet fabric uses less yarn than a single crochet fabric of the same size. The taller stitch covers more height per row. If you are planning a heavily textured blanket, budget toward the top of any yardage range.

Lace works the opposite way. Open, airy patterns with lots of yarn overs and decreases can use 20 to 25 percent less yarn than a solid fabric. There is simply less fiber per square inch.

## How do I calculate yarn for a pattern I made up myself?

[Make a gauge swatch, weigh it, and do the math](/blog/accurately-calculating-your-yarn-yardage-with-a-customizable/). This is the most reliable method. It works for any fiber, needle size, or stitch pattern.

Here is the process:

1. Knit or crochet a swatch at least 6x6 inches in your actual stitch pattern.
2. Weigh it in grams on a kitchen or postal scale.
3. Measure the swatch dimensions precisely.
4. Calculate the area of your swatch: width x height.
5. Calculate the area of your finished blanket: width x height.
6. Divide the blanket area by the swatch area to get a multiplier.
7. Multiply the swatch weight by that number.
8. Add 15% for safety.

Example: Your 6x6 swatch weighs 12 grams. Your blanket is 50x60 inches. Swatch area = 36 sq in. Blanket area = 3,000 sq in. Multiplier = 3,000/36 = 83.3. Yarn needed = 12g x 83.3 = 1,000 grams. Add 15% = 1,150 grams total.

Then check your yarn's grams per skein and buy accordingly. This method beats any online calculator. It accounts for your actual hands, your actual tension, and your actual stitch pattern.

## Should I buy all my yarn at once?

Buy everything at once, from the same dye lot. Blankets are large enough that any color difference between lots will show as a visible stripe across the fabric. If your local shop does not have enough stock, ask them to check other locations. Or ask them to order the rest before you start anything.

Dye lots matter. They matter a lot with blankets.

Even two skeins from the same colorway can look different if they come from different dye lots. On a small project you might not notice. On a blanket with thousands of yards of solid or semi-solid color, a dye lot difference will show as a visible stripe or shift. [Most yarn makers print dye lot numbers on the label](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-label) and retailers like [LoveCrafts](https://www.lovecrafts.com) and [Webs](https://www.yarn.com) will match lots if you call ahead or note it in your order.

Buy your full estimated amount from the same dye lot. If your retailer runs out, ask them to check other locations or contact the maker directly.

## What if I have leftover yarn?

Label it right away with the weight, yardage, and fiber content before it goes into your stash. Leftover blanket yarn is genuinely useful. A few hundred worsted yards makes a hat. Collect enough scraps from different projects and you have a striped throw waiting to happen. That is honestly one of the best blanket formats anyway.

Leftover yarn is the start of your next project. A 200-yard worsted remnant is enough for a hat. Several remnants together can become a scrappy striped throw. Weigh and label your leftovers when you finish a project. That way you know exactly what you have when you need it. A postal scale and a few zip-lock bags go a long way toward keeping your stash useful instead of mysterious.

The goal is to buy enough to finish without running out in the middle. Measure your gauge, use the swatch method, and round up. That is really all there is to it.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much yarn do I need for a baby blanket?
[A baby blanket typically requires 1,000 to 2,000 yards of yarn](/blog/how-much-yarn-for-a-blanket/), depending on the stitch pattern and finished size. A standard baby blanket measures around 30x36 inches. Bulky yarn with simple stitches like garter or single crochet uses less yardage. Lacy or textured patterns use more. Always check your pattern's yarn requirements and add a 10 to 15% buffer so you do not run short mid-project.

### How much yarn do I need for a throw blanket?
A standard throw blanket (50x60 inches) generally needs 3,000 to 6,000 yards of worsted-weight yarn. Bulkier yarns reduce the yardage you need. Finer weights require more. Crochet stitches tend to use about 30% more yarn than similar knit stitches. Using a yardage calculator on a tool like fibertools.app helps you estimate more accurately based on your yarn weight, gauge, and target size.

### How do I calculate how much yarn I need for a blanket?
To calculate blanket yarn yardage, multiply your blanket's square inches by a yardage factor based on yarn weight and stitch. A reliable method is to knit or crochet a gauge swatch, measure how many yards it uses per square inch, then multiply by your total blanket area. Online calculators make this easier by factoring in yarn weight, stitch pattern, and dimensions all at once.

### How many skeins of yarn do I need for a blanket?
Most blankets need 6 to 20 skeins, depending on skein size, yarn weight, and blanket dimensions. A bulky yarn skein (100 to 200 yards) may mean you need only 10 to 15 skeins for a throw. A fingering-weight project could need 20 or more. Always buy all skeins from the same dye lot to keep the color consistent. Buy one extra skein as insurance against miscalculations.

### How much chunky yarn do I need for a blanket?
A chunky or super-bulky blanket typically needs 500 to 1,500 yards for a throw-sized project. Chunky yarn works up quickly on large needles or hooks, or even with arm knitting. So you need far fewer yards than with finer weights. A 50x60 inch arm-knit blanket, for example, uses roughly 200 to 300 yards of super-bulky yarn. Always check your specific pattern for the most accurate estimate.
