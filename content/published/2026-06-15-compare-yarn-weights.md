# Yarn Weight Comparison: What Every Number on the Label Actually Means

Yarn weight describes how thick a strand is. Thickness controls how many stitches fit in an inch and how fast a project works up. The [Craft Yarn Council](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) sets up seven weight categories numbered 0 through 7. They go from lace to jumbo. Knowing where your yarn lands on that scale can save you from frogging half a sweater.

---

> **When I started:** I thought all yarn worked for all patterns. I did not check yarn weight, and it changed how everything looked and fit. The [yarn weight chart](/yarn-weight-chart) shows what each weight is good for.

## What do the yarn weight numbers actually mean?

The numbers 0 through 7 are a standard system. They are not a brand code. Each number matches a thickness range, a recommended needle or hook size, and a gauge range. The higher the number, the thicker the yarn and the fewer stitches per inch.

Here is the full breakdown according to the [Craft Yarn Council weight system](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system):

| Weight | Name | Knit Gauge (sts per 4 in) | Needle Size (US) | Hook Size (US) |
|--------|------|--------------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| 0 | Lace | 33-40+ | 000-1 | Steel 6-8 |
| 1 | Super Fine | 27-32 | 1-3 | B-1 to E-4 |
| 2 | Fine | 23-26 | 3-5 | E-4 to 7 |
| 3 | Light | 21-24 | 5-7 | 7 to I-9 |
| 4 | Medium | 16-20 | 7-9 | I-9 to K-10.5 |
| 5 | Bulky | 12-15 | 9-11 | K-10.5 to M-13 |
| 6 | Super Bulky | 7-11 | 11-17 | M-13 to Q |
| 7 | Jumbo | 6 or fewer | 17+ | Q and larger |

These gauge ranges are starting points, not guarantees. Check your gauge on every project before you commit your yardage.

## How does yarn weight affect yardage requirements?

Thicker yarn uses more fiber per yard. So you get less length per skein at the same gram weight. A 100g skein of lace weight might give you 800 yards. That same 100g in bulky gives you roughly 100 to 200 yards.

This matters a lot for planning your project. A standard adult sweater in worsted (weight 4) usually needs 1,000 to 1,500 yards. The same sweater shape in super bulky (weight 6) might need only 400 to 600 yards. But each skein costs more per yard at the store because the fiber content per unit of length is higher. When you budget a project, calculate the yards you need, not the number of skeins.

## Which yarn weight is best for beginners?

Worsted weight (size 4) is the standard starting point. There are real reasons for that, beyond just tradition. The stitches are large enough to see clearly. The fabric builds up fast enough to keep you motivated. The yarn also forgives uneven tension.

A US size 7 to 9 needle or an I-9 to K-10.5 hook gives you a stitch size that is easy to spot when you need to tink back or count rows. Lace weight on 000 needles will show every tension mistake clearly. Jumbo yarn on giant needles works up fast but gives you no room to practice stitch definition. Worsted sits in the middle. Mistakes show up, but they are not blown up to a huge size.

## What is the difference between DK and worsted?

DK (double knitting, weight 3) is thinner than worsted (weight 4). DK has a gauge of roughly 21 to 24 stitches per 4 inches. Worsted has a gauge of 16 to 20 stitches per 4 inches. That gap is bigger than it sounds when you are actually knitting.

A pattern written for worsted, made with DK instead, will come out noticeably smaller. That is true unless you adjust your needle size or stitch count. DK makes a drapier, lighter fabric at the same needle size. That is why it is popular for layering pieces and baby items. Worsted makes a denser, warmer fabric. That is why it shows up so often in hat and mitten patterns. You cannot swap one for the other without doing some math.

## Can you substitute one yarn weight for another?

Yes, but you have to recalculate. This is called gauge substitution. You need to swatch the new yarn, measure your actual gauge, and adjust stitch counts to match the finished measurements in the original pattern.

A basic rule: if you go up one full weight category, you need fewer stitches to get the same width. If you go down one weight category, you need more stitches. Here is an example. A 40-stitch cast-on at worsted gauge (5 stitches per inch) gives you 8 inches. At DK gauge (6 stitches per inch), that same 40 stitches gives you only 6.7 inches. That is a real difference in a sleeve cap or a neckline. Measure as you go. Do not trust that the needle size on the label will match what the pattern expects.

## How do fiber content and ply interact with weight?

Yarn weight and ply count are not the same thing. Ply means how many strands are twisted together. Two yarns can have the same ply count but land in totally different weight categories. It depends on how thick each single strand is.

Fiber content also changes how a yarn behaves at a given weight. A 100% merino worsted and a 100% cotton worsted both sit in weight category 4. But the cotton will knit up denser and heavier, with less stretch. The merino will spring back. The cotton will not. A linen-cotton blend at fingering weight (category 1) will drape in a completely different way than a superwash wool in the same category. The weight number tells you about thickness and approximate gauge. It does not tell you about feel, drape, or stretch. Those things come from fiber content and how the yarn is made. They matter just as much for fit and wearability.

## Where do you find the weight category on a yarn label?

The skein band should show a yarn weight symbol. It looks like a small skein icon with a number inside it. The band should also show recommended needle and hook sizes and a suggested gauge. The [Craft Yarn Council label standards](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/how-to-read-yarn-label) define what that information should include.

If the label is missing, or you are working from a destash with no band, wrap the yarn around a ruler for one inch and count the wraps. That wraps-per-inch (WPI) number maps to weight categories. Roughly 30 or more WPI is lace. 14 to 16 WPI is worsted. 6 to 8 WPI is bulky. The [Yarn Sub tool at fibertools.app](https://fibertools.app) can help you match WPI to weight categories and find substitutes when you are working without a label.

## Frequently asked questions

### What are the standard yarn weight categories?
Yarn weights are sorted into 8 categories. They are Lace (0), Super Fine (1), Fine (2), Light (3), Medium/Worsted (4), Bulky (5), Super Bulky (6), and Jumbo (7). The Craft Yarn Council created these categories. They help crafters match yarn to patterns in a consistent way. Each weight comes with a recommended needle or hook size and a gauge range. This makes it easier to substitute yarns or pick the right fiber for your project.

### What is the difference between worsted and bulky yarn?
Worsted weight yarn is medium thickness. It typically knits at 16 to 20 stitches per 4 inches. Bulky yarn is noticeably thicker. It knits at 12 to 15 stitches per 4 inches. Worsted is the most versatile and widely used weight. It works well for garments, accessories, and home goods. Bulky yarn works up much faster. It is great for cozy blankets, scarves, and outerwear. Bulky projects need larger needles, usually US size 9 to 11. Worsted uses US size 7 to 9.

### How do I know which yarn weight to use for my project?
Always start with your pattern. It will tell you the recommended yarn weight and gauge. If you are designing your own project or swapping yarns, think about the drape, warmth, and stitch definition you want. Lightweight yarns like fingering or DK make a delicate, drapey fabric. That works well for shawls and garments. Heavier weights like bulky make warm, structured pieces. Swatching is a must. It confirms that your yarn will behave the way you expect before you start the full project.

### Can I substitute a different yarn weight than what a pattern calls for?
Yes, you can use a different yarn weight. But you will need to adjust your needle or hook size and recalculate your gauge and stitch counts. Going up a weight makes a larger, airier fabric. Going down makes something denser and smaller. Many experienced crafters swap one nearby weight for another, like DK for worsted. But big changes can seriously alter the finished size and drape of a project. Always swatch carefully when you substitute weights.

### What yarn weight is best for beginners?
Medium weight yarn, also called worsted weight, is widely recommended for beginners in both knitting and crochet. Its thickness makes it easy to see each stitch. That helps learners spot and fix mistakes quickly. Worsted yarn works with mid-sized needles and hooks. That means US size 7 to 9 for knitting and 5mm to 5.5mm for crochet. Those sizes are comfortable to hold and use. Worsted is also easy to find in a wide range of colors and fiber types. That makes it simple to find affordable options for practice projects.
