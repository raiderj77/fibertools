# Cross Stitch Conversion: How to Turn Embroidery Patterns into Knit or Crochet

Cross stitch patterns are basically pixel grids. Knit and crochet stitches are basically pixels too. You can turn almost any cross stitch chart into colorwork. Just treat each stitch symbol as one knit or crochet stitch. The main problem is stitch gauge. Knit and crochet stitches are not square. Your finished piece will look different from the original unless you plan for that.

---

## Why does my converted pattern look stretched or squished?

Your stitch gauge is not 1:1 in height and width. Cross stitch assumes it is. A cross stitch grid is perfectly square. Each X takes up the same space going across as it does going up and down. Knit stitches are usually wider than they are tall. Crochet stitches vary by type.

Here is an example. A common stockinette gauge is about 5 stitches and 7 rows per inch. That gives a stitch ratio of roughly 5 to 7, or about 0.71. So if you knit a chart that is 50 stitches wide and 50 rows tall, your finished fabric will be noticeably wider than it is tall. The [Craft Yarn Council's standard gauge tables](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) show how much this changes across different yarn weights.

You have two ways to fix this:

1. **Compress the chart horizontally.** Remove about every 3rd or 4th column from the original cross stitch chart. The stitches that are left will fill a shape that looks right in knit fabric. This takes time but keeps the same row count.
2. **Scale the chart in software first.** Open the chart image in a tool like [KG-Chart](https://kg-chart.com/) or [StitchFiddle](https://www.stitchfiddle.com/). Resize it to match your stitch ratio before you start. Use your real gauge swatch numbers, not a guess.

Always swatch and measure before you start a full conversion. A 4x4 inch swatch in your actual yarn and needle size will show you your real stitch to row ratio.

## Which cross stitch patterns convert best?

Bold, simple designs with large blocks of color work best. Fine detail and diagonal lines are the hardest to convert.

Cross stitch handles diagonals well. The X shape looks diagonal from a distance. In knitting or crochet, a diagonal is a staircase of single stitches. Each stitch is offset by one per row. At fine gauges like fingering or sport weight, that staircase looks smooth. At bulky gauges it looks chunky and blocky. Patterns with lots of thin diagonal lines or small text will look blurry or hard to read after you convert them.

Good candidates:

- Geometric repeats like Fair Isle motifs and stripes with simple shapes
- Bold folk-art florals where petals are at least 3 stitches wide
- Large animal silhouettes
- Alphabet charts where each letter is at least 7 stitches tall

Poor candidates:

- Photorealistic portraits with shading
- Patterns that use fractional stitches like quarter or half cross stitches
- Text shorter than about 10 rows tall
- Anything with more than 5 to 6 colors per row if you plan to strand knit it

## How do I handle the color symbols?

Map each cross stitch thread color to a yarn color before you start. Keep that key where you can see it while you work. Cross stitch patterns use DMC or Anchor thread numbers. Those numbers have no direct yarn match.

Most colorwork knitters print the chart. Then they tape or staple a small yarn sample next to each symbol in the key. When you are in the middle of a row late at night, you do not want to search a list to remember which symbol was the dusty blue.

For color matching, [DMC publishes a conversion chart](https://www.dmc.com/us/color-card.html) that maps thread colors to approximate values. Sites like [Yarnsub](https://yarnsub.com/) let you search yarn by color family. Neither one is a perfect science. Hold your yarn skeins up to your printed chart in natural light and make your best call.

If the original pattern has 12 colors but you want to simplify, look at which colors appear the least. A pattern that uses three slightly different greens for shading can often be reduced to two greens. The overall look of the design usually stays the same.

## Can I use a cross stitch chart for crochet colorwork?

Yes, and the same stitch ratio issue applies. Single crochet (sc) worked in the round or in rows is your closest match to stockinette in terms of stitch shape. A typical sc gauge is about 16 stitches and 20 rows per 4 inches. That gives a ratio of about 0.80. That is closer to square than stockinette, but still not 1:1.

Tapestry crochet is the most common way to convert cross stitch charts for crochet. You carry unused colors across the row and work over them. [Craftsy's tapestry crochet overview](https://www.craftsy.com/post/tapestry-crochet/) covers the carry method in detail. The key limit is that carrying more than 2 to 3 colors at once gets bulky fast. Simplify your color count before you start.

Double crochet (dc) stitches are taller. They create a more rectangular pixel. That distorts chart designs more than single crochet does. Stick with sc for chart-based colorwork. Only use dc if you adjust your chart for the dc stitch ratio first.

## What software actually helps with this?

A few tools do the math for you. You do not have to work it all out on paper. Enter your gauge swatch results and they resize the chart for you.

- **[KG-Chart LE](https://kg-chart.com/)** - free, made specifically for knitting charts, lets you import images and adjust for stitch ratio
- **[StitchFiddle](https://www.stitchfiddle.com/)** - works in your browser, handles both knitting and crochet, the free version is usable
- **[Stitchboard](https://stitchboard.com/)** - converts images to knitting charts with gauge adjustment built in

None of these replace a physical swatch. They help you start with a better chart. But your actual yarn and needles or hook will behave differently than any software can predict. Measure as you go. This is especially important on bigger projects. A small distortion can add up over hundreds of rows.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is cross stitch conversion and why do I need it?
Cross stitch conversion means translating thread colors, fabric counts, or pattern measurements between different brands or systems. You need it when you swap one embroidery thread brand for another, like DMC to Anchor. You also need it when resizing a pattern for a different fabric count. Without accurate conversion, colors can look noticeably different. Stitch counts may not line up right either. Both of those problems affect your finished piece.

### How do I convert DMC floss colors to Anchor or Madeira?
Use a cross stitch conversion chart or digital tool to find the closest Anchor or Madeira match for your DMC floss numbers. Thread color systems are not identical, so conversions are close but not exact. Tools like the converter on fibertools.app can quickly find the nearest match. Always compare physical thread samples when color accuracy really matters for your project.

### Can I convert a cross stitch pattern to a different fabric count?
Yes, you can resize a cross stitch pattern by changing the fabric count. Divide the stitch width and height by the new count to get the finished size in inches. For example, a design that is 100 stitches wide on 14-count Aida measures about 7 inches. On 18-count it measures roughly 5.5 inches. The number of stitches stays the same. Only the physical size of the finished embroidery changes.

### How do I convert cross stitch patterns into knitting or crochet charts?
Cross stitch grid patterns can be adapted into knitting or crochet colorwork charts. Both use a similar grid structure. Each cross stitch square becomes one knit stitch or one crochet stitch. Keep in mind that knitting and crochet stitches are not perfectly square. The finished design may look a little wider or taller than the original. Adjusting the chart's proportions ahead of time helps fix that distortion.

### What information do I need before using a cross stitch conversion tool?
Before you use a conversion tool, gather your original thread brand and color numbers. Also note your current fabric count and your target brand or fabric count. Knowing your finished size goal helps too. Having all of this ready lets the tool give you accurate thread matches and recalculated dimensions in one step. That saves time and helps you avoid buying the wrong materials.
