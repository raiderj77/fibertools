# How to Crochet a Graphghan (Picture Blanket)

You may have seen them on Instagram and Reddit. Blankets with Mario, pet portraits, band logos, or mountain scenes crocheted right into the fabric. These are called graphghans. They are one of the most impressive things you can make with a hook and yarn.

A graphghan turns a pixel grid into a crochet blanket. Each square on the chart equals one stitch block in a specific color. The idea is simple. But it takes planning, patience, and a good system for handling multiple yarn colors. Here is how to do it right.

## What Is a Graphghan and When Would You Make One?

A graphghan (graph plus afghan) is a blanket made from a pixel chart. Each cell on the chart stands for one stitch or stitch block in a specific color. The technique turns any image into a crocheted picture.

Most graphghans use corner-to-corner crochet, also called C2C. In C2C, each "pixel" is a small block of 3 double crochet stitches. You can also use single crochet, half double crochet, or bobble stitch. Each one gives a different texture and level of detail. C2C is the most popular choice. Each block is roughly square, so the image does not look stretched or squished.

Graphghans work for almost any image you can turn into a pixel grid. Think video game characters, pet photos, sports logos, landscapes, text quotes, or geometric designs. More colors and more pixels means higher detail. It also means more work managing all those colors.

## How Do You Plan a Graphghan Step by Step?

### Step 1: Create or Find Your Chart

Start with a pixel chart. You can make one from any image using free tools like StitchFiddle, Pixel Stitch, or even a spreadsheet. Each cell equals one stitch block. Each color in the chart equals one yarn color.

**Resolution guidelines:**
- Simple designs (text, logos): 50 to 80 pixels wide
- Medium detail (cartoon characters, simple portraits): 80 to 120 pixels wide
- High detail (photo-realistic): 150 or more pixels wide

A 100-pixel-wide chart at C2C gauge makes a blanket about 40 inches wide. C2C gauge is roughly 2.5 blocks per inch. A 150-pixel chart at the same gauge makes a blanket about 60 inches wide.

### Step 2: Choose Your Stitch Method

**C2C (corner-to-corner)** is the standard method. Each block is 3 chains plus 3 double crochet stitches. This makes a square pixel. The blanket starts at one corner. It increases diagonally to the widest point. Then it decreases to the opposite corner. The [C2C Blanket Calculator](/c2c-calculator) does all the math for block counts, diagonal rows, and yardage.

**Single crochet graphghans** make a tighter, heavier fabric. They also give higher detail because there are more stitches per inch. This means more pixels per inch. Single crochet works better for small, detailed images. But it takes longer to finish.

### Step 3: Calculate Your Yarn

Managing colors is where graphghans get tricky. You need to know how much yarn each color needs before you begin.

**Count your pixels per color.** Go through the chart and count how many blocks each color uses. For example, a chart with 10,000 total blocks might break down like this: white 4,200 blocks, black 2,800, red 1,500, blue 1,000, yellow 500.

**Convert blocks to yardage.** In worsted weight C2C, each block uses about 1.5 to 2 yards of yarn. So 4,200 white blocks times 1.75 yards equals 7,350 yards of white. The [Yarn Calculator](/yarn-calculator) helps you estimate total yardage once you know your block counts.

**Add 15% per color.** Graphghans waste more yarn than solid-color blankets. Color changes, carrying yarn, and cutting tails all use extra yarn. Budget 15% more for each color.

## What Are the Best Tips for Managing Colors?

**Use bobbins, not full skeins.** Wind each color onto a small bobbin or butterfly. Working from full skeins creates a tangled mess of 8 to 12 skeins hanging from your blanket.

**Carry yarn for short runs.** If a color shows up again within 3 to 4 blocks, carry the yarn behind your work instead of cutting and rejoining. This cuts down the number of ends to weave in. For runs longer than 4 blocks, cut the yarn and rejoin it.

**Work from a printed chart.** Print your chart in color. Use a highlighter to mark off each row as you finish it. Digital charts work too. But paper lets you see the whole picture and track your progress without scrolling.

**Weave in ends as you go.** A graphghan with 15 color changes per row makes hundreds of ends. If you save them all for the end, you will spend 10 to 20 hours just weaving. Instead, crochet over your tails as you work each row. This traps them inside the next few blocks.

**Use consistent dye lots per color.** Buy all skeins of each color at the same time. Color sections in a graphghan are large. Dye lot differences between skeins will show.

**Common mistakes:**
- Not counting pixels per color before buying yarn, then running out mid-project
- Using too many colors (8 or more makes things chaotic for beginners; start with 3 to 5)
- Forgetting that C2C works diagonally, so row 1 of the chart is not the first row you crochet
- Not checking that the chart size will make the blanket size you want

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many colors should a beginner graphghan use?

Start with 2 to 3 colors for your first graphghan. Two colors, one for the background and one for the image, teaches you the color-change method without being overwhelming. Three colors adds some depth. Save projects with 6 or more colors for after you finish at least one graphghan. Each extra color adds more yarn ends and more bobbins to manage.

### How do I read a C2C graphghan chart?

C2C charts read diagonally, not row by row. Start in the bottom-right corner with block 1. Row 2 is the next diagonal up. Each diagonal row adds one block until you reach the widest point. Then the blocks decrease. Many free chart tools include diagonal row numbers so you do not have to count them yourself.

### Can I make a graphghan with single crochet instead of C2C?

Yes. Single crochet graphghans work row by row, from bottom to top and right to left. This is simpler to follow than diagonal C2C. Each stitch equals one pixel. The trade-off is that single crochet stitches are not square. Your image will look shorter and wider than the chart unless you adjust. Use extended single crochet to get a more square pixel.

### How long does a graphghan take to complete?

A 10,000-block C2C graphghan in worsted weight takes about 80 to 120 hours of active crocheting. This depends on your speed and how many color changes there are. At 2 hours a day, that is 6 to 8 weeks. Larger or more detailed designs with 18,000 or more blocks can take 3 to 5 months. Color changes slow you down more than the actual stitching does.
