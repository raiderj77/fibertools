# Best Crochet Hooks: What Actually Works at the Needle

**The short answer:** For most crocheters, an ergonomic aluminum hook in a mid-range size (4.0 to 6.0 mm) from Clover, Furls, or Tulip will cover 80% of projects without hand fatigue. Use steel hooks for thread work. Choose inline vs. tapered heads based on your tension style. Buy a basic set before you spend money on premium singles. Details below.

## What is the difference between inline and tapered hooks?

Inline hooks have a throat that lines up with the shaft. This makes the diameter the same all the way through. Tapered hooks, also called Bates-style, have a throat that slopes more gradually into the shaft. Your tension style decides which one works better for you.

If you crochet tightly, an inline hook like Susan Bates gives you a more consistent stitch size. The yarn sits at the same depth every time. If you crochet loosely or work a lot of open, lacy stitches, a tapered hook like Boye lets the yarn slide off more easily. Neither style is better than the other. That is exactly why gauge swatches exist. Two people can use the same pattern and yarn and get very different stitch counts. It all depends on hook style and hand tension. Measure as you go.

## Which hook brands are actually worth buying?

Clover Amour, Tulip Etimo, and Furls Streamline are worth the money for most crocheters. Their handles cut down on hand fatigue during long sessions. The head shape stays consistent across sizes. Boye and Susan Bates metal hooks are reliable budget options for beginners. Skip unbranded bulk packs. Their sizing is not reliable.

**Clover Amour, Tulip Etimo, and Furls Streamline are the three most recommended by working crocheters for a reason: they hold up, the handles reduce fatigue, and the heads are consistent across the size range.** Budget picks like Boye and Susan Bates metal hooks are solid for beginners. Avoid unbranded hooks from bulk packs if you want consistent sizing.

Here is how they break down:

- **Clover Amour** (around $8 to $10 per hook): Soft rubber grip, inline head, consistent sizing. The grip is wide enough that most people with hand pain or arthritis report real relief. [Clover's own size chart](https://www.clover-mfg.com/) shows these run true to metric.
- **Tulip Etimo** (around $12 to $15 per hook): Made in Japan, very smooth head, tapered style. The grip is slimmer than the Clover Amour. Popular for amigurumi and tight work because the yarn releases cleanly.
- **Furls Streamline** (around $28 to $35 per hook): Aluminum shaft, resin handle, inline head. More expensive, but the balance feels noticeably different from plastic-handled hooks. Worth it if you crochet every day and your hands are feeling it.
- **Susan Bates Silvalume** (around $3 to $5 per hook): Aluminum, inline, no frills. A great starter hook. No grip handle means longer sessions can cause fatigue, but for occasional use these work fine.
- **Boye** (around $3 to $5 per hook): Tapered, aluminum. The standard example of a tapered hook. Works well for loose crocheters.

## What size hooks do I actually need?

A set covering 3.5 mm to 8.0 mm handles most projects you will actually make. Most everyday yarn weights, from worsted through bulky, land in the 4.0 to 6.0 mm range. Only add smaller or larger hooks when a specific project calls for them. Do not buy a full range upfront.

**Start with a set covering 3.5 mm to 8.0 mm and you will handle the vast majority of yarn weights from fingering through bulky.** Most everyday projects land in the 4.0 to 6.0 mm range.

The [Craft Yarn Council's standard yarn weight system](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) gives hook size recommendations for each yarn weight:

- Lace / thread: 1.5 to 2.25 mm (or steel hooks, see below)
- Fingering / sock: 2.25 to 3.5 mm
- DK / light worsted: 3.75 to 4.5 mm
- Worsted: 4.5 to 5.5 mm
- Bulky: 6.5 to 9.0 mm
- Super bulky: 9.0 mm and up

These are starting points, not rules. [Check your gauge against the pattern before committing to a full project](/blog/analyzing-the-impact-of-yarn-weight-and-fiber-content-on-gau/). A 5.0 mm hook in your hands might behave like a 5.5 mm hook in someone else's.

## Do I need steel crochet hooks for thread crochet?

Yes, steel hooks are necessary for thread size 10 and finer. Standard aluminum hooks flex too much at those tiny sizes to make consistent stitches. Also, steel hook sizing runs opposite to standard hooks. A higher number means a smaller hook. That surprises a lot of people when they go shopping.

**Yes, for thread sizes 10 and finer, steel hooks are the right tool.** Standard aluminum hooks at those tiny sizes would bend or flex and make consistent stitches nearly impossible.

Steel hooks are sized differently from standard hooks, which trips up a lot of people. Steel size 7 is about 1.65 mm. The numbers run in reverse. A higher number means a smaller hook. [Lion Brand's steel hook conversion chart](https://www.lionbrand.com/pages/yarn-hook-needle-size-chart) is a reliable reference for matching steel sizes to thread weights.

For doilies, thread appliques, or any project calling for size 10 crochet thread, pick up a basic set of steel hooks from Boye or Susan Bates. You do not need to spend a lot here. Thread projects are slow and the hooks take very little wear.

## What about wooden or resin hooks?

Wooden and resin hooks are a good choice for slippery fibers like silk or bamboo. Metal hooks let those yarns slide too fast to control. The natural friction of wood or resin slows things down just enough. The trade-off is durability. Wood can crack if dropped, so these hooks work best as targeted additions rather than your everyday hooks.

**Wooden and resin hooks work well for slippery yarns like bamboo or silk, where a metal hook causes the yarn to slide too fast.** The slight friction of wood or resin gives you more control.

Furls makes resin hooks in their Odyssey line (around $30 to $40 each). ChiaoGoo offers wooden hooks that are well-regarded for smooth finishing. The trade-off is durability. Wood can crack if dropped or stored carelessly. Some resin handles chip over time. For most everyday yarns like wool, acrylic, or cotton, aluminum is faster and more consistent than wood.

## How do I store hooks so they actually stay organized?

A zippered roll case with individual slots is the most practical way to store a working hook collection. Loose hooks in a bag bend tips and get lost constantly. A dedicated hook roll from Clover works well. An art supply pencil roll does the same job for less money if you want to keep costs down.

**A zippered roll case with individual slots is the most practical solution for a working hook collection.** Loose hooks in a bag means bent tips and lost sizes.

Clover sells a hook case that holds about 10 hooks and lies flat. A pencil roll from any art supply store works just as well for less money. If you travel with your hooks, a hard-shell case protects tips better than a fabric roll. Keep steel hooks separate from standard hooks. The two sizing systems are different enough to cause real confusion in the middle of a project.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the best crochet hook for beginners?
Ergonomic aluminum hooks in a size 5 mm (H-8) are the best choice for beginners. The medium size works well with worsted weight yarn, which is the most forgiving for learning. Aluminum hooks glide smoothly through yarn without snagging. Ergonomic handles reduce hand fatigue during longer sessions. Brands like Clover Amour and Susan Bates offer beginner-friendly sets with a range of sizes so you can try different options as your skills grow.

### What is the difference between aluminum and ergonomic crochet hooks?
Ergonomic crochet hooks have a soft, cushioned grip handle. Aluminum hooks have a thin, uniform metal shaft. Aluminum hooks are lightweight, affordable, and precise. They work great for detailed work and tight stitches. Ergonomic hooks spread pressure across your palm. This reduces hand and wrist strain during long crochet sessions. Crafters with arthritis, carpal tunnel, or those who crochet for hours at a time usually prefer ergonomic hooks for comfort.

### What size crochet hook do I need for worsted weight yarn?
A 5 mm (H-8) crochet hook is the standard recommended size for worsted weight yarn. Most worsted weight yarn labels suggest a hook between 4.5 mm and 5.5 mm. Always check the yarn's label as a starting point. Hook size affects tension and drape, so you may need to adjust based on your personal style. Going up a size loosens stitches. Going down a size creates a tighter, denser fabric.

### Are expensive crochet hooks worth it?
Yes, premium crochet hooks are often worth the investment, especially for frequent crafters. Higher-end hooks from brands like Addi, Clover Amour, or Furls offer better materials, better balance, and ergonomic designs. These improve both comfort and stitch consistency. Budget hooks work fine for casual projects. But crafters who crochet regularly often notice less hand fatigue and smoother yarn flow with quality tools. A good hook can improve your crocheting experience and your finished results.

### What crochet hook material is best for slippery yarn?
Wooden or bamboo crochet hooks work best with slippery yarn types like silk, bamboo, or mercerized cotton. Their naturally textured surface creates just enough friction to keep stitches from sliding off. This gives you better stitch control. Metal hooks are smooth and fast, but they can cause slippery yarn to move too freely and lead to dropped stitches. Resin hooks offer a middle ground. They provide some grip while staying lightweight and comfortable for longer crochet sessions.
