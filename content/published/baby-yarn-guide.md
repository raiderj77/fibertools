# How to Choose Yarn for Baby Projects

Baby projects are different from adult projects. The yarn must be soft on newborn skin. It needs to survive weekly machine washing. It has to hold up to drool and spit-up. It must be free of anything that could hurt or irritate a baby. You cannot just grab whatever looks pretty on the shelf.

The right baby yarn does three things: it has safe fiber content, it is machine washable, and it is the right weight for the project. Here is how to pick the best yarn for every baby project, from blankets to booties.

> **A mistake I made:** I used fluffy yarn before I could read my stitches, and dark yarn too early, so I spent more time guessing than crocheting. Start with a smooth, light colored, medium weight yarn so you can see your stitches.

## What Makes Yarn Baby-Safe?

Baby yarn needs to meet three must-have requirements.

**Softness.** Baby skin is thinner and more sensitive than adult skin. Scratchy wool, stiff cotton, or rough acrylic will cause irritation. Test yarn by pressing it against the inside of your wrist or your neck. If it prickles there, it will bother a baby.

**Washability.** Baby items get dirty all the time. Any yarn that needs hand washing or dry cleaning will not work for baby items. The yarn must survive regular machine washing on warm or hot. It should ideally survive machine drying too. Check the yarn label for "superwash" on wool, or "machine wash and dry" on acrylic and cotton blends.

**Safety.** Avoid yarns with loose fibers that can shed into a baby's mouth or nose. Stay away from mohair, angora, brushed yarns, and eyelash or novelty yarns. Stick to smooth, tightly spun yarns that shed very little. Also avoid yarns with metallic threads or beads.

## Which Fibers Work Best for Babies?

**Acrylic (best for everyday items).** Acrylic is machine washable, machine dryable, hypoallergenic, and affordable. It comes in every color. Modern baby acrylics are very soft. Brands like Caron Simply Soft Baby and Red Heart Soft Baby are made just for infant items. Acrylic keeps its shape wash after wash.

**Superwash merino wool.** This is softer than acrylic and naturally regulates temperature. It costs more, around $8 to $15 per skein compared to $3 to $6 for acrylic. It must be superwash-treated, or it will felt in the washing machine. It is great for baby hats, sweaters, and special-occasion items.

**Cotton and cotton blends.** Cotton is breathable and cool for warm-weather baby items. Pure cotton can feel stiff. Look for cotton and acrylic blends, like 60/40 or 50/50, that mix cotton's breathability with acrylic's softness. These work well for summer blankets, bibs, and washcloths.

**Bamboo blends.** Bamboo blends are silky-soft and naturally antibacterial. Bamboo and cotton or bamboo and acrylic blends make beautiful baby blankets. They cost more than pure acrylic, but the softness and drape make them worth it for special projects.

**Fibers to avoid for babies:** mohair, angora, alpaca (too warm and can shed), regular untreated wool (itchy and felts), and any novelty or textured yarn.

## What Yarn Weight Should You Use?

The [Yarn Weight Chart](/yarn-weight-chart) shows the full CYC standard. For baby projects, three weights are used most often.

**DK weight (CYC 3)** is the classic baby yarn weight. It makes a fabric that is warm but not heavy. It works up at a good pace and has a soft drape that is perfect for blankets and garments. Most "baby yarn" sold in stores is DK weight. Gauge: roughly 21 to 24 stitches per 4 inches on US 5 to 7 needles or size 7 to I/9 hooks.

**Worsted weight (CYC 4)** works up faster and makes a slightly heavier fabric. It is good for blankets, stuffed toys, and winter items. Some crafters find worsted-weight baby blankets too thick for warm climates. Gauge: roughly 16 to 20 stitches per 4 inches on US 7 to 9 needles or I/9 to K/10.5 hooks.

**Sport weight (CYC 2)** makes a lighter, more delicate fabric. It is ideal for baby garments, lace-trimmed items, and lightweight summer blankets. It takes longer to work up. Gauge: roughly 23 to 26 stitches per 4 inches on US 3 to 5 needles or E/4 to 7 hooks.

## What Are the Best Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Wash your swatch the way the item will be washed.** If the blanket will go through a hot machine wash every week, wash your swatch the same way. Some yarns shrink 5 to 10 percent after the first wash. You need to know that before you start.

**Make it machine-dryable.** New parents do not have time to lay blankets flat to dry. If your yarn cannot handle a dryer, pick a different yarn. Most acrylic and superwash wool survives machine drying on low or medium heat.

**Choose light or medium colors for visibility.** Babies spit up a lot. Dark colors hide stains but also hide small objects, threads, or hair that could be a safety concern. Medium tones show dirt without looking like a constant mess.

**Avoid long fringe, pom-poms, and buttons on items for babies under 12 months.** These are choking hazards. If the pattern calls for buttons, sew them on with yarn instead of thread and check their tightness often. For decorative elements, crochet or knit them directly into the fabric.

**Common mistakes:**

- Choosing yarn that requires hand washing (the gift will never get used)
- Using rough, cheap acrylic that pills after one wash
- Making a blanket too large (oversized blankets are not recommended for infant sleep)
- Not checking the recipient's fiber preferences (some parents avoid synthetic fibers)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is acrylic yarn safe for babies?

Yes. Modern acrylic yarn is hypoallergenic, non-toxic, and one of the safest choices for baby items. It will not trigger wool allergies. It is machine washable and dryable and holds color well. Look for "baby" acrylic lines from major brands. These are specifically tested for softness and safety against infant skin.

### Can I use wool for baby items?

Only superwash wool. Regular wool felts in the washing machine and can irritate sensitive baby skin. Superwash merino is machine washable, very soft, and naturally regulates temperature. It costs more than acrylic, around $8 to $15 per skein, but it makes beautiful, high-quality baby garments and blankets.

### What yarn weight is best for a baby blanket?

DK weight (CYC 3) is the most popular choice. It makes a blanket that is warm enough for comfort without being too heavy for a small baby. Worsted weight works for winter blankets or faster projects. Sport weight makes a lighter, drapier blanket that is ideal for warmer climates or layering.

### How do I make sure a baby gift will actually get used?

Make it machine washable and machine dryable. That is the single most important thing. Also check with the parents about color preferences and fiber sensitivities. Choose a practical item. Blankets and hats get the most use. Keep the size right. Newborn clothes are outgrown in weeks, but a blanket lasts for years.

## Pick the Right Yarn and Start Making

The best baby yarn is soft, safe, and survives the washing machine. Do not overthink fiber content. A well-chosen acrylic baby yarn will outperform an expensive hand-wash-only merino in a real nursery.
