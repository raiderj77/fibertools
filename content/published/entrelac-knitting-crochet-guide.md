# Entrelac Knitting and Crochet Guide: Woven-Basket Texture from One Strand

Picture a blanket that looks like dozens of fabric strips woven over and under each other in a classic basket pattern. Now imagine that the entire piece was made from a single continuous strand of yarn, worked in one direction without cutting or weaving a single strip. That is entrelac, a modular technique that creates the illusion of interlocking woven squares using nothing more than standard knit or crochet stitches. The effect is stunning, and the process is far more approachable than the finished fabric suggests.

## What Is Entrelac and When Should You Use It?

Entrelac is a modular construction method where small blocks, triangles and rectangles, are worked individually but joined to their neighbors as they are created. Each new block picks up stitches from the edge of a previous block, building a grid of tilted squares that appears to be woven. The technique works in both knitting and crochet, with slightly different mechanics but the same visual result.

Entrelac shines in projects where you want dramatic visual texture without complicated stitch patterns. Blankets and afghans are the most popular application because the block structure translates beautifully to large flat pieces. Scarves and cowls show off the basketweave pattern at a wearable scale. Bags and totes gain both visual interest and structural integrity from the interlocking blocks. Pillows and cushion covers make excellent practice projects with a useful finished product.

The technique also pairs exceptionally well with variegated, gradient, and self-striping yarns. Because each block is worked separately, color transitions land in different positions across the grid, producing a patchwork look from a single skein.

## Step-by-Step: How Entrelac Works

### Base Triangles (Knitting)

Cast on the total number of stitches for your row. If your block size is 8 stitches and you want 5 blocks across, cast on 40 stitches. Work the first triangle by knitting progressively more stitches: knit 2 and turn, purl 2 and turn, knit 3 and turn, purl 3 and turn, continuing until you have worked all 8 stitches of the first block. Repeat across the row to create a sawtooth bottom edge of triangles.

### Right-Leaning Rectangles

With the wrong side facing, pick up and knit stitches along the left edge of the last base triangle. Work each rectangle in short rows: purl across, then on the return row, work to the last stitch and knit it together (SSK) with the first unworked stitch from the adjacent block. This joins the new rectangle to its neighbor one stitch at a time. Continue until all adjacent stitches are consumed. The rectangle tilts to the right.

### Left-Leaning Rectangles

Turn the work to the right side. Pick up and knit stitches along the right edge of the block below. Work short rows again, but this time the joining decrease happens on the purl side (P2tog with the adjacent stitch). These rectangles tilt to the left, creating the characteristic over-under weave pattern when they alternate with the right-leaning blocks.

### Top Triangles

Finish the piece with a row of top triangles that mirror the base triangles. Work progressively fewer stitches in each block, decreasing one stitch per right-side row until the triangle closes. This creates a straight top edge that matches the bottom.

### Crochet Entrelac Variant

Crochet entrelac follows the same grid logic but uses single crochet or half double crochet blocks instead of knit short rows. Chain the width of one block, then work rows of single crochet, joining to the adjacent block with a slip stitch at the end of each row. The result is a slightly thicker fabric with the same woven appearance. Crochet entrelac tends to be faster because crochet stitches are taller, so fewer rows are needed per block.

## Tips, Yarn Selection, and Common Mistakes

### Yarn Selection

Variegated and self-striping yarns are where entrelac truly shines. Because each block starts at a different point in the color sequence, you get a randomized patchwork effect from a single skein. Long-repeat gradient yarns create a flowing color transition across the piece. Even solid-color entrelac looks sophisticated, the tilted stockinette blocks catch light at different angles, creating subtle shadow and texture.

Choose a smooth yarn in DK weight (CYC 3) or worsted weight (CYC 4) for your first project. These weights produce blocks large enough to see the pattern clearly but small enough to keep the project manageable. Avoid heavily textured or boucle yarns, which obscure the clean block edges that make entrelac recognizable.

### Block Size Matters

Smaller blocks (6 to 8 stitches) create a finer, more intricate woven look. Larger blocks (10 to 12 stitches) show more of each color and work up faster but can look bulky on small projects. For scarves, 8-stitch blocks with 3 blocks across is a reliable starting point. For blankets, 10-stitch blocks with 10 to 15 blocks across covers a throw-size width efficiently.

### Tension at Pickup Points

The most visible flaw in entrelac is loose stitches where you pick up along the edge of a previous block. Pull the first picked-up stitch firmly, and consider using a needle one size smaller for the pickup row only. Blocking the finished piece also evens out minor tension differences at the joins.

### Common Mistakes

Picking up too many or too few stitches along block edges throws off the grid alignment. Count carefully and ensure you pick up exactly the same number of stitches as your block width. Forgetting which direction your rectangles lean is another frequent error, right-leaning blocks are worked on wrong-side rows, left-leaning on right-side rows. Place a stitch marker on the right side of the fabric so you always know your orientation.

## Real Projects to Try

### Gradient Entrelac Blanket

Choose a gradient yarn cake in worsted weight with a long color transition. Cast on for 12 blocks of 10 stitches each (120 stitches). Work rows of alternating right-leaning and left-leaning rectangles until the blanket reaches your desired length. The gradient creates a natural flow from one color to the next across the woven blocks. Expect to use approximately 1,800 to 2,200 yards for a throw-size blanket.

### Solid-Color Entrelac Cowl

Cast on for 3 blocks of 8 stitches in a DK-weight merino. Work in the round (entrelac in the round eliminates the need for edge triangles on the sides) until the cowl is about 10 inches tall. A single solid color highlights the geometric texture and shadow play of the tilted blocks. This project uses roughly 250 to 300 yards and makes an excellent gift.

### Crochet Entrelac Tote

Using worsted-weight cotton, chain and work a flat entrelac panel of 5 blocks by 8 rows for the front, and a matching panel for the back. Single crochet blocks in alternating colors, try a natural cream and dusty sage, give a rustic woven look. Seam the sides and bottom, add crocheted strap handles, and optionally line the interior with fabric. The double-thick entrelac construction is sturdy enough for groceries and books.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is entrelac hard to learn?

Entrelac looks complex but uses only basic skills, knit, purl, picking up stitches, and simple decreases like SSK and P2tog. The challenge is conceptual rather than technical. Once you understand the sequence of base triangles, right-leaning rectangles, and left-leaning rectangles, the pattern repeats predictably. Most knitters grasp the rhythm within the first two tiers of blocks.

### Can you crochet entrelac?

Yes. Crochet entrelac replaces the knit short rows with rows of single crochet or half double crochet, joining to adjacent blocks with slip stitches along the edge. The construction logic is identical to knit entrelac, base triangles, alternating rectangle tiers, and top triangles. Crochet entrelac produces a slightly thicker and more structured fabric that works especially well for bags, baskets, and home decor items.

### Does entrelac use more yarn than regular knitting?

Entrelac uses roughly 10 to 15 percent more yarn than flat stockinette of the same dimensions. The extra consumption comes from the frequent turning, edge joins, and the slightly denser fabric at the pickup points. When planning a project, calculate your yardage for standard stockinette and add a comfortable buffer. Buying one extra skein is always wise for entrelac projects.

### What is the best yarn for entrelac?

Variegated and self-striping yarns produce the most dramatic results because each block lands on a different part of the color sequence, creating an organic patchwork look. Long-repeat gradient cakes are especially popular for entrelac blankets. For texture-focused projects, solid colors in smooth plied yarn showcase the block geometry and light play. Avoid fuzzy or textured yarns that hide the clean block edges.
