# How to Resize a Knitting or Crochet Pattern

You found the perfect sweater pattern, but it does not come in your size. Maybe you want to turn a baby blanket into a throw. Or maybe you need a hat pattern to fit a child instead of an adult. Whatever the reason, resizing is one of the most useful skills a crafter can learn. It is much less scary than it sounds.

Resizing comes down to one simple idea. If you know your gauge and your target measurements, you can figure out exactly how many stitches and rows you need. Let's go through the process step by step.

## What Is Pattern Resizing and When Do You Need It?

Pattern resizing means changing the stitch counts, row counts, and shaping in a pattern. The goal is to make the finished project a different size than what the pattern says. You might need to resize when a pattern does not include your size. You might also need it when you want to use a different yarn weight than the pattern calls for. It also helps when your gauge does not match the pattern gauge, or when you want to change the size of something like a blanket or pillow cover.

Resizing is simple for basic shapes like rectangles, squares, and tubes. It gets harder with shaped garments like sweaters and cardigans. With those, you have to adjust armholes, necklines, and sleeve caps too. But even garment resizing is totally doable once you understand the math.

## The Math of Resizing: A Step-by-Step Guide

Do not worry. This is basic arithmetic, not calculus. Here is the process.

### Step 1: Determine Your Gauge

Knit or crochet a swatch using your chosen yarn and needle or hook size. Measure it carefully after blocking. You need two numbers: stitches per inch and rows per inch.

For example, your swatch might give you 4.5 stitches per inch and 6 rows per inch in stockinette stitch.

### Step 2: Determine Your Target Measurements

Decide exactly how wide and how long you want each piece to be. For a garment, take your body measurements. Then add the ease the pattern suggests, or the ease you prefer. For a blanket, just decide on the finished size you want.

For example, say you want a sweater front that is 20 inches wide and 26 inches long.

### Step 3: Calculate Stitch and Row Counts

Multiply your target width by your stitch gauge:

**20 inches x 4.5 stitches per inch = 90 stitches**

Multiply your target length by your row gauge:

**26 inches x 6 rows per inch = 156 rows**

That is the main idea. Now you know how many stitches to cast on and about how many rows to work.

### Step 4: Check Stitch Pattern Multiples

Some stitch patterns repeat over a set number of stitches. Your total stitch count must be a multiple of that repeat, plus any edge stitches. Say a pattern repeats over 6 stitches and has 2 edge stitches. Your total could be 86 (14 repeats x 6 + 2), 92 (15 repeats x 6 + 2), or 98 (16 repeats x 6 + 2).

Pick the multiple closest to your calculated number. In our example, 90 stitches would round up to 92. That is 15 repeats of 6, plus 2 edge stitches.

### Step 5: Scale the Shaping

Garments have increases, decreases, armhole shaping, and neckline shaping. You will need to recalculate where those happen. The idea is the same. Convert the pattern's measurements to your gauge.

Say the original pattern decreases for the armhole over 2 inches. Your row gauge is 6 rows per inch. That gives you 12 rows to work the armhole decreases. Count how many stitches need to be decreased. Then space them out evenly across those rows.

## Resizing Different Project Types

### Flat Pieces (Blankets, Scarves, Dishcloths)

These are the easiest projects to resize because they are simple rectangles. Calculate your stitch count for the width you want. Adjust for any stitch pattern multiple. Then work rows until you hit the length you need. No shaping math required.

One common mistake is forgetting that stitch gauge and row gauge do not always grow at the same rate. If you double the width, you will not always need exactly double the rows to keep the same proportions. Always calculate width and length separately.

### Hats and Beanies

Hats are worked as tubes with decreases at the crown. To resize, measure the head circumference. Subtract 1 to 2 inches for negative ease. Then multiply by your stitch gauge. The crown decreases follow standard steps. Usually you decrease evenly across each round until 8 to 10 stitches remain.

### Garments (Sweaters, Cardigans, Vests)

Garments are the hardest projects to resize. But the same ideas apply. Break the garment into its pieces, such as the front, back, and sleeves. Then resize each piece to fit your measurements.

Pay close attention to chest width and ease, armhole depth and width, shoulder width, neckline width and depth, and sleeve length and taper. Compare your target measurements to the measurements on the pattern's schematic. The difference tells you how much to adjust each part.

### Amigurumi and Toys

The easiest way to resize amigurumi is to change your yarn weight and hook size. A pattern made for Worsted (4) weight yarn on a 3.5mm hook will come out smaller in Sport (2) weight on a 2.75mm hook. It will come out larger in Bulky (5) weight on a 5mm hook. The stitch counts stay the same. Only the finished size changes.

## Tips, Common Mistakes, and Variations

**Always swatch in the actual stitch pattern.** Stockinette, garter, ribbing, and cables all give different gauges, even with the same yarn and needles. A cable pattern will be narrower than stockinette because the cables pull the fabric in.

**Do not forget about ease.** Ease is the difference between your body measurement and the finished garment measurement. A close-fitting sweater might have 1 to 2 inches of positive ease. A relaxed pullover might have 4 to 6 inches. When resizing, add the right amount of ease to your body measurements before you calculate stitch counts.

**Block your swatch before measuring.** Unblocked fabric can look very different from blocked fabric. This is especially true with wool and cotton. If you measure an unblocked swatch, your numbers will be off from the start.

**Watch out for proportional changes.** When you make a sweater wider, you may also need to make the armholes deeper and the shoulders wider. Just adding stitches to the width without changing other parts can make the garment fit strangely.

**Use a different yarn weight on purpose.** Say the pattern uses DK (3) weight and you want to use Worsted (4). You will get fewer stitches per inch. That means wider fabric per stitch, which could be a quick way to size up. Just remember that the fabric weight and drape will also change.

**Keep notes on everything.** Write down every calculation and every change you make. When you need the second sleeve to match the first, or you want to resize the same pattern again later, your notes will save you a lot of time.

## Real Project Examples

**Resizing a baby blanket to a lap throw.** The pattern says to cast on 120 stitches in Worsted (4) weight at a gauge of 4 stitches per inch. That makes a 30-inch blanket. You want 48 inches. At the same gauge: 48 x 4 = 192 stitches. If the stitch pattern repeats over 8 stitches, 192 already works because it is a multiple of 8. Add rows in proportion for the length.

**Making a medium sweater into an XL.** The medium has a 40-inch chest, which is 160 stitches at 4 stitches per inch. You need 48 inches: 48 x 4 = 192 stitches. That is 32 more stitches total. Add 16 stitches to the front and 16 to the back. You will also need to widen the shoulders and deepen the armholes by about an inch. **Switching yarn weight on a scarf pattern.** The pattern uses Bulky (5) weight at 3 stitches per inch for a 7-inch wide scarf, which is 21 stitches. You want to use DK (3) weight at 5.5 stitches per inch. For the same 7-inch width: 7 x 5.5 = 38.5, so round to 39 stitches. The scarf will be thinner with more drape, which may be exactly what you want.

**Resizing a crochet amigurumi bear.** The pattern uses Worsted (4) weight yarn and a 3.5mm hook to make a 10-inch bear. You want a 6-inch version. Switch to Sport (2) weight yarn and a 2.75mm hook. Follow the exact same stitch counts. The smaller gauge will make a proportionally smaller finished toy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I just use bigger needles to make a pattern larger?

Going up a needle size will loosen your gauge and make the fabric slightly larger. But it also changes how the fabric drapes and how dense it is. A better approach is to calculate the right stitch count for your target size at your preferred gauge. That way the fabric quality stays close to what the designer intended.

### How do I resize a pattern that uses multiple stitch patterns?

Swatch each stitch pattern separately and measure the gauge of each one. Ribbing, cables, and colorwork all have different gauges. Calculate the stitch count for each section on its own, based on its gauge. Then make sure the transitions between sections work out. The [increase/decrease calculator](https://fibertools.app/increase-decrease-calculator) helps you space any needed adjustments between sections evenly.

### What if my row gauge does not match even though my stitch gauge is correct?

This is very common and trips up a lot of knitters. If your stitch gauge matches but your row gauge is off, adjust the number of rows you work instead of the stitches. Work to measurements rather than row counts. Knit until the piece measures 15 inches instead of working exactly 90 rows. Most well-written patterns give measurements alongside row counts for this reason.

### Is it easier to resize knitting or crochet patterns?

Crochet is generally easier to resize. Crochet stitches are taller, so fewer rows are needed. That means less shaping math. It is also easier to try on a crochet project as you go and adjust in real time. Knitting patterns with complex cables, lace, or colorwork are harder to resize because those stitch patterns have fixed repeats that limit your stitch counts.
