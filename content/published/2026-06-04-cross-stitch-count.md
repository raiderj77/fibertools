# Cross Stitch Count: What It Is and How to Use It in Your Fiber Work

**Cross stitch count tells you how many stitches fit into one inch of fabric. A 14-count Aida cloth holds 14 stitches per inch. A 28-count evenweave holds 28 threads per inch but is usually stitched over two threads, so it also gives you 14 stitches per inch. Count decides your finished piece size before you make a single stitch.**

---

## What does "count" actually mean in cross stitch?

Count refers to the [number of stitches or threads per inch in your fabric](/blog/cross-stitch-fabric-count/). A higher count means smaller stitches and finer detail. A lower count means bigger stitches, faster work, and easier visibility for beginners or anyone stitching without magnification.

The most common fabric is [Aida cloth](https://www.dmc.com/us/craft-basics/cross-stitch/what-is-aida-cloth). It is a woven cotton with visible square holes. Those holes make it easy to place each cross stitch in the right spot. Aida comes in counts like 11, 14, 16, 18, and 28. A 14-count Aida is the most widely used. Most patterns sold in stores are made with 14-count in mind.

Evenweave fabrics like linen and Jobelan are different. A 28-count linen has 28 threads per inch. But you stitch over two threads at a time. That gives you the same 14 stitches per inch as 14-count Aida. A 32-count linen stitched over two threads gives you 16 stitches per inch. This math matters because it changes your finished size.

---

## How do I [calculate the finished size of a cross stitch project](/blog/cross-stitch-size/)?

Divide the stitch count of your design by the fabric count. Then add margin. That is the whole formula.

If your pattern is [140 stitches wide and 196 stitches tall](/blog/cross-stitch-size-chart/), and you are using 14-count Aida:

- 140 divided by 14 = 10 inches wide
- 196 divided by 14 = 14 inches tall

Add at least 3 inches of unstitched fabric on each side for framing or finishing. That means you need a piece of Aida at least 16 inches wide and 20 inches tall to work comfortably.

Change the fabric count and the numbers shift. Put that same 140 x 196 stitch design on 18-count Aida:

- 140 divided by 18 = roughly 7.8 inches wide
- 196 divided by 18 = roughly 10.9 inches tall

Same design, noticeably smaller finished piece. This is how you resize a pattern without redrawing a single square on the chart.

## Does thread strand count change anything?

Yes, and it works directly with fabric count. The number of strands you use affects coverage and texture. It does not change the stitch count itself. But the two need to match, or your work will not look right.

[DMC's general guidelines](https://www.dmc.com/us/craft-basics/cross-stitch/how-many-strands-of-thread-to-use) suggest 2 strands of embroidery floss for 14-count Aida. They suggest 1 strand for 18-count or higher, and 3 strands for 11-count. These are starting points, not strict rules. Some stitchers prefer 3 strands on 14-count for dense, textured coverage. Some use 1 strand on 14-count for a delicate, painterly look.

Here is a simple test. Hold your threaded needle up to a fabric hole. The thread should pass through without forcing. It should not rattle around loosely either. If it forces, use one fewer strand. If it flops, add one more.

## Can I use a cross stitch count chart for knitting or crochet planning?

Not directly, but the idea transfers. Cross stitch count is basically a grid-based gauge system. Knitters use the same logic when working colorwork charts.

A stranded colorwork chart is drawn on a grid where each square equals one knit stitch. If your gauge is 22 stitches and 28 rows per 4 inches, that is 5.5 stitches per inch and 7 rows per inch. If your chart is 44 stitches wide, your motif will be 8 inches wide. That is the same division you use in cross stitch count.

The difference is that knit stitches are not square. Most knit gauges are wider than they are tall per stitch. So a chart that looks like a circle on graph paper will knit up as a slightly squashed oval. Cross stitch on Aida stitches up square because the fabric holes are square. [This is why knitters sometimes use knitter's graph paper](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/knitting-gauge-standard). It adjusts the grid to match typical stitch gauge ratios.

The same idea applies to crochet. If you are working a single crochet colorwork grid and your gauge is 16 single crochet and 18 rows per 4 inches, each stitch is not square either. A chart made for square stitches will look distorted. Measure as you go and compare your actual swatch to the chart grid before starting a large piece.

## What fabric count should a beginner start with?

Start with 14-count Aida and 2 strands of floss. The holes are large enough to see without squinting. Nearly every beginner pattern is charted for it. The fabric is widely available. You can find it at any craft store or from online suppliers like [Wichelt Imports](https://www.wichelt.com) or through major retailers. Finish a few small projects on 14-count before moving to finer counts where the margin for error shrinks fast.

Once you finish two or three small pieces on 14-count, you will know how the fabric behaves. You will know how tight your tension runs. You will know if you want to move up to 18-count for finer work or down to 11-count for faster, chunkier projects. There is no shortcut for that knowledge. You build it by stitching.

## Quick reference: common counts and their uses

Fabric count tells you how many stitches fit in one inch. Higher numbers mean smaller, finer work. Eleven-count suits beginners and bold designs. Fourteen-count covers most commercial patterns. Eighteen-count or 28-count evenweave is where detail work lives. Match your count to the design scale, not just to your ambition.

| Fabric Count | Stitches Per Inch | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| 11-count Aida | 11 | Beginners, chunky designs, kids projects |
| 14-count Aida | 14 | General use, most commercial patterns |
| 16-count Aida | 16 | Medium detail, slightly smaller finish |
| 18-count Aida | 18 | Fine detail, small finished pieces |
| 28-count evenweave (over 2) | 14 | Same as 14-count Aida, softer drape |
| 32-count linen (over 2) | 16 | Heirloom work, fine detail |

Check your gauge on any new fabric before cutting your full piece to size. A small test stitch in the corner costs you nothing. Cutting a piece too small costs you the whole project.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is cross stitch count and why does it matter?
Cross stitch count is the number of stitches per inch on your fabric. It is often called "count" or "Aida count." It decides how large or small your finished design will be. A higher count, like 28-count evenweave, makes finer and smaller stitches. A lower count, like 14-count Aida, makes larger and more visible stitches. Picking the right count helps your finished piece match the size and detail level shown in your pattern.

### How do I calculate the finished size of a cross stitch project?
Divide the total number of stitches in your pattern by the fabric count. That gives you your finished size in inches. For example, a pattern that is 140 stitches wide on 14-count Aida equals 10 inches wide. Always add at least 3 to 4 inches of extra fabric on each side for framing or finishing. A cross stitch count calculator tool can do this math for you quickly.

### What is the most popular cross stitch fabric count for beginners?
14-count Aida is the most recommended choice for beginners. The holes are large enough to see clearly. Threading a needle through them is comfortable and easy. The fabric is forgiving and widely available in craft stores. It comes in many colors. Most beginner patterns are also made for 14-count, so finding a matching project is simple.

### Can I use a different fabric count than what my pattern recommends?
Yes, you can use a different count. But your finished size will change. Switching to a higher count makes a smaller design. A lower count makes a larger one. Use a cross stitch count calculator to find the new finished size before you cut your fabric. The design stays the same. Only the physical size and thread coverage change based on your chosen count.

### What is the difference between single and double cross stitch count on evenweave fabric?
On evenweave fabric, you can stitch over one thread or two threads. This changes your working count. Stitching over two threads on 28-count evenweave gives results similar to 14-count Aida. That gives you more fabric choices for the same pattern. Stitching over one thread on 28-count creates a much finer and more detailed result. Knowing this helps you pick the right fabric and get the finished size and look you want.
