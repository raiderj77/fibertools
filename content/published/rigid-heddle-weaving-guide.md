# Beginner's Guide to Rigid Heddle Weaving

There is something very satisfying about pulling a shuttle through a shed and watching fabric form in front of you. If you are curious about weaving but scared of big floor looms, a rigid heddle loom is the perfect place to start. It is portable and affordable, costing $100 to $300 for a good loom. You can weave your first scarf in an afternoon.

This guide walks you through everything from threading your first warp to cutting your finished fabric off the loom.

## What Is a Rigid Heddle Loom?

A rigid heddle loom is the simplest type of frame loom that creates a true shed. The shed is the opening between warp threads. You pass the weft yarn through that opening. The "rigid heddle" is a flat frame with slots and holes that alternate. When you raise the heddle, half the warp threads go up. When you lower it, they go down. That creates your shed. That is all you need for plain weave.

A floor loom has 4 to 8 shafts. A rigid heddle loom has just one heddle doing all the work. You will not weave complex twills or overshot patterns. But you can weave beautiful plain weave, stripes, color blocks, lace effects, and pick-up stick patterns.

Rigid heddle looms come in widths from 10 inches to 32 inches. A 15 to 20 inch loom is best for beginners. It is wide enough for scarves and dish towels. It is also narrow enough to manage easily.

## How Do You Set Up Your First Project?

### Choose Your Yarn

For a first project, use a smooth, medium-weight yarn. Pick one that will not snag or stick. Worsted weight (CYC 4) cotton or wool works well. Avoid fuzzy yarns like mohair. Avoid splitty singles or textured novelty yarn. These make threading frustrating and they stick in the heddle.

**Good first-project yarns:**
- Worsted weight mercerized cotton, smooth, strong, easy to see
- DK weight wool, slight grip helps keep selvedges neat
- Sport weight cotton/linen blend, makes a gorgeous dish towel

### Calculate Your Sett

Sett is the number of warp threads per inch. This is also called EPI, which stands for ends per inch. The right sett depends on your yarn weight and weave structure. For plain weave on a rigid heddle:

| Yarn Weight | Recommended Sett (EPI) | Common Heddle Size |
|---|---|---|
| Bulky (5) | 5 to 6 EPI | 5-dent |
| Worsted (4) | 8 to 10 EPI | 8 or 10-dent |
| DK (3) | 10 to 12 EPI | 10 or 12-dent |
| Sport (2) | 12 to 15 EPI | 12 or 15-dent |
| Fingering (1) | 15 to 18 EPI | 15-dent |

### Calculate Warp Length

Your warp length equals your finished length plus loom waste plus a shrinkage allowance.

**Loom waste** is the yarn tied to the front and back beams that you cannot weave through. On a rigid heddle loom, this is usually 18 to 24 inches total. **Shrinkage** depends on the fiber. Cotton shrinks about 5 to 10 percent. Wool shrinks 10 to 15 percent after wet finishing.

Here is an example for a 60-inch scarf. Take 60 inches plus 22 inches of loom waste plus 8 inches for shrinkage. That equals 90 inches of warp, which is 2.5 yards.

### Warp the Loom

There are two main ways to warp a rigid heddle loom. The first is direct warping, which uses a warping peg clamped to a table. The second is indirect warping, which uses a warping board. Direct warping is simpler for beginners.

1. **Clamp the warping peg** to a table at the right distance from your loom. That distance should equal your total warp length.
2. **Thread the first end through a slot** in the heddle. Tie it to the back apron rod. Carry the yarn to the warping peg and back. Each round trip makes 2 warp ends.
3. **Continue until you have enough ends** for your desired width. For a 10-inch-wide scarf at 8 EPI, multiply 10 by 8 to get 80 warp ends.
4. **Wind the warp onto the back beam.** Place paper or stiff cardboard between layers to keep the tension even.
5. **Thread the heddle** by moving every other thread from a slot into the hole next to it. Now half your threads are in slots and half are in holes.
6. **Tie onto the front apron rod** in small groups of 4 to 6 ends. Adjust the tension until all groups feel equal.

### Weave

1. **Raise the heddle** to create the first shed. Pass the shuttle through from right to left.
2. **Beat** the weft into place by pulling the heddle firmly toward you.
3. **Lower the heddle** to create the second shed. Pass the shuttle back from left to right.
4. **Beat** again. That is two rows of plain weave, which is one complete cycle.

Keep your selvedges even by angling the weft at about 30 degrees in the shed before you beat. This gives the weft enough slack to travel over and under the warp threads. It keeps the edges from pulling in.

## What Are the Best Tips for Beginners?

**Tension is everything.** Uneven tension causes wavy, bumpy fabric. Wind your warp onto the back beam slowly. Insert paper or cardboard every 6 to 8 inches to separate the layers. Before you start weaving, press down on each warp end with a finger. They should all bend down the same amount.

**Beat consistently.** The number one beginner mistake is beating unevenly. This creates stripes of dense and loose weaving. Beat with the same force every time. Pull the heddle toward you in one smooth motion. Do not slam it.

**Check your width every few inches.** Use a ruler to measure your weaving width. If it is getting narrower, you are pulling your weft too tight. If it is getting wider, you are leaving too much slack. Try to keep your original width within 1/4 inch.

**Weave a header first.** Before you start your project yarn, weave 1 inch of scrap yarn in a different color. This spreads the warp to the correct spacing. It also gives you a base to beat against. Remove it after you cut the project off the loom.

**Do not fight tangles. Prevent them.** If your yarn tangles on the shuttle, wind less yarn onto it. A half-full shuttle passes through the shed more cleanly than an overstuffed one.

## What Can You Make on a Rigid Heddle Loom?

**First scarf (2 to 4 hours).** Warp 80 ends of worsted cotton at 8 EPI, 10 inches wide, 72 inches long plus waste. Weave plain weave in a contrasting weft color. Finish with twisted fringe. Total yarn is about 350 yards for the warp and 320 yards for the weft.

**Cotton dish towels (3 to 5 hours each).** Warp 120 ends of DK cotton at 12 EPI, 10 inches wide, 30 inches long. Hemstitch both ends. Wash in hot water for a soft, absorbent kitchen towel. Total yarn is about 200 yards for the warp and 180 yards for the weft per towel.

**Table runner (4 to 6 hours).** Warp 130 ends of sport weight linen at 13 EPI, 10 inches wide, 50 inches long. Use pick-up stick patterns for decorative bands. Total yarn is about 350 yards for the warp and 300 yards for the weft.

## How Do You Finish Your Woven Fabric?

Cutting your weaving off the loom is exciting and a little nerve-wracking. Here is how to do it right.

1. **Secure the ends.** Before cutting, tie groups of 4 to 6 warp threads in overhand knots right against the last row of weaving. Do this on both ends. If you skip this step, your weft will unravel.
2. **Cut the warp.** Cut the warp threads from the loom at both the front and back.
3. **Wet finish.** Soak the fabric in lukewarm water for 20 to 30 minutes. For wool, agitate gently for a lightly fulled fabric. For cotton, a gentle machine wash on the delicate cycle works well. Wet finishing relaxes the fibers, evens out the weave, and gives the fabric its final feel.
4. **Dry flat.** Lay the piece on a towel. Roll it up to remove extra water, then dry it flat. Press with a steam iron if you like.
5. **Trim fringe.** Even up your fringe with sharp scissors. Twisted fringe or braided fringe gives a nice finished look.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does a rigid heddle loom cost?

A good rigid heddle loom costs $100 to $300 depending on the brand and width. A 15-inch Schacht Cricket or Ashford SampleIt runs about $150 to $180. A 20-inch loom runs $200 to $260. You will also need a shuttle ($8 to $15), a warping peg ($15 to $25), and extra heddles ($30 to $50 for additional sizes).

### What size rigid heddle loom should a beginner buy?

A 15 to 20 inch loom is best for beginners. It handles scarves, dish towels, and table runners without being too hard to warp. Smaller looms (10 inch) are too narrow for most projects. Larger looms (24 to 32 inch) are wonderful but harder to manage for a first-timer.

### Can you weave patterns other than plain weave on a rigid heddle?

Yes. A pick-up stick lets you create simple patterns like lace, basket weave, and textured stripes. A second heddle, sold separately, opens up twill and more complex structures. But plain weave alone makes beautiful fabric. Do not feel pressured to add complexity early on.

### How long does it take to warp a rigid heddle loom?

Warping takes 30 to 60 minutes for beginners. That is about as long as the actual weaving for a short project. It gets faster with practice. Experienced weavers can warp a scarf in 15 to 20 minutes. The key is to not rush. A well-warped loom weaves smoothly. A sloppy warp causes problems for the entire project.
