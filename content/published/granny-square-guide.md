# How to Plan a Granny Square Blanket: Layout, Yardage and Joining Guide

You crocheted a dozen granny squares. They look great stacked on the coffee table. Now what? Joining them into a blanket takes more planning than most people think. How many squares do you need? How much yarn per color? Which joining method will not make you want to quit halfway through?

A granny square blanket is one of the most fun crochet projects. But it falls apart fast without a plan. Running out of yarn 80% of the way through is a real problem. Ending up with a blanket that is 4 inches too narrow is a real problem too. A little basic math prevents both.

> **True story:** I thought all four corners of my granny square would match. They did not. Even tension and careful counting fixed it. The [granny square planner](/granny-square-planner) helps.

## What Goes Into Planning a Granny Square Blanket

Three numbers drive the whole project. They are your finished square size, your target blanket size, and your gauge. Everything else flows from those three numbers. That includes total squares, yardage per color, and border width.

Common granny square sizes:

| Square Size | Best For | Typical Rounds |
|---|---|---|
| 6 inches | Baby blankets, quick projects | 4 to 5 rounds |
| 8 inches | Throw blankets, lap blankets | 5 to 7 rounds |
| 12 inches | Afghan blankets, bedspreads | 7 to 10 rounds |

The 6-inch square is the most popular size. A baby blanket (30" x 36") needs 30 of them in a 5 x 6 layout. A throw (50" x 60") takes 80 squares in an 8 x 10 grid. A queen-size blanket (90" x 100") needs about 225 squares. Yes, that is a big time commitment.

## Calculating Yardage Per Square

This is where most blanket projects go wrong. Guessing yardage by feel does not work when you are making 80 identical squares.

The most reliable method:

1. Crochet one complete square with your yarn and hook.
2. Weigh the finished square on a kitchen scale in grams.
3. Check the skein label for total grams and total yards.
4. Divide: (yarn yards per skein divided by grams per skein) multiplied by grams per square equals yards per square.

**Example:** Your skein has 220 yards and weighs 100g. Your 6-inch square weighs 14g. That is 2.2 yards per gram multiplied by 14g, which equals 30.8 yards per square. For 30 squares, you need 924 yards. That is about 5 skeins with a small buffer.

Typical yardage by square size (worsted weight, CYC 4):

| Square Size | Yards Per Square | 30 Squares | 80 Squares |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6 inch | 28 to 35 yds | 840 to 1,050 yds | 2,240 to 2,800 yds |
| 8 inch | 45 to 55 yds | 1,350 to 1,650 yds | 3,600 to 4,400 yds |
| 12 inch | 80 to 100 yds | 2,400 to 3,000 yds | 6,400 to 8,000 yds |

Always buy 10 to 15% extra. Dye lots matter. If you run out and cannot match the color, you will see it in the finished blanket.

## Joining Methods Compared

The join is what turns a pile of squares into a real blanket. Each method looks different, stretches differently, and takes a different amount of effort.

### Whip Stitch

This is the simplest join. Hold two squares together with wrong sides facing. Sew through the back loops with a tapestry needle.

- **Pros:** Fast, flat seam, uses very little extra yarn.
- **Cons:** Less stretch, leaves a visible seam line on the back.
- **Yarn needed:** About 1.5 times the length of the edge you are joining.
- **Best for:** Beginners, baby blankets, and projects where the back will not show.

### Single Crochet Join

Hold two squares together and single crochet through both layers. This creates a raised ridge on one side.

- **Pros:** Sturdy, even tension, and the ridge looks decorative.
- **Cons:** Uses more yarn and adds bulk.
- **Yarn needed:** About 2 times the edge length.
- **Best for:** Blankets that get heavy use, or joins in a contrasting color.

### Slip Stitch Join

This is similar to the single crochet join but uses slip stitches. It is flatter than the single crochet join but still crocheted.

- **Pros:** Flatter than a single crochet join and faster to work.
- **Cons:** Can be too tight if your tension is not loose.
- **Yarn needed:** About 1.5 times the edge length.
- **Best for:** When you want a crocheted join without extra bulk.

### Join-As-You-Go (JAYG)

You connect each square to the ones next to it during the last round of crocheting. There is no separate joining step.

- **Pros:** No seaming phase at all. Flexible and lies flat.
- **Cons:** You must plan your layout before you start. Mistakes are harder to fix.
- **Yarn needed:** Built into the last round. No extra yarn needed.
- **Best for:** Experienced crocheters and large blankets. It saves hours of seaming.

## Tips and Common Mistakes

**Block your squares before joining.** Even if they look fine, blocking them to the same size stops the seams from puckering. Pin each square to your target size on a foam mat. Mist with water and let it dry. This step takes time but saves a lot of frustration.

**Work in rows, not one square at a time.** Join squares into strips first. Then join the strips together. This is faster and helps you catch layout mistakes early.

**Watch your color placement.** If you are using a color pattern, lay all your squares on the floor or a bed before you join them. Take a photo. It is much easier to rearrange them now than to undo 40 joins later.

**Do not forget the border.** A 2-round single crochet border adds about 1 inch to each side. A 4-round border adds about 2 inches. Factor this into your target size. If you want a 50" x 60" finished blanket with a 2-inch border, your joined squares should measure 46" x 56".

**Watch your tension on joins.** Too tight and the blanket will not drape well. Too loose and the seams will gap. Practice your chosen join on two test squares before you start the whole blanket.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many granny squares do I need for a throw blanket?**
For a standard 50" x 60" throw using 6-inch squares, you need 80 squares in a 10 x 8 grid. With 8-inch squares, that drops to 42 squares in a 7 x 6 grid. Bigger squares mean fewer to crochet and fewer joins. But each one takes longer to finish. The [Granny Square Planner](/granny-square-planner) gives you the exact count for any size.

**Which joining method is best for beginners?**
Whip stitch is the easiest to learn and the hardest to mess up. You just need a tapestry needle and matching yarn. Hold two squares with wrong sides together. Sew through the matching stitches along the edge. It makes a flat, nearly invisible seam. You can join an entire baby blanket in one evening.

**How much extra yarn should I buy for joining and borders?**
Plan on 10 to 15% of your total square yardage for joining. Join-as-you-go is the exception. It uses no extra yarn. For borders, estimate 40 to 60 yards per round on a throw-size blanket. A 3-round border on a 50" x 60" throw needs about 150 yards. It is always better to have a leftover skein than to search for a matching dye lot.

**Can I mix different size granny squares in one blanket?**
You can, but it takes careful planning. The most common approach is pairing one 12-inch square with four 6-inch squares to fill the same space. All pieces must block to exact measurements. If they do not, the blanket will not lie flat. This works well for sampler blankets where each section has a different pattern.
