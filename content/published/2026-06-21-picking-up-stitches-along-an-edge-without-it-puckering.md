# Picking Up Stitches Along an Edge Without It Puckering

**The short answer:** Space your pick-up points evenly. Match your ratio to the stitch type. Use roughly 3 stitches for every 4 row ends on stockinette. Use 1 stitch for every 1 row end on garter. Go through both legs of the edge stitch, not just the outer loop. Do those three things and the puckering mostly solves itself before you even start knitting.

---

## Why does my picked-up edge pucker in the first place?

Puckering happens when you pick up too many or too few stitches for the length of the edge. Too many stitches and the new fabric ruffles outward. Too few stitches and it draws in and buckles.

The root cause is simple. Knit stitches and knit rows are not square. A standard stockinette stitch is wider than it is tall. If you pick up one stitch per row end, you end up with more stitches than the edge can hold. The [Craft Yarn Council's standard gauge guidelines](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) show this difference across yarn weights. It shows up in pick-up ratios too. Understanding this one fact fixes most puckering problems before you even touch the yarn.

---

## What ratio should I use when picking up stitches?

The standard starting ratio for stockinette is **3 stitches for every 4 row ends**. For garter stitch, pick up **1 stitch per garter ridge**. Each ridge equals 2 rows. So that works out to 1 stitch for every 2 rows.

These are starting points, not rules set in stone. Here is how to use them:

- Count your row ends along the edge. Say you have 60 row ends of stockinette.
- Multiply by 0.75. That gives you 45 stitches to pick up.
- Divide the edge into quarters. Pick up roughly 11 to 12 stitches per quarter. Working in sections keeps your spacing even.

For ribbing or seed stitch borders, the same 3 to 4 ratio usually works. But always knit a few inches and lay the work flat before you commit. If the border flares, rip back and drop 5 to 10% of your stitch count. If it pulls, add stitches in the same amount. Measure as you go instead of trusting any single number.

---

## Does it matter where exactly I insert the needle?

Yes, and this is where many knitters run into trouble. Always insert the needle **one full stitch in from the edge**. Do not go into the outermost half-stitch or the very tip of the selvedge.

That outer column of edge stitches is often uneven. Tension changes and yarn-overs at the turn can distort it. Picking up into it creates a wobbly, uneven line. Going one stitch in gives you a clean, steady column to work from. It also hides the selvedge inside the seam. [Interweave's technique library](https://www.interweave.com/) has shown this in many video breakdowns. It is the one change that makes the biggest visual difference.

Helpful tip: use a crochet hook one or two sizes smaller than your knitting needle to pull the loop through. The hook grabs the yarn cleanly without splitting it. You can also work faster this way. Then slide the live stitch onto your needle.

## How do I handle corners without getting a hole or a lump?

At an outside corner, pick up one extra stitch at the corner point. Then on the first working row, knit into the front and back of that stitch (kfb). This keeps the corner lying flat. At an inside corner, pick up one stitch right in the corner. On the next row, slip-slip-knit (ssk) that stitch together with its neighbor. This keeps a hole from opening up.

The corner stitch acts like a hinge point. Treating it differently from the rest of the edge is not a trick. It just recognizes that a corner has more work to do than a straight edge. [TECHknitter's detailed post on corners](http://techknitting.blogspot.com/2012/01/knitting-corners.html) explains the geometry clearly if you want the full explanation.

## What if I'm picking up along a bound-off edge versus a row-end edge?

These two situations follow different rules. Along a bound-off edge, like a shoulder or a neckline, you pick up **1 stitch for every bound-off stitch**. Almost always. The stitches sit in a neat row and the ratio is basically 1 to 1.

Along a row-end edge, which runs vertically or diagonally, you use the ratios above. Patterns sometimes do not say which type of edge they mean. So you have to look at the piece and figure it out yourself.

A diagonal edge, like a raglan or a V-neck, is a mix of both. Work the bound-off sections at 1 to 1. Work the row-end sections at 3 to 4. Then count your total and adjust by a few stitches if needed to match the pattern's target number. Do not try to use one average ratio across the whole diagonal. Work each section on its own.

---

## Any quick checks before I knit the whole border?

Pick up all your stitches. Then hold the work up and let it hang freely. A puckered edge will show up right away, before you knit a single row of border. This is the time to fix it.

Count your stitches and compare to your target number. Spread them out again if any section looks crowded or thin. Then knit three or four rows of your border stitch and lay it flat again. If it still ruffles or pulls, you caught it early. Ripping back now costs you about ten minutes, not two hours.

This process sounds fussy. But once you have done it a few times, it only takes about five minutes of checking. That is a small price for a border that lies flat and looks like it belongs there.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I pick up stitches along an edge without puckering?
Pick up stitches at a steady ratio. Do not pull the yarn too tight. For vertical edges, a common ratio is 3 stitches for every 4 rows. Using a needle one size smaller than your main needle can also help keep tension even. Work slowly. Insert your needle under both legs of the edge stitch, not just one. This spreads the tension more evenly and keeps the fabric from bunching.

### What is the correct ratio for picking up stitches along a knitted edge?
The standard ratio is 3 stitches for every 4 row ends along a vertical edge. Along a horizontal bound-off edge, pick up one stitch for every stitch. These ratios stop extra fabric from ruffling or too few stitches from causing puckering. You may need to adjust your stitch count a little depending on your yarn weight and gauge. Always check that your picked-up edge lies flat before you keep going.

### Why does my picked-up edge pucker or pull inward?
Puckering usually happens because you picked up too few stitches or pulled the yarn too tight while picking up. When there are not enough stitches to match the row length, the edge draws inward. Try picking up a few more stitches than you think you need. Then work a row and see if it lies flat. Blocking the finished piece can also relax minor puckering caused by uneven tension.

### Should I use a smaller needle when picking up stitches along an edge?
Yes. Using a needle one size smaller than your working needle helps create a neater, flatter edge. The smaller needle is easier to insert into tight edge stitches. It also creates a firmer foundation row. Once you have picked up all the stitches, switch to your regular needle size for the body of your new section, such as a button band, collar, or sleeve.

### Does it matter where exactly I insert the needle when picking up edge stitches?
Yes, placement matters a lot. It helps you avoid puckering and gaps. Always insert your needle under both legs of the edge stitch, not just one strand. This anchors the stitch firmly and prevents holes. Along a vertical edge, pick up inside the chain edge stitch rather than at the very outermost loop. Keeping your placement the same across the whole edge creates even tension. It also gives you a smooth, neat join between the two sections of fabric.
