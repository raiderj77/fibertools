# How to Identify Mystery Yarn Using WPI

You have a bag of beautiful yarn with no label. Maybe you got it from a grandmother, found it at a thrift store, or lost the ball band after moving. The color is perfect and the fiber feels right. But you do not know the weight, and you need that to plan a project.

Wraps per inch (WPI) is the fastest and most reliable way to identify mystery yarn. All you need is a pencil, a ruler, and 60 seconds.

> **A mistake I made:** When my yarn was out of stock, I grabbed whatever looked close without checking the weight. It changed my whole gauge. Match the weight and fiber, and check the [yarn weight chart](/yarn-weight-chart) before you swap.

## What Is WPI and Why Does It Work?

WPI stands for wraps per inch. It is the number of times a strand of yarn wraps around a cylinder, like a pencil or dowel, within one inch. Thicker yarn makes fewer wraps. Thinner yarn makes more.

Every yarn weight falls within a set WPI range. A worsted weight yarn wraps 9 to 10 times per inch. A fingering weight wraps 14 to 20 times. By counting wraps, you can figure out the weight of any mystery yarn. No label needed.

This method works because yarn thickness is a physical property. It does not change over time, after washing, or when labels go missing. The yarn is what it is, and WPI reveals it.

## How Do You Measure WPI Step by Step?

Find a smooth, round cylinder. A pencil, pen, or 1/4-inch dowel all work well. Do not use a rough or textured surface. Rough wood grabs the yarn and throws off your count.

**Step 1: Wrap the yarn.** Hold one end of the yarn against the cylinder with your thumb. Wrap the yarn around the cylinder side by side. Do not overlap the wraps. Do not stretch the yarn or push the wraps together. Let each wrap sit naturally next to the last one.

**Step 2: Fill one inch.** Keep wrapping until you cover exactly one inch. Hold a ruler next to the wraps to measure the inch.

**Step 3: Count the wraps.** Count every wrap within that one inch. If the count lands between two numbers, like 11.5, write that down.

**Step 4: Repeat twice more.** Measure WPI three separate times and find the average. Yarn can vary in thickness, so three measurements give you a more accurate number.

**Step 5: Match to a weight category.** Compare your WPI count to the standard ranges in the chart below.

## What Other Tests Can You Do Beyond WPI?

WPI tells you the weight, but it will not tell you the fiber content. Here are three quick tests to help narrow that down.

**The burn test.** Cut a 2-inch piece and hold it with tweezers over a flame. Wool smells like burning hair, turns into a crumbly ash, and goes out on its own. Cotton smells like burning paper and leaves a fine gray ash. Acrylic melts into a hard bead and smells chemical. Blends act somewhere in between.

**The water test.** Drop a 4-inch piece into a glass of water. Wool and animal fibers resist getting wet and float for a while. Cotton and plant fibers sink within seconds. Superwash wool sinks faster than untreated wool.

**The break test.** Pull a single strand until it snaps. Wool stretches and springs back before it breaks. Cotton snaps with little stretch. Acrylic stretches but does not bounce back. Silk is very strong and hard to break.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Do not wrap too tightly. You will get a higher WPI and think the yarn is thinner than it really is.
- Do not wrap too loosely. Gaps between wraps will make the count too high.
- Do not use a flat surface like a ruler or credit card. The yarn needs to wrap around a cylinder to sit naturally.
- Do not split plied yarn to measure each ply. Measure the full strand the way you would knit or crochet with it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can WPI measurements vary for the same yarn?

Yes. WPI can shift by 1 to 2 wraps depending on how tightly you wrap and the texture of your cylinder. That is why measuring three times and averaging gives the most reliable result. Fuzzy yarns like mohair blends can measure about 1 to 2 WPI thinner than their actual weight. The halo fills the gaps between wraps.

### Does yarn ply affect WPI?

Ply structure changes the shape of the yarn. That affects how wraps sit next to each other. A tightly spun 4-ply yarn and a loosely spun singles can have different WPI counts even at the same weight. Always measure the full strand the way you plan to use it. Do not separate the plies.

### What if my WPI falls between two weight categories?

Yarn does not always fit neatly into one category. If you are at 13 WPI, you are between sport and DK. Swatch at the gauge for both weights and pick the fabric you like. A denser fabric at sport gauge works well for structured items. A looser fabric at DK gauge gives better drape.

### Is WPI accurate for novelty or textured yarn?

WPI is less reliable for boucle, eyelash, and heavily textured yarns. The bumps keep wraps from sitting flat. For these yarns, try wrapping loosely and measuring over 2 inches instead of 1. Then divide by 2. You will get a rough category, but swatching is the final answer.
