# How to Plan a Tapestry Crochet Project

Tapestry crochet makes detailed, multicolor designs. It works by carrying unused yarn inside the stitches. The result is a thick, sturdy fabric with sharp color patterns on both sides. You can see this style in Wayuu bags from Colombia, southwestern baskets, geometric pouches, and bold colorwork blankets.

Intarsia crochet gives each color section its own bobbin. Tapestry crochet is different. It carries all colors across every row and crochets over the unused strands. The basic technique is simple. Tension control and chart reading take practice.

## What Is Tapestry Crochet?

Tapestry crochet uses single crochet, or sometimes extended single crochet. The non-working color is carried inside the stitches the whole time. You never cut the yarn between color changes. Both colors travel across the entire row. Only one color shows at a time.

**How it works:**
1. Work single crochet with Color A.
2. When the chart says to switch to Color B, drop Color A and pick up Color B. Keep working single crochet with Color B.
3. Color A is now the carried yarn. It lies flat inside the single crochet stitches, hidden from view.
4. Switch back to Color A when the chart says to. Now Color B becomes the carried yarn.

The carried yarn makes the fabric double thick. This is great for bags and baskets because it adds structure. It does make blankets heavier than regular crochet, though.

## How Do You Read a Tapestry Crochet Chart?

Tapestry crochet charts look like regular grid charts. Each cell equals one single crochet stitch. The color of the cell shows which yarn should be visible for that stitch.

**For flat tapestry crochet:** Read right to left on odd rows. Read left to right on even rows. Always follow your pattern's instructions.

**For circular tapestry crochet (bags, baskets):** Read every row right to left. You always work in the same direction. Place a marker at the start of the round.

**Stitch multiples:** Tapestry patterns repeat. Say a bag has 100 stitches around and a 10-stitch repeat. That gives you 10 repeats. Your total stitch count must be a multiple of the pattern repeat.

**Chart distortion:** Single crochet stitches are not perfectly square. They are wider than they are tall. The ratio is roughly 1 to 0.85. Your finished design will be a little shorter and wider than the chart looks. Some charts account for this. Most do not.

## How Do You Start a Tapestry Crochet Project?

### Step 1: Choose Your Project

**Best starter projects:**
- Small pouch or coin purse (40 to 60 stitches around, 20 to 30 rounds)
- Coaster set (worked flat, 20x20 stitches)
- Flat potholder (simple geometric patterns)

**Intermediate projects:**
- Wayuu-style bag (100 to 150 stitches around)
- Basket (50 to 80 stitches around)
- Pillow cover

### Step 2: Select Your Yarn

Use the same yarn weight and fiber for all colors. Cotton and cotton blends are the most popular choice. They make a firm, structured fabric. Acrylic works too but is a little softer.

**Avoid:** Fuzzy yarns (the carried color shows through), splitty yarns (hard to crochet over the carried yarn), and stretchy yarns (they stretch unevenly).

Worsted weight (CYC 4) or DK weight (CYC 3) mercerized cotton works best. You can expect about 4 to 5 single crochets per inch with the right hook.

### Step 3: Calculate Yarn

Both colors travel across every row. So each color uses more yarn than the stitch count alone suggests. The carried color adds roughly 30 to 40% to the per-color yardage. It lies inside every stitch even when it is hidden.

**Rule of thumb:** Calculate total yardage as if the whole project used one color. Then give 60% to the dominant color and 50% to the secondary color. Yes, that adds up to more than 100%. The extra amount covers the carrying overhead.

The [Yarn Calculator](/yarn-calculator) gives you total project yardage. Use the 60/50 split for a 2-color project. For a 3-color project, use roughly equal shares plus 30% each.

### Step 4: Work a Practice Swatch

Tapestry crochet gauge is different from regular single crochet gauge. The carried yarn stiffens the fabric. Swatch at least 4 inches using the tapestry technique with color changes. Do this before you figure out your final stitch count.

## What Are the Best Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Keep your tension on the carried yarn steady.** If you carry the unused yarn too loosely, it pokes through the stitches on the right side. If you carry it too tightly, the fabric puckers and gets narrower. Practice until the carried yarn lies flat inside the stitch without showing.

**Switch colors cleanly.** On the last pull-through before a color change, use the new color for the final yarn over. This puts the new color at the top of the stitch where it shows. Switching one stitch too late makes the color edge look jagged.

**Use a smaller hook than normal.** Tight stitches hide the carried yarn better. If you normally use an I/9 (5.5mm) for worsted single crochet, try an H/8 (5.0mm) or G/6 (4.0mm) for tapestry. The tighter gauge hides the carried color.

**Avoid long floats of one color.** If one color goes unused for 5 or more stitches, the carried yarn forms a long, loose strand inside the fabric. Catch the carried yarn every 3 to 4 stitches. Crochet over it even when it is not the working color. This anchors it without changing the visible color.

**Common mistakes:**
- Carried yarn showing through on the right side (hook is too large or tension is too loose)
- Fabric getting narrower with each row (carried yarn is too tight)
- Forgetting to carry the unused color (leaves a gap when you need that color next)
- Not anchoring long floats (loose strands catch on fingers and snag)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between tapestry crochet and mosaic crochet?

Tapestry crochet carries all colors across every row. This makes a double-thick fabric. Both colors are always ready to use. Mosaic crochet works one color per row. It uses skipped stitches to show the color underneath. Mosaic fabric is lighter and easier to keep even tension on. But it is limited to geometric patterns. Tapestry crochet can create any image or design.

### Does tapestry crochet use more yarn than regular crochet?

Yes, about 30 to 40% more total yarn. The carried color adds length inside every stitch. A regular single crochet bag using 300 yards would need 400 to 420 yards in tapestry crochet across all colors. Plan for this and buy extra. The double-thick fabric also makes the finished item heavier.

### Can I do tapestry crochet with more than 2 colors?

Yes, but each extra color makes the fabric thicker and tension harder to control. Two colors is standard. Three colors is common for complex designs. Four or more colors is possible, but the fabric gets very thick. You also have to carry 3 or more unused strands at once. Beginners should get comfortable with 2 colors before trying a third.

### Why does the carried yarn show through my stitches?

Your hook is probably too large or your tension is too loose. Try going down 1 to 2 hook sizes and keep your stitches tight. High-contrast color pairs also show through more. For example, white carried inside black stitches is more noticeable than low-contrast pairs. Some show-through is hard to avoid when a very light color is carried inside very dark stitches. Using a slightly thinner yarn for the lighter color can help.
