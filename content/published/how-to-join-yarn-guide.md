# How to Join Yarn Without Knots in Knitting and Crochet

You are halfway through a beautiful blanket when your skein runs out. You tie a quick knot, keep going, and three rows later you see it. A hard little lump pokes through the fabric like a pebble in your shoe. This happens to everyone, and it is one of the most frustrating moments in any project.

The good news? You never have to tie another ugly knot again. There are at least five reliable ways to join yarn. Each one creates a smooth, invisible connection in your finished fabric. Whether you are adding a new skein of the same color or switching to something bold and bright, the right join makes a big difference.

> **A mistake I made:** I tied knots everywhere because I wanted my ends secure, and I weaved them in like I was hiding evidence. Loose ends still found a way out. Weave ends back through several stitches instead of knotting. Woven ends stay.

## What Is a Yarn Join and When Do You Need One?

A yarn join connects two yarn ends so you can keep working. You might connect a new skein of the same yarn or a different color. You will need a join every time you run out of yarn. You will also need one when you start a new color for stripes or colorwork. You may also need one if you find a knot or defect in your skein and cut it out.

A good join should be strong, invisible, and add as little bulk as possible. Different methods work better for different yarn types. Knowing several techniques lets you pick the right one every time.

Planning your yarn needs before you start helps cut down on the number of joins you will need. That way you can buy enough and avoid running out in the middle of a row.

## Five Proven Methods for Joining Yarn

Here are the most reliable yarn joining methods. Each one comes with step-by-step instructions.

### 1. Russian Join (Best for Plied Yarns)

The Russian join weaves each yarn tail back through its own plies. This creates a doubled section that is strong and completely knot-free. It works well with plied yarns in any fiber, including wool, cotton, acrylic, and blends.

1. Thread the tail of your old yarn onto a tapestry needle.
2. Weave the needle back through the plies of the old yarn for about 2 inches. This creates a small loop at the end.
3. Thread the new yarn through that loop.
4. Now thread the new yarn's tail onto the needle. Weave it back through its own plies for 2 inches. This encloses the old yarn's loop.
5. Gently pull both ends to tighten. Trim any short tails flush.

The join creates a slightly thicker section for about 2 inches. It disappears into the fabric once you work a few stitches over it. Experienced knitters report this join almost never comes undone.

### 2. Magic Knot (Best for Smooth Yarns)

The magic knot is tiny, strong, and works on almost every fiber type. This includes acrylic, cotton, and wool. Despite the name, it does involve a knot. But the knot is so small it is nearly invisible.

1. Lay both yarn ends side by side, overlapping by about 6 inches. Point them in opposite directions.
2. Take the old yarn and tie a simple overhand knot around the new yarn. Do not tighten yet.
3. Take the new yarn and tie a simple overhand knot around the old yarn. Do not tighten yet.
4. Now pull both knots toward each other. Tug the working ends, not the tails. The knots will slide together and lock.
5. Trim the tails close to the knot. About 1/4 inch is safe.

The magic knot holds very well. But some crafters prefer not to use it for garments that will be machine washed often. Rough washing can sometimes work the knot loose over time.

### 3. Spit Splice / Felt Join (Best for Wool and Animal Fibers)

The spit splice uses moisture and friction to felt two yarn ends together. This creates a truly seamless join with zero bulk. It only works with animal fibers that felt, such as wool, alpaca, and mohair. It will not work with superwash wool, acrylic, cotton, or silk.

1. Unravel the plies of both yarn ends for about 3 inches.
2. Thin out each end by breaking or cutting away half the plies. This keeps you from doubling the thickness at the join.
3. Overlap the thinned ends by about 2 inches.
4. Wet the overlapping section with water or saliva. The enzymes in saliva help the felting process.
5. Place the wet section between your palms and rub hard for 10 to 20 seconds. The friction and moisture will felt the fibers together.
6. Tug firmly to test the join. A good spit splice is surprisingly strong.

This is the best method for wool projects. The join is invisible and adds no bulk at all.

### 4. Overlap Method (Best for Beginners)

The overlap method is the simplest join. It works with any yarn. It is perfect for beginners or for projects where a slightly thicker section will not matter, like blankets and scarves.

1. When you have about 6 inches of old yarn left, start working stitches with both the old and new yarn held together.
2. Work 4 to 6 stitches with the doubled yarn.
3. Drop the old yarn tail and continue with the new yarn only.
4. Weave in both tails later on the wrong side.

The trade-off is a slightly thicker section of fabric. But on textured stitch patterns it is nearly invisible. This method also works well for joining yarn in the middle of a row when you cannot easily reach an edge.

### 5. Joining at the Edge (Best for Garments)

For garments worked flat, joining at the beginning of a row keeps all your ends at the seam line. They will be hidden there.

1. Work to the end of the row with your old yarn.
2. Drop the old yarn and pick up the new yarn. Leave a 6-inch tail.
3. Work the first stitch of the new row with the new yarn. Pull snugly.
4. Weave in both tails along the seam edge later.

Experienced knitters prefer this method for sweater pieces, scarves, and any project with seams. The joins are completely hidden once the garment is put together.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

**Match your method to your fiber.** The spit splice is great for wool but will not work for acrylic. The Russian join works on almost everything but adds a little bulk. Know your yarn before you choose a method.

**Do not join in the middle of a visible section.** For garments, try to time your joins so they fall at the beginning of a row, near a seam, or in a spot that is hard to see. For blankets, avoid joins in the center of a row where they will stand out.

**Leave long enough tails.** Most join failures happen because the tails were trimmed too short. Leave at least 6 inches for weaving in. Leave at least 2 inches of overlap for the Russian join.

**Test your join before continuing.** Give it a firm tug. If it pulls apart now, it will pull apart after washing too. It is better to redo the join than to find a hole in your finished project.

**Avoid regular square knots.** A standard knot creates a hard lump. It can come loose in the wash and is visible on both sides of the fabric. Every method above is better than a plain knot.

**Joining new colors in crochet.** When changing colors, work the last stitch of the old color until the final yarn over. Then complete that yarn over with the new color. This keeps the color change clean and on the correct side of the fabric.

## Real Project Examples

**Striped baby blanket in acrylic yarn.** You are changing colors every 4 rows. Use the magic knot for each color change. It is fast, works great with acrylic, and with this many joins you will be glad you do not have dozens of tails to weave in.

**Wool cardigan in Worsted (4) weight.** You need three skeins for the body. Use the spit splice where same-color skeins meet. Use the edge join for the best invisibility. Place joins at side seam edges so they are hidden in the seaming.

**Crochet granny square blanket with many color changes.** Join new colors on the last yarn over of the final stitch before the color change. For joining squares together, use the overlap method so tails get woven into the seam.

**Knit scarf in cotton DK (3) weight.** Cotton does not felt, so skip the spit splice. The Russian join is your best choice here. It creates a strong connection in cotton without adding a hard knot.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Which yarn join is the strongest?

The spit splice is the strongest join for animal fibers. The felted connection becomes one continuous piece of yarn. For fibers that will not felt, like acrylic or cotton, the Russian join is the most secure option. The yarn is woven back through its own plies and will not slip or loosen over time, even after repeated washing.

### Can I use the magic knot with bulky yarn?

The magic knot works best with fingering through worsted weight yarns. With Bulky (5) or Super Bulky (6) weight yarns, the knot becomes large enough to feel through the fabric. For heavier yarns, the Russian join or the overlap method gives a much less noticeable result in your finished project.

### How do I join yarn in the round without a gap?

When joining a new skein in circular knitting or crochet, overlap the old and new yarns for 4 to 6 stitches using the overlap method. You can also use a Russian join placed a few stitches before the round marker. Avoid joining right at the beginning of the round. That can create a visible jog. Stagger your joins at different points around the circle.

### Do I always need to weave in ends after joining?

It depends on the method. The spit splice and Russian join leave no tails to weave in. That is their biggest advantage. The magic knot has tiny trimmed tails that usually do not need weaving. The overlap method and edge joins do leave tails. Those need to be woven in on the wrong side for at least 2 inches in each direction.
