# How to Knit and Crochet with Handspun Yarn

You spent hours at a wheel or spindle making yarn from raw fiber. Now you want to turn that handspun into a finished project. But handspun yarn does not act like store-bought yarn. It is thicker in some spots and thinner in others. The twist changes throughout. There is no ball band to tell you what needle size to use. And you have a fixed amount with no way to buy more.

Working with handspun is one of the most rewarding things a fiber artist can do. It is also very unforgiving of poor planning. Here is how to set yourself up for success.

## What Makes Handspun Different from Commercial Yarn?

**Inconsistent thickness.** Even skilled spinners make yarn that changes in diameter. Commercial yarn runs through machines that keep the thickness the same. Handspun has character. That means thick and thin spots that affect gauge.

**Variable twist.** Sections with too much twist feel wiry and make the fabric lean. Sections with too little twist feel soft but pill faster. This twist variation gives the fabric more texture and visual interest than store-bought yarn. But it makes matching gauge precisely much harder.

**Limited yardage.** You have what you spun. If you run out, you cannot buy another skein in the same dye lot. Your handspun is truly one of a kind. Every yard matters.

**Unknown weight category.** Handspun does not come with a CYC weight number. You need to figure out what it is before you plan a project.

## How Do You Characterize Your Handspun?

### Step 1: Measure WPI

Wrap your handspun around a pencil or dowel. Count the wraps per inch. Use the [WPI to Yarn Weight Converter](/wpi-calculator) to find the weight category. Measure in 3 to 5 different spots along the yarn and average the results. Thickness varies, so measuring in multiple spots gives you a better number.

Typical handspun WPI ranges:
- Bulky: 7 to 8 WPI
- Worsted: 9 to 10 WPI
- DK: 11 to 12 WPI
- Sport: 12 to 14 WPI
- Fingering: 14 to 20 WPI

### Step 2: Measure Yardage

Wrap your yarn around a niddy-noddy or something with a known size, like a chair back. Count the wraps. Multiply the number of wraps by the circumference. Or weigh the yarn. Use a small measured sample to find yards per gram, then multiply by the total weight.

Example: A 6-yard sample weighs 3 grams. Your full skein weighs 120 grams. Yardage: (6/3) x 120 = 240 yards.

### Step 3: Calculate Twist Direction

S-twist (counterclockwise) or Z-twist (clockwise) matters for some stitch patterns. Hold a strand straight up and down. If the twist angle matches the middle of the letter Z, going from upper right to lower left, it is Z-twist. If it matches the middle of the letter S, it is S-twist. Most spinning wheels make Z-twist singles.

### Step 4: Check Fiber Content

You know what you spun. But if someone gave you handspun or you bought it, do a burn test or feel test to confirm the fiber content. The [WPI Calculator](/wpi-calculator) also suggests project ideas based on the weight category it finds. That can help with planning.

## How Do You Plan a Project with Handspun?

### Choose Projects That Embrace Variation

Handspun's thick-and-thin character is a feature in the right projects. It is a flaw in the wrong ones.

**Best projects for handspun:**
- Scarves and cowls (forgiving about gauge changes)
- Shawls (especially garter stitch or lace, which hide thickness changes)
- Hats (small enough to finish with limited yardage)
- Blankets in garter stitch (the texture adds visual interest)
- Weaving projects (handspun makes gorgeous weft)

**Projects to avoid with inconsistent handspun:**
- Fitted garments (gauge changes create sizing problems)
- Socks (need consistent thickness for an even fit)
- Fair Isle colorwork (requires uniform weight for even tension)
- Anything requiring a precise stitch-per-inch match

### Size Your Project to Your Yarn

You have 240 yards. You cannot get more. Work backward from your yardage, not forward from "I want a blanket."

240 yards of worsted handspun could make:
- A hat (120 to 140 yards) with yarn left over for a headband
- A cowl (150 to 200 yards)
- A small scarf (200 to 280 yards, might be tight)

It cannot make a blanket. Match your project to your supply.

## What Are the Best Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Swatch generously.** Swatch at least 6 inches square. This averages out the thick-and-thin variation. A 2-inch swatch might only measure gauge from one thick spot. That gives you misleading numbers. Measure gauge across the full 6 inches for accuracy.

**Go up a needle or hook size.** Handspun's variable thickness needs room. A needle or hook that is slightly larger than what is recommended for the weight lets thick spots pass through without bunching up.

**Wash and block your swatch.** Handspun changes after the first wash. The twist settles. The fibers bloom. Some thick spots compress. Measure gauge after blocking for accurate sizing.

**Embrace the irregularity.** Do not try to fix handspun's character. Projects that show off the variation look intentional and beautiful. Think textured stitches, garter stitch, and simple patterns. Complex stitch patterns fight against the variation and make it look like a mistake.

**Weigh as you go.** Weigh your remaining yarn every few inches of progress. If you have used 60 grams after 80 rows and the yarn weighs 120 grams total, you have roughly 80 more rows. This keeps you from running out near the end.

**Common mistakes:**
- Choosing a fitted garment for inconsistent handspun (gauge will not be stable enough)
- Not measuring yardage before starting (running out mid-project with no solution)
- Using a needle size from a store-bought yarn's label without swatching
- Trying to match handspun gauge to a commercial pattern exactly (aim for close, not identical)
- Plying in the wrong direction and creating biased fabric (Z-twist singles should be S-plied)

## What Do Real Handspun Projects Look Like?

**The first handspun scarf.** A new spinner's first finished yarn measured 9 to 11 WPI, which put it between worsted and DK weight. She swatched on US 8 needles in garter stitch and got a soft, textured fabric. She had 195 yards total. She knit a scarf 6 inches wide and 48 inches long. The thick-and-thin variation gave it a rustic, artisanal texture. Project time: 8 hours.

**The handspun shawl.** An experienced spinner made 450 yards of consistent DK-weight merino, measuring 11 to 12 WPI throughout. She knit a triangular shawl with lace sections, starting on US 6 needles. The lace pattern opened up the fabric and showed off the yarn's hand-dyed colors. She weighed the remaining yarn every 10 rows to make sure she would not run out before the bind-off.

**The handspun woven scarf.** A spinner-weaver used 200 yards of handspun worsted as weft on a rigid heddle loom with a commercial cotton warp. The handspun's thickness variation created a lovely textured pattern. Using handspun as weft instead of warp is forgiving because weft does not need uniform tension.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my handspun is consistent enough for a garment?

Measure WPI in 10 spots along the yarn. If the range is 2 WPI or less, for example 9 to 11 WPI, the yarn is consistent enough for a sweater or hat. If the range is 3 or more WPI, stick with scarves, shawls, and projects where gauge changes will not affect fit. Plied yarn is more consistent than singles.

### Can I combine handspun with commercial yarn?

Yes. Use commercial yarn for structured parts like ribbing, cuffs, and waistbands. Use handspun for the body, where gauge changes are more forgiving. Match the weight categories and swatch both together. Stripes that alternate handspun and commercial yarn create an interesting visual contrast.

### How do I estimate yardage for handspun without a ball band?

Weigh your yarn in grams. Measure a 10-yard sample and weigh it. Divide the total weight by the sample weight, then multiply by 10. Example: total yarn = 100g, 10-yard sample = 4g. Yardage = (100/4) x 10 = 250 yards. The [WPI Calculator](/wpi-calculator) helps you find the weight category. Then you can look up typical yardage per project for that weight.

### Should I set the twist before knitting or crocheting?

Yes. Soak your finished yarn in warm water for 20 minutes. Gently squeeze out the extra water. Hang the yarn with a light weight and let it dry. This sets the twist and stops the fabric from biasing, or leaning to one side. Unset twist causes stockinette to skew and single crochet to lean.
