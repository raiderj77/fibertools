# How to Calculate Yarn for Accessories: Hats, Mittens, Cowls, and More

Accessories are some of the fastest and most fun fiber arts projects. A hat can take one evening. Mittens can take a weekend. A cowl fills a few quiet hours. But "small project" does not mean you should grab any random ball from your stash and hope for the best. Each accessory needs a certain amount of yarn. Running out 3 inches from the top of a hat is just as frustrating as running out on a sweater sleeve.

Here is the full yardage guide for every common accessory, plus the things that push your numbers up or down.

## How Much Yarn Do Hats Need?

Hats are the most common accessory project. How much yarn you need depends on three things: head size, yarn weight, and hat style.

**Basic beanie yardage by yarn weight and size:**

| Size | Fingering (1) | DK (3) | Worsted (4) | Bulky (5) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Newborn | 60 to 80 yds | 45 to 60 yds | 35 to 50 yds | 25 to 40 yds |
| Baby (6 to 12 mo) | 80 to 110 yds | 65 to 90 yds | 50 to 75 yds | 40 to 55 yds |
| Toddler (1 to 3 yr) | 110 to 140 yds | 85 to 110 yds | 70 to 90 yds | 50 to 70 yds |
| Child (4 to 10 yr) | 140 to 180 yds | 110 to 140 yds | 90 to 115 yds | 65 to 85 yds |
| Adult S/M | 175 to 220 yds | 140 to 175 yds | 110 to 140 yds | 80 to 110 yds |
| Adult L/XL | 200 to 260 yds | 160 to 210 yds | 130 to 165 yds | 100 to 130 yds |

**Style adjustments:**
- Slouchy beanie: add 30 to 40% (extra fabric in the crown)
- Beanie with folded brim: add 20 to 25% (the brim is double thick)
- Earflap hat: add 15 to 20% (extra fabric for the flaps and ties)
- Pom-pom: add 10 to 15 yards for a yarn pom-pom

## How Much Yarn Do Mittens and Gloves Need?

Mittens and gloves are worked in the round with thumb gusset shaping. Fingerless mitts skip the fingers but still need the thumb.

**Mitten yardage by size (worsted weight):**

| Size | Full Mittens (pair) | Fingerless Mitts (pair) |
|---|---|---|
| Toddler (1 to 3 yr) | 80 to 110 yds | 50 to 70 yds |
| Child (4 to 10 yr) | 110 to 150 yds | 75 to 100 yds |
| Adult S/M | 150 to 200 yds | 100 to 140 yds |
| Adult L/XL | 190 to 250 yds | 130 to 170 yds |

**For fingering weight:** multiply by 1.3 to 1.5. More stitches per inch means more yarn, but thinner fabric.

**For bulky weight:** multiply by 0.7 to 0.8. Fewer stitches give a chunkier result.

**Full-fingered gloves** use 20 to 30% more yarn than mittens. Each finger is worked separately with its own joins and decreases. Adult worsted weight gloves need 190 to 260 yards per pair.

## How Much Yarn Do Cowls and Scarves Need?

**Cowl yardage by style (worsted weight):**

| Cowl Type | Dimensions | Yardage |
|---|---|---|
| Snug single-loop | 22 to 24" circumference, 8" tall | 100 to 150 yds |
| Standard single-loop | 26 to 28" circ, 10" tall | 150 to 200 yds |
| Infinity scarf (double-loop) | 50 to 60" circ, 8" tall | 200 to 300 yds |
| Oversized infinity | 60 to 70" circ, 10 to 12" tall | 300 to 400 yds |

**Scarf yardage (worsted weight):**

| Scarf Type | Dimensions | Yardage |
|---|---|---|
| Narrow scarf | 5 to 6" wide, 60" long | 200 to 280 yds |
| Standard scarf | 7 to 8" wide, 60" long | 280 to 380 yds |
| Wide scarf | 10 to 12" wide, 72" long | 400 to 550 yds |
| Blanket scarf | 16 to 20" wide, 72" long | 600 to 850 yds |

For super bulky weight, multiply by 0.6 to 0.7. For fingering weight, multiply by 1.4 to 1.6.

## What Factors Change Accessory Yardage?

**Stitch pattern.** Cables use 15 to 30% more yarn than stockinette. The crossed stitches pull yarn from both sides. A cable-knit cowl in worsted weight might need 250 yards. A stockinette cowl the same size might only need 180 yards. Lace uses 10 to 15% less yarn because yarn overs create holes that use no yarn.

**Crochet vs. knitting.** Crochet accessories use 25 to 30% more yarn than knit ones. A knit worsted beanie that needs 120 yards will need 150 to 160 yards in crochet. Always apply the crochet multiplier when you adapt a knitting pattern.

**Colorwork.** Stranded colorwork, like Fair Isle, uses about 20% more yarn than solid-color knitting. Both colors are carried across each row. If your hat pattern uses 2-color stranded work, add 20% to the solid-color yardage estimate. Then split that between the two colors, often 60% main color and 40% contrast.

**Lining.** A lined hat uses roughly double the yarn of an unlined hat. A fleece-lined hat uses hat yardage plus the cost of fleece fabric. A knit-lined hat literally doubles the yarn.

**Embellishments.** Knit or crochet flowers add 10 to 20 yards each. Pom-poms made from yarn use 15 to 25 yards depending on size. Applied i-cord trim uses about 1 yard per inch of trim.

## What Are Common Mistakes and Tips?

**Do not assume one skein equals one hat.** A standard skein of worsted weight is usually 180 to 220 yards. An adult beanie needs 110 to 140 yards, so one skein usually works. But a slouchy hat with a folded brim might need 180 to 200 yards. That cuts it very close. Check your yardage before you assume.

**Swatch for accessories too.** A half-stitch-per-inch gauge difference on a hat can mean the difference between a hat that fits and one that is too tight. Hats and mittens need to fit. Swatch.

**Buy a matching skein.** If you are making a hat and fingerless mitts from the same yarn, you need 220 to 340 yards total. That is usually 2 skeins. Buy both from the same dye lot.

**Weigh partial skeins.** If you are pulling from your stash, weigh what you have. A 100g skein of worsted at 220 yards per 100g that weighs 55g has about 121 yards left. That is enough for a hat, but probably not enough for a hat plus mitts. The stash estimator on fibertools.app can help with this calculation.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using a blanket pattern's yarn weight recommendation for a hat. Hats need denser fabric.
- Not counting the yarn for a pom-pom or tassel
- Forgetting that ribbed brims use more yarn per inch than stockinette body sections
- Buying one skein for a hat and cowl matching set when you need two

## What Do Real Accessory Projects Look Like?

**The one-skein hat.** A knitter made an adult beanie in worsted weight on US 7 needles. The skein had 220 yards. She made a stockinette body with a 2x2 ribbed brim. She used 125 yards and had 95 yards left. That was enough for a matching pair of fingerless mitts. Total: 1 skein, 2 finished accessories.

**The matching set.** A crocheter planned a cowl and hat set in bulky weight. The hat needed 95 yards. The cowl, at 28" circumference and 9" tall, needed 180 yards. Total: 275 yards. She bought 2 skeins at 130 yards each, which gave her 260 yards. She ran short by 15 yards. A third skein saved the project. The lesson: always round up.

**The colorwork mittens.** A knitter made stranded mittens in fingering weight with 2 colors. The base yardage for solid mittens was 180 yards. With the 20% colorwork addition, she needed 216 yards total. She split that as 130 yards of main color and 86 yards of contrast. She bought one skein of each, both at 230 yards, and finished with plenty to spare.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I make a hat from leftover yarn?

Yes, if you have enough. An adult beanie needs 110 to 140 yards of worsted weight. Weigh your leftover yarn and calculate the remaining yardage using this formula: grams remaining divided by total grams, then multiplied by total yards. If you are close to the minimum, pick a simpler pattern without a folded brim or pom-pom. That will lower your yardage needs.

### How much yarn do I need for a matching hat and scarf set?

A worsted weight adult beanie needs about 130 yards. A standard scarf needs about 300 yards. Together that is about 430 yards, or 2 to 3 skeins depending on how the yarn is put up. Add a pair of fingerless mitts at 120 yards for a 3-piece set, and you need about 550 yards total. Buy from the same dye lot. The Hat Calculator and Yarn Calculator together give you exact numbers.

### Should I use the same yarn weight for all accessories?

You can, but some accessories work better in certain weights. Hats and mittens work well in worsted or DK for warmth and structure. Cowls drape better in DK or sport weight. Headbands work in any weight. Matching sets should use the same yarn and weight so everything looks consistent.

### How do I calculate yarn for a pattern that only lists grams, not yards?

Check the yarn label for the yards-per-gram ratio. If your yarn has 220 yards per 100g skein, that is 2.2 yards per gram. If the pattern says "75g of main color," multiply: 75 x 2.2 = 165 yards. The [Yarn Calculator](/yarn-calculator) handles this conversion for you.
