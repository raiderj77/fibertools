# How to Plan a Temperature Blanket: The Complete Guide

A temperature blanket turns a year of weather into a one-of-a-kind piece of textile art. Each day gets one row or stripe in a color that matches that day's high temperature. By December 31, you have a blanket that shows the whole year in color. No two are ever alike.

This idea is very popular on Reddit, Instagram, and TikTok. But the planning part trips up a lot of crafters. How much yarn do you need? How many colors? How big will it turn out? Here is everything you need to figure out before you start.

## What Is a Temperature Blanket?

A temperature blanket gives each temperature range its own yarn color. For example, dark blue might stand for anything below 20°F. Light blue could cover 21 to 30°F. Green might cover 31 to 40°F. The colors go up through reds and oranges for hot summer days. You work one row per day based on your local high, low, or average temperature.

After 365 days, you have 365 rows of color. They form a pattern that shows your local seasons from start to finish. Crafters in Minnesota end up with blankets full of blues and purples. Crafters in Florida see a lot of coral and red. The weather writes the pattern for you.

## How Do You Plan Temperature Ranges and Colors?

Start by looking up your local temperature extremes. Find your city's record high and low for the past 5 years. If your range runs from 5°F to 98°F, you need to cover about 95 degrees.

**Divide into 10 to 12 ranges.** Most temperature blankets use 10 to 12 colors. Each color covers an 8 to 10 degree range. Here is a sample setup for a temperate climate:

| Temperature Range | Color | Example Yarn |
|---|---|---|
| Below 10°F | Dark navy | Deep winter |
| 10 to 19°F | Royal blue | Cold snaps |
| 20 to 29°F | Medium blue | Winter days |
| 30 to 39°F | Light blue | Early spring |
| 40 to 49°F | Teal | Spring nights |
| 50 to 59°F | Green | Mild days |
| 60 to 69°F | Yellow-green | Late spring |
| 70 to 79°F | Yellow | Summer starts |
| 80 to 89°F | Orange | Peak summer |
| 90°F and above | Red | Heat waves |

**Pick colors that look clearly different from each other.** Hold all your skeins together before you buy them. Colors that look different on the shelf can blend together in the finished blanket. Do not pick three shades of blue that are almost the same. You want each stripe to be easy to see from across the room.

## How Do You Calculate Size and Yarn?

A temperature blanket always has 365 rows for a full year. You choose the width. The final length depends on your gauge and how tall each stitch is.

**For crochet using single crochet and worsted weight yarn:** Each row is about 0.3 inches tall. Multiply 365 rows by 0.3 inches and you get about 109 inches, which is close to 9 feet. That is longer than most beds. You can shorten it by using a taller stitch. Half double crochet rows are about 0.5 inches tall, giving you around 182 inches. Double crochet rows are about 0.75 inches tall, giving you around 274 inches.

**For knitting using stockinette and worsted weight yarn:** Each row is about 0.15 inches tall. At 365 rows, that is about 55 inches. That is a comfortable throw length.

**Yarn estimate for a worsted weight throw that is 50 inches wide:**

Each daily row in worsted weight at 50 inches wide uses about 6 to 8 yards of yarn. Over 365 days, that is about 2,200 to 2,900 yards in total. The yarn will not be split evenly across colors. Warm climates use more of the summer colors. Cold climates use more of the winter colors.

A safe rule is to buy at least 300 yards of each color. Buy 400 to 500 yards of your most-used colors. Those are the colors that match your area's longest season. Use the [Yarn Calculator](/yarn-calculator) to estimate how much you need per color based on your expected row counts.

## What Are the Best Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Track your temperatures every day.** Use a weather app, a paper calendar, or a spreadsheet. Missing days adds up fast. By March, you will not remember what the temperature was on February 14. Some free apps, like Weather Underground, let you look up past data if you fall behind.

**Pick one type of temperature reading and stick with it.** High, low, or average all work. But mixing them makes your blanket inconsistent. Most crafters use the daily high because it gives the widest range of colors.

**Think about making a half-year blanket.** If 365 rows feels like too much, a 6-month blanket covers January through June or July through December. That gives you 180 to 183 rows. In knit stockinette, that is about 55 inches long. In crochet single crochet, it is also about 55 inches long.

**Budget for extra yarn.** Temperature blankets usually cost $60 to $150 in yarn if you use acrylic. They cost $150 to $400 if you use wool. The price depends on width and yarn weight. Buy all your yarn at the start so the dye lots match.

**Do not skip the border.** Plan for 50 to 100 extra yards to make a border. A simple single crochet or garter stitch border in a neutral color frames the whole blanket nicely.

**Common mistakes:**

- Starting without checking the final length. 365 rows of double crochet can make a blanket that is 20 feet long.
- Choosing too many colors, like 15 or more, so the differences become hard to see.
- Not buying all your yarn upfront and then finding a color is discontinued mid-year.
- Forgetting that crochet uses about 30% more yarn than knitting for the same size blanket.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many colors do I need for a temperature blanket?

Most temperature blankets use 10 to 12 colors. Each color covers an 8 to 10 degree temperature range. Fewer than 8 colors makes the blanket look flat with too little variety. More than 14 colors makes it hard to tell them apart. Look up your local temperature range and divide it evenly to decide.

### Can I use any stitch for a temperature blanket?

Yes, but stitch height changes your finished length. Single crochet and garter stitch make the shortest rows. Stockinette knitting works well for throw-length blankets. Avoid tall stitches like treble crochet unless you want a very long blanket or plan to work every other day instead.

### How much does a temperature blanket cost to make?

A worsted weight acrylic temperature blanket costs $60 to $150. The price depends on the brand and blanket width. Wool or cotton versions cost $150 to $400. Budget about $8 to $15 per color for acrylic. Buying all your yarn upfront keeps the dye lots consistent and locks in your total cost before you begin.

### What if I miss tracking a day's temperature?

Look up the historical high for your zip code on Weather Underground or the National Weather Service website. Both offer free historical data going back many years. If you fall several weeks behind, look up all the temperatures at once and work the rows in one catch-up session.
