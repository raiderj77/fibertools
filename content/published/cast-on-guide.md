# Cast On Calculator: How Many Stitches Do You Need?

You picked your yarn. You chose your needles. You made a gauge swatch. Now you are staring at the yarn tail and asking: how many stitches do I cast on for a 22-inch sweater front? The formula is simple. But a few details, like pattern repeats, edge stitches, and ease, can trip you up.

This guide walks through the cast-on math step by step. It compares common cast-on methods. It also shows how the [Cast On Calculator](/cast-on-calculator) handles the rounding and adjustments for you.

## When You Need a Cast-On Calculation

Every time you knit without a pattern, or change a pattern to fit a different gauge or size, you need to find your cast-on count. The basic formula is:

**Stitches to cast on = desired width in inches x stitches per inch (gauge)**

That sounds easy. But real projects add a few wrinkles:

- Your stitch pattern repeats over a set number of stitches, like cables every 8 stitches.
- You need edge stitches for seaming or a clean selvedge.
- You want ease, which is extra width, built into the measurement.
- Crochet foundation chains need one or two extra chains for turning.

The calculator helps because "24.6 stitches" is not a real answer. You need a whole number that also works with your pattern repeat.

## The Math: Width x Gauge = Stitches

Here is the full process, broken into steps.

### Step 1: Get Your Gauge

Knit a swatch at least 6 inches wide and 6 inches tall. Use the same stitch pattern you plan to use in your project. Measure the center 4 inches. Stay away from the edges because they can distort your reading. Count the stitches across those 4 inches. Then divide by 4.

**Example:** 22 stitches over 4 inches = 5.5 stitches per inch.

Common gauge ranges by yarn weight:

| Yarn Weight | CYC | Typical Gauge (st/in) | Typical Needle |
|-------------|-----|-----------------------|----------------|
| Fingering | 1 | 7 to 8 | US 1 to 3 (2.25 to 3.25mm) |
| Sport | 2 | 5.5 to 6.5 | US 3 to 5 (3.25 to 3.75mm) |
| DK | 3 | 5 to 5.5 | US 5 to 7 (3.75 to 4.5mm) |
| Worsted | 4 | 4 to 5 | US 7 to 9 (4.5 to 5.5mm) |
| Bulky | 5 | 3 to 3.75 | US 9 to 11 (5.5 to 8mm) |
| Super Bulky | 6 | 2 to 3 | US 11 to 17 (8 to 12mm) |

### Step 2: Multiply

Target width x stitches per inch = raw stitch count.

**Example:** 20-inch sweater front x 5.5 st/in = 110 stitches.

### Step 3: Adjust for Pattern Multiple

If your stitch pattern repeats every N stitches, round your raw count to the nearest multiple of N. Then add any extra stitches the pattern needs for symmetry.

**2x2 Ribbing (multiple of 4 + 2):** Start with 110 stitches. The nearest multiple of 4 is 108 or 112. Round up to 112. Then add 2 for symmetry. That gives you 114 stitches. This creates a balanced rib that starts and ends with K2.

**Cable Panel (multiple of 12 + 2 edge):** Start with 110 stitches. The nearest multiple of 12 is 108. Add 2 edge stitches. That gives you 110 stitches. That works.

### Step 4: Add Edge Stitches

If you plan to seam the piece, add 1 selvedge stitch to each side. This gives you a clean column of stitches for mattress stitch seaming. It also keeps those extra stitches from eating into your stitch pattern.

For pieces that will not be seamed, like a scarf, you might add 2 to 3 garter stitches on each side. This helps stop the edges from curling.

## Long-Tail Cast On: How Much Yarn?

The long-tail cast on is the most common method in knitting. But you have to estimate how much tail to pull out before you start. If you run out of tail, you have to rip it out and start over.

**The rule of thumb:** Allow 1 inch of tail per stitch. Then add 6 extra inches for weaving in.

For 110 stitches: 110 + 6 = 116 inches, or about 3.2 yards of tail.

**A more precise method:** Wrap the yarn around your needle 10 times. Measure that length. Then multiply it by your total stitch count divided by 10. Different yarn weights use up different amounts per stitch.

| Yarn Weight | Tail per Stitch (approx.) | 100-Stitch Tail |
|-------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Fingering (1) | 0.5 inch | 50 inches (1.4 yd) |
| DK (3) | 0.75 inch | 75 inches (2.1 yd) |
| Worsted (4) | 1 inch | 100 inches (2.8 yd) |
| Bulky (5) | 1.5 inches | 150 inches (4.2 yd) |
| Super Bulky (6) | 2 inches | 200 inches (5.6 yd) |

**Pro tip:** Always add 20% more than your estimate. Running short wastes time. Running long just means you trim a tail.

## Cast-On Methods Compared

Different cast-on methods work best for different projects. Here is when to use each one:

**Long-Tail Cast On.** This is the all-purpose default. It makes a stretchy, neat edge. It works well for most projects. You do need to pre-measure the tail.

**Cable Cast On.** This method uses two needles. You do not need to estimate a tail. It is slightly less stretchy than the long-tail. It is good for adding stitches in the middle of a row, like for buttonholes. It also works when you do not want to guess at tail length.

**Provisional Cast On.** This creates a temporary edge you can remove later. Removing it exposes live stitches. It is a must for projects where you will knit in both directions from the cast-on row, like toe-up sock afterthought heels or double-knit scarves.

**German Twisted (Old Norwegian) Cast On.** This is extra stretchy. It is the best choice for sock cuffs, hat brims, and any edge that needs to stretch over a body part.

**Knitted Cast On.** This is great for beginners. You just knit into the first stitch and place the new stitch on the left needle. It is not as stretchy as the long-tail, but it is easy to learn.

### Crochet Equivalent: Foundation Chain

For crocheters, the foundation chain is the same as a cast on. The formula works the same way, width x gauge, but you add extra chains:

- For single crochet (sc): chain count = stitches needed + 1 turning chain
- For half double crochet (hdc): stitches needed + 2
- For double crochet (dc): stitches needed + 3

**Foundation single crochet (fsc)** is the crochet version of a stretchy cast on. Instead of chaining first and then working into the chain, you create the chain and the first row at the same time. This makes a more elastic edge. It also gets rid of the too-tight chain problem.

## Tips and Common Mistakes

**Always swatch in pattern.** Stockinette and ribbing have different gauges, even with the same yarn and needles. Ribbing pulls in. A 20-stitch ribbing swatch will be narrower than a 20-stitch stockinette swatch.

**Block your swatch before you measure it.** Some yarns change gauge a lot after washing and blocking. Superwash merino and cotton are especially likely to grow. Measuring an unblocked swatch gives you wrong numbers.

**Common mistakes:**

- Using the gauge from the yarn label instead of swatching. Your tension is not the same as the sample knitter's tension.
- Forgetting to add edge or selvedge stitches before adjusting for pattern multiple.
- Measuring gauge over 1 inch instead of 4 inches. Small errors in a short measurement multiply across the full width.
- Ignoring ease. A 36-inch bust needs 38 to 40 inches of fabric for a comfortable fit, not 36 inches.

**Negative ease is on purpose for fitted items.** Socks, fitted hats, and snug cuffs are made to stretch over the body. For a hat, cast on for a circumference 1 to 2 inches less than the actual head measurement.

## Real Project Examples

**Worsted Weight Scarf (6 inches wide):** Gauge: 4.5 st/in. Raw count: 6 x 4.5 = 27. Pattern: seed stitch (multiple of 2 + 1). Nearest valid count: 27, which is already odd. Add 0 edge stitches. Cast on 27.

**DK Weight Sweater Front (19 inches):** Gauge: 5.25 st/in. Raw count: 19 x 5.25 = 99.75. Pattern: stockinette with 6-stitch cable panel. Multiple of 6 + 2 selvedge. Round to 98 (multiple of 6 = 96 + 2 selvedge). Check: 98 divided by 5.25 = 18.67 inches. That is close enough. Blocking will bring it to 19 inches.

**Bulky Blanket (48 inches wide):** Gauge: 3 st/in. Raw count: 48 x 3 = 144. Pattern: basketweave (multiple of 8). 144 divided by 8 = 18, which is an exact multiple. Cast on 144. Long-tail estimate: 144 x 1.5 inches (bulky) = 216 inches = 6 yards of tail.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I figure out how many stitches to cast on for a blanket?

Measure your desired width. Then multiply by your stitch gauge. For a 48-inch blanket at 4.5 stitches per inch, you need 216 stitches. Adjust that number to the nearest multiple that works with your stitch pattern repeat. Always swatch in your chosen stitch pattern first. Do not rely on the yarn label gauge.

### What if my stitch count does not match a pattern multiple?

Round to the nearest multiple that keeps you close to your target width. Going 2 to 4 stitches over or under is usually fine. That is less than an inch of difference in most gauges. For garments where fit matters, round up. Accept the slightly wider piece rather than going too narrow.

### How much yarn do I need for long-tail cast on?

Estimate 1 inch of tail per stitch for worsted weight. Use 0.5 inch for fingering. Use 1.5 to 2 inches for bulky. Add 6 inches for the leftover tail. For 100 worsted-weight stitches, pull about 3 yards of tail. When you are not sure, pull more. You can trim extra length, but you cannot add it back.

### Is foundation chain gauge the same as stitch gauge in crochet?

Your foundation chain gauge can differ from your working stitch gauge. Chains tend to be tighter than the stitches above them. If your chains run tight, go up one hook size for the chain row only. Then switch back to your regular hook for Row 1. Foundation single crochet (fsc) avoids this problem entirely.
