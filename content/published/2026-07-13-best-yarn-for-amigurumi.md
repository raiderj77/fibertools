# Best Yarn For Amigurumi

For most amigurumi, worsted weight 100% cotton or cotton-acrylic blend yarn is your best choice. It holds stitch definition, hides gaps between stitches, and gives you a firm little body that stands up on its own. Save the fuzzy or splitty yarns for later projects once you've got a few dolls under your belt.

## What Makes A Yarn Good For Amigurumi?

A good amigurumi yarn does three things well. It shows your stitches clearly, it doesn't split while you work, and it holds up to stuffing without stretching out of shape.

Amigurumi is different from a sweater or a blanket. You're crocheting tight, small stitches in a spiral, usually with a hook a size or two smaller than the yarn label suggests. That tight gauge is what keeps stuffing from poking through. But it only works if the yarn cooperates.

A yarn with good "stitch definition" lets you see each stitch clearly, which matters for shaping faces, counting rounds, and spotting mistakes early. A yarn that splits easily will catch on your hook mid-stitch and slow you down, especially on tiny pieces like ears or fingers. And a yarn with some body holds its shape once stuffed, instead of sagging or going limp.

Fiber content, ply, and weight all affect these three things. Let's go through the main options.

## Is Cotton Yarn The Best Choice For Amigurumi?

Cotton is the top pick for most amigurumi makers, especially beginners. It has great stitch definition, little to no splitting, and a matte finish that photographs and displays well. The main downside is that it's less stretchy than wool or acrylic, so it takes a bit more effort to work with over long sessions.

Cotton yarn is a plant fiber, so it has no natural stretch or bounce. That sounds like a downside, but for amigurumi it's a strength. Your stitches stay exactly where you put them. They don't relax open over time the way wool stitches can.

Mercerized cotton (look for the word "mercerized" on the label) has a slight sheen and even smoother stitch definition. Brands like Lion Brand 24/7 Cotton, Sugar 'n Cream, and Hobbii Rainbow Cotton 8/8 fall into this general category, though exact product lines change over time, so check current labels.

The tradeoff is hand fatigue. Cotton doesn't give the way wool or acrylic does, so tight amigurumi stitches in 100% cotton can be tougher on your hands, especially in long sessions. If you have any hand or wrist strain, take more breaks with cotton than you would with a springier yarn.

## What About Acrylic Yarn?

Acrylic is a solid budget-friendly option with decent stitch definition, though usually not quite as crisp as cotton. It's easy to find in every color imaginable, and it's machine washable, which matters if your amigurumi is going to a kid.

Worsted weight acrylic, like Red Heart Super Saver or Caron Simply Soft, is easy to find in craft stores and comes in huge color ranges. That matters more than you'd think for amigurumi, since you often need small amounts of many colors for one project.

The catch with acrylic is consistency. Quality varies a lot between brands and even between dye lots. Cheaper acrylics can be splitty, and some have a slight halo or fuzziness that blurs your stitch detail. If you're making something with fine detail work, like small facial features, that fuzziness can work against you.

Acrylic does have one real advantage over cotton: it's softer and has slightly more give, so it's easier on your hands over a long project.

## Should I Use A Cotton-Acrylic Blend?

Cotton-acrylic blends are a strong middle ground. You get much of cotton's crisp stitch definition, plus some of acrylic's softness and stretch. Many amigurumi-specific yarn lines are built this way on purpose.

Yarns like Lion Brand 24/7 Cotton are 100% cotton, but many mills also sell cotton-acrylic or cotton-poly blends marketed specifically for amigurumi and toy making. These blends often run around 80/20 or 75/25 cotton to synthetic.

The blend softens the stiffness of pure cotton while keeping most of the stitch clarity. It's a good pick if you found 100% cotton too tough on your hands but didn't love how blurry pure acrylic stitches looked.

If you see a yarn labeled "amigurumi yarn" or "toy yarn," it's very often one of these blends. Read the fiber content on the label before buying, since marketing names don't always tell you the actual fiber mix.

## Can I Use Wool For Amigurumi?

Wool works, but it comes with two real tradeoffs: less crisp stitch definition, and it's not machine washable unless it's superwash. It's a fine choice for adult collectors or display pieces, but it's not the easiest yarn for a first amigurumi project.

Wool has natural bounce and elasticity. That's great for socks and sweaters, but it means your amigurumi stitches can relax and open slightly after you stuff the piece, letting bits of stuffing peek through. Working a size smaller hook than usual helps offset this.

Felted or feltable wool (non-superwash) has one interesting use case: some makers crochet loosely, felt the piece in the wash, and get a dense, sturdy fabric with no visible stitches at all. This is a specific technique, not a beginner default, but it's worth knowing about if you want a different look.

If you do use wool, check the care label. Non-superwash wool amigurumi should be labeled "hand wash only" or "surface wash only" if the toy is going to a child, since machine washing can felt and shrink it unevenly.

## What Yarn Weight Is Best For Amigurumi?

Worsted weight (also called medium or category 4) is the most common choice, and the best starting point if you're new to amigurumi. It's easy to find, easy to see your stitches in, and most amigurumi patterns are written for it.

Here's how the common weights compare for amigurumi work:

**Worsted weight (4)**
This is the default for most published amigurumi patterns. It works up at a reasonable pace, is easy to source, and gives you a finished piece that's a good gift or display size, usually 6 to 10 inches tall depending on the pattern.

**DK weight (3)**
A little thinner than worsted. Good for smaller, more detailed amigurumi, like tiny animals or accessories. Takes more time and more rounds to reach the same size as worsted weight, but you get finer detail in faces and features.

**Sport or fingering weight (1-2)**
Used for miniature amigurumi, often under 3 inches tall. This is not beginner territory. The stitches are small, the hook is tiny, and mistakes are hard to fix. Save this for after you've made several worsted weight pieces.

**Bulky weight (5)**
Used for big, chunky amigurumi or fast, simple projects. Stitch definition is harder to control at this size, and it's tougher to get the tight gauge amigurumi needs, since the stitches themselves are large. It works better for simple round shapes than detailed characters.

If you're unsure where to start, worsted weight cotton or cotton-blend is the safest first purchase. It's forgiving, easy to find, and most free patterns online are written for it.

## Does Yarn Color Or Dye Matter?

Yes, but mainly for practical reasons, not just looks. Dark colors like black and navy make it hard to see individual stitches, which is frustrating when you're learning to count rounds or spot mistakes. Save dark colors for after you're comfortable with the pattern.

Light and medium colors like cream, yellow, light blue, and pale pink show stitch definition clearly. This makes it much easier to see where you are in a round and to catch a missed stitch before it becomes a bigger problem several rounds later.

Variegated or self-striping yarns look fun on the skein, but they can hide the shape of a small amigurumi piece. The color changes fight against the shaping, so a carefully shaped ear or paw can end up visually flattened by a stripe. Solid colors are almost always the better choice for amigurumi, especially for facial features and small parts.

## How Much Yarn Do I Actually Need?

Most small to medium amigurumi (around 6 to 8 inches tall) use less than one full skein of worsted weight yarn. This is why many amigurumi makers build up a stash of partial skeins and small quantities in many colors, rather than buying full skeins of just a few colors.

Because you often need small amounts of several colors, cotton yarns sold in smaller balls, like Sugar 'n Cream's 2-ounce balls, are practical. You're not stuck with 7 unused skeins of a color you only needed for two rounds of ears.

If you're working from a pattern, check the materials list for yardage per color rather than assuming you'll need a full skein of each. Many patterns list this out separately for the body, head, and small features.

## So Which Yarn Should You Actually Buy?

If this is your first amigurumi project, buy worsted weight 100% cotton or a cotton-acrylic blend in a light or medium solid color. This combination gives you the clearest stitches, the easiest troubleshooting, and the most forgiving learning experience.

Once you've made a few pieces and know how your hands feel after a long session, branch out. Try a cotton-acrylic blend if pure cotton felt too stiff. Try DK weight if you want more detail. Try wool if you're making a display piece that won't need washing.

Always check your gauge swatch, even for amigurumi, especially if you're substituting a yarn a pattern didn't call for. A tighter or looser gauge than the pattern expects will change your finished size and how much stuffing shows through your stitches. Measure as you go, and adjust your hook size up or down until your fabric feels dense enough to hold stuffing without gaps.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the best yarn weight for amigurumi?
Worsted weight (medium, #4) yarn is the best choice for amigurumi beginners and most patterns. It's thick enough to work up quickly and hold its shape, but not so bulky that fine details get lost. Most published amigurumi patterns are written for worsted weight, making it easy to find gauge-matched projects. DK or sport weight yarns work well for smaller, more delicate toys, while bulky yarn suits large, chunky designs. Sticking with worsted weight as you learn will give you consistent, predictable results and the widest range of pattern options.

### Should I use cotton or acrylic yarn for amigurumi?
Cotton yarn is generally preferred because its low stretch and smooth texture create crisp, defined stitches that show detail well. It also holds stuffing firmly in place without sagging over time. Acrylic yarn is a solid budget-friendly alternative, offering great color variety, machine washability, and durability for kids' toys. However, acrylic's slight stretchiness can make stitches look less uniform. Many makers choose 100% cotton or a cotton blend for polished, gallery-worthy amigurumi, and acrylic for everyday, kid-safe stuffed toys that need frequent washing.

### Can I use fuzzy or textured yarn for amigurumi?
Fuzzy or highly textured yarns are not recommended for standard amigurumi because they obscure stitch definition, making it hard to see where to insert your hook. This causes uneven tension and missed stitches, especially for beginners. Smooth, plied yarns like cotton or acrylic show each stitch clearly, which is essential for shaping faces, limbs, and other precise details. Textured yarns like chenille or eyelash can work for accent pieces, like hair or fur trim, but should be avoided for the main body of the amigurumi.

### What size crochet hook should I use with amigurumi yarn?
Use a hook one to two sizes smaller than the yarn label recommends to create tight, dense stitches. For worsted weight yarn, this typically means a 2.5mm–3.5mm hook instead of the standard 5mm–6mm size. Tighter stitches prevent stuffing from poking through gaps and give amigurumi a firm, sturdy structure that holds its shape. This is different from garment crochet, where looser, drapier fabric is desirable. Always test your gauge on a small swatch first to confirm the stuffing stays hidden.

### How much yarn do I need for a small amigurumi project?
A single 50g skein of worsted weight yarn is usually enough for a small amigurumi, roughly 4–6 inches tall. Most beginner patterns for simple shapes like round animals or basic figures use well under 100 yards total. Larger or more detailed amigurumi with multiple colors, clothing, or accessories may require two or three skeins in different colors. It's smart to buy a little extra of your main color to account for tension differences or mistakes, since dye lots can vary between batches.
