# How to Calculate Warp for a Weaving Project

Warp math is what makes a weaving project work. Too little warp and you run out before you finish. Too much and you waste expensive yarn. Getting the numbers right before you wind the warp saves time, money, and heartbreak. Nobody wants to cut a project off the loom 6 inches too short.

Every warp calculation has three parts: how many ends (threads) you need, how long each end needs to be, and the total yardage for all ends. Here is the formula and every adjustment you need.

## How Do You Calculate the Number of Warp Ends?

Number of warp ends = finished width x sett (ends per inch).

**Sett** is the number of warp threads per inch. It depends on yarn weight and weave structure. The [Weaving Sett Calculator](/weaving-sett-calculator) can recommend a sett for your yarn. Here are common ranges for plain weave:

| Yarn Weight | Recommended Sett (EPI) |
|---|---|
| Bulky (CYC 5) | 5 to 6 EPI |
| Worsted (CYC 4) | 8 to 10 EPI |
| DK (CYC 3) | 10 to 12 EPI |
| Sport (CYC 2) | 12 to 15 EPI |
| Fingering (CYC 1) | 15 to 20 EPI |

**Width adjustment:** Fabric gets narrower on the loom. This is called draw-in. It also shrinks after washing. Add 10 to 15% to your desired finished width.

Example: You want a 10-inch-wide scarf at 10 EPI. Add 10% for draw-in. That gives you 11 inches. Total warp ends: 11 x 10 = 110 ends.

## How Do You Calculate Warp Length?

Warp length = finished length + loom waste + take-up + sampling + shrinkage.

**Finished length:** This is how long you want the finished piece. A scarf might be 60 inches. A dish towel might be 28 inches. If you are making more than one item on one warp, add all the lengths together.

**Loom waste:** This is the warp tied to the front and back beams that you cannot weave. Rigid heddle looms use 18 to 27 inches. Floor looms use 24 to 36 inches. This varies by loom. Measure yours by wrapping a tape measure around both beams.

**Take-up:** The warp bends over and under each weft thread. That bending uses up some length. Take-up is usually 10 to 15% of the finished length. Closer weaves have less take-up. Looser weaves have more.

**Sampling:** Weave a short test section at the start. This lets you check the sett, color, and pattern before you commit to the full project. Budget 6 to 8 inches of warp for sampling. That includes the space between the sample and the project.

**Shrinkage:** Fiber shrinks after wet finishing. Cotton shrinks 5 to 10%. Wool shrinks 10 to 15%. Linen shrinks 3 to 5%. Synthetic fibers shrink 0 to 3%. Apply the shrinkage percentage to the combined finished length plus take-up.

### The Full Formula

Warp length = (finished length x 1.10 for take-up x 1.10 for shrinkage) + loom waste + sampling

Example: 60-inch cotton scarf.
- Take-up: 60 x 1.10 = 66 inches
- Shrinkage: 66 x 1.08 = 71.3 inches
- Loom waste: 24 inches
- Sampling: 8 inches
- **Total warp length: 103 inches = 2.86 yards per end**

## How Do You Calculate Total Warp Yardage?

Total warp yardage = number of ends x warp length per end.

From our example: 110 ends x 2.86 yards = 314.6 yards of warp yarn.

**Add a 5 to 10% safety margin.** Winding mistakes, broken threads, and counting errors happen. Budget: 314.6 x 1.07 = 337 yards of warp yarn.

## How Do You Calculate Weft Yardage?

Weft yardage is easy to forget. Here is the formula:

Weft yardage = (finished width + 10% for take-up) x picks per inch x finished length

**Picks per inch (PPI)** is how many weft threads fit in one inch. For a balanced plain weave, PPI is roughly equal to EPI. For weft-dominant weaves, PPI is higher.

Example: 10-inch scarf, 10 PPI, 60 inches long.
Weft = (10 x 1.10) x 10 x 60 = 6,600 inches = 183 yards.

Add 10% for shrinkage and waste: 201 yards of weft.

**Total project yarn:** 337 yards warp + 201 yards weft = 538 yards total.

## What Are Common Mistakes and Tips?

**Measure your loom waste. Do not guess.** Loom waste varies. A small rigid heddle loom might use 18 inches. A floor loom with long tie-up cords might use 36 inches or more. Thread a tape measure through your loom's path to find your actual waste length.

**Plan for multiple items on one warp.** Warping is the slowest part of weaving. If you are making dish towels, warp for 4 at once instead of 1. Calculate: (4 towels x 30 inches each) + spacing between towels (3 gaps x 2 inches = 6 inches) + loom waste + take-up + shrinkage.

**Wind extra ends for broken threads.** Warp threads break under tension. This happens more with sticky or brittle yarn. Wind 2 to 3 extra ends beyond your calculated number. If nothing breaks, trim the extras. If a thread snaps, you have a replacement ready.

**Use a warping cross.** The warping cross keeps every thread in order while you wind. Without it, threads tangle during beaming. The whole warp can become unusable. Never skip the cross.

**Common mistakes:**
- Forgetting loom waste, which makes the project end up too short
- Not adding take-up, which makes the finished piece narrower and shorter than planned
- Confusing EPI with PPI, they are often similar but not always the same
- Not sampling at the beginning, which means committing to a full project with the wrong sett
- Winding the warp too tight or too loose, which causes uneven tension throughout the project

## What Do Real Warp Calculations Look Like?

**The rigid heddle scarf.** A weaver planned a 7-inch-wide cotton scarf at 12 EPI and 65 inches long. Width with draw-in: 7.7 inches, rounded to 8 inches. Ends: 8 x 12 = 96. Warp length: (65 x 1.10 x 1.08) + 22 + 6 = 105 inches = 2.92 yards. Total warp: 96 x 2.92 = 280 yards. Weft: 195 yards. Total project yarn: 475 yards. She wound 100 ends to have 4 spares.

**The floor loom towel set.** A weaver warped for 4 dish towels on a floor loom. Each towel was 18 x 28 inches. Total weaving length: (4 x 28) + (3 x 3 inch spacing) = 121 inches. Width with draw-in: 20 inches at 15 EPI = 300 ends. Warp length: (121 x 1.12 x 1.08) + 32 + 8 = 186 inches = 5.17 yards. Total warp: 300 x 5.17 = 1,551 yards. She wound a cone of 8/2 cotton with 3,360 yards per pound and had plenty for both warp and weft.

**The wool blanket.** A weaver planned a 45-inch-wide wool blanket at 8 EPI on a floor loom. Width with draw-in: 50 inches. Ends: 400. Finished length: 72 inches. Warp length: (72 x 1.10 x 1.14) + 34 + 8 = 132 inches = 3.67 yards. Total warp: 400 x 3.67 = 1,468 yards. Weft: about 1,200 yards. Total yarn: 2,668 yards of worsted weight wool.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is loom waste and can I reduce it?

Loom waste is the warp length tied to the front and back beams that you cannot weave through. On rigid heddle looms, it is usually 18 to 27 inches. On floor looms, it is 24 to 36 inches. You can reduce it by tying shorter knots, moving the apron rods closer, or using a floating selvedge setup. Some weavers weave header bands to use a few more inches.

### How do I calculate warp for multiple projects on one warp?

Add all finished lengths together. Then add 2 to 3 inches between each project for cutting and fringe. Then add a single loom waste amount, not one per project. Example: 3 scarves x 60 inches + 2 gaps x 3 inches + 24 inches loom waste + take-up and shrinkage. This is the most efficient use of warp yarn and loom time.

### Why is my finished weaving narrower than planned?

Draw-in causes the woven fabric to be narrower than the warp on the loom. The weft pulls the outer warp threads inward as it interlaces. Typical draw-in is 5 to 10% of the width. Budget 10% extra width to compensate. If draw-in is more than 10%, your selvedge tension may be too tight. Try angling the weft, also called bubbling it, in the shed before beating.

### How do I estimate yarn for a weaving project without a calculator?

Quick estimate: total warp yards = number of ends x total warp length in yards. Total weft yards = (width in inches x PPI x finished length in inches) divided by 36. Add 15% to each number for waste and shrinkage. Or use the [Weaving Sett Calculator](/weaving-sett-calculator) to do it all in one step.
