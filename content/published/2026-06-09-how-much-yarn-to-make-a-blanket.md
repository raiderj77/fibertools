# How Much Yarn Do You Need to Make a Blanket?

**Quick answer:** A typical knit or crochet throw blanket (roughly 50" x 60") takes between 1,000 and 3,000 yards of yarn. It depends on yarn weight, stitch pattern, and needle or hook size. Bulky yarn on big needles needs closer to 1,000 to 1,500 yards. Fingering or DK weight can go past 3,000 yards. Always buy an extra skein.

---

> **A mistake I made:** I bought yarn without checking how much the pattern needed, then ran out near the end. I could not match the dye lot, so the colors were off. Now I use the [yarn calculator](/yarn-calculator) and buy a little extra from the same dye lot.

## What size blanket are you actually making?

Size is the biggest factor. Before you look at any yarn label or pattern, decide on your dimensions. A baby blanket and a king bed blanket are very different projects.

Here are the sizes most knitters and crocheters work with:

| Blanket Type | Approximate Dimensions |
|---|---|
| Baby / Stroller | 30" x 36" |
| Lap / Throw | 50" x 60" |
| Twin Bed | 60" x 80" |
| Queen Bed | 80" x 90" |
| King Bed | 108" x 90" |

A lap throw has about 3,000 square inches of fabric. A king bed blanket has nearly 10,000 square inches. That difference matters when you are scaling a pattern or estimating on your own.

## How does yarn weight change the yardage?

Heavier yarn needs fewer yards because each yard covers more space. A super bulky throw might need 600 to 1,200 yards. The same size blanket in worsted weight can need 1,500 to 2,200 yards. Yarn weight is the biggest factor in any yardage estimate.

Heavier yarn covers more area per yard, so you need fewer yards total. Lighter yarn is thinner, so you need more yards to fill the same space.

Here are realistic yardage ranges for [a standard 50" x 60" throw blanket, broken down by weight](/blog/how-much-yarn-for-blanket/):

- **Super Bulky (weight 6):** 600 to 1,200 yards
- **Bulky (weight 5):** 1,000 to 1,800 yards
- **Worsted (weight 4):** 1,500 to 2,500 yards
- **DK (weight 3):** 2,000 to 3,000 yards
- **Fingering (weight 1):** 2,800 to 4,500+ yards

These ranges are wide because stitch pattern, tension, and fiber content all change the number. A dense moss stitch blanket in worsted will use more yarn than a simple stockinette one in the same weight.

The [Craft Yarn Council's standard yarn weight system](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) is what most US yarn labels follow. You can use it to check which category your skein falls into.

## Does stitch pattern really affect yardage that much?

Yes. Stitch pattern can change your yardage by 30 to 50 percent. Textured stitches like cables and seed stitch pull the fabric in. They also force the yarn to travel farther per square inch. A cabled throw in worsted can easily need 1,000 more yards than a plain stockinette version.

Textured stitches use more yarn than flat ones. The yarn goes in and out of the fabric more times per inch.

Here are some rough comparisons for knitters using worsted on a 50" x 60" throw:

- **Stockinette:** about 1,500 to 1,800 yards
- **Garter stitch:** about 1,800 to 2,200 yards (more rows per inch means more yarn)
- **Cables:** about 2,000 to 2,600 yards (cables pull in the width and use extra yarn in the twists)
- **Brioche:** about 2,200 to 2,800 yards

For crochet, the same idea applies. A simple single crochet (sc) fabric uses more yarn than a double crochet (dc) fabric. Single crochet is a denser stitch. A granny square blanket with lots of chain spaces will use less yarn per square inch than a solid sc blanket of the same size.

## How do I calculate yardage from a gauge swatch?

Make a swatch, weigh it in grams, and divide your yarn label yardage by the skein weight in grams. That gives you yards per gram. Then multiply by how many grams your swatch used per square inch. Scale that up to your full blanket size. Real math beats guessing every time.

Swatching gives you real data instead of guesses. Here is the method to use when working without a pattern.

1. Knit or crochet a swatch at least 6" x 6" in your chosen stitch.
2. Measure how many stitches and rows fit in 4 inches. Block the swatch first if the fiber tends to open up.
3. Weigh your swatch on a kitchen scale in grams.
4. Find the yards per gram on your yarn label. Divide total yards by grams per skein.
5. Calculate the total square inches of your finished blanket.
6. Divide blanket square inches by swatch square inches. Then multiply by swatch weight in grams.
7. Multiply that number by yards per gram to get your total yardage estimate.

It sounds like a lot of steps. But it only takes about five minutes once you have the swatch ready. This method also helps you spot dye lot issues early. You will know exactly how many skeins you need before you commit to buying.

## How many skeins is that?

Divide your total yardage estimate by the yards in one skein of your chosen yarn. A 1,800 yard worsted blanket takes about nine standard indie skeins at 200 yards each. But it only takes two skeins of a big box worsted sold at 840 yards. Always check the label, not just the weight.

It depends on how the yarn is sold. Skeins vary a lot in yardage.

Common skein sizes in the US:

- **Big box craft store worsted (Lion Brand Pound of Love, Red Heart Super Saver):** 840 to 1,000+ yards per skein
- **Standard indie dyer or LYS skein (worsted):** 200 to 220 yards per skein
- **Bulky indie skein:** 100 to 150 yards per skein
- **Cake or cone yarn:** anywhere from 400 to 2,000+ yards

For [a worsted throw needing 2,000 yards](/blog/how-much-yarn-for-a-blanket/), that is about two big box skeins or nine to ten indie skeins at 220 yards each. Always round up. Dye lots change, yarn gets discontinued, and running short near the end of a blanket is very frustrating.

Most experienced makers follow this rule: buy one extra skein beyond your estimate. If you do not use it, many local yarn stores will take back unused, unwound skeins within a reasonable time.

## Any tips for not running out mid-project?

Buy one extra skein from the same dye lot. Return it unopened if you finish without needing it. Weigh your remaining yarn at the halfway point. If the math looks tight, adjust your dimensions now. Do not wait until you have finished three quarters of the blanket.

Here are a few habits that help blanket makers avoid running out:

- **Write down your dye lot number** from the ball band before you throw it away.
- **Weigh your remaining yarn often** if you are working without a pattern. If you are halfway through your yarn but only a third of the way through the blanket, recalculate now.
- **Work from two skeins at once**, switching every two rows. This blends any small dye lot differences and helps you see how fast the yarn is going.
- **Measure as you go.** Do not assume your gauge matches the pattern exactly. Check your dimensions after the first 10 rows and adjust if needed.

Blankets are big projects. A little math at the start saves a lot of frustration later.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much yarn do I need to make a throw blanket?
A typical throw blanket (50" x 60") needs about 1,500 to 3,000 yards of yarn. It depends on yarn weight and stitch pattern. Bulky or super bulky yarn uses fewer yards. Fingering or DK weight needs a lot more. Your pattern's gauge and stitch density also matter a lot. Always check the recommended yardage on your pattern before buying yarn.

### Does yarn weight affect how much I need for a blanket?
Yes. Yarn weight makes a big difference. Bulky weight yarn (sizes 5 to 6) might only need 500 to 1,000 yards for a lap blanket. Worsted weight needs 1,500 to 2,000 yards for the same size. Lighter weights like DK or sport can need 3,000+ yards. Always match your yarn weight to your pattern to get an accurate estimate before you buy.

### How do I calculate yarn needed for a custom blanket size?
Make a gauge swatch first. Then figure out how many stitches and rows cover your desired dimensions. Count the yards used in your swatch. Calculate how many swatches it would take to fill the blanket area and multiply. Add a 10 to 15 percent buffer for finishing and weaving in ends. This helps you avoid running short in the middle of the project.

### How much yarn do I need for a baby blanket?
A standard baby blanket (30" x 36") usually needs 500 to 1,500 yards. It depends on yarn weight and stitch pattern. Bulky yarn can finish a baby blanket with as little as 300 to 500 yards. That makes it a great beginner project. Worsted weight is the most common choice for baby blankets. It usually falls in the 800 to 1,200 yard range. Always check your pattern for the exact amount.

### Should I buy extra yarn when making a blanket?
Yes. Always buy at least 10 to 15 percent more yarn than your pattern calls for. Blankets are large projects and running out is a real risk. Dye lots can also vary between skeins bought at different times. Having extra yarn on hand prevents color mismatches. It also means you can finish seams, borders, or fringe without scrambling to find a matching batch.
