# How to Estimate Knitting and Crochet Project Time

"When will it be done?" Every crafter dreads that question. Family members ask it when they order a Christmas gift in November. Your own brain asks it when you are 40% through a blanket and not sure if it will take another month or another six.

The answer is not a guess. It is math. If you know your stitching speed and your total stitch count, you can figure out your finish time within a few hours. Here is exactly how to do it.

## What Affects How Long a Project Takes?

Three things decide how long a project takes. They are your stitching speed, your total stitch count, and your finishing work.

**Stitching speed** changes a lot from person to person. A beginner knitter works about 15 to 20 stitches per minute. An intermediate knitter hits 25 to 35. Fast knitters and continental-style knitters can reach 40 to 60 stitches per minute. Crochet is faster per stitch. Beginners manage 20 to 30 stitches per minute. Experienced crocheters hit 35 to 50.

**Total stitch count** is the number of stitches in the whole project. A hat might have 5,000 to 8,000 stitches. A scarf has 10,000 to 20,000. A throw blanket has 80,000 to 150,000. A sweater has 50,000 to 120,000. This number tells you if you are in for a weekend or a whole season.

**Finishing work** includes seaming, weaving in ends, blocking, adding buttons, and attaching closures. A simple scarf adds about 30 minutes of finishing. A multi-piece sweater with set-in sleeves can add 3 to 6 hours.

## How Do You Calculate Your Personal Stitching Speed?

**Step 1: Set a timer for 5 minutes.** Work your project stitch at a comfortable, steady pace. Do not go as fast as you possibly can. Use the speed you would keep during a normal crafting session.

**Step 2: Count your stitches.** When the timer goes off, count how many stitches you finished. If you worked 130 stitches in 5 minutes, your speed is 26 stitches per minute.

**Step 3: Repeat at different times.** Your speed changes depending on the time of day, how tired you are, and how complex the stitch is. Measure in the morning and the evening across 2 to 3 sessions. Then average the results.

**Step 4: Adjust for pattern complexity.** Plain stockinette or single crochet is your fastest speed. Cables slow you down by about 20 to 30%. Colorwork slows you by 30 to 50%. Lace slows you by 40 to 60%. Multiply your base speed by the right factor for your pattern.

## How Do You Calculate Total Project Time?

Once you know your speed, the formula is simple.

**Total project time = total stitches / stitches per minute / 60**

That gives you hours. Here is a worked example.

A throw blanket worked in worsted weight single crochet: 200 stitches wide times 400 rows equals 80,000 total stitches. At 35 stitches per minute: 80,000 / 35 / 60 = 38 hours of active crocheting. Add 2 to 3 hours for weaving in ends and blocking. That is roughly 40 to 41 hours total.

At 2 hours of crafting per day, that is about 20 days, or roughly 3 weeks. At 1 hour per day, it is closer to 6 weeks.

## What Are the Best Tips for Managing Project Time?

**Track your time.** Use a phone timer, a notebook, or an app like Toggl to log your actual crafting hours. After a few projects, you will have real speed data. You will not need to estimate anymore.

**Break big projects into milestones.** Instead of saying "finish the blanket," set smaller goals. Try "complete 50 rows this week" or "finish the back panel by Friday." Milestones keep you motivated. They also help you spot pacing problems early.

**Account for the startup tax.** The first 10% of any project is the slowest part. You are checking the pattern a lot, adjusting your tension, and finding your rhythm. Your steady speed kicks in after the first few inches.

**Do not underestimate finishing.** Seaming a sweater can take 3 to 6 hours. A granny square blanket with 48 squares has 192 or more ends to weave in. That can take longer than crocheting several of the squares. Blocking a large blanket needs a full day of drying time.

**Schedule your crafting time.** If your blanket needs 50 hours and you craft 5 hours per week, that is 10 weeks. If you need it done in 6 weeks, you need 8 to 9 hours per week. Knowing this upfront stops last-minute panic.

**Common mistakes:**

- Estimating speed from your fastest stitch without accounting for pattern reads, end-of-row turns, and breaks
- Forgetting that colorwork and cables cut your speed by 20 to 50%
- Not adding time for learning new techniques mid-project
- Promising a deadline based on ideal speed instead of your realistic pace

## What Do Real Project Timelines Look Like?

**The Christmas blanket.** A crocheter promised her mom a throw blanket by December 25. She started on October 15. She calculated that a 50-inch-wide throw in single crochet was roughly 90,000 stitches. At her tested speed of 40 stitches per minute, that was 37.5 hours. At 1.5 hours per day, she needed 25 crafting days. She had 70 days available. She finished on December 3 with three weeks to spare, plus time to block and add a border.

**The rushed baby gift.** A knitter started a baby blanket 2 weeks before the shower. The total stitch count was 35,000. At 28 stitches per minute, that was 21 hours. That was doable in 14 days at 1.5 hours per day. She finished the knitting on day 12 and blocked on day 13. It was tight, but she made it.

**The sweater that took six months.** A beginning knitter thought a cable sweater would take "a few weeks." The total stitch count was 85,000. At 18 stitches per minute, which accounts for beginner speed plus cable slowdown, that was 79 hours. At 3 hours per week, the math said 26 weeks. That is exactly how long it took. The sweater was worth it, but she wished she had known upfront.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many stitches per minute is normal?

Beginner knitters average 15 to 20 stitches per minute. Intermediate knitters hit 25 to 35. Fast knitters reach 40 to 60. Crocheters are about 25 to 40% faster per stitch because crochet motions are simpler. Continental knitting is faster than English or throwing style by about 15 to 20%. Your personal speed depends on your experience, technique, and stitch pattern.

### Is crochet faster than knitting?

Crochet is faster per stitch. But crochet stitches are taller than knit stitches, so you need fewer rows to reach the same length. For the same finished item, crochet finishes about 20 to 30% faster than knitting. A knit hat that takes 5 hours might take 3.5 hours in crochet, but the crochet version uses more yarn.

### How do I speed up my knitting or crochet?

Try switching to continental knitting if you currently use English or throwing style. It reduces hand movement by about 30%. For crochet, practice keeping a steady rhythm. Try not to lift your hook too far between stitches. Yarn choice also helps. Smooth worsted acrylic moves faster than sticky wool or splitty singles.

### Should I account for mistakes in my time estimate?

Yes. Add 10 to 15% to your estimate for frogging, re-reading the pattern, and fixing errors. On complex patterns with cables or lace, add 20%. Beginners should add 25 to 30%. If you have made the same pattern before, you can skip this buffer. You already know the tricky spots.
