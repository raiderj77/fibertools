# How to Wash and Care for Handknits and Crochet

You spent 60 hours knitting a sweater from $80 yarn. Now it needs washing. The thought of ruining it in the machine is scary. Or worse, someone else tosses it in a hot wash. They hand you back a tiny, felted version of your beautiful cardigan.

Caring for handmade items is not hard, but it is different from caring for store-bought clothes. The fiber content decides everything. Here is how to wash, dry, and store your handmade pieces so they last for decades.

## How Do You Know What Care a Handknit Needs?

Check the yarn label. Every label has care symbols. Those symbols tell you exactly how to wash, dry, and iron that fiber. If you lost the label, figure out the fiber type and follow the care rules for that fiber.

**The golden rule: when in doubt, hand wash cold and lay flat to dry.** This method is safe for every fiber. You can always be more careful than the label says, just not less.

## How Do You Wash Different Fibers?

### Superwash Wool and Superwash Blends

**Machine wash:** Use the gentle or delicate cycle. Use cold or warm water, up to 86 degrees F or 30 degrees C. Put the item in a mesh laundry bag for extra protection.

**Machine dry:** Use low heat or tumble dry low. Superwash wool can handle it. But heat may slowly change the shape over many cycles. Laying flat to dry helps the garment last longer.

**Detergent:** Use a wool-safe detergent like Eucalan, Soak, or Kookaburra. Regular detergent works in a pinch. Avoid anything with enzymes or bleach.

### Untreated Wool (Non-Superwash)

**Hand wash ONLY.** Fill a basin with cool water, between 65 and 75 degrees F. Add a drop of wool wash. Put the item in the water without moving it around. Let it soak for 15 to 20 minutes. Gently press out the water. Do not wring or twist. Never put untreated wool in a washing machine, even on gentle.

**Dry flat.** Roll the item in a clean towel to remove extra water. Then lay it flat on a blocking mat or dry towel. Reshape it to the right size while it is still damp.

**What causes felting:** Hot water plus agitation causes felting. The tiny scales on wool fibers lock together when they get hot and are rubbed. One accidental hot wash will felt an untreated wool item forever. There is no way to undo felting.

### Acrylic

**Machine wash:** Any cycle, any temperature. Acrylic is the most forgiving fiber. It will not felt, shrink, or bleed.

**Machine dry:** Use low to medium heat. High heat can make acrylic lose its shape. It can also make the texture feel a little crunchy over time. Some crafters skip the dryer to keep acrylic items softer.

**Note:** High heat can "kill" acrylic. This means the drape gets permanently set. Sometimes crafters do this on purpose for lace blocking. But for most garments, you do not want this to happen.

### Cotton and Linen

**Machine wash:** Use the gentle cycle with warm or cool water. Cotton can shrink 5 to 10 percent on the first wash if it was not pre-washed. Linen gets softer with each wash.

**Machine dry:** Use low heat or air dry. Cotton handles the dryer better than wool, but it may shrink a little. Linen wrinkles more when machine dried.

**Tip:** Wash cotton items before gifting or wearing them. That way the person who gets it has the post-shrinkage size.

### Silk and Silk Blends

**Hand wash in cool water** with a gentle detergent. Silk does not like heat, agitation, or alkaline detergents. Do not wring it. Press it gently between towels.

**Dry flat and away from direct sunlight.** Sunlight can fade silk.

### Alpaca

**Hand wash in cool water.** Alpaca acts like wool but does not felt as easily. It does stretch when wet, so handle it carefully. Reshape it while it dries. Never hang wet alpaca. It will stretch out of shape and stay that way.

## How Do You Dry Handknits Safely?

**Lay flat to dry.** This is the safest method for every fiber. Place the item on a clean towel or blocking mat. Reshape it to the right size. Flip it after 12 hours so both sides dry evenly.

**Never hang wet knits.** Gravity pulls on wet fabric, especially wool and alpaca. A sweater hung to dry can grow 3 to 6 inches longer. Lay it flat or use a drying rack with a mesh surface that supports the full width.

**Speed up drying.** Place the item on a dry towel. Roll the towel up tightly. Press on it or step on it to soak up water. Unroll it, move the item to a fresh dry towel, and lay it flat. A fan pointed at the item cuts drying time in half.

**Machine drying rules:**
- Superwash wool: low heat, remove promptly
- Acrylic: low to medium heat
- Cotton: low heat (may shrink)
- Untreated wool: NEVER
- Silk: NEVER
- Alpaca: NEVER

## What Are Common Care Mistakes?

**Felting a non-superwash wool item.** This is the number one handknit disaster. It happens when someone does not check the fiber and tosses it in a hot wash. To prevent this, attach a care tag to every handmade gift. Write or print the washing instructions. Tie them to the item with scrap yarn.

**Using fabric softener.** Fabric softener coats fibers and reduces how well they absorb moisture. It makes wool feel slimy and acrylic feel waxy. Skip it for all handknits. Wool wash products like Eucalan or Soak add a light conditioner without that coating.

**Wringing or twisting.** Wringing stretches wet fibers and can permanently change the shape. Gently squeeze or press the water out. Never twist.

**Washing too often.** Wool naturally resists odors and bacteria. A wool sweater worn over a base layer only needs washing every 5 to 10 wears. Airing it out overnight between wears freshens it without any water. Washing too often causes pilling and shortens the life of the garment.

**Storing folded in drawers without moth protection.** Moths eat wool, silk, alpaca, and cashmere. Store natural fiber items in sealed bags or containers. Add cedar blocks or lavender sachets. Check stored items every 2 to 3 months for moth damage.

## What Do Real Care Situations Look Like?

**The felting disaster.** A knitter gave her sister a merino wool cardigan as a gift. There was no care tag on it. The sister washed it in hot water on a regular cycle. The cardigan felted down to child size. Now that knitter attaches a printed care tag to every gift. The tag lists the fiber content and washing instructions.

**The 10-year blanket.** A crocheter made an acrylic throw blanket. It has been machine washed and dried every week for ten years. It pilled in the first year. She shaved the pills off with a fabric shaver. The structure, color, and size have not changed. Acrylic lasts a very long time with basic care.

**The resurrected cotton sweater.** A crocheted cotton cardigan stretched out of shape after washing. The owner laid it flat and pinned it to a blocking mat at the original size. She misted it with water and let it dry pinned for 24 hours. The sweater went back to its original shape. Cotton responds well to re-blocking.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I machine wash handknit items?

It depends on the fiber. Superwash wool, acrylic, and cotton blends can go in the machine on a gentle cycle. Untreated wool, silk, and alpaca must be hand washed. Check the yarn label for the care symbol. When in doubt, hand wash cold and lay flat to dry. That method is safe for all fibers.

### How often should I wash handknit sweaters?

Wool sweaters need washing every 5 to 10 wears if worn over a base layer. Acrylic and cotton items can be washed after every 2 to 3 wears. Washing too often causes pilling and breaks down fibers. Air out wool overnight between wears to freshen it without water. Spot-clean stains right away instead of washing the whole garment.

### How do I prevent pilling on handknits?

Pilling happens when loose fibers tangle on the surface of the fabric. It is worse with loosely spun yarns and in areas with lots of friction, like underarms and sides. Use a fabric shaver or sweater stone to remove pills. Wash items inside-out in a mesh bag to cut down on friction. Tighter-spun yarns and plied yarns pill less than loosely spun singles.

### Should I attach care instructions to handmade gifts?

Yes, always. Write the fiber content, washing method, and drying instructions on a small card. Tie it to the item with scrap yarn or put it in the gift bag. Most people who receive handmade items will not know how to care for them. A care tag prevents felting disasters and helps your gift last longer.

## Care for Your Handmade Pieces

Every handmade item deserves good care. The fiber decides the rules. Wash gently, dry flat, store smart, and your handknits will last for decades.
