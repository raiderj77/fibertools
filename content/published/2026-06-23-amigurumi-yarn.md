# Amigurumi Yarn: What Actually Works and Why

**The short answer:** Use a smooth, tightly twisted worsted-weight yarn (around 200 yards per 100g) in a solid or semi-solid color. Cotton and acrylic both work well. Avoid fuzzy, loosely spun, or heavily textured yarns. You need to see your finished stitches clearly so you can count them. Your fabric also needs to be dense enough to hide the stuffing inside.

---

## What weight yarn should I use for amigurumi?

Worsted weight (CYC 4) is the most common starting point. But the real answer depends on the size you want. Sport or DK weight gives you a smaller, firmer toy. Bulky yarn makes a large, squishy one. The weight matters less than how your yarn and hook size work together.

Most amigurumi patterns tell you to work tighter than the yarn label says. If a worsted yarn suggests a 5mm hook, you might use a 3.5mm or 4mm instead. A smaller hook closes up the fabric so stuffing does not peek through. This is on purpose. Tight tension is a feature, not a mistake. Every crocheter's hands are different, so measure as you go. Adjust your hook until your fabric feels firm but not stiff.

A 100g skein of worsted yarn usually has 180 to 220 yards. For a small amigurumi figure (palm-sized, about 4 to 6 inches tall), you will likely use less than 50 yards of your main color. Larger pieces can use 150 to 200 yards total across all colors.

## Is cotton or acrylic better for amigurumi?

Both work. The choice depends on what you are making and how you like to work.

**Acrylic** is easy to use, simple to find, and machine washable. That last part matters a lot if the toy is for a child. Acrylic has a little stretch, which helps when you stuff a piece and pull yarn through tight stitches. Brands like [Lion Brand Pound of Love](https://www.lionbrand.com/collections/yarn/products/pound-of-love-yarn) and Red Heart Soft are popular because they are durable, colorfast, and low cost.

**Cotton** gives you crisper, cleaner stitches and a slightly stiffer fabric with no stretch. That stiffness is helpful for pieces that need to hold a shape, like a flat face or a structured body. The tradeoff is that cotton is harder on your hands during long sessions. It also does not have the give that acrylic does. [Paintbox Simply DK Cotton](https://www.lovecrafts.com/en-us/p/paintbox-yarns-simply-dk) and Drops Safran are often recommended in amigurumi communities. Both have a tight twist and a smooth surface.

If the toy is decorative or for an adult, either yarn works fine. If it is for a baby or toddler, check that the yarn label says machine washable. Also check that it meets any safety standards in your area.

## Why does stitch definition matter so much?

Amigurumi is almost entirely worked in continuous rounds of single crochet (sc). You need to see every stitch clearly so you can count accurately.

Fuzzy yarns, like mohair blends or brushed acrylic, hide the "V" shape of each stitch. When you cannot see your stitches, you miscount. A missed increase or decrease in a small piece throws off the shape fast. On a piece that is only 12 stitches around, one extra stitch is an 8% error. That shows.

Heathered or variegated yarns cause a similar problem. The color changes make it hard to see where one stitch ends and the next begins. Solid colors or very subtle semi-solids are much easier to work with. This is especially true for the head and body, where the shaping is most important.

## What yarn do experienced amigurumi makers actually recommend?

A few names come up again and again in amigurumi communities. They come up for specific reasons.

[Paintbox Simply DK](https://www.lovecrafts.com/en-us/p/paintbox-yarns-simply-dk) is a cotton-acrylic blend with a very tight twist and a huge color range. The stitch definition is excellent. It runs slightly thin for DK weight, so check your gauge against the pattern you are using.

[Scheepjes Catona](https://www.scheepjes.com/en/catona-269/) is 100% mercerized cotton. It is very popular in European amigurumi communities and is widely used in the US too. It comes in over 100 colors. The twist is very tight, and the finished fabric has a slight sheen. It is a fingering or sport weight, so your finished pieces will be smaller than a pattern written for worsted weight.

[Lion Brand 24/7 Cotton](https://www.lionbrand.com/collections/yarn/products/24-7-cotton-yarn) is a mercerized worsted cotton. It is easy to find at chain craft stores in the US. It is stiffer than acrylic but holds its shape well. The stitch definition is sharp.

For acrylic, [Paintbox Simply DK Acrylic](https://www.lovecrafts.com/en-us/p/paintbox-yarns-simply-dk-acrylic) and Hobby Lobby's I Love This Yarn both have tight plies and consistent yardage. Consistent yardage matters when you are buying several colors for one project.

## Does yarn color affect the finished look?

More than most people expect. Dark colors, especially black and navy, are genuinely hard to work with. The stitches disappear even in good light. If you are new to amigurumi, start with a medium-value solid color. You will be able to see your work, count your rounds, and fix mistakes before they pile up.

White and very light colors show dirt easily. They are also less forgiving if your tension is uneven. Save those for when you have a good feel for how tight your fabric needs to be.

## Can I use leftover yarn from other projects?

Yes, with one condition. The yarns need to be close enough in weight so your tension stays consistent across pieces. A head made in worsted and a body made in DK will look mismatched, even if the colors are the same. If you are mixing stash yarn, swatch each one at the same hook size. Compare how the fabric feels and how the stitches look. Measure as you go to make sure your pieces match the sizes the pattern intends.

Leftover yarn is one of the best things about amigurumi. Small figures use small amounts of yarn. A well-organized scrap bin can cover an entire project.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the best yarn for amigurumi?
Cotton yarn is widely considered the best choice for amigurumi. Its low stretch helps stitches hold their shape and keeps stuffing from showing through. Worsted or DK weight cotton gives you clean, clear stitches that are easy to count. Acrylic yarn is a popular lower-cost option. It is soft and comes in a wide range of colors, but it stretches a little more than cotton. Avoid fuzzy or textured yarns. They make it hard to see individual stitches.

### What weight yarn should I use for amigurumi?
DK weight (size 3) and worsted weight (size 4) are the most commonly recommended yarn weights for amigurumi. DK weight makes smaller, more detailed figures. Worsted weight works up faster and makes slightly larger pieces. Your finished size also depends on your hook size. Using a smaller hook than the yarn label suggests creates a tighter fabric. That tighter fabric keeps stuffing from poking through the gaps.

### Can I use acrylic yarn for amigurumi?
Yes, acrylic yarn works well for amigurumi and is one of the most popular choices. It is affordable, machine washable, easy to find, and comes in hundreds of colors. It is also soft enough for toys. The main downside is that acrylic stretches more than cotton. This can make stitches look slightly uneven or let stuffing show through. Choosing a tightly spun acrylic and using a smaller hook helps reduce these problems.

### How much yarn do I need to make amigurumi?
Most small amigurumi figures need between 50 and 200 yards of yarn. This depends on the size and complexity of the project. A simple animal under 4 inches tall typically uses one 50g skein or less. Larger figures with separate limbs and accessories may need several skeins across different colors. It is always smart to buy a little extra of your main color. Running out mid-project and finding a matching dye lot can be very difficult.

### What hook size should I use for amigurumi yarn?
For amigurumi, use a hook one or two sizes smaller than what the yarn label recommends. For example, if your worsted weight yarn suggests a 5mm hook, try a 3.5mm or 4mm hook instead. This tighter gauge creates a denser fabric with smaller gaps between stitches. It keeps the polyfill stuffing from showing through and gives your finished figure a neater look. Always crochet a small swatch first to check your stitch density.
