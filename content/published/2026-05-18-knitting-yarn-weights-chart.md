# Knitting Yarn Weights Chart: What Every Number and Label Actually Means

Yarn [weight describes how thick a strand is, which controls gauge](/blog/knitting-yarn-weight-chart/), drape, and how fast a project works up. The Craft Yarn Council recognizes [seven standard weight categories](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system), numbered 0 through 7. Knowing where your yarn falls on that scale tells you which needle to grab. It also tells you roughly how many stitches fit in an inch.

---

> **A mistake I made:** I treated yarn weight like a suggestion. It is not. The wrong weight changed the whole size and feel of my project. Check it against the [yarn weight chart](/yarn-weight-chart).

## What are the official yarn weight categories?

The Craft Yarn Council's numbering system runs from 0 (lace) to 7 (jumbo). Each category has a name, a suggested needle range, and an expected gauge over 4 inches in stockinette. Here is the full reference chart:

| Weight # | Name | Needle Size (US) | Needle Size (mm) | Stitches per 4 in (stockinette) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Lace | 000 to 1 | 1.5 to 2.25 mm | 33 to 40 sts |
| 1 | Super Fine | 1 to 3 | 2.25 to 3.25 mm | 27 to 32 sts |
| 2 | Fine | 3 to 5 | 3.25 to 3.75 mm | 23 to 26 sts |
| 3 | Light | 5 to 7 | 3.75 to 4.5 mm | 21 to 24 sts |
| 4 | Medium | 7 to 9 | 4.5 to 5.5 mm | 16 to 20 sts |
| 5 | Bulky | 9 to 11 | 5.5 to 8 mm | 12 to 15 sts |
| 6 | Super Bulky | 11 to 17 | 8 to 12.75 mm | 7 to 11 sts |
| 7 | Jumbo | 17 and larger | 12.75 mm and up | 6 sts and fewer |

These numbers come directly from the [Craft Yarn Council's standardized weight system](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system). Treat them as starting points, not guarantees. Always knit a gauge swatch with your actual yarn and needles before starting a fitted project.

## What do the yarn weight names mean in plain language?

Each name matches a real feel and a common use. Weight 4 (Medium) is what most beginners know as worsted. It fills most of the shelves at big-box craft stores. Weight 1 (Super Fine) is what most people call sock yarn.

Here is a quick guide:

- **Lace (0):** Very thin, like a cobweb. Good for heirloom shawls and detailed doilies. Often sold on cones.
- **Super Fine (1):** Sock yarn. Four-ply fingering weight. Great for socks, light shawls, and colorwork mittens.
- **Fine (2):** Sport weight. A little thicker than sock yarn. Good for baby items and lighter sweaters.
- **Light (3):** DK weight. Common in Europe and growing fast in US patterns. Makes sweaters with nice drape and lightweight cardigans.
- **Medium (4):** Worsted and aran weight live here. This is the most common gauge in US patterns. A good starting target is 18 to 20 stitches per 4 inches on a US 8 needle, but your swatch decides.
- **Bulky (5):** Great for quick hats, cowls, and chunky scarves. Works up fast on US 10 or 10.5 needles.
- **Super Bulky (6):** Good for big blankets, arm-knitting projects, and statement cowls. US 13 to 15 needles are common.
- **Jumbo (7):** Extreme knitting. Uses roving-weight yarn on US 19 needles and beyond.

## Why does the same yarn weight feel different across brands?

Yarn [weight categories are guidelines, not regulated measurements](/blog/demystifying-yarn-weights-for-crochet-using-the-yarn-weight-/). A worsted yarn from one brand can be noticeably thicker or thinner than a worsted from another brand. Fiber type, twist, and ply structure all change how a yarn behaves on the needle. This is true even when two labels say the same thing.

The most reliable check is wraps per inch (WPI). Wrap your yarn loosely around a ruler for one inch. Do not overlap or squish the strands. [According to the Craft Yarn Council](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system), a weight 4 worsted yarn usually measures around 9 to 12 WPI. Lace weight can reach 30 or more WPI. If you are swapping yarn in a pattern, match the WPI first and then swatch. This gives you a much better chance of matching the original gauge than trusting the label alone.

## How do I pick the right yarn weight for my project?

Match the weight to the feel the pattern needs. Do not just look at the needle size on the label. A lacy summer top needs drape. Reach for a weight 1 or 2 in a smooth plant fiber. A structured colorwork yoke sweater needs clear stitch definition. A tightly spun weight 3 or 4 in wool works better for that.

Start by asking these questions:

1. **Is this a wearable or a home item?** Wearables usually work best in lighter weights (1 to 4) for comfort and movement. Blankets and bags work fine in any weight.
2. **How much time do I have?** A weight 6 blanket on US 15 needles can be done in a weekend. A weight 1 shawl on US 2 needles might take six weeks.
3. **Does the pattern list a weight?** If yes, start there. If you want to swap yarns, compare yardage by weight, not by skein count. A 200-yard skein of worsted is not the same as a 200-yard skein of bulky.

## What is the difference between ply and weight?

Ply means how many strands are twisted together to make the yarn. Weight describes how thick the yarn is. These two things are related, but they are not the same. A 4-ply yarn in the UK tradition is close to fingering or sock weight in the US system. This confuses many knitters who are new to patterns from other countries.

In the US, "2-ply" just means two strands twisted together. But those strands can be thick or thin. The finished yarn could land anywhere from lace to worsted, depending on how thick each strand is. When you use a pattern from a UK source, check whether "4-ply" refers to the weight or just the construction. The [Craft Yarn Council's weight standards](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) use thickness and gauge to define weight, not ply count.

## How do I read a yarn label to find the weight?

Look for the skein symbol. It is a small icon that looks like a ball of yarn with a number inside. That number is the CYC weight category. Below it you will usually see a suggested needle size and a gauge note. It might say something like "18 sts = 4 inches on US 8 needles in stockinette."

If the label does not show the symbol, check the yardage compared to the weight of the skein. A 100-gram skein with 220 yards is almost certainly worsted (weight 4). The same 100 grams with 400 yards is likely fingering (weight 1). Here is a rough guide for yards per 100 grams:

- Lace: 600 to 1000 or more yards
- Fingering/Sock: 350 to 500 yards
- Sport: 250 to 350 yards
- DK: 200 to 250 yards
- Worsted: 180 to 220 yards
- Bulky: 100 to 140 yards
- Super Bulky: 60 to 100 yards

These ranges can shift depending on the fiber. Wool and cotton at the same weight will have different yardage because cotton is denser. Use this guide as a first filter, then swatch to confirm.

## Frequently asked questions

### What are the standard yarn weight categories?
There are 7 standard yarn weight categories: Lace (0), Super Fine (1), Fine (2), Light (3), Worsted (4), Bulky (5), and Super Bulky (6). The Craft Yarn Council created these categories. They help knitters and crocheters choose the right yarn for a pattern. Each weight has a suggested needle or hook size and an expected gauge. This makes it easier to swap yarns or understand pattern requirements.

### How do I know what weight my yarn is without a label?
You can find the weight by wrapping the yarn around a ruler and counting wraps per inch (WPI). Lace yarn measures around 30 to 40 WPI. Super bulky yarn measures fewer than 7 WPI. Compare your count to a standard yarn weight chart. You can also knit or crochet a small swatch using the needle size listed on similar yarns. This helps you confirm the gauge and weight category before you start your project.

### What is the difference between DK and worsted weight yarn?
DK weight is lighter and thinner than worsted weight. It makes a finer, more delicate fabric. DK yarn usually knits at 21 to 24 stitches per 4 inches on US 5 to 7 needles. Worsted knits at 16 to 20 stitches per 4 inches on US 7 to 9 needles. Worsted is one of the most popular weights for beginners because it works up quickly and is easy to handle. DK is a good choice for lightweight garments, baby items, and projects that need more drape and stitch definition.

### Can I substitute a different yarn weight in a knitting pattern?
Yes, you can swap yarn weights. But you will need to adjust your needle size and recheck your gauge to match the original pattern size. Going up a weight may make your finished item larger or stiffer. Going down a weight can make it smaller and drapier. Always knit a gauge swatch before substituting. For the best results, choose a yarn within one weight category of the original. Then check that your stitch and row counts match the pattern's stated gauge.

### What yarn weight is best for beginners?
Worsted weight yarn is widely considered the best choice for beginners. It is thick enough to see and handle easily. That makes it simple to spot individual stitches and fix mistakes. Worsted works up quickly on US size 7 to 9 needles, so new knitters see progress fast. This helps keep motivation up. It is also the most widely available weight in craft stores. Beginners have plenty of affordable options to practice with.
