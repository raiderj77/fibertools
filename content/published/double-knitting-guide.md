# What Is Double Knitting and Why Learn It

Double knitting is a colorwork method that makes a two-sided, reversible fabric. The pattern shows on both sides in opposite colors. There are no floats on the back like in stranded colorwork. That is because there is no back. Both sides are finished stockinette surfaces.

This technique is great when both sides of your project will be seen. Scarves, blankets, potholders, coasters, and cowls are the most popular double knit projects. The fabric is also twice as thick as regular stockinette. That makes it warmer and sturdier without being stiff. Double knitting has grown more popular over the past decade. Knitters love projects that look neat from every angle.

## When to Use Double Knitting

Choose double knitting when you want a truly reversible finished piece. A double knit scarf shows a clean pattern on both sides. It looks great no matter how it drapes. Double knit potholders are thick enough to handle hot dishes. You do not need extra layers. Cowls and headbands work well too, because the inside is always visible when worn.

Double knitting is also great for bold, graphic colorwork. Each stitch is either Color A or Color B. That naturally creates high-contrast designs. Geometric patterns, letters, and simple motifs all look great in double knit charts. If your design has only two colors and you want it to look finished on both sides, double knitting is the right choice.

## Step-by-Step: How to Double Knit

**Cast On with Both Colors:**

Use a two-color long-tail cast on. Hold both yarns together. Alternate one stitch in Color A and one stitch in Color B across the whole cast on. You will cast on twice the number of stitches shown in your chart. That is because each square on the chart stands for a pair. One knit stitch and one purl stitch.

**Read the Chart:**

Double knitting charts look like standard colorwork charts. Each colored square tells you what to do with the knit-purl pair. A Color A square means knit with Color A, then purl with Color B. A Color B square means knit with Color B, then purl with Color A. Work every row from right to left on the right side and left to right on the wrong side. Or work in the round to avoid purling altogether.

**Work the Stitches:**

Bring both yarns to the front. Knit the first stitch with the color shown. Bring both yarns to the front again. Purl the next stitch with the opposite color. Repeat across the row. Always move both yarns together between knit and purl positions. This helps you avoid tangling.

**Bind Off:**

Use a standard bind off. Work each knit-purl pair together as one stitch. This creates a clean, single-layer edge that matches the thickness of the fabric.

## Tips and Common Mistakes

Keep your tension even between the knit and purl stitches. Uneven tension causes puckering. One side pulls tighter than the other. Practice on a small swatch until both sides look smooth.

Never twist your yarns between the knit and purl stitches. Twisting locks the two layers together. It ruins the reversible drape of the fabric. The two layers should move on their own except at the edges.

Pick yarns with high contrast. Double knitting makes a two-color pattern. The design disappears if the colors are too similar. A light color and a dark color in the same fiber and weight works best. Worsted weight (4) yarn on US 6 or US 7 needles is the most forgiving combo for beginners.

Count your stitch pairs after every row. It is easy to knit two stitches with the same color by accident. That merges the layers. If your needle has an odd number of stitches at the end of a row, you made an error.

## Real Projects to Try

A double knit scarf is the classic starter project. Cast on 30 to 40 stitch pairs for a scarf about six inches wide. Pick a simple checkerboard or heart chart. Work until the scarf is as long as you want. The finished scarf will be warm, reversible, and impressive.

A double knit potholder is a quick weekend project. Cast on 20 to 25 stitch pairs for an eight-inch square. Use cotton yarn in two bold colors. The double thickness gives real heat protection. The reversible design means both sides look intentional.

A double knit cowl works up fast in bulky weight (5) yarn. Cast on in the round to skip purling entirely. Every stitch is a knit stitch alternating colors. A 60-stitch-pair cast on makes a comfortable circumference. Working 30 to 40 rounds makes a cowl that folds beautifully.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much yarn does double knitting use compared to regular knitting?**

Double knitting uses about twice as much yarn as single stockinette. You are making two layers of fabric at the same time. Each color takes up about half the total yardage. Plan your yarn purchase with that in mind. Always buy an extra skein of each color. That covers gauge differences and tension changes across the project.

**Can I double knit in the round?**

Yes, and many knitters prefer it. Working in the round removes purl stitches completely. Every stitch is a knit stitch, alternating colors for the front and back layers. This makes the hand movements simpler. It often gives more even tension too. Cowls, hats, and mittens are great choices for circular double knitting.

**What needle size should I use for double knitting?**

Start with the needle size listed on your yarn label. Then adjust based on your gauge swatch. Many knitters need to go up one needle size. Managing two strands tends to tighten tension. Knit a swatch at least four inches square in double knitting. That will show you whether you need to adjust.

**Is double knitting the same as knitting with double-pointed needles?**

No. Double knitting is a specific two-color, two-layer method that makes reversible fabric. Double-pointed needles are just a type of needle. They are used for small-circumference circular knitting like socks and hat crowns. The two ideas have nothing to do with each other, even though the names sound similar.
