# 4 Worsted Weight Yarn Australian Equivalent

**The short answer:** In Australia, the closest match to US worsted weight yarn is **10-ply yarn**. Both usually knit to around 18 to 22 stitches per 4 inches (10 cm) on 4.5 to 5.5 mm needles. If you see "aran" on an Australian or UK label, that sits just above worsted and is also a reasonable match. Always swatch before starting a project.

---

> **A mistake I made:** I treated yarn weight like a suggestion. It is not. The wrong weight changed the whole size and feel of my project. Check it against the [yarn weight chart](/yarn-weight-chart).

## Why does the ply system confuse everyone?

Australian yarn labels use a ply system. But the ply number no longer tells you how many strands are twisted together. Long ago, "8-ply" meant eight strands. Modern manufacturing changed that. Now the ply number is just a name for a weight category, not a literal strand count.

Many knitters think 4-ply means thin and 12-ply means thick in a neat, even line. That part is still true. But the numbers are old shorthand, not a technical measurement. The [Craft Yarn Council's standard weight system](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) uses categories 0 to 7. Worsted sits at category 4. Australian ply names match that system roughly, but not perfectly. That is why you need to cross-reference before buying.

---

## What exactly is 10-ply yarn in Australia?

10-ply is the Australian and New Zealand standard match for US worsted weight. It knits to about 18 to 22 stitches per 4 inches on 4.5 to 5.5 mm needles. That lines up with the Craft Yarn Council's gauge range for category 4 worsted.

You will find 10-ply on popular Australian brands like Cleckheaton Country, Bendigo Woollen Mills Classic 10-ply, and Malabrigo Rios. Malabrigo Rios is sold worldwide and is easy to find in Australian yarn shops. Bendigo Woollen Mills lists their Classic 10-ply as best for 4.5 to 5.0 mm needles. They show a [recommended gauge of 19 stitches per 10 cm](https://www.bendigowoollenmill.com.au/product/classic-10-ply/). That puts it right in worsted territory.

## Is aran weight the same as worsted in Australia?

Aran is close, but not the same. Aran weight usually knits to 16 to 18 stitches per 4 inches on 5.0 to 5.5 mm needles. That makes it a little heavier than standard worsted. Some patterns labeled "worsted" will work fine in aran. Others will not, especially fitted garments.

This difference matters when you swap yarn in a pattern. Say a US pattern calls for worsted at 20 stitches per 4 inches. You pick an aran weight Australian yarn instead. Your swatch might come in at 17 or 18 stitches. That gap can add several inches to a sweater before you notice. Measure your swatch. Compare it to the gauge in the pattern. Adjust your needle size or stitch count as needed. Do not skip this step just because the label says "aran" and the pattern says "worsted."

## How do I convert a US worsted pattern to Australian yarn?

Start with the gauge, not the ply number. The pattern will say something like "20 sts and 26 rows = 4 inches in stockinette on 5.0 mm needles." Find an Australian 10-ply with a similar gauge on its ball band. Swatch it on the suggested needle size. Then measure.

Here is a simple workflow:

1. Write down the pattern gauge: stitches per 4 inches and needle size.
2. Find an Australian 10-ply with a ball band gauge in the same range.
3. Cast on at least 24 stitches. More is better. Knit 4 to 5 inches in pattern.
4. Wash and block the swatch the same way you plan to care for the finished piece.
5. Measure after blocking, not before.

Skipping the wash step is the most common mistake. Wool can grow or shrink a lot after the first wash. A Merino 10-ply might measure 20 stitches per 4 inches when dry. After blocking, it might measure 18 stitches per 4 inches. That two-stitch difference across a 200-stitch sweater body adds about 4 inches of extra width.

## What about crochet projects using worsted weight?

The same 10-ply match applies in crochet. US worsted weight is common for crochet blankets, dishcloths, bags, and garments. Australian 10-ply will behave the same way with your hook.

For crochet, US worsted usually works on a 5.0 to 5.5 mm hook. A basic single crochet (sc) swatch in 10-ply on a 5.0 mm hook should give you about 14 to 16 sc per 4 inches. A double crochet (dc) swatch will usually give you 13 to 14 dc per 4 inches. Those numbers change based on yarn fiber and your personal tension. Measure your own swatch. Do not use someone else's gauge and assume it will match yours.

## Are there Australian yarn brands that label in both systems?

Some do, but not all. Bendigo Woollen Mills labels their yarns by ply and includes needle size suggestions. Cleckheaton uses ply labels and lists gauge on the ball band. Brands sold worldwide, like Malabrigo, Cascade 220, and Lion Brand, use the Craft Yarn Council weight symbols. You will know them by the small skein icon with a number inside.

When you shop online from an Australian retailer and the listing only says "10-ply," check the needle size and gauge on the product page. If it says 4.5 to 5.5 mm needles and 18 to 22 stitches per 10 cm, you have found your worsted match. If the gauge is tighter or looser than that range, the yarn is in a different weight category. It does not matter what the label calls it.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the Australian equivalent of worsted weight yarn?
The closest Australian match for worsted weight yarn is 10-ply yarn. Australian yarn weights use a ply-based naming system. Ten-ply sits in the same thickness range as US worsted weight. It typically knits to a gauge of 18 to 20 stitches per 10 cm on 5 to 6 mm needles. When swapping yarn in a pattern, always check the recommended gauge. Do not rely only on the ply label. Thickness can vary between brands.

### Is Australian 10-ply the same as worsted weight yarn?
Australian 10-ply is generally considered equal to US worsted weight, but they are not identical. Both fall in a similar thickness range. But "worsted" is a weight category name, while "10-ply" once referred to the number of plied strands. Modern Australian 10-ply yarns are standardized by thickness, not actual ply count. Swatching and matching gauge is still the most reliable way to confirm they will work together.

### What needle size do I use for worsted weight yarn in Australia?
For worsted weight yarn, Australian knitters typically use 5 mm to 6 mm needles. This matches the standard suggestion for 10-ply yarn, which is the local equivalent. Needle size can change based on the yarn's thickness, your personal tension, and the pattern. Always knit a gauge swatch before starting. This makes sure your needle choice gives you the right stitch and row count.

### Can I use Australian 8-ply instead of worsted weight yarn?
Australian 8-ply is not a direct swap for worsted weight yarn. Eight-ply matches DK (double knit) weight, which is noticeably thinner than worsted. Using 8-ply in a worsted weight pattern will give you a smaller, lighter fabric than the pattern intends. If you need to substitute, 10-ply is the right Australian match for worsted weight projects. Still swatch to make sure your gauge matches the pattern.

### How do I convert a US worsted weight pattern to Australian yarn weights?
To convert a US worsted weight pattern, choose an Australian 10-ply yarn as your substitute. Check that the yarn's recommended gauge matches the pattern gauge. That is usually around 18 to 20 stitches per 10 cm. Adjust your needle size as needed to get the right tension. Most Australian yarn labels include gauge information. That makes it easy to find a matching 10-ply yarn at your local craft store or online.
