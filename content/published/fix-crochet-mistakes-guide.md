# How to Fix Common Crochet Mistakes

Every crocheter makes mistakes. The difference between a frustrated beginner and a confident maker is not avoiding errors. It is knowing how to spot them early and fix them fast.

Crochet has one advantage over knitting when things go wrong. You can frog (rip out) and re-crochet without worrying about dropped stitches falling apart. There is always just one live loop on your hook. That makes fixing mistakes less scary. But you still need to know what went wrong so it does not happen again.

Here are the most common crochet mistakes, how to find them, and how to fix each one.

## How Do You Fix a Wrong Stitch Count?

This is the number one crochet mistake. You started with 100 stitches, and now you have 98. Or 103. The fabric is slowly getting wider or narrower, and you did not notice for 10 rows.

**Why it happens:**
- You worked 2 stitches into the same stitch by accident. This adds to your count.
- You skipped a stitch, especially the last stitch in a row. This lowers your count.
- You miscounted the turning chain as a stitch, or forgot to count it when the pattern says to.
- You lost track of the first stitch after the turning chain.

**How to fix it:**
1. Count your stitches right now. Use the [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) and count every stitch in the current row.
2. Compare to your target count. If you are off by 1 to 2 stitches and it happened recently (last 2 to 3 rows), frog back to the error row and re-crochet.
3. If the error happened 10 or more rows ago and the piece still looks straight, you can quietly add or remove a stitch in the next row. Work a decrease (sc2tog) to lose a stitch. Or work 2 stitches into 1 stitch to add a stitch. Place it near the center of the row where it is hardest to see.

**How to prevent it:** Count after every single row. Set up the [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) and tap it after counting each row. Those 5 seconds of counting can save hours of frogging.

## How Do You Fix Uneven Edges?

Your blanket edges look wavy. Some rows stick out and others pull in. The sides are not straight, and the fabric will not lie flat.

**Why it happens:**
- Your turning chain height is not the same each time. Sometimes it is too tight and sometimes too loose.
- You missed the first stitch after the turn, or you worked into the turning chain by accident.
- Your tension changes between the edge and the center of the row.

**How to fix it:**
- If the edges are only a little uneven, a border can cover it. One round of single crochet around the whole piece evens out mild waviness.
- If the edges are very uneven (more than 1/4 inch of variation), you will need to frog and re-crochet. Focus on keeping your edge technique steady.

**How to prevent it:**
- Use the same number of chain stitches for every turn. Single crochet uses 1 chain, half double crochet uses 2 chains, and double crochet uses 3 chains. Do not guess.
- Decide once whether your turning chain counts as the first stitch, then stick with it. Most modern patterns tell you which to do.
- Place a marker in the first stitch of every row. That way you always know where to work the last stitch of the return row.

## How Do You Fix Twisted or Skewed Fabric?

Your crochet rectangle is not actually rectangular. It leans to one side like a parallelogram. The corners should be 90 degrees, but they are closer to 80 and 100 degrees.

**Why it happens:** Crochet stitches naturally lean a little to one side because of how you insert the hook. Over many rows, this lean builds up into a visible skew. Single crochet is the most likely stitch to skew.

**How to fix it:**
- **Mild skew:** Aggressive blocking can fix up to 10 to 15 degrees of lean. Soak the piece, pin it to shape at perfect right angles, and let it dry all the way.
- **Moderate skew:** Work the next rows in alternating directions. Instead of turning the work at the end of each row, fasten off and rejoin at the same edge. This fights the lean.
- **Severe skew:** Switch to a stitch that does not skew, like half double crochet or extended single crochet. Single crochet worked in the back loop only also helps reduce skew.

## How Do You Fix Tension Problems?

Your fabric is too tight (stiff and will not drape) or too loose (floppy and see-through). Or your tension changes throughout the piece, making visible stripes of tight and loose sections.

**Why it happens:**
- You are crocheting too tightly by gripping the hook and yarn too hard.
- You are crocheting too loosely by not pulling through yarn in a steady way.
- Your tension changes when you get tired, stressed, or sit in a different position.
- Different hook grip styles can produce different tensions.

**How to fix it:**
- **Too tight:** Go up 1 to 2 hook sizes. If the pattern says H/8 (5.0mm), try I/9 (5.5mm) or J/10 (6.0mm). Your gauge will loosen and the fabric will drape better.
- **Too loose:** Go down 1 to 2 hook sizes. Switch from I/9 to H/8 or G/6.
- **Inconsistent tension:** Crochet in shorter sessions of 30 to 45 minutes so tiredness does not change your tension. Check your gauge every 4 to 6 inches by counting stitches per inch.

Use the [Gauge Calculator](/gauge-calculator) to check whether your tension matches what the pattern needs. If the pattern calls for 14 single crochets per 4 inches and you are getting 16, your fabric is too tight. The calculator shows how that difference changes your finished size.

## How Do You Fix the Wrong Stitch Height?

You are working double crochet but your stitches look too short. Or your half double crochet rows are not all the same height.

**Why it happens:**
- You did not pull the loop up tall enough before finishing the stitch.
- You yanked the yarn after each stitch, which tightens the loop.
- You did the wrong number of yarn overs. A double crochet needs 2 pull-throughs, not 1.

**How to fix it:** Practice the motion slowly. Yarn over, insert the hook, yarn over and pull up a loop to the height of the turning chain. Then finish the pull-throughs without tightening. The loop you pull up sets the stitch height. Pull it up tall and the stitch will be tall.

## How Do You Frog and Recover?

Frogging (ripping out work) is the big fix. In crochet, it is fast and clean.

**How to frog safely:**
1. Remove your hook from the live loop.
2. Pull the yarn to unravel. It comes out one stitch at a time.
3. Frog until you are one row past the mistake.
4. Slow down. For the last row of frogging, pull out one stitch at a time. Insert your hook into the last good stitch before it comes undone.
5. Count your stitches to make sure you are back at the right count.

**Tips for clean frogging:**
- Go slowly for the last few stitches so you do not pull past your target row.
- If the yarn gets tangled, do not force it. Follow the strand and untangle it before pulling.
- Check your stitch count right after you re-insert the hook.
- Mark the correct row with a locking stitch marker before frogging so you know when to stop.

## What Do Real Mistake Fixes Look Like?

**The growing blanket.** A crocheter noticed her blanket was 2 inches wider on row 40 than at the foundation chain. She had been accidentally working 2 stitches into the last stitch of every other row. This added about 20 stitches over 40 rows. She frogged back to row 30, where the width difference was still small. She placed a marker at the last stitch of every row going forward and finished with straight edges.

**The skewed dishcloth.** A beginner's single crochet dishcloth leaned 15 degrees to the right. Instead of frogging, she blocked it hard. She soaked it for 20 minutes, pinned it to a blocking mat at perfect right angles, and let it dry for 24 hours. The blocking fixed the lean completely. For her next dishcloth, she switched to half double crochet, which did not skew.

**The tension stripe sweater.** A crocheter noticed horizontal bands of tighter and looser fabric on a sweater panel. The tight rows matched the evenings when she was tired. She could not fix it without frogging the whole panel. So she switched to crocheting only in the morning and took breaks every 30 minutes. The second panel had no visible tension bands.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I frog a mistake or leave it?

If the mistake affects the stitch count, fabric shape, or final fit, frog it. A missed decrease in a sweater means the garment will not fit. If the mistake is small and only affects how it looks (a slightly uneven stitch or a minor color difference), leave it. Nobody except you will notice one wonky stitch in a 50,000-stitch blanket.

### How do I prevent losing stitch count?

Count after every row. Place a locking stitch marker in the first stitch of each row so you always know where the row starts and ends. Use the Stitch Counter tool to track your count. Those 5 seconds of prevention save hours of frogging. The most common count error is missing the last stitch of the row.

### Why does my crochet curl at the edges?

Curling happens when the turning chain is too tight, when you work into the wrong loop, or when your tension is too tight for the stitch. Try going up one hook size. If it keeps curling, add a border of single crochet around the finished piece. This steadies the edges and pulls them flat.

### Can I fix a mistake without frogging?

For stitch count errors within the last 2 to 3 rows, yes. Work an invisible increase (2 stitches into 1) or decrease (sc2tog) in the center of the next row. For bigger errors like the wrong stitch type or pattern mistakes, frogging is usually the only clean fix. The further back the error is, the more likely you will need to frog.

## Catch Mistakes Early, Fix Them Fast

Every mistake is fixable. The key is catching it early, before 20 more rows lock it in. Count every row, use markers, check your edges, and do not be afraid to frog. Three minutes of ripping out beats three hours of trying to hide a mistake.
