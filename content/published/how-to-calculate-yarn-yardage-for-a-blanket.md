## Standard Blanket Sizes for Knitting and Crochet

Blanket sizes aren't one-size-fits-all, and picking the right dimensions prevents both wasted yarn and a blanket that doesn't fit the bed. These are the standard sizes used across both knitting and crochet patterns:

- **Lovey/Security Blanket**: 12" x 12", a small comfort square for infants, often paired with a stuffed animal
- **Baby Blanket**: 30" x 36", fits a crib but works best as a tummy-time or stroller blanket
- **Crib Blanket**: 36" x 52", full crib coverage, the most common baby gift size
- **Throw Blanket**: 50" x 60", the classic couch blanket and the most popular project size
- **Twin Bed**: 66" x 90", covers a twin mattress with a modest drape on the sides
- **Full/Queen Bed**: 80" x 90", fits full and queen mattresses with side drape
- **King Bed**: 108" x 90", a serious commitment in both time and yarn

If you want side drape (the blanket hanging over the edges of the bed), add 10-12 inches to the width on each side. A 60" wide mattress with drape becomes 80-84" wide.

## How to Calculate Yarn Yardage Step by Step

Reference tables get you close, but your personal gauge, stitch pattern, and tension make every project unique. Here's how to calculate your exact yardage using a gauge swatch, the most reliable method.

### What You'll Need
- Your chosen yarn and needles/hook
- A ruler or tape measure
- A kitchen scale (optional but helpful)
- A calculator

### The Formula

**Total Yardage = (Blanket Area / Swatch Area) x Swatch Yardage x 1.15**

The 1.15 multiplier adds a 15% buffer for bind-offs, cast-ons, weaving in ends, and the inevitable tension variations across a large project.

### Worked Example: Throw Blanket in Worsted Weight

Let's walk through a real calculation. Say you're knitting a 50" x 60" throw with worsted weight yarn in stockinette stitch.

**Step 1: Knit a gauge swatch.**
Cast on enough stitches for a 6" x 6" swatch (roughly 24 stitches at standard worsted gauge). Knit it in your chosen stitch pattern. Wash and block it, yarn relaxes after washing, and skipping this step throws off your numbers.

**Step 2: Measure your swatch.**
After blocking, measure the exact dimensions. Let's say yours came out to 5.5" x 5.5" = 30.25 square inches.

**Step 3: Measure the yarn you used.**
Unravel the swatch and measure the yarn length. Or, if you weighed the yarn before and after, calculate yardage from the label's yards-per-skein and weight. Let's say you used 18 yards.

**Step 4: Calculate blanket area.**
50" x 60" = 3,000 square inches.

**Step 5: Do the math.**
(3,000 / 30.25) x 18 x 1.15 = **2,054 yards**

**Step 6: Convert to skeins.**
If your yarn has 220 yards per skein: 2,054 / 220 = 9.3 skeins. Round up to **10 skeins**.

That's it. Six steps, and you've got a precise estimate tailored to your exact yarn, gauge, and stitch pattern.

## The Kitchen Scale Shortcut (How Experienced Crafters Do It)

Here's a method you won't find in most articles, but it's the one seasoned crafters swear by in every Reddit thread and knitting circle: weigh your swatch instead of measuring the yarn.

**Step 1:** Knit or crochet your gauge swatch (at least 6" x 6").

**Step 2:** Weigh the swatch on a kitchen scale in grams. Let's say it weighs 12 grams.

**Step 3:** Calculate your blanket's area relative to the swatch.
- Swatch: 36 square inches
- Blanket (50" x 60"): 3,000 square inches
- Ratio: 3,000 / 36 = 83.3

**Step 4:** Multiply swatch weight by the ratio.
- 12g x 83.3 = 1,000g

**Step 5:** Add 15% buffer.
- 1,000g x 1.15 = 1,150g

**Step 6:** Convert to skeins.
- If each skein weighs 100g: 1,150 / 100 = 11.5 skeins. Buy 12.

This method is faster, works regardless of yarn weight, and avoids the tedious step of unraveling your swatch. The only tool you need beyond your craft supplies is a kitchen scale, and a basic one costs less than a single skein of hand-dyed yarn.

## 5 Factors That Change How Much Yarn You Need

"It depends" is the honest answer, but here's exactly what it depends on, with numbers, not hand-waving.

### 1. Yarn Weight

This is the biggest single factor. A baby blanket in fingering weight (CYC 1) needs roughly 1,600 yards for knitting, while the same blanket in super bulky (CYC 6) needs about 700 yards. Thinner yarn means more yards per square inch of fabric. Check the [Craft Yarn Council's weight system](https://www. craftyarncouncil. com/standards/yarn-weight-system) if you're unsure which weight you're working with.

### 2. Stitch Type

Your stitch pattern can swing yarn consumption by 25-40%. Dense stitches (single crochet, seed stitch) eat yarn. Open, airy stitches (double crochet, lace patterns) stretch it further. Cables and bobbles are the biggest yarn hogs; a cabled blanket can use 35% more yarn than the same size in plain stockinette.

### 3. Your Tension and Gauge

Tight knitters and crocheters use more yarn per square inch because they're packing stitches closer together. Loose crafters use less yarn but get a drapier, more open fabric. This is why swatching matters; your tension is uniquely yours, and no table can predict it.

### 4. Knitting vs. Crochet

Crochet uses approximately 30% more yarn than knitting for the same project size. This is because crochet stitches are structurally bulkier, each stitch wraps the yarn more times around the hook than a knitting stitch wraps around the needle. If you're converting a knitting pattern to crochet (or vice versa), adjust your yardage estimate accordingly.

### 5. Color Changes and Colorwork

Multi-color blankets need more total yarn than solid-color ones. Every color change leaves a tail that gets woven in, and stranded colorwork (like Fair Isle) carries unused colors behind the fabric, consuming yarn that doesn't show on the front. Budget an extra 10-15% for colorwork projects beyond the standard buffer.

## Which Stitches Use the Most (and Least) Yarn?

If you haven't committed to a stitch pattern yet, this table can help you decide. These are approximate yarn usage comparisons relative to basic stockinette (knitting) or single crochet (crochet) as the baseline.

### Knitting Stitches

| Stitch Pattern | Yarn Usage vs. Stockinette |
|---|---|
| Stockinette / Stocking Stitch | Baseline (100%) |
| Garter Stitch | +5% |
| Seed / Moss Stitch | +10% |
| Ribbing (1x1 or 2x2) | +10-15% |
| Cables | +25-35% |
| Brioche | +40-50% |
| Lace (with yarn-overs) | -10-15% |

### Crochet Stitches

| Stitch Pattern | Yarn Usage vs. Single Crochet |
|---|---|
| Single Crochet (sc) | Baseline (100%) |
| Half Double Crochet (hdc) | -10% |
| Double Crochet (dc) | -20-25% |
| Treble Crochet (tr) | -30% |
| Granny Square | -15-20% |
| Bobble / Popcorn Stitch | +25-35% |
| C2C (Corner to Corner) | -15% |

The pattern here is straightforward: taller crochet stitches use less yarn per square inch because they create a more open fabric. In knitting, textured stitches that pull yarn to the front (cables, brioche) use the most.

## How to Never Run Out of Yarn Mid-Blanket

Running out of yarn isn't just inconvenient, it can mean your blanket stays a WIP forever if you can't find a matching dye lot. Here are the rules experienced crafters live by.

**Buy from the same dye lot.** Every skein from a single dye lot was dyed in the same bath, so the color is identical. Skeins from different dye lots can look noticeably different side by side, even if the colorway name is the same. Check the dye lot number on the label and buy all your skeins at once.

**Always buy one extra skein.** Yarn stores accept returns on unused skeins (check the store's policy). The cost of one extra skein is cheap insurance against the agony of running out. Most experienced crafters call this the "insurance skein" for good reason.

**Weigh your remaining yarn as you go.** Halfway through your blanket, weigh the yarn you've used and the yarn you have left. If you've used more than half your total weight before reaching the halfway point of your blanket, you need more yarn, and it's better to discover this now than at row 247 of 250.

**Track your yardage by section.** If you're working a blanket in strips, panels, or blocks, note the yardage for the first completed section. Multiply by the total number of sections for a project-specific estimate that's more accurate than any table.

**Keep the yarn label.** Even after you start knitting, save at least one label with the brand, colorway, weight, and dye lot number. If you need more yarn later, this is the information you'll need to find a match.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does crochet use more yarn than knitting?
Yes. Crochet uses approximately 30% more yarn than knitting for an identical blanket size, because crochet stitches wrap yarn around the hook more times per stitch. A knitted throw needing 2,000 yards would require roughly 2,600 yards in crochet.

### How do I calculate yarn for a multi-color blanket?
Calculate total yardage for the full blanket first, then divide proportionally by how much of the blanket each color covers. Add 15-20% buffer per color instead of the usual 10-15%, since color changes create extra tails to weave in.

### What if I can't find the same dye lot?
Alternate rows from the old and new dye lots for 6-8 rows where they meet. This gradual transition makes the color shift invisible. Some crafters call this technique "planned pooling" across dye lots.

### How many skeins do I need for a throw blanket?
For a 50" x 60" throw in worsted weight (CYC 4) with 220 yards per skein: about 10 skeins for knitting or 13 skeins for crochet. Always check your specific skein's yardage, it varies by brand from 180 to 260 yards per skein.

### Can I use a kitchen scale to estimate yarn needs?
Absolutely, and many experienced crafters prefer this method. Weigh your gauge swatch, calculate the area ratio between your swatch and blanket, multiply, and add 15%. It's faster than measuring yarn length and just as accurate.
