# How Many Cross Stitches Per Skein?

A skein of DMC floss (8.7 yards, 6-strand) covers about 700 to 800 full cross stitches on 14-count Aida using two strands. That number changes fast with fabric count, coverage type, and thread thickness. Use it as a starting point, not a promise, and always buy extra before you start.

## Why Do People Run Out of Floss Mid-Project?

This happens because stitchers buy the exact amount a chart calls for, with nothing extra. Charts often estimate skein counts using perfect, waste-free stitching, which almost nobody actually does.

Every time you start a new thread, you leave a tail. Every time you end one, you leave another tail. Those tails don't stitch anything. They just get buried under future stitches. On a small project this waste might only cost you a few inches per skein. On a large piece with hundreds of color changes, it adds up to a surprising amount of lost floss.

[Fabric count matters too.](/blog/cross-stitch-fabric-count/) A pattern designed for 14-count Aida uses smaller stitches than the same pattern on 11-count Aida. Smaller stitches use less floss per stitch, but you need more stitches to cover the same physical area. The math doesn't always work out the way you'd guess.

**How to avoid it:** Buy one extra skein for any color that covers more than 100 stitches in a pattern. For your most-used colors, like background colors, buy two extra. Floss dye lots can vary, so buying extra from the same batch at the start beats trying to match dye lots later.

## Why Does the Same Chart Use Different Amounts of Floss for Different People?

Two stitchers can use the same chart and end up with very different floss needs. This comes down to stitch tension, thread management, and how much waste each person creates while working.

Someone who stitches with a loose, relaxed tension pulls more floss through the fabric with each stitch. Someone with a tight tension uses less. Neither is wrong, but the difference shows up in your floss usage over a full project.

Thread management matters just as much. If you cut long lengths of floss, say 24 inches or more, you'll get more knots, more fraying, and more wasted thread from constant re-threading. Shorter lengths, around 15 to 18 inches, waste less floss overall because you get cleaner pulls with less friction damage.

The stitching method also plays a role. The loop method, where you fold a single strand in half and thread the loop through your needle, saves floss because you skip the knot and don't need a separate anchor stitch. But it only works for even numbers of strands.

**How to avoid it:** Track your own floss usage on a small test area before committing to skein counts for a big project. Stitch a 2-inch by 2-inch square of full coverage on your fabric, count how much floss you used, and multiply that out to your project size. This gives you a personal ratio instead of relying on a generic chart estimate.

## Why Do Cross Stitch Kits Sometimes Include Too Little Floss?

Kit makers often calculate floss amounts based on average stitch counts without accounting for waste, mistakes, or frogging (ripping out stitches). This means the included floss covers the chart exactly, with zero room for error.

Kits are built to a price point. Extra floss costs money, and kit companies want their kits priced competitively. So they include close to the exact amount needed, sometimes even less if they're estimating conservatively to save on materials.

This becomes a real problem when you make a mistake. Frogging out stitches doesn't give you the floss back in usable form. Once floss goes through fabric holes, it gets worn and fuzzy. You usually can't reuse it for visible stitching, especially not on the front of your work.

Beginners run into this constantly. A new stitcher might frog out a whole section three or four times while learning tension and technique. That eats through floss that was only ever budgeted for a single clean pass.

**How to avoid it:** If you're new to cross stitch, buy backup floss in your main colors before you start a kit, even if the kit says it includes enough. Check the kit's floss list against the chart's color key. If a color covers a large area of the design, like a solid background, assume you'll want at least one extra skein in that shade.

## Why Do People Miscalculate Floss Needs for Full Coverage vs. Partial Stitches?

Full cross stitches use twice the floss of half stitches for the same space, and quarter stitches use even less per unit of coverage. Mixing these stitch types in one chart means a flat "stitches per skein" number won't tell the whole story.

A lot of cross stitch charts include a mix of stitch types. Full crosses form the bulk of most designs. But backstitching outlines, French knots, quarter stitches for curves, and three-quarter stitches for detail work all pull from your floss supply differently.

Backstitch is often the silent floss killer. It's usually stitched with fewer strands, maybe one or two instead of the two used for cross stitch, but it covers long, unbroken lines. A single backstitched outline around a large motif can use as much floss as a decent patch of cross stitch, and people forget to budget for it separately.

French knots also cost more floss per stitch than you'd expect, because you wrap the thread around the needle multiple times before pulling it through. A design with 50 French knots might use up nearly as much floss as 200 regular cross stitches, depending on how many wraps each knot uses.

**How to avoid it:** Break your floss estimate into categories. Count your full cross stitches, your backstitch inches, and your specialty stitches (French knots, lazy daisies, etc.) separately. Estimate floss for each category on its own, then add them together. This gives a far more accurate total than treating the whole design as one uniform stitch count.

## Why Do Stitchers Underestimate Floss Needs When Changing Fabric Count?

Switching fabric count changes your stitch size, which changes how much floss each stitch uses, and people often assume the relationship is a simple straight-line scale. It isn't.

Move from 14-count to 18-count Aida, and your stitches get smaller. Smaller stitches should use less floss per stitch. But you also need more stitches to cover the same physical area, since the holes are packed closer together. These two effects work against each other, and the final floss total doesn't move in a way you can guess without doing the math.

Evenweave and linen fabrics complicate this further. Many stitchers work these fabrics "over two," meaning each cross stitch spans two threads instead of one. This effectively doubles your stitch size compared to working over one thread, which changes your floss math again.

People also forget that changing fabric count changes your finished size, not just your floss usage. A design that finishes at 8 inches by 10 inches on 14-count might finish at 6 inches by 7.5 inches on 18-count. Smaller finished size can mean less total floss even though individual stitches behave differently. Always measure as you go rather than assuming a size or floss total based on the original chart's suggested fabric.

**How to avoid it:** Any time you change fabric count from what the pattern suggests, recalculate your floss needs from scratch. Don't try to scale the original floss estimate up or down using a simple ratio. Stitch a small test swatch on your actual fabric choice and measure your real usage before buying floss for the whole project.

## Why Do People Forget to Account for Strand Count?

Cross stitch floss usage scales directly with how many strands you use, and people sometimes read a "skein covers X stitches" estimate without checking what strand count that estimate assumes. A number based on two strands means something very different if you're stitching with three.

DMC floss comes as six loosely twisted strands in one thread. Most 14-count Aida projects use two strands for cross stitch and one for backstitch. But patterns for lower counts, like 11-count Aida, often call for three strands to get good coverage. Higher counts, like 22-count or 25-count linen, might only need one strand.

If a supply estimate or a chart's floss requirement doesn't specify strand count, that number is close to useless. A skein that covers 750 stitches at two strands might only cover 500 stitches at three strands, or 1,500 stitches at one strand. The math is proportional but people forget to apply it.

This trips up stitchers who switch a pattern's suggested fabric count without adjusting strand count. If you move from 14-count to 11-count and keep using two strands out of habit, your coverage will look thin and gappy. Fixing that later usually means switching to three strands partway through, which can create a visible texture difference in the finished piece.

**How to avoid it:** Always check what strand count any floss estimate assumes before applying it to your project. If you change fabric count, decide your new strand count first, then recalculate floss needs based on that. The Craft Yarn Council and most major floss brands publish general strand-to-fabric-count guidelines you can use as a starting reference, but always confirm with a small test swatch since personal tension changes the outcome.

## Frequently asked questions

### How many cross stitches can you get from one skein of embroidery floss?
A single skein of DMC embroidery floss (8.7 yards) typically yields around 500 to 1,000 full cross stitches, depending on fabric count and thread coverage. On 14-count Aida using two strands, expect roughly 800 stitches per skein. Higher fabric counts like 18 or 22 require less floss per stitch, stretching the skein further, while lower counts like 11-count use more floss per stitch. Stitch coverage style (full vs. half stitches) and how much thread is wasted in tails and knots also affect the final count.

### Does fabric count change how many stitches I get per skein?
Yes, fabric count has a major impact on stitches per skein. Higher counts (16, 18, 22) mean smaller stitches that use less floss each, so a single skein can cover 900–1,200 stitches. Lower counts (11 or 14) require larger stitches with more floss per stitch, reducing yield to 500–800 stitches. This is why the same skein color can seem to "run out" much faster on Aida 11 than on linen 22. Always test on a scrap piece of your specific fabric to estimate skein needs accurately for a project.

### How many strands of floss should I use, and does it affect skein yield?
The number of strands directly affects yield since more strands mean more floss consumed per stitch. Standard cross stitch on 14-count Aida uses two strands, giving roughly 800 stitches per skein. Using three or four strands for texture or blending increases floss consumption significantly, potentially cutting your yield to 400–500 stitches per skein. Conversely, single-strand delicate work on high-count fabric can stretch a skein past 1,500 stitches. Always match strand count to your pattern's recommendation before estimating how many skeins to buy.

### How do I calculate how many skeins I need for a project?
Start by counting the total stitches of a single color in your pattern, then divide by the estimated stitches-per-skein for your fabric count and strand number (roughly 800 for 14-count with two strands). Add 10–15% extra for waste, mistakes, and thread tails. For large background-fill colors, buy an extra skein upfront since dye lots can vary. Tools like fibertools.app's stitch calculators can automate this by factoring in your specific pattern, fabric, and thread choices for a more precise skein estimate.

### Why did I run out of floss before finishing my project?
Running out early usually means your actual stitch tension, fabric count, or strand thickness differed from the pattern's assumptions. Overstitching, frequent restarting, longer thread tails, or using a coarser weave than expected all increase floss consumption. Backstitching and French knots also draw from the same skein as cross stitches, depleting it faster than a straightforward stitch count would suggest. To avoid running short, buy one extra skein per color for large projects and track usage as you stitch rather than estimating only at the start.
