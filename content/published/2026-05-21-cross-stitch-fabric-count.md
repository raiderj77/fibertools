# Cross Stitch Fabric Count: What It Means and How to Choose the Right One

**Cross stitch fabric count is the number of threads or holes per inch in your fabric.** A 14-count Aida has 14 squares per inch. Each cross stitch covers one square. Higher count means smaller stitches and finer detail. Lower count means bigger stitches and faster work. That one number controls the finished size of every design you stitch.

---

## What exactly does "fabric count" mean?

Fabric count tells you how many stitchable units fit into one inch. On Aida cloth, you count the woven blocks. On evenweave or linen, you count individual threads. With evenweave and linen, you usually stitch over two threads at a time.

If you stitch a 100-stitch-wide design on 14-count Aida, the finished width is about 7.1 inches (100 divided by 14). Move that same design to 18-count and the finished width drops to about 5.6 inches. Same pattern, completely different result on the fabric. This is why fabric count is the first thing to figure out before you buy a single skein of floss.

---

## What are the most common fabric counts and when do I use each one?

The most common counts are 11, 14, 16, 18, 22, 25, and 28. Most beginners start with 14-count Aida. More experienced stitchers often move to 18 or 28-count for detailed portraits or miniatures.

Here is a simple breakdown:

- **11-count:** Large, bold stitches. Great for kids' projects, quick gifts, or anyone with vision difficulties. Use 6 strands of floss for full coverage.
- **14-count:** The most popular choice. Most patterns are sized for 14-count. Use 2 to 3 strands of floss.
- **16-count:** A middle ground between 14 and 18. Less common, but useful when a 14-count design comes out too large for your frame.
- **18-count:** Noticeably finer. Portraits and complex shading look cleaner here. Use 2 strands, sometimes 1 for backstitching.
- **22 to 28-count evenweave or linen:** Good for fine detail, heirloom pieces, and miniatures. Most stitchers work over two threads on these fabrics. That gives an effective stitch count of 14 per inch on 28-count.

[DMC's fabric guide](https://www.dmc.com/us/cross-stitch-fabric/) confirms that 14-count Aida with 2 strands of floss is the most widely recommended combination for beginners.

---

## How do I calculate finished design size from fabric count?

Divide the stitch count of your design by the fabric count. That gives you inches. Do it for both width and height. Then add at least 3 inches on each side for framing allowance.

**Example:** A design that is 140 stitches wide by 196 stitches tall on 14-count Aida:

- Width: 140 divided by 14 = 10 inches
- Height: 196 divided by 14 = 14 inches
- Fabric to buy: at minimum 16 x 20 inches (adding 3 inches per side)

Move that same design to 18-count:

- Width: 140 divided by 18 = 7.8 inches
- Height: 196 divided by 18 = 10.9 inches
- Fabric to buy: at minimum 13.8 x 16.9 inches

The [Stitch Count Calculator at HoopLove](https://www.hooplove.com.au/pages/stitch-count-calculator) is a fast tool if you want to skip the math.

## What is the difference between Aida and evenweave counts?

Aida is woven in a basket-weave pattern. That creates visible squares. Each square equals one stitch. Evenweave and linen are woven in individual threads. You almost always stitch over two threads at a time with these fabrics.

This matters when you compare counts. A 28-count evenweave stitched over two threads produces the same stitch size as 14-count Aida. A 32-count linen stitched over two threads is equal to 16-count Aida. If a pattern calls for 14-count Aida and you want to use linen instead, choose 28-count linen and stitch over two threads. [Needle 'n Thread's evenweave guide](https://www.needlenthread.com/2007/09/evenweave-fabrics-for-embroidery-and.html) explains the thread-counting mechanics clearly if you want to learn more.

## Does fabric count affect [how many strands of floss I use](/blog/how-many-cross-stitches-per-skein/)?

Yes, fabric count directly affects how many strands fit cleanly through the holes. Higher counts need fewer strands. So 14-count usually takes 2 to 3 strands, while 11-count handles 6. Too many strands on fine fabric crowds the holes. It distorts the weave and makes your stitches look lumpy instead of crisp.

The higher the count, the fewer strands you need for clean coverage without crowding the holes.

Here are general guidelines most stitchers follow:

- 11-count: 6 strands (or 3 for a lighter look)
- 14-count: 2 to 3 strands
- 16-count: 2 strands
- 18-count: 1 to 2 strands
- 25-count and above stitched over two: 1 strand for fine detail, 2 for solid areas

Too many strands on high-count fabric makes your stitches look lumpy. It can also distort the weave. Too few strands on low-count fabric lets the fabric show through and looks sparse. Neither is a disaster. But matching strand count to fabric count is a small change that makes a finished piece look much better.

---

## Can I use any fabric count for any pattern?

Technically yes, but some combinations work better than others. A simple geometric sampler scales up or down nicely. A 50-color portrait with tiny 1-stitch details gets muddy on 11-count. Those single stitches become enormous blobs.

If you are adapting a pattern to a different count than listed, check that the finest details still look clear at the new stitch size. A single-stitch highlight in an eye or a one-stitch-wide border line needs to still be visible. Print the pattern at the new physical size and look at it from arm's length. If the detail disappears, go up in count.

---

## What is plastic canvas count and how does it compare?

Plastic canvas is sold by mesh count, just like fabric. The most common is 7-count. That is why plastic canvas projects look chunky and graphic. You can also find 10-count and 14-count plastic canvas for finer work.

The math is the same: [stitch count divided by mesh count = finished inches](/blog/knitting-yarn-weights-chart/). The main difference is that plastic canvas does not fray. It holds its shape without a hoop. It is used mostly for three-dimensional projects like boxes, ornaments, and coasters rather than framed pieces.

---

Fabric count is one of the few numbers in cross stitch that you cannot guess at and fix later. Get it right before you cut the fabric, and the rest of the math takes care of itself.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is fabric count in cross stitch?
Fabric count is the number of threads or holes per inch in cross stitch fabric. A higher count means more threads packed into each inch. That gives you smaller, finer stitches. Common counts include 14-count Aida, 18-count Aida, and 28-count evenweave. The count you choose directly affects the finished size of your project and how much detail you can show in your design.

### What is the most popular cross stitch fabric count for beginners?
14-count Aida is the most recommended choice for beginners. Its larger holes and clear grid make it easy to see where to place each stitch. The hole size works well with standard embroidery floss and a size 24 tapestry needle. This makes learning more comfortable and forgiving for people just starting out in cross stitch.

### How does fabric count affect the finished size of a cross stitch project?
Fabric count determines how large or small your finished design will be. Divide the number of stitches in your pattern by the fabric count to get the finished size in inches. For example, a 140-stitch-wide design on 14-count fabric produces a 10-inch-wide piece. The same design on 28-count fabric would measure only 5 inches wide, giving a much more detailed, miniature result.

### What is the difference between Aida and evenweave fabric counts?
Aida fabric is woven in distinct blocks. That makes each stitch placement easy to see. Evenweave fabrics like linen and Jobelan have individual threads that you count by hand. When using evenweave, stitchers usually work over two threads. So a 28-count evenweave produces the same stitch size as 14-count Aida. Evenweave has a smoother, more elegant finish. It is preferred for advanced projects, fractional stitches, and heirloom pieces.

### Can I use any fabric count with any cross stitch pattern?
Yes, any pattern can be stitched on any fabric count. But the finished size will change. Most patterns list a recommended count and the resulting dimensions to help you plan. Switching to a higher count makes the design smaller and more detailed. A lower count produces a larger, bolder piece. Always recalculate your fabric needs using the new count before buying materials. That way you know you have enough fabric.
