# How to Calculate Gauge in Knitting (And Actually Use the Numbers)

Gauge is the number of stitches and rows that fit into a measured area of your knitting, usually 4 inches (10 cm). To find your gauge, knit a swatch, block it, let it rest, then count stitches and rows across a ruler. That number tells you if your finished piece will match the pattern's measurements.

---

> **A mistake I made:** I ignored gauge because I wanted to start right away. My hat came out big enough to fit a watermelon. A quick gauge swatch would have saved me hours. The [gauge calculator](/gauge-calculator) tells you if your size will come out right.

## Why does gauge even matter for my project?

Gauge connects a pattern's instructions to your hands. If your gauge is off by even half a stitch per inch, a 40-inch sweater can come out 3 to 4 inches too wide or too narrow. That is not a small mistake. That is a sweater that does not fit.

Every knitter holds yarn with different tension. Two people using the same yarn, needles, and pattern will almost never make the same fabric. Your job is to find your gauge, then use math to adjust from there. The pattern's gauge is a target, not a promise.

---

## How do I knit a gauge swatch correctly?

Knit a square that is at least 6 inches wide and 6 inches tall in your project stitch. Four inches is the measuring window, but you need extra fabric on the edges. The first and last few stitches pull in and mess up the count. A 6-inch swatch gives you clean space in the center to measure.

Use the needle size the pattern recommends. But you do not have to stick with it. If you are knitting in the round for your project, swatch in the round too. Flat and circular knitting can pull at different tensions for most people, sometimes by a full stitch over 4 inches. The [Craft Yarn Council](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/how-to-measure-gauge) recommends washing and blocking your swatch the same way you plan to wash the finished piece before you measure it. [Wool especially changes size after a soak and a gentle press](/blog/analyzing-the-impact-of-yarn-weight-and-fiber-content-on-gau/).

Let the swatch dry completely and rest flat for at least an hour before you measure. Wet fiber is stretched out. If you measure it wet, your numbers will be wrong.

---

## How do I count stitches and rows to get the gauge number?

Lay your swatch on a flat surface. Place a ruler or tape measure across the center, away from the cast-on and bind-off edges. Mark 4 inches with two pins or two pieces of tape. Count every stitch between the pins, including any half stitches at the edges.

For example, if you count 22 stitches across 4 inches, your stitch gauge is 22 stitches per 4 inches, or 5.5 stitches per inch.

For row gauge, turn the ruler the other way. Place it up and down and count the rows between two pins set 4 inches apart. In stockinette, rows look like horizontal ridges or stacked V shapes. If you count 28 rows, your row gauge is 28 rows per 4 inches, or 7 rows per inch.

Write both numbers down. Stitch gauge controls width. Row gauge controls length.

---

## What do I do if my gauge does not match the pattern?

Go up a needle size if your swatch is too small. Too small means you have more stitches per inch than the pattern calls for. Go down a needle size if your swatch is too big, meaning fewer stitches per inch. Swatch again. Keep going until you hit the right number, or do the math to adjust instead.

If your gauge will not match exactly, you can figure out how many stitches to cast on for your target measurement. The formula is simple:

**Target width (inches) x your stitch gauge (stitches per inch) = stitches to cast on**

Say the pattern wants a 20-inch-wide body at 5 stitches per inch. That is 100 stitches. But your gauge is 5.5 stitches per inch. You would need 110 stitches to reach 20 inches. If the pattern uses a stitch repeat, round to the nearest number that works with it.

The same idea works for length. If you need 14 inches of body and your row gauge is 7 rows per inch, you need to knit 98 rows, not however many the pattern lists.

---

## Does row gauge matter as much as stitch gauge?

For many patterns, row gauge matters less than stitch gauge. This is especially true when length is written in inches instead of row counts. If the pattern says "knit until piece measures 15 inches," you just knit until your tape measure shows 15 inches. Stitch gauge still has to be right for the width.

Row gauge becomes very important when a pattern uses short rows, raglan shaping, or colorwork charted by rows. A yoke worked over 60 rows at 6 rows per inch is 10 inches deep. At 8 rows per inch, those same 60 rows give you only 7.5 inches. That is a noticeably different fit across the shoulders.

## Can I use a gauge calculator instead of doing the math by hand?

Yes, and you should. The math is simple, but doing it over and over across a whole pattern is easy to get wrong. A knitting gauge calculator, like the one at [fibertools.app](https://fibertools.app), lets you enter your actual gauge and your target measurements. It gives you the adjusted stitch counts right away.

The calculator does not replace swatching. It just handles the math once you have your real numbers. Measure as you go, especially on bigger projects. Your gauge can shift as you get into a rhythm, switch yarn balls, or knit in different conditions.

Your swatch gives you the one number that makes everything else work. Get that right first, then let the tools handle the rest.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is gauge in knitting and why does it matter?
Gauge is the number of stitches and rows per inch, or centimeter, in a knitted fabric. It matters because even a small difference between your gauge and the pattern's gauge can make a finished project come out much larger or smaller than planned. A sweater meant to fit a 36-inch chest could end up 40 inches if your gauge is off. That makes swatching an important first step before any sized project.

### How do I knit a gauge swatch correctly?
Cast on enough stitches to make at least 4 to 6 inches of width using your chosen yarn and needles. Work in the pattern stitch until your swatch is 4 to 6 inches tall. Bind off, then wash and block the swatch the same way you plan to treat the finished item. Measure the center of the fabric, not the edges. Count stitches and rows over 4 inches and divide to get your gauge.

### What should I do if my gauge doesn't match the pattern?
Switch needle sizes until your gauge matches. If you have too many stitches per inch, go up a needle size to loosen the fabric. If you have too few, go down a size to tighten it. Needle material, yarn fiber, and your personal tension all affect gauge. Always swatch again after switching needles. A gauge calculator can help you figure out any adjustments you need.

### How do I calculate gauge to resize a knitting pattern?
Divide the number of stitches in the pattern by the pattern's gauge to find the intended measurement in inches. Then multiply by your own gauge to get your stitch count. For example, if a pattern calls for 80 stitches at 4 stitches per inch, that is 20 inches wide. If your gauge is 5 stitches per inch, you need 100 stitches to get the same width. A knitting gauge calculator does this math for you right away.

### Does gauge change between different knitting needle materials?
Yes, needle material can affect your gauge. Slippery metal needles tend to make a slightly looser stitch. Wooden or bamboo needles create more friction and can make stitches a little tighter. Some knitters also find their tension changes between circular and straight needles. Always swatch with the exact needles you plan to use for the project, not just the same size in a different material.
