# Cross Stitch Size: What Actually Controls How Big Your Finished Piece Will Be

**The short answer:** Cross stitch size depends on two things working together. The first is your fabric count, which is the number of stitches per inch. The second is the number of stitches in your design. A 100x100 stitch design on 14-count Aida finishes at about 7x7 inches. On 28-count evenweave stitched over two threads, that same design still finishes at 7x7 inches. Change the fabric count and the size changes automatically. No math degree required.

---

## What does "fabric count" actually mean?

Fabric count is [the number of holes (and therefore possible stitches) per inch](/blog/cross-stitch-fabric-count/). A higher count means smaller stitches and a smaller finished piece. A lower count means bigger stitches and a larger piece.

The most common counts you will see are 11, 14, 18, and 28. [DMC's fabric guide](https://www.dmc.com/us/cross-stitch-fabric-c2051.html) says 14-count Aida is the standard starting point for most beginners and intermediate stitchers. That matches what you see in pattern kits. It gives you a stitch that is easy to see without looking chunky.

Here is what those counts look like in practice for a 140-stitch-wide design:

- **11-count Aida:** 140 divided by 11 equals about 12.7 inches wide
- **14-count Aida:** 140 divided by 14 equals exactly 10 inches wide
- **18-count Aida:** 140 divided by 18 equals about 7.8 inches wide
- **28-count evenweave (stitched over 2):** 140 divided by 14 equals 10 inches wide (same as 14-count)

That last one confuses a lot of people. When you stitch over two threads on 28-count fabric, you are working at 14 stitches per inch. The fabric is finer, but the stitch size matches 14-count Aida.

## How do I calculate the finished size of a cross stitch design?

Divide the stitch count by the fabric count to get finished inches. A design that is 180 stitches wide on 14-count Aida finishes at about 12.9 inches wide. Do that for both dimensions. Then add at least 3 inches of unstitched margin on every side before you cut your fabric.

Divide the stitch count by the fabric count. That is the whole formula.

**Width in inches = stitch width divided by fabric count**
**Height in inches = stitch height divided by fabric count**

Say a pattern is 180 stitches wide and 120 stitches tall. You are working on 14-count Aida:

- 180 divided by 14 equals 12.9 inches wide
- 120 divided by 14 equals 8.6 inches tall

Then add at least 3 inches of margin on every side for framing or finishing. That brings your fabric cut to about 18.9 x 14.6 inches. Most stitchers round up to the nearest half-inch and cut 19 x 15 inches. Forgetting the margin and running short on fabric is one of the most common mistakes in this craft. It is also one of the easiest to avoid.

## Does the number of strands I use change the size?

No, strand count does not change the physical size of your stitching. A cross stitch on 14-count Aida covers the same area whether you use one strand or four. What changes is how dense and saturated the color looks, not the size of the finished piece.

No, [strand count changes coverage and texture, not the physical dimensions](/blog/how-many-cross-stitches-per-skein/) of the stitch.

A cross stitch on 14-count Aida is the same size whether you work it with one strand or four strands. What changes is how full and saturated the color looks. [The Spruce Crafts notes](https://www.thesprucecrafts.com/how-many-strands-of-embroidery-floss-4051117) that most patterns on 14-count call for two strands. Two strands give solid coverage without the thread getting too bulky or the stitches losing their shape. On 18-count or higher, one strand is often enough. On 11-count, three strands can look better.

There is one way strand count does interact with how a piece looks. If you use too many strands on a high-count fabric, the stitches will crowd each other. The piece can pucker or look distorted. That is a tension and fit issue, not a size calculation issue.

## What if I want to resize a pattern to fit a specific space?

Work backwards by dividing the stitch count by your target finished size to find the fabric count you need. A 200-stitch-wide design meant to finish at 8 inches requires 25-count fabric. From there, find the closest standard count and adjust your target size slightly to match what is actually available.

Work backwards from the space to find the fabric count you need.

Say you want a design that is 200 stitches wide to finish at 8 inches wide. Divide 200 by 8 and you get 25. You need 25-count fabric or a 25-count equivalent. That is not a standard count, so you would look at the closest options. 28-count stitched over two threads gives you 14 stitches per inch, which is too small. You could try 28-count stitched over one thread for 28 stitches per inch, but that is slightly too big and finishes at about 7.1 inches. Sometimes you adjust the design by adding or removing a border. Other times you accept that the piece will finish a little larger or smaller than your target.

You can also use an online size calculator to check multiple fabric counts quickly. [Stitchboard's cross stitch calculator](https://www.stitchboard.com/tools/crossstitch-size-calculator) lets you enter your stitch count and fabric count and see the finished size right away. That saves a lot of pencil-and-paper work.

## Does framing or finishing change the size I need to plan for?

Yes, always plan for more fabric than just the stitched area. Standard framing needs 2 to 3 inches of unstitched fabric on every side. Scroll frames or stretcher bars need even more to grip. If you are finishing into a pillow or bag, add seam allowance on top of that margin.

Yes, and it matters more than most people expect when they are cutting fabric.

The visible stitched area is one measurement. The fabric you need to cut is a different, larger measurement. Standard framing needs at least 2 to 3 inches of unstitched fabric on each side. Scroll frames and stretcher bars need even more to grip properly. If you are finishing into a pillow, a bag, or a hoop ornament, the margin you need will change based on your seam allowance or hoop depth.

A good rule to follow: always cut more fabric than you think you need. Fabric is cheap compared to the hours you will spend stitching. Running out of margin when you go to frame a finished piece is a painful moment, and there is no fix except re-stitching on new fabric.

## What is the most beginner-friendly size to start with?

A design around 50x70 stitches on 14-count Aida is a great first project. It finishes at about 3.6 by 5 inches. That is small enough to finish in a few evenings but large enough to practice tension and see how your coverage develops before you try something bigger.

A design that is about 50x70 stitches on 14-count Aida is a solid first project.

That finishes at about 3.6 x 5 inches. It is small enough to complete in a few evenings. It is also large enough to practice steady tension and learn how coverage looks as you go. [Craftsy's beginner cross stitch guide](https://www.craftsy.com/post/cross-stitch-for-beginners/) recommends starting on 14-count because the holes are easy to see without magnification. The stitches are also forgiving if your tension is not perfect yet.

Once you finish one small piece, you will have a much better feel for how your stitch tension affects the look of the fabric. That makes planning the size of your next project a lot easier.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I calculate the size of a cross stitch project?
To find the finished size, divide the stitch count by the fabric count. For example, a 140x140 stitch design on 14-count Aida produces a 10x10 inch finished piece. Divide both the width and height stitch counts separately by your fabric count. Then add at least 3 inches of border on each side. That extra fabric gives you enough room for framing or finishing.

### What is fabric count and how does it affect cross stitch size?
Fabric count is the number of stitches per inch on cross stitch fabric. Higher counts like 18 or 28 produce smaller, finer stitches. Lower counts like 11 or 14 create larger stitches. Choosing a higher count makes your finished design smaller without changing the pattern at all. That makes fabric count one of the easiest ways to adjust the final size of your project.

### What size Aida cloth should I use for a beginner cross stitch project?
Beginners should start with 14-count Aida fabric. It offers a stitch size that is easy to see and work with. The holes are clearly defined, which reduces eye strain and counting errors. As your skills improve, you can try higher counts like 18 or 28 for more detailed, smaller finished pieces.

### How much fabric do I need for a cross stitch project?
You need the design's stitch area plus at least 3 inches of extra fabric on every side. Calculate the stitched area by dividing stitch count by fabric count. Then add 6 inches to both the width and height. That extra fabric lets you hoop the piece comfortably while stitching. It also gives you enough material for framing, mounting, or finishing the project neatly.

### Can I change the finished size of a cross stitch pattern without redrawing it?
Yes. You can resize a cross stitch project by switching to a different fabric count. You do not need to change the pattern at all. Using a higher count fabric makes the design smaller. Using a lower count makes it larger.
