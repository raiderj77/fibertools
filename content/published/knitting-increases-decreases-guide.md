## Why Shaping Is the Heart of Every Knitted Project

How does flat knitting become a fitted sleeve, a curved neckline, or a hat crown? The answer is shaping. Increases add stitches to make your fabric wider. Decreases remove stitches to make it narrower. Learning both gives you control over any project. It also gives you the confidence to change patterns.

Maybe you are a newer knitter seeing your first "M1L" instruction. Maybe you are more experienced and want to know why SSK looks different from K2tog. Either way, this guide covers every common method. It also shows how the [FiberTools Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) helps you keep an accurate count while you shape.

## What Are Increases and Decreases, and When Do You Need Them?

**Increases** create new stitches in a row. This makes the fabric wider. You use them for sleeve caps, raglan yokes, shawl bodies, and anywhere a pattern fans out. **Decreases** remove stitches. You use them for hat crowns, armhole shaping, waist cinching, neckline curves, and sock toes.

Every shaping method leans in a direction. It leans left, right, or center. Picking the right lean creates clean, even lines. A right-leaning decrease on the left side paired with a left-leaning decrease on the right side gives you the mirror-image lines you see in well-made garments.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Every Major Increase

### KFB (Knit Front and Back)

Knit into the front loop of the next stitch as normal. Do not slide it off the needle yet. Then knit into the back loop of that same stitch. Now slide it off. You have turned one stitch into two.

**Best for:** Beginner projects, garter stitch fabrics, and times when a small purl bump on the right side is okay or even looks nice.

**Good to know:** KFB uses an existing stitch. So if you swap it for M1, change *k2, M1* to *k1, KFB* to keep the placement right.

### M1L (Make One Left) and M1R (Make One Right)

Both methods lift the horizontal strand between two stitches onto the left needle. Then you knit through it in a twisted way to close the hole.

- **M1L:** Insert the left needle under the bar from front to back. Then knit through the back loop. The new stitch leans left.
- **M1R:** Insert the left needle under the bar from back to front. Then knit through the front loop. The new stitch leans right.

**Best for:** Nearly invisible increases in stockinette, paired increases on raglan lines, and anywhere you want clean symmetry.

### YO (Yarn Over)

Wrap the working yarn over the needle before you knit the next stitch. This creates a new stitch and a decorative eyelet hole.

**Best for:** Lace patterns, decorative buttonholes, and any design that is meant to have open holes.

### Lifted Increase (LLI and RLI)

Pick up the right or left leg of the stitch one row below. Then knit into it.

- **RLI (Right Lifted Increase):** Lift the right leg of the stitch below the next stitch on the left needle. Knit it.
- **LLI (Left Lifted Increase):** Lift the left leg of the stitch below the stitch you just worked. Knit it.

**Best for:** The most invisible increase you can make. Great for sock gussets and subtle shaping where you want no visual disruption at all.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Every Major Decrease

### K2tog (Knit Two Together), Right-Leaning

Insert your needle knitwise through two stitches at once. Knit them as one. The decrease slants to the right on the right side of the fabric.

### SSK (Slip, Slip, Knit), Left-Leaning

Slip one stitch knitwise. Slip the next stitch knitwise. Then insert the left needle into the fronts of both slipped stitches. Knit them together. The decrease slants to the left.

### P2tog (Purl Two Together), Right-Leaning on Purl Side

Insert the needle purlwise through two stitches. Purl them as one. Use this on wrong-side rows when you need a right-leaning decrease on the right side.

### SK2P (Slip 1, Knit 2 Together, Pass Slipped Stitch Over), Centered Double Decrease

Slip one stitch knitwise. Knit two together. Then pass the slipped stitch over. This removes two stitches at once. It creates a centered decrease that leans neither left nor right.

### S2KP (Slip 2, Knit 1, Pass 2 Slipped Stitches Over), Centered Double Decrease

Slip two stitches together knitwise. Knit one. Then pass both slipped stitches over. This is another centered double decrease. Many knitters prefer it because the center stitch sits neatly on top.

**Why left vs. right matters:** When shaping a V-neckline, you work SSK on the right side and K2tog on the left side. This makes the decreases point toward the center. It creates clean diagonal lines. The same idea applies to raglan seams and hat crowns.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

**Pair your increases and decreases for symmetry.** Use M1L with M1R, SSK with K2tog, or LLI with RLI. Mixing unpaired methods, like KFB on one side and M1L on the other, creates uneven lines.

**Watch your tension on SSK.** Many knitters find SSK looks looser than K2tog. Try slipping the first stitch knitwise and the second purlwise before knitting them together. This tightens the result.

**Place shaping one or two stitches from the edge.** This creates a selvedge that is easier to seam. Patterns often say "K2, SSK ... K2tog, K2" for this reason.

**S2KP vs. SK2P:** Both are centered double decreases. But S2KP tends to look neater because the center stitch sits on top. Swatch both to see which one you like better.

## Real Project Examples

### Raglan Sweater (Top-Down)

You start at the neckline with four increase points. On every right-side row, you work M1R before each raglan marker and M1L after each marker. This creates eight new stitches every two rows. It also makes clean diagonal lines. The Stitch Counter is very helpful here. Raglan yokes can run 60 or more increase rows. Losing count means ripping back.

### Hat Crown Decreases

A typical hat crown divides stitches into six or eight equal sections. You work K2tog or SSK, depending on the pattern, before each marker every other round. Then you work it every round as you get close to the top. The stitch count drops fast, from 96 to 84 to 72, and so on. The counter confirms each round is correct.

### Waist Shaping on a Pullover

To shape a fitted waist, you work paired decreases every several rows. You use SSK on the right and K2tog on the left of each side seam. This continues until you reach the waist. Then you work paired increases, M1L and M1R, back up to the bust. This hourglass shaping requires careful stitch tracking across many rows.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I substitute KFB for M1L in a pattern?

You can, but the results will look different. KFB leaves a small purl bump and uses an existing stitch. M1L is nearly invisible and creates a stitch from the bar between stitches. If you substitute, adjust your stitch position. Change "k2, M1L" to "k1, KFB" to keep the increase in the same spot.

### Why does my SSK look sloppier than my K2tog?

SSK often looks looser because of how the slipped stitches sit on the needle. Try this improved SSK: slip the first stitch knitwise and the second purlwise. Then knit them together through the back loops. This reseats the stitches and makes a tidier decrease that better matches K2tog.

### When should I use a centered double decrease instead of two single decreases?

Use a centered double decrease like S2KP when you want a single vertical line down the center of your shaping. For example, use it at the top of a triangular shawl or the center front of a V-neck. Two single decreases placed next to each other create a wider decrease band instead of a clean center line.
