# How to Calculate Aida Cloth Size for Cross Stitch Projects

Aida size calculation comes down to two numbers: your [stitch count and your fabric count](/blog/cross-stitch-size-chart/). Divide the design's width in stitches by the fabric count (stitches per inch). Then add at least 3 inches of margin on each side. Do the same for height. That gives you the smallest fabric rectangle you need to cut or order.

---

## What exactly is "fabric count" and why does it matter?

Fabric count is [the number of holes (and therefore stitches) per inch](/blog/cross-stitch-count/). Common Aida counts are 11, 14, 16, 18, and 28. A higher count means each stitch is smaller. That also means your finished design will be smaller on the fabric.

Say you stitch a 140 x 140 stitch design on 14-count Aida. You get a 10 x 10 inch design. Stitch that same design on 18-count and it shrinks to about 7.8 x 7.8 inches. This one number controls everything else, including how much fabric you need to buy.

[DMC's guide to Aida fabric](https://www.dmc.com/us/blog/how-to-choose-the-right-cross-stitch-fabric-for-your-project-n9000021901) explains count in plain terms if you want a second source to bookmark.

---

## How do I find the stitch count for my design?

The stitch count is listed on every commercial pattern. It is usually on the cover page or in the materials section. It looks something like "Stitch count: 120w x 98h." That means 120 stitches across and 98 stitches tall.

If you are making your own design, count the filled squares on your grid. Go from the leftmost filled square to the rightmost. Then count from top to bottom. Software like [PC Stitch](https://www.pcstitch.com/) or [KG Chart](https://www.kg-chart.com/) will find this number for you. If you are converting an image, the pixel size of your chart grid equals your stitch count.

---

## What is the actual formula I should use?

Divide your stitch count by the fabric count. That gives you the stitched area in inches. Then add your margin. A 140-stitch-wide design on 14-count gives you 10 inches of stitching. Add 8 inches of total margin and you need 18 inches of fabric.

Here it is, no fluff:

**Fabric width needed = (stitch width divided by fabric count) + margin**

**Fabric height needed = (stitch height divided by fabric count) + margin**

Standard margin is 3 to 6 inches per side. Use 4 inches per side (8 inches total per dimension) for anything going into a hoop or frame. You need enough fabric to grip. You also lose a little to fraying even with sealed edges.

**Worked example:**

- Design stitch count: 168w x 210h
- Fabric count: 14-count Aida
- Margin: 4 inches per side

Width: (168 divided by 14) + 8 = 12 + 8 = **20 inches**
Height: (210 divided by 14) + 8 = 15 + 8 = **23 inches**

You need to cut or order a piece at least 20 x 23 inches. When buying off a bolt, round up to the nearest half-yard to give yourself a buffer.

## Does the fabric count change if I stitch over two threads?

Yes, and this trips people up a lot. When you stitch over two threads on evenweave or linen, your effective stitch count per inch is cut in half. A 28-count evenweave stitched over two threads gives you the same stitch size as 14-count Aida.

Aida is woven in blocks so you stitch over one block at a time. You usually do not stitch over two on Aida. This mostly matters when you swap evenweave for Aida or the other way around. If your pattern says "designed for 28-count over two," treat it exactly like 14-count in your size calculation.

---

## How do I calculate size for an irregularly shaped design?

Use the bounding box. Picture a rectangle drawn around the outermost stitches in every direction. Measure that rectangle's stitch dimensions. You are calculating fabric size, not design coverage. The empty space inside the bounding box does not matter.

Say a wreath design is 200 stitches wide and 200 stitches tall but mostly hollow in the center. You still calculate fabric based on 200 x 200. The fabric does not know your design has a gap in the middle.

---

## What if I want to center the design on my fabric?

Centering does not change your fabric size calculation. It does change how you mark your starting point. Once your fabric is cut, fold it in half left to right, then top to bottom. The point where the folds cross is the center. Most commercial patterns mark the chart center with arrows or a center symbol. Match that point to your fabric center.

If your chart has no marked center, count the total stitches in each direction. Divide by two. Count outward from your fabric center to find where row 1, stitch 1 of the chart should go.

---

## Is there a quick reference for common stitch counts?

Yes. Fabric count makes less difference than most beginners expect. A 100 x 100 design ranges from about 13.6 inches on 18-count to 17.1 inches on 11-count. Both numbers include 4 inches of margin per side. Count matters most when your design is large or has a lot of detail.

Here is a table with common sizes. All numbers assume 4 inches of margin per side (8 inches total added per dimension).

| Stitch Count | 11-count | 14-count | 18-count |
|---|---|---|---|
| 50 x 50 | 12.5 x 12.5 in | 11.6 x 11.6 in | 10.8 x 10.8 in |
| 100 x 100 | 17.1 x 17.1 in | 15.1 x 15.1 in | 13.6 x 13.6 in |
| 150 x 200 | 21.6 x 26.2 in | 18.7 x 22.3 in | 16.3 x 19.1 in |
| 200 x 250 | 26.2 x 30.7 in | 22.3 x 25.9 in | 19.1 x 21.9 in |

These are starting points. Measure your actual cut piece before you start stitching. Confirm the fabric count matches the label. Fabric count can vary by manufacturer even within the same nominal count. A quick thread count on your piece can save a lot of frustration later.

## Any tools that do this math automatically?

[Stitchboard's fabric size calculator](https://www.stitchboard.com/tools/fabricsizecalculator) is free and does the math for you. Enter your stitch count, fabric count, and margin. It gives you the fabric dimensions. That is handy when you are ordering from a shop and want to check before you buy.

For spreadsheets, a simple formula in Google Sheets works fine: `=(stitch_count/fabric_count)+margin_total`. One column for width, one for height, and you are done.

The math is simple enough to do in your head at the store. Knowing the formula means you never have to wait for a tool. You can figure it out right there at the bolt of fabric.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is Aida cloth count and how does it affect my cross stitch project?
Aida count is the number of squares (or holes) per inch on Aida fabric. Common counts are 14, 16, 18, and 28. A higher count means smaller squares, which gives you finer and more detailed stitching. A lower count gives you larger, bolder stitches and is easier for beginners. The count you choose controls the finished size of your piece. Calculating it correctly before you start saves fabric and makes sure your design fits the way you want.

### How do I calculate how much Aida fabric I need for my design?
Divide your design's stitch width and height by the Aida count. That gives you the stitched area in inches. Then add at least 2 to 3 inches of border on each side. For example, a 140-stitch wide design on 14-count Aida gives you a 10-inch wide stitched area. Adding 3 inches on each side means you need at least 16 inches of fabric width. A calculator tool like the one on fibertools.app handles this math quickly and accurately.

### Can I use the Aida size calculator for different fabric counts?
Yes. The Aida size calculator works for any fabric count, including 11, 14, 16, 18, 22, and 28-count Aida or evenweave fabrics. Enter your stitch count and pick your fabric count. You will see the finished dimensions change right away. This makes it easy to compare how the same design looks on different fabric counts before you buy anything. That helps you pick the best option for your skill level and the amount of detail you want.

### What is the difference between Aida and evenweave fabric for cross stitch?
Aida has a visible block weave that clearly shows each stitch square. That makes it great for beginners. Evenweave fabrics like linen or Jobelan have a smoother, even weave. They are usually stitched over two threads. You can calculate both using stitch count and thread count per inch. For evenweave, divide by half the count to get the same effective grid. The fibertools.app calculator supports both fabric types so you can plan your project no matter which material you use.

### Why does my finished cross stitch look smaller or larger than I expected?
The most common reason is a mismatch between the design's stitch count and the Aida count you used when planning. Stitching a 200-count design on 18-count Aida gives you a noticeably smaller piece than the same design on 11-count Aida. Forgetting to add border fabric or starting with the wrong stitch count can also cause surprises. Using an Aida size calculator before you begin makes sure your fabric and framing plans match your actual finished size.
