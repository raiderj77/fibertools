# How to Handwash Knitwear

Fill a basin with cool water and a wool wash. Soak your sweater for 15-20 minutes. Press out water, don't wring. Roll in a towel to squeeze out more. Lay flat to dry. That's the basic method, but which soap, soak time, and drying setup you use depends on your fiber and how dirty the piece is.

## Do You Even Need to Handwash This?

Check the yarn label first. If it says "superwash" or "machine washable," you can skip handwashing and use your machine's wool or delicate cycle instead. If it says "hand wash only" or has no care instructions, handwashing is your safest bet.

Superwash wool has been treated so the scales on the fiber won't felt or shrink in a machine. Regular wool, alpaca, mohair, and cashmere have not been treated this way. Machine agitation and heat can felt these fibers fast. Once wool felts, you cannot undo it. The sweater shrinks and gets stiff and dense.

Cotton and acrylic are more forgiving. You can often machine wash these on gentle. But even machine washable yarns tend to look better and last longer with a hand wash. Less agitation means less pilling and less stretching out of shape.

If you're not sure what you're working with, treat it like delicate wool. That's the safer default.

## Which Soap Should You Use?

Use a wool wash made for fiber, like Soak, Eucalan, or Kookaburra Scour. These are designed to clean without stripping natural oils from wool. Avoid regular laundry detergent and dish soap, since both are made to cut grease and can dry out fiber.

Wool washes fall into two types: rinse-free and rinse-required.

Rinse-free wool washes, like Eucalan and Soak, let you skip the rinsing step. You add the soap to your soak water, let the sweater sit, then press out the water and move straight to drying. This saves time and reduces how much you handle wet wool, which cuts down on stretching.

Rinse-required washes work more like regular soap. You wash, then rinse with clean water until the soap is gone. This takes longer and means more handling of the wet garment.

For animal fibers like wool, alpaca, and cashmere, a rinse-free wash is usually the better choice. Less handling means less chance of stretching a wet sweater out of shape.

For plant fibers like cotton and linen, plain mild soap works fine. These fibers don't have the same lanolin content wool washes are designed to protect, so you don't need a specialty product. A few drops of a gentle dish soap or baby shampoo will do the job.

If you're washing something with sequins, beads, or delicate trim, skip the soak entirely and consider a very gentle spot clean with a damp cloth instead. Long soaks can loosen glue or thread on embellishments.

## Should You Soak It or Just Rinse It?

Soak for a lightly worn sweater that just needs freshening up. Soak longer, or add a second soak, for anything with visible stains or heavy wear, like a sweater worn all winter. A quick rinse alone rarely gets a knit fully clean.

A basic soak looks like this. Fill a clean basin, sink, or bathtub with cool or lukewarm water. Hot water can felt wool and fade colors, so stick to a temperature that feels neutral on your wrist, similar to a baby's bath water. Add your wool wash following the bottle's directions, usually a capful or a tablespoon per few gallons of water.

Submerge the sweater fully. Press it down gently so the water soaks through. Don't scrub, twist, or agitate it. Just let it sit still in the water for 15 to 20 minutes.

If the sweater has visible dirt, sweat stains under the arms, or food spots, give those spots a very gentle press between your fingers before the soak, and consider a second soak in fresh soapy water after the first one.

For a sweater that's just been worn a few times and only needs a refresh, a shorter soak of 10 minutes is often enough.

Avoid soaking for hours. Long soaks won't get a sweater cleaner, but they will keep it wet longer, which isn't ideal for delicate fibers.

## How Do You Get the Water Out Without Ruining the Shape?

Press the water out gently with your hands, then roll the sweater in a dry towel and press again. Never wring, twist, or lift a wet sweater by the shoulders. Wet wool is heavy and stretches easily under its own weight.

Here's the safe method, step by step.

First, let the water drain out of the basin, or lift the sweater into a colander sitting in the sink. Press down gently to push water out of the fibers. Don't squeeze hard or twist.

Next, lay a large dry towel on a flat surface. A bath towel works well for most sweaters. Lay the sweater flat on top of the towel, keeping it in its natural shape.

Roll the towel up with the sweater inside, like a jelly roll. Press down along the length of the roll, or stand on it gently, to push more water into the towel. You can unroll and switch to a second dry towel if the first one gets soaked through, especially with bulky sweaters or thick cabled knits.

This towel method pulls out a surprising amount of water. Your sweater should feel just damp, not dripping, when you unroll it.

Whatever you do, don't hang a wet wool sweater on a hanger. The weight of the water will pull the shoulders and body out of shape, sometimes permanently.

## What's the Best Way to Dry It?

Dry your sweater flat, away from direct sun and heat. This is the one step you should never skip or shortcut, since wrong drying is the most common way knitwear gets ruined.

Set up a flat drying space. A mesh sweater drying rack works great and lets air flow underneath. If you don't have one, a clean towel on a flat surface, like a table or the floor, works too. Just swap in a dry towel partway through if the first one gets damp.

Lay the sweater out and gently shape it to match its measurements. If you have the original schematic or know the finished measurements from your pattern, use those as a guide. Smooth out the sleeves, straighten the body, and pat the ribbing back into place.

Keep it away from direct sunlight and heating vents. Sunlight can fade color, and direct heat can cause uneven drying and, in some cases, scorching.

Drying time varies by fiber and thickness. A thin cotton tee might dry in a few hours. A bulky wool cardigan can take a full day or two. Flip it over partway through drying so both sides get even air exposure, especially with thick or cabled knits where water can get trapped in the stitches.

Resist the urge to speed things up with a hair dryer or by putting it near a radiator. Fast, uneven drying can distort the shape and, with wool, may cause the fibers to felt if there's any heat and friction involved.

## What If You're Short on Time?

Reach for a rinse-free wool wash and skip the full soak for a quick refresh. This works well when a sweater just smells a little musty or has been worn once or twice, but isn't visibly dirty.

For a fast refresh, fill a basin with cool water and rinse-free wash. Dunk the sweater for just a minute or two, agitating gently with your hands. Press out the water, roll in a towel, and lay flat to dry as usual. This skips the long soak but still gets soap through the fibers.

Another option for light refreshing, without any water at all, is airing out the sweater. Lay it flat outside in the shade, or hang it in a steamy bathroom after a shower. The moisture and air can loosen wrinkles and freshen the smell without a full wash. This won't remove stains or deep dirt, but it's useful between full washes to cut down on how often you need to wash a delicate sweater.

Handwashing wool too often can wear it out faster, since each wash is a chance for accidental felting or stretching. Many wool sweaters only need washing every few wears, unless they're visibly dirty or you've been sweating in them.

## Which Method Fits Your Situation?

| Situation | Best method |
|---|---|
| Superwash wool, everyday wear | Machine wash, wool cycle, cold water |
| Non-superwash wool, lightly worn | Rinse-free wool wash, 15-minute soak |
| Non-superwash wool, visibly dirty | Rinse-free wool wash, longer soak or double soak |
| Cotton or linen knit | Mild soap, short soak, can often machine wash gentle |
| Cashmere or alpaca | Rinse-free wool wash, gentle handling, short soak |
| Sweater with beads or sequins | Spot clean with damp cloth, skip soaking |
| Just needs a quick freshen | Rinse-free wash, 1-2 minute dunk, or just air out |

## A Few Mistakes Worth Avoiding

Hot water is the biggest risk for wool. Even water that feels warm to you can be enough to start felting fibers, especially combined with rubbing or wringing. Stick to cool or lukewarm.

Wringing is the second big mistake. It twists fibers and can permanently distort a sweater's shape. Press, don't twist.

Hanging wet knitwear is the third common error. Gravity plus water weight stretches sweaters out, and wool especially may not bounce back. Always dry flat.

Skipping the gauge and shape check is a smaller but real mistake. Before you wash a hand-knit sweater for the first time, take note of its measurements while dry: length, width, sleeve length. After washing, check it against those numbers while it's still damp on the drying rack. This lets you gently ease it back into shape if it's shifted, before it dries fully in the wrong dimensions.

Handwashing takes a little more time than tossing something in the machine. But for hand-knit sweaters, especially ones made from real wool, alpaca, or cashmere, it's the difference between a sweater that lasts for years and one that felts or stretches out after a single wash.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do you handwash knitwear without ruining it?
Fill a basin with lukewarm water, add a wool-safe detergent, and let the garment soak for 10-15 minutes without agitating or rubbing it. Gently press soapy water through the fibers instead of scrubbing, then drain and refill with clean water to rinse until no suds remain. Avoid wringing, press out excess water instead. This gentle approach prevents felting and keeps fibers like wool, alpaca, and cashmere from stretching or shrinking during cleaning.

### What temperature water should I use to handwash a sweater?
Use lukewarm or room-temperature water, ideally around 85-95°F (30-35°C). Extreme temperature changes between washing and rinsing water are the main cause of accidental felting in animal fibers like wool. Keep both the wash and rinse water at a similar temperature to avoid shocking the fibers. Cold water works too for delicate items, but avoid hot water entirely unless you're intentionally trying to felt the piece.

### Can you use regular laundry detergent on hand-knit sweaters?
Regular detergent isn't ideal because it often contains enzymes and alkaline ingredients that break down protein fibers like wool and silk. Instead, use a specially formulated wool wash or a gentle, pH-neutral soap without brighteners or enzymes. Many wool washes are rinse-free, saving time and reducing handling. If you only have regular detergent on hand, a small amount of mild baby shampoo is a safer substitute in a pinch.

### How do you dry a hand-knit sweater after washing?
Lay it flat on a clean towel, roll the towel up to squeeze out excess water, then reshape the sweater on a dry towel or mesh drying rack away from direct sunlight. Never hang wet knitwear, since the weight of the water will stretch it out of shape permanently. Reshape sleeves, hems, and neckline while damp to match the garment's original measurements, and let it air dry fully before wearing or storing.

### How often should you handwash knitted garments?
Most sweaters only need washing after 3-5 wears unless visibly soiled or odorous, since over-washing weakens fibers and strips natural oils from wool. Spot-clean small stains between washes and air out garments after each wear to refresh them. Delicate lace or heavily textured pieces may need even less frequent washing. Following care instructions for your specific yarn fiber helps extend the garment's lifespan and maintain its shape.
