# How Many Yards of Yarn for a Blanket?

**Quick answer:** Most blankets need between 1,000 and 3,000 yards of yarn. It depends on size and stitch pattern. A baby blanket usually needs 1,000 to 1,500 yards. A lap blanket needs 1,500 to 2,000 yards. A full throw or bed blanket needs 2,500 to 3,500 or more yards. These are starting points, not guarantees. Always swatch and measure before you buy.

---

## What size blanket am I actually making?

Size affects your yardage more than anything else. A baby blanket in worsted weight needs about 1,000 to 1,500 yards. A throw needs about 3,000 to 4,000 yards. A full bed blanket can need more than 6,000 yards. Figure out your dimensions before you estimate anything else.

A crib blanket and a king-size bed blanket are not even close to the same amount of yarn.

Here are common blanket sizes and rough yardage ranges. These are based on worsted weight yarn at a typical gauge:

| Blanket Type | Approximate Size | Estimated Yardage (Worsted) |
|---|---|---|
| Baby/Crib | 30" x 36" | 1,000 to 1,500 yds |
| Lap/Stroller | 36" x 48" | 1,500 to 2,000 yds |
| Throw | 50" x 60" | 2,000 to 3,000 yds |
| Full/Queen Bed | 60" x 80" | 3,000 to 4,500 yds |
| King Bed | 80" x 90" | 4,500 to 6,000+ yds |

These numbers assume a solid fabric with no lace panels or heavy texture. Your actual yardage will vary. That is why the next sections matter.

## Does yarn weight change how much I need?

Yes, yarn weight changes your yardage a lot. But it may not work the way you expect. Bulky yarn uses fewer total yards because each stitch covers more area. Fingering weight yarn has more yards per skein, but your blanket uses more of them. Heavier yarn means fewer yards needed for the same finished size.

Thinner yarn gives you more yards per ounce, but your blanket also eats through more yards per square inch of fabric.

A bulky yarn might give you 100 yards per 100 grams. A fingering weight might give you 400 yards per 100 grams. But a fingering weight blanket uses up yards faster because of stitch count. A bulky blanket works up quickly and uses fewer yards overall because each stitch covers more ground. For a 50" x 60" throw:

- **Bulky (size 6):** roughly 800 to 1,200 yards
- **Worsted (size 4):** roughly 2,000 to 3,000 yards
- **DK (size 3):** roughly 2,500 to 3,500 yards
- **Fingering (size 1):** roughly 4,000 to 6,000 or more yards

The [Craft Yarn Council's standard yarn weight system](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) is worth bookmarking. It helps you understand what the weight category on a yarn label really means.

## How does stitch pattern affect yardage?

Your stitch pattern can shift your yardage estimate by 20 to 30 percent in either direction. Dense, textured stitches like seed stitch or moss stitch use more yarn than stockinette. Open lace patterns and mesh stitches use less. Always swatch your actual pattern, not just plain fabric, before buying yarn.

Dense stitches eat yarn. Open stitches save it.

In crochet, a fabric made with all double crochet uses more yarn than the same size fabric in single crochet. That is because double crochet wraps the hook more times. A lacy mesh pattern with lots of chain spaces can cut yardage by 20 to 30 percent compared to a solid stitch. In knitting, seed stitch and moss stitch use more yarn than stockinette. The yarn travels back and forth more across the fabric surface.

Here is a practical test. Knit or crochet a 4" x 4" swatch and weigh it on a kitchen scale in grams. Then [calculate how many of those swatches fit in your finished blanket](/blog/accurately-calculating-your-yarn-yardage-with-a-customizable/). Multiply the swatch weight by that number. Then convert to yardage using the yards per gram on your ball band. It takes time, but it works.

## How do I calculate yardage for a pattern I found online?

If the pattern lists materials, start there. Most published patterns will tell you exactly [how many yards were used in the sample](/blog/how-much-yarn-for-a-blanket/) and the sample size.

If you are scaling the pattern up or down, [yardage scales roughly with area](/blog/creating-a-realistic-yarn-yardage-estimate-incorporating-fib/). A blanket that is twice as wide and twice as long needs four times the yarn, not two times. Area grows by squaring, not by doubling. So if a pattern calls for 1,500 yards for a 36" x 48" blanket and you want to make a 48" x 64" blanket, you need about 2,667 yards, not 2,250.

The math: (new length x new width) divided by (original length x original width), then multiplied by original yardage equals your estimated new yardage.

## Should I buy extra yarn, and how much?

Always buy at least 10 to 15 percent more than your estimate. Round up to the next full skein. If your yarn has dye lots, that extra amount is not optional. Most yarn shops accept returns on unopened skeins. One extra skein is cheap protection against running out near the end.

Buy more than you think you need. Always. The standard advice is to add 10 to 15 percent to your estimate as a buffer.

If you are working with a dye lot yarn, the stakes are higher. Running out and finding that a new skein is a slightly different shade is a bad situation. Most yarn stores will let you return unopened skeins. Buying one extra skein is almost always the right call. Check the store's return policy before you buy.

If you are using a hand-dyed or small-batch yarn, a second dye lot may not exist. Buy everything you think you need plus one extra skein the moment you find the colorway. With some indie dyers, there is no going back for more.

## What if I am designing my own blanket without a pattern?

Swatch first, then do the math. Knit or crochet a six-inch square and weigh it in grams. Use your yarn label to find yards per gram. Calculate how many swatches fit in your finished blanket, then multiply. Add 15 percent at the end. This method works for any stitch pattern and gives you a real number, not a guess.

Work a 6" x 6" swatch in your chosen stitch and yarn. Weigh it. Find the yards per gram on your yarn label by dividing total yardage by total grams. Then calculate how many 6" x 6" squares fit in your planned blanket and multiply. Add 15 percent for seaming, borders, and any frogging.

For example: your swatch weighs 12 grams. Your yarn is 220 yards per 100 grams, so 2.2 yards per gram. Your swatch used about 26 yards. Your blanket is 50" x 60", which fits about 83 of those 6" squares. 83 x 26 = 2,158 yards. Add 15 percent and you get about 2,482 yards. Round up to the nearest full skein.

It is not a perfect system, but it gets you close enough to shop with confidence.

## Any quick rules of thumb for when I just need a ballpark?

For worsted weight, figure about 1 yard of yarn per square inch of finished fabric. A 50" x 60" blanket is 3,000 square inches, so about 3,000 yards. This runs a little high for open stitches and a little low for dense ones. But it is a fast mental calculation that rarely leaves you short.

For bulky yarn, cut that estimate roughly in half. For fingering weight, add 50 percent or more.

Write down your yardage as you go, especially if you are working without a pattern. Knowing you used 800 yards for the first quarter of the blanket tells you exactly where you stand before you reach the halfway point.

## Frequently asked questions

### How many yards of yarn do I need for a baby blanket?
A baby blanket usually needs 500 to 1,500 yards of yarn. It depends on the stitch pattern and yarn weight. Bulky yarn with simple stitches like single crochet or garter stitch lands at the lower end. Fingering or DK weight with detailed patterns can push toward the higher end. A standard baby blanket around 30" x 36" in worsted weight usually needs about 800 to 1,000 yards. Always buy an extra skein to account for differences in tension and pattern repeats.

### How many yards of yarn do I need for a throw blanket?
A throw blanket usually needs 1,500 to 3,000 yards for a finished size of about 50" x 60". Yarn weight makes the biggest difference. Bulky or super bulky yarn can finish a throw in as few as 1,000 yards. A lacy fingering weight design may need 3,500 or more. Crochet stitches tend to use more yarn than knitting. Use a yardage calculator and your gauge swatch to get the most accurate estimate before you buy.

### How many yards of yarn do I need for a queen-size blanket?
A queen-size blanket usually needs 4,000 to 6,000 yards for dimensions around 60" x 80". Heavier yarn weights like bulky or chunky reduce yardage needs by a lot. Worsted or DK weight projects in that size range often land closer to 4,500 to 5,500 yards. The stitch pattern also matters. Dense stitches like moss stitch or basketweave use much more yarn than open lace patterns. Always add 10 to 15 percent extra yardage as a safety buffer.

### Does crochet or knitting use more yarn for a blanket?
Crochet generally uses about 30 percent more yarn than knitting for a blanket of the same size. Most crochet stitches create a thicker, denser fabric that uses more yardage per square inch. For example, a knitted stockinette blanket and a single crochet blanket of the same size can differ by hundreds of yards. If you are switching a pattern from one craft to the other, adjust your yardage estimate and swatch first to confirm your gauge.

### How do I calculate how many yards of yarn I need for a blanket?
Calculate blanket yardage by making a gauge swatch. Then use this formula: total square inches of the blanket divided by square inches in your swatch, multiplied by yards used in the swatch. For example, if your 4" x 4" swatch used 20 yards, a 50" x 60" blanket has 3,000 square inches. That is 187.5 swatches, so you need about 3,750 yards.
