# How to Plan an Intarsia Colorwork Project

Intarsia is a technique that adds pictures, shapes, and large color blocks to knitted or crocheted fabric. Unlike stranded colorwork (Fair Isle), you do not carry both colors across every row. Instead, intarsia uses separate lengths of yarn for each color section. There are no floats on the back. The fabric stays a single layer. The colors look clean and painted on.

If you want to knit a heart on a sweater, a diamond shape on a pillow, or a dog on a blanket, intarsia is how you do it.

## What Is Intarsia and When Should You Use It?

Intarsia gives each color block its own bobbin or small bundle of yarn. Where Color A meets Color B, you twist the two yarns around each other. This stops a hole from forming. Then you keep going with the new color.

**Use intarsia when:**
- Color sections are wider than 5 to 6 stitches
- Colors do not repeat across the same row
- You want single-thickness fabric (not double like stranded colorwork)
- Working large geometric shapes, picture motifs, or argyle patterns

**Use stranded colorwork (Fair Isle) instead when:**
- Colors switch often across the row (every 1 to 5 stitches)
- Both colors appear across the whole row
- You want a thicker, warmer fabric

**The key difference:** Stranded colorwork carries unused yarn behind the work. Intarsia does not. Each color block has its own yarn supply. It hangs on the back of the work and waits for its section.

## How Do You Work Intarsia Step by Step?

### Step 1: Prepare Your Yarn

Wind a separate bobbin, butterfly, or small ball for each color section. Say your chart shows 3 blocks of blue on the left side, center, and right side. You need 3 separate blue yarn supplies. You cannot use one skein and carry it across.

**Bobbins** are small plastic or cardboard cards that hold 5 to 15 yards of yarn. They stop tangles and hang neatly behind the work. You can buy plastic bobbins for $3 to $5 per pack of 10. You can also cut your own from cardboard.

**Butterflies** are figure-eight wraps of yarn around your thumb and pinky. They hold about 3 to 5 yards and unwind from the center. They work well for small sections.

### Step 2: Read the Chart

Intarsia charts work like any colorwork chart. Read right to left on right-side rows. Read left to right on wrong-side rows. Each cell equals one stitch in the color shown. Count the stitches in each color block before you start the row. That way you know when to switch colors.

### Step 3: Twist at Color Changes

When you switch from one color to the next, bring the new color up from under the old color. This twists the two strands around each other. It closes the gap between sections. Without the twist, you get a hole at every color boundary.

**On knit (RS) rows:** Drop the old color to the left. Pick up the new color from under the old color and knit with it.

**On purl (WS) rows:** Drop the old color to the left. Pick up the new color from under the old color and purl with it.

You must twist at every color boundary on every row. Miss one, and you will see a gap.

### Step 4: Manage the Tangles

This is the hardest part. Multiple yarn supplies hang from the back of your work. They will twist around each other. Every few rows, stop and untangle them. Hold up your work and let the bobbins dangle and spin free.

Keep bobbins short. Less yarn hanging means less tangling. Reload bobbins often instead of winding 50 yards at once.

## What Are the Best Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Keep bobbins small.** Wind 5 to 10 yards per bobbin at most. Small bobbins tangle less. Reload when they run out. Reloading takes 30 seconds and saves minutes of untangling.

**Work from the chart, not from memory.** Intarsia has no room for guessing. Do not think "I believe the next section is 8 stitches." Count from the chart every row. Use a magnetic chart keeper or sticky notes to mark your current row.

**Weave in ends as you go.** Each color change makes 2 tails. A project with 10 color changes per row over 100 rows makes 2,000 tails. On the next row, crochet or knit over the tails to trap them in the fabric. Do not save them all for the end.

**Practice the twist.** The color-change twist feels awkward at first. Practice on a 20-stitch swatch with 2 colors. Switch at stitch 10. After 10 rows, the motion will feel natural.

**Common mistakes:**
- Forgetting to twist at color changes (creates holes)
- Twisting in the wrong direction (creates bumps on the right side)
- Using one skein and carrying yarn across the back (that is stranded colorwork, not intarsia)
- Pulling too tight at color changes (creates puckered seams between color blocks)
- Not reading the chart direction correctly on wrong-side rows

**The heart sweater.** A knitter added a 20x18 stitch heart motif to the center of a stockinette pullover. She used 3 bobbins: background-left, heart, and background-right. The heart section used 45 yards of contrast color. She twisted at both color boundaries on every row and wove in tails as she worked. The intarsia section took 3 extra hours. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between intarsia and Fair Isle?

Intarsia uses separate yarn for each color block. No yarn is carried across the back. Fair Isle (stranded colorwork) carries both colors across every row. This creates floats on the back. Intarsia makes single-thickness fabric. Fair Isle makes double-thickness fabric. Use intarsia for large color sections of 6 or more stitches. Use Fair Isle for small, repeating color patterns.

### Can I do intarsia in crochet?

Yes, though it is less common. In crochet intarsia, you use separate bobbins for each color section. You twist the yarns at color changes, just like in knitting. Crochet intarsia works well for straight vertical or horizontal color boundaries. It is harder for diagonals. Crochet stitches are taller than knit stitches, so diagonal lines look coarser.

### How many bobbins do I need for an intarsia project?

Count the number of color sections in your widest row. Say row 25 has 3 sections of Color A and 2 sections of Color B. You need 5 bobbins for that row: 3 of A and 2 of B. The number of bobbins changes row by row as the design shifts. Always keep 5 to 10 extra empty bobbins ready to wind during the project.

### How do I prevent holes at intarsia color changes?

Twist the old and new yarns around each other at every color change on every row. Bring the new yarn up from under the old yarn before working the next stitch. The twist links the two sections of fabric together. If you see a hole forming, you missed a twist on that row. You can close small gaps with a tapestry needle and matching yarn when you finish the project.
