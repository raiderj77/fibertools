# How Much Yarn Do I Need? Complete Yardage Estimation Guide

Running out of yarn before a project is done is a stressful feeling. The color might be discontinued. The dye lot might be sold out. Then you end up searching every online shop late at night, hoping someone has one more skein. This problem is easy to avoid.

Estimating yardage before you buy is one of the most useful skills in knitting and crochet. Knowing how much yarn you need saves money, prevents problems mid-project, and keeps crafting fun.

> **A mistake I made:** I bought yarn without checking how much the pattern needed, then ran out near the end. I could not match the dye lot, so the colors were off. Now I use the [yarn calculator](/yarn-calculator) and buy a little extra from the same dye lot.

## When You Need a Yardage Estimate

You need a yardage estimate every time you start a project. This is true even when the pattern lists a yarn amount. Patterns list yardage for a specific yarn at a specific gauge. If you swap in a different yarn, those numbers no longer apply. You also need estimates when:

- Designing your own pattern from scratch
- Working from a vintage pattern with outdated yarn references
- Using yarn from your stash without labels
- Scaling a pattern up or down (baby blanket to queen-size)
- Combining multiple colors and needing per-color amounts

Buying all your yarn at once from the same dye lot is always the safest choice.

## How to Calculate Yardage From Pattern Gauge

If you have a pattern gauge and finished measurements, you can figure out yardage yourself:

1. **Find your stitch and row gauge.** For example: 18 stitches and 24 rows equals 4 inches in worsted weight.
2. **Calculate total stitches.** Say a sweater body is 40 inches around and 24 inches long. You would calculate: (18 divided by 4) times 40 equals 180 stitches per round. Then: (24 divided by 4) times 24 equals 144 rows. Total stitches equal 180 times 144, which is 25,920 stitches.
3. **Measure yarn per stitch.** Knit a small swatch. Measure how many yards it takes. Then divide by the total stitch count. For worsted stockinette, a rough estimate is about 1 inch of yarn per stitch.
4. **Convert to yards.** 25,920 inches divided by 36 equals 720 yards. That is just for the body. You still need to add sleeves, neckband, and finishing.

A calculator is very helpful here. The math is simple but takes time, and small mistakes can grow across a full project.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

**Always buy extra.** The standard advice is to buy 10 to 15% more than your estimate. This covers tension changes, mistakes and re-dos, weaving in ends, and any gauge drift over the life of the project. If you are doing colorwork or cables, bump that up to 20%.

**Dye lots matter.** Yarn dyed in different batches can look noticeably different when worked side by side. This can happen even if the color name is the same. Buy all your yarn at once from the same dye lot. If you must use more than one dye lot, alternate skeins every two rows to blend the difference.

**Stitch pattern affects yardage a lot.** Cables pull fabric inward and use 15 to 25% more yarn than stockinette. Lace uses less yarn because of the open structure. Colorwork uses more yarn because of carried floats and yarn changes. Moss stitch and seed stitch use slightly more than stockinette.

**Crochet versus knitting.** Crochet uses roughly 25 to 30% more yarn than knitting for the same project. Double crochet is more efficient than single crochet. Keep this in mind when converting patterns between crafts.

**Do not trust "number of skeins" alone.** If you substitute yarn, recalculate based on total yardage. Three skeins at 220 yards each gives you 660 yards total. Three skeins at 137 yards each gives you only 411 yards total. Those are very different amounts.

**Weigh your partial skeins.** A kitchen scale accurate to 1 to 2 grams tells you exactly how much yarn is left in a partial ball. No guessing needed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much extra yarn should I buy beyond my estimate?

Buy 10 to 15% more yarn than your calculated estimate for simple projects in stockinette or basic crochet stitches. For cables, colorwork, or textured stitch patterns, increase that buffer to 20%. The small extra cost is worth avoiding a dye lot mismatch later.

### Does crochet really use more yarn than knitting?

Yes. Crochet typically uses 25 to 30% more yarn than knitting for the same project size. Crochet stitches wrap yarn around the hook more times per stitch. This creates a denser fabric that uses more yardage. Always account for this difference when converting a knitting pattern to crochet, or the other way around.

### How do I estimate yardage for partial skeins from my stash?

Weigh your partial skein on a kitchen scale in grams. Check the label for the full skein weight and yardage. Then calculate: divide the partial weight by the full skein weight, then multiply by the full skein yardage. For example, a 55g piece from a 100g skein with 220 yards has roughly 121 yards remaining.

### What if I can't find the same dye lot when I run out?

If the exact dye lot is not available, buy the closest match. Then alternate rows between the old and new yarn for several inches to blend the color change. This technique makes dye lot differences nearly invisible. For future projects, always buy all your yarn at once from the same dye lot.
