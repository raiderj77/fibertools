# Knitting Yarn Weight Chart: How to Pick the Right Yarn Every Time

A yarn weight chart lines up yarn types (lace, fingering, sport, DK, worsted, bulky) with the needle size and stitch gauge each one usually needs. Use it to check if a yarn will work for your pattern, or to swap one yarn for another. The Craft Yarn Council's numbered system (0 through 7) is the standard most patterns use.

## Why do I keep buying the wrong yarn weight?

You're probably shopping by name instead of by number. Yarn names like "worsted" or "DK" get used loosely by different yarn companies, so the label alone can trick you. The fix is to check the yarn's weight category number and its actual yardage per gram, not just the name on the label.

Here's what happens. You find a pattern that calls for worsted weight. You go to the yarn store or scroll online and grab something labeled "worsted." But one brand's worsted knits up thinner than another brand's DK. You get home, swatch, and your gauge is way off. This happens because yarn weight names were never locked down by law. They're a general guide, not a guarantee.

The Craft Yarn Council created a numbering system to fix this confusion. Every yarn label should show a small number in a skein icon, from 0 to 7. That number is more reliable than the word next to it. Learn to check the number first.

## What are the actual yarn weight categories?

There are 8 standard categories, numbered 0 through 7. Each one has a typical needle size range and a typical gauge (stitches per 4 inches) that goes with it. Once you know these ranges, you can check any yarn against them.

Here is the full chart, based on the Craft Yarn Council standard:

**0 - Lace**
Also called: thread, cobweb, lace weight
Knitting needle size: US 000-1 (1.5mm-2.25mm)
Gauge: 33-40 stitches per 4 inches
Common use: shawls, doilies, fine lace projects

**1 - Super Fine**
Also called: fingering, sock, baby weight
Knitting needle size: US 1-3 (2.25mm-3.25mm)
Gauge: 27-32 stitches per 4 inches
Common use: socks, baby garments, light shawls

**2 - Fine**
Also called: sport weight
Knitting needle size: US 3-5 (3.25mm-3.75mm)
Gauge: 23-26 stitches per 4 inches
Common use: light sweaters, baby items, accessories

**3 - Light**
Also called: DK (double knit), light worsted
Knitting needle size: US 5-7 (3.75mm-4.5mm)
Gauge: 21-24 stitches per 4 inches
Common use: sweaters, light blankets, hats

**4 - Medium**
Also called: worsted, afghan, aran
Knitting needle size: US 7-9 (4.5mm-5.5mm)
Gauge: 16-20 stitches per 4 inches
Common use: sweaters, blankets, mittens, hats

**5 - Bulky**
Also called: chunky, craft, rug
Knitting needle size: US 9-11 (5.5mm-8mm)
Gauge: 12-15 stitches per 4 inches
Common use: heavy sweaters, blankets, rugs

**6 - Super Bulky**
Also called: super chunky, roving
Knitting needle size: US 11-17 (8mm-12.75mm)
Gauge: 7-11 stitches per 4 inches
Common use: heavy blankets, winter accessories

**7 - Jumbo**
Also called: roving, jumbo
Knitting needle size: US 17 and up (12.75mm and larger)
Gauge: fewer than 6 stitches per 4 inches
Common use: arm knitting, quick blankets, floor poufs

Keep in mind these are starting points. Check your gauge with a swatch before you trust any chart number for your actual project.

## How do I know what weight my mystery yarn is?

Check the yardage per gram or per ounce. This number tells you more than the name on the label ever will. A thicker yarn has less yardage per gram. A thinner yarn has more.

This trick works even if the label is missing, faded, or written in a language you don't read. Here's roughly what to expect:

- Lace weight: around 400-1000 yards per 100 grams
- Fingering weight: around 350-450 yards per 100 grams
- Sport weight: around 300-350 yards per 100 grams
- DK weight: around 240-280 yards per 100 grams
- Worsted weight: around 190-220 yards per 100 grams
- Bulky weight: around 100-150 yards per 100 grams
- Super bulky: around 60-100 yards per 100 grams

If you have a kitchen scale and a tape measure, you can figure out an unlabeled yarn yourself. Wind off 10 yards, weigh it in grams, then do the math to scale up to 100 grams. Compare that number to the list above. This method works even for yarn from your stash with no ball band left.

Another simple test: wrap the yarn around a ruler for one inch, keeping the strands snug but not stretched. Count how many wraps fit in that inch. This is called a wraps per inch (WPI) test.

- Lace: 30+ wraps per inch
- Fingering: 14-18 wraps per inch
- Sport: 12-14 wraps per inch
- DK: 11-13 wraps per inch
- Worsted: 9-11 wraps per inch
- Bulky: 7-9 wraps per inch
- Super bulky: 5-6 wraps per inch

WPI is not exact. Twist and fiber content change the count. But it gives you a fast, tool-free way to sort mystery yarn into a rough category.

## Can I substitute a different yarn weight than the pattern calls for?

Yes, but you have to do some math and always swatch first. Substituting yarn weight changes your stitch count, yardage needed, and finished size. It can work well if you plan for it instead of guessing.

Say your pattern calls for worsted weight (about 200 yards per 100 grams) and you want to use a DK yarn instead (about 260 yards per 100 grams). Your DK yarn is thinner, so you'll need more yardage to knit the same size, and your fabric will come out lighter unless you adjust your needle size or stitch count.

Steps to substitute yarn weight safely:

1. Find the yardage of the pattern's called-for yarn. This is usually listed near the materials section.
2. Find the yardage of your substitute yarn. Check the label or use the yardage-per-gram method above.
3. Divide the original yarn's total yardage by its yards-per-100-grams number. This tells you roughly how many 100-gram balls the pattern uses.
4. Do the same math for your substitute yarn to figure out how many balls you'll need.
5. Swatch in your substitute yarn using a needle size one or two sizes different from what the pattern suggests, to try to match the pattern's gauge.
6. Measure your swatch gauge, not just needle size, against the pattern's gauge requirement.

If you can't match gauge even after trying a few needle sizes, the yarn weight might be too different from the original to substitute directly. In that case, look for a pattern written for the weight of yarn you already have. There are pattern databases like Ravelry where you can filter by yarn weight to find a better match.

## Why does my gauge swatch not match the chart?

Charts give average ranges, not guarantees. Fiber content, your tension, needle material, and even the day you're knitting can all shift your actual gauge away from the chart number. This is normal, and it's exactly why every pattern tells you to swatch first.

A worsted weight wool will behave differently than a worsted weight cotton, even at the same needle size. Wool has more give and bounces back into shape. Cotton is stiffer and drapes flatter. Acrylic sits somewhere in between, depending on the brand. The Craft Yarn Council chart assumes an average fiber, but your yarn is not average. It's specific.

Your own tension matters too. Some knitters wrap yarn tighter, some looser, and this changes stitch count even with the exact same yarn and needles. This is why two knitters using the same pattern and same yarn can end up with two different sized sweaters.

Always knit a swatch that's at least 4 inches by 4 inches before starting a project. Wash and dry it the way you plan to treat the finished piece, since some fibers shrink or relax with washing. Measure your actual stitch count over 4 inches. Compare that to the pattern's gauge, not the general chart. The chart gets you in the right neighborhood. Your swatch tells you the truth for your yarn, your needles, and your hands.

## What needle size should I start with for a given yarn weight?

Start in the middle of the range listed for that yarn's weight category, then adjust based on your swatch. The chart above gives you a starting needle size, not a fixed rule.

For example, if you're using a worsted weight yarn, start with a US 8 (5mm) needle, since that sits in the middle of the US 7-9 range. Knit your swatch. If your stitches are coming out too loose or your fabric looks holey, drop down to a US 7. If your fabric feels stiff and tight, go up to a US 9.

This adjustment is completely normal and doesn't mean you're doing anything wrong. Needle size ranges on yarn labels and charts are suggestions based on average tension. Your job is to test and adjust until your fabric looks and feels right for the project, whether that's a drapey shawl or a sturdy bag.

Keep a small notebook or note on your phone of which needle size worked for which yarn. Over time, you'll build your own personal reference that's more useful than any general chart, because it's based on your actual tension.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the difference between yarn weight numbers and names?
Yarn weight numbers (0-7) are the Craft Yarn Council's standardized system, while names like "worsted" or "sport" are traditional descriptive terms. The numbers were introduced to reduce confusion since brands sometimes labeled similar yarns differently. For example, weight 4 corresponds to "worsted/aran," and weight 6 corresponds to "super bulky." Using both the number and name together, as shown on most yarn weight charts, helps you match patterns and substitute yarns accurately regardless of brand terminology.

### How do I know what weight my yarn is if the label is missing?
Wrap the yarn around a ruler for one inch and count the wraps, then compare that number to a wraps-per-inch (WPI) chart. Lace weight typically measures 18-20 WPI, while super bulky measures 5-6 WPI. You can also check the yarn's ply count and diameter against a physical yarn weight chart or gauge tool. If you have leftover yardage and weight in grams, calculating yards per pound can further help identify the weight category.

### Can I substitute one yarn weight for another in a pattern?
Yes, but only with adjustments to needle size, gauge, and yardage calculations. Substituting a lighter or heavier weight changes the finished garment's drape, size, and required yarn amount, so you'll need to recalculate using a gauge swatch. Sticking within one weight category up or down (like worsted for aran) usually works better than jumping multiple categories. Always check the pattern's finished measurements and adjust needle size accordingly to maintain proper stitch gauge.

### What yarn weight is best for beginners?
Worsted weight (category 4) is best for beginners because it's thick enough to see stitches clearly but not so bulky that it's hard to control. It works with widely available needle sizes (US 7-9) and knits up quickly, making projects like scarves and hats satisfying to complete. Worsted weight is also the most commonly stocked yarn in stores, offering beginners plenty of color and fiber choices while learning basic stitches like knit, purl, and simple cables.

### Does yarn weight affect crochet projects the same way as knitting?
Yes, yarn weight affects gauge, hook size, and drape in crochet just as it does in knitting, though crochet typically uses more yarn per stitch. A yarn weight chart lists recommended hook sizes alongside needle sizes, since crochet hooks run on a different sizing scale (like sizes E-4 through N-15). Because crochet fabric is generally denser than knitted fabric, choosing the correct weight matters for achieving the right drape, especially in garments, amigurumi, or lace-weight doily patterns.
