# How to Use Stitch Markers in Knitting and Crochet

Stitch markers are the cheapest tool that stops the most expensive mistakes. A $3 pack of markers can save you from frogging hours of work. You might miscount a cable repeat, miss a decrease, or lose track of the beginning of a round. Markers help you avoid all of that.

If you have been "just counting" without markers, you are making every project harder than it needs to be. Here is how to use markers for every situation.

## What Are Stitch Markers and When Do You Need Them?

Stitch markers are small rings or clips. They sit on your needle or attach to your stitches. They mark important spots. Think of them as reference points that say "something happens here." You do not have to count from the edge every single time.

**You need markers when:**
- Working in the round (marking the beginning of the round)
- Following a pattern with repeating stitch motifs
- Tracking increase or decrease points in shaping
- Separating sections (front, back, sleeves in a raglan)
- Marking a specific stitch for later reference (like the center stitch of a shawl)
- Working lace, cables, or colorwork where miscounting is costly

**You can skip markers when:**
- Working a simple scarf in garter stitch or stockinette with no shaping
- Making a dishcloth in basic stitches
- Working any project where the pattern never changes across the row

## What Types of Stitch Markers Exist?

### Ring Markers (Knitting)

These are small rings that slide onto the needle between stitches. They sit on the needle and move from left to right as you knit. When you reach a marker, slip it from one needle to the other. Then do whatever the pattern says.

Ring markers come in different sizes. Use small rings for thin needles (US 0 to 4). Use medium rings for standard needles (US 5 to 9). Use large rings for bulky needles (US 10 and up). A ring that is too small will not slide. One that is too large will fall off.

**Cost:** $3 to $8 for a pack of 20 to 40.

### Locking Markers (Crochet and Knitting)

These are clips that open and close. They attach right to a stitch. They are a must for crochet because ring markers fall off the hook. Locking markers clip into the top of a stitch and stay there until you take them out.

Use locking markers to mark the first stitch of a round in crochet. Use them to flag a row for counting later. Use them to mark where you will pick up stitches for a border.

**Cost:** $3 to $6 for a pack of 20 to 30.

### Removable Split-Ring Markers

These are small split rings, like tiny key rings. They hook onto a stitch without opening. They work for both knitting and crochet. They cost less than locking markers, but they can be a little harder to remove from tight stitches.

### DIY Markers

In a pinch, use scrap yarn tied in a loop. That works like a ring marker. Use safety pins to work like locking markers. Bobby pins, paper clips, and earring hoops also work. The fanciest marker does the same job as a piece of contrasting yarn.

## How Do You Place Markers for Common Situations?

### Marking Pattern Repeats

Say your pattern says "repeat [k2, p2, cable 6, p2, k2] across the row." Place a marker after each complete repeat. On a row with 8 repeats, you will have 8 markers. When you reach each marker, you should be at the start of the next repeat. If your stitch count does not match, you made an error in the repeat you just finished. You do not have to hunt back 8 repeats to find the problem.

### Marking the Beginning of a Round

In circular knitting, place one marker at the join point. Use a color that stands out. This is your "beginning of round" marker. Slip it every round. In crochet, clip a locking marker into the first stitch of each round. Move it up every round.

The [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) works great with round markers. Click the counter each time you pass the beginning-of-round marker. You will always know exactly what round you are on.

### Marking Raglan Increase Points

A top-down raglan has 4 increase points. Two are at the front-yoke boundaries. Two are at the back-yoke boundaries. Place markers at all 4 points. On each increase round, work an increase on either side of each marker. The markers keep your 4 sections perfectly even.

### Marking Center Stitches

Shawls and triangle motifs often have a center stitch. Increases fan out from that stitch. Place a unique marker on either side of the center stitch. Work increases before the marker. Work the center stitch. Then work increases after the marker.

### Marking Row Counts

Use a locking marker on the fabric to mark every 10 or 20 rows. When you need to count how many rows you have worked, count the markers instead of staring at every single row. Use this along with the [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter) to track rows both on the fabric and digitally.

## What Are the Best Tips and Common Mistakes?

**Use color-coded markers for different purposes.** Use red markers for increase points. Use blue for pattern repeats. Use green for beginning of round. When you reach a marker, the color tells you what to do right away. You do not have to check the pattern every time.

**Do not place markers too tight.** Ring markers should slide freely on the needle. If you have to force one past, it is too small. Tight markers can bend the stitches next to them and slow you down.

**Move crochet markers every round.** In crochet, a locking marker stays in the stitch you clipped it to. If you do not move it up each round, you will be counting from a marker that is 10 rounds below where you are now.

**Count after placing markers, not before.** Place your markers as you work the setup row. Count each section as you go. Do not count the whole row first and then try to place markers at the right spots.

**Upgrade to quality markers.** Cheap plastic markers break, snag yarn, and have rough edges. A $6 pack of Clover locking markers or metal ring markers lasts for years and never catches your yarn.

**Common mistakes:**
- Forgetting to move the beginning-of-round marker and working a partial round
- Accidentally knitting or crocheting the marker into the fabric (this happens most with yarn loop markers)
- Not using enough markers (one marker per repeat is better than one every 3 repeats)
- Placing markers on the wrong side of a stitch, which causes off-by-one errors in shaping

## What Do Real Marker Strategies Look Like?

**The cable sweater.** A knitter working a cabled pullover placed markers around every cable panel. There were 8 cables total, so she used 16 markers. Each cable crossed every 8 rows. Without markers, she would have lost her place often in the 14-stitch cable panels. With markers, she never miscounted. She finished the sweater in 5 weeks.

**The crochet amigurumi.** A crocheter making a stuffed bear placed a locking marker at the beginning of each round. She moved it every round. The project had 60 or more rounds of increases and decreases. The marker was the only way to track round boundaries in continuous spiral crochet. She used the Stitch Counter to track round numbers at the same time.

**The lace shawl.** A knitter worked a 24-stitch lace repeat across 192 stitches. She placed 8 markers, one per repeat. On row 47, the stitch count after marker 5 did not add up. She only had to tink back to marker 5, not all the way to the beginning of the row. The markers saved her from frogging 120 stitches.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I really need stitch markers or can I just count?

You can count, but markers stop you from having to re-count. Say you have a 200-stitch row with a 12-stitch pattern repeat. Counting from the beginning to check your spot at stitch 150 takes about 30 seconds. It also opens the door for errors. Checking against the nearest marker takes 2 seconds. Markers do not replace counting. They cut down on how much counting you need to do.

### What's the difference between ring and locking markers?

Ring markers sit on the needle between stitches. They are used for knitting. Locking markers clip right into a stitch. They work for both knitting and crochet. Crochet requires locking markers because ring markers fall off the hook. For knitting, use ring markers on the needle. Use locking markers when you need to flag a specific stitch in the fabric.

### How many stitch markers do I need?

A basic set of 20 to 30 markers covers most projects. A cable sweater might use 16. A lace shawl with markers at every repeat might use 15 to 20. Keep 40 or more on hand so you never run short in the middle of a project. Buy at least two colors so you can tell apart your beginning-of-round markers from your pattern-repeat markers.

### Can I make my own stitch markers?

Yes. Cut a 3-inch piece of contrasting scrap yarn and tie it into a loop. That works as a needle marker. Use safety pins or small binder clips as locking markers. Handmade markers work fine, but they are bulkier and can snag delicate yarn. Once you are past the beginner stage, it is worth buying a set of real markers.
