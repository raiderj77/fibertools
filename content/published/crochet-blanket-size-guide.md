# Crochet Blanket Size Guide: Dimensions, Yarn Amounts & Stitch Counts

Running out of yarn at row 150 is no fun. Neither is finishing a "throw" that barely covers your lap. Blanket sizing sounds simple, but stitch pattern, yarn weight, and border width all change the math. Getting it right before you start saves money, time, and a lot of frustration.

This guide covers every standard blanket size from lovey to king. You will find dimensions, stitch count estimates, and yardage needs broken down by yarn weight. Whether you are making a baby shower gift or a queen-size bedspread, you will know what you need before you buy a single skein.

## Standard Crochet Blanket Sizes

Blanket sizes follow general bedding and gift standards. Crochet blankets often run a little smaller than store-bought ones. That is because people often use them as a decorative layer, not as main bedding.

| Blanket Type | Width x Length (inches) | Width x Length (cm) | Common Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lovey | 12 x 12 | 30 x 30 | Baby comfort blanket |
| Preemie | 18 x 24 | 46 x 61 | NICU / hospital donations |
| Baby | 30 x 36 | 76 x 91 | Stroller, tummy time |
| Toddler | 36 x 42 | 91 x 107 | Crib transition |
| Lap | 36 x 48 | 91 x 122 | Wheelchair, reading chair |
| Throw | 50 x 60 | 127 x 152 | Couch, movie night |
| Twin | 66 x 90 | 168 x 229 | Twin bed with drape |
| Full/Double | 80 x 90 | 203 x 229 | Full bed with drape |
| Queen | 90 x 100 | 229 x 254 | Queen bed with drape |
| King | 108 x 100 | 274 x 254 | King bed with drape |

These are the finished sizes you are aiming for. If you want the blanket to hang over the sides of the bed, add 10 to 12 inches to both the width and length beyond the top of the mattress.

## Calculating Stitch Counts by Yarn Weight

Your starting chain and total stitch count depend on your yarn weight and hook size. Here is how many stitches per inch you can expect with a standard single crochet (sc) gauge:

| Yarn Weight (CYC) | Hook Size | Stitches per Inch | Rows per Inch |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fingering (1) | 2.75 to 3.5mm | 6 to 7 | 7 to 8 |
| Sport (2) | 3.5 to 4.5mm | 5 to 6 | 6 to 7 |
| DK (3) | 4.5 to 5.5mm | 4 to 5 | 5 to 6 |
| Worsted (4) | 5.5 to 6.5mm | 3.5 to 4 | 4 to 5 |
| Bulky (5) | 6.5 to 9mm | 3 to 3.5 | 3 to 4 |
| Super Bulky (6) | 9 to 11.5mm | 2 to 2.5 | 2 to 3 |

To find your starting chain, multiply stitches per inch by your target width. For a 50-inch throw in worsted weight at 4 stitches per inch, that is 200 stitches, plus 1 for turning. For rows, multiply rows per inch by your target length. A 60-inch throw at 4.5 rows per inch means about 270 rows.

Always crochet a gauge swatch first. Your tension, hook brand, and stitch pattern will all shift these numbers. A 4-inch square swatch gives you a solid per-inch count. That makes the rest of the math easy.

## Tips, Variations, and Common Mistakes

**Border considerations.** Borders add both width and yarn. A simple single crochet border adds about half an inch per round. A shell or scallop border can add 1 to 2 inches per round. For a throw with a 3-round shell border, expect to add 3 to 4 inches to each dimension and 200 to 400 extra yards of yarn. Plan your base size slightly smaller than your target to leave room for the border.

**The too-narrow trap.** The most common blanket mistake is starting too narrow. Your foundation chain relaxes and stretches differently than the body of the blanket. Measure your first 4 to 5 rows of actual fabric, not just the chain, before you keep going. If it is even half an inch too narrow, frogging 5 rows is much better than frogging 50.

**Dye lots matter at scale.** A baby blanket might use 3 skeins from the same dye lot. A queen-size blanket needs 20 or more. Buy all your yarn at once from the same dye lot. If that is not possible, alternate skeins every few rows to blend any small color differences.

**Yarn weight affects drape.** A worsted-weight throw in single crochet will feel stiff and heavy. The same throw in half double crochet will drape better. Bulky yarn in a simple stitch makes a cozy, chunky blanket. But it can weigh 8 to 10 pounds at queen size. Think about how the blanket will actually be used before you pick a yarn weight.

**Always add a yarn buffer.** Buy 15 percent more than your total estimate. Blanket projects take weeks or months. If you need one more skein and your dye lot is gone, you are stuck. Extra yarn works great for matching accessories or can be given to another crafter.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much yarn do I need for a crochet baby blanket?

A standard 30 x 36 inch baby blanket in worsted weight yarn needs about 900 to 1,200 yards. That depends on your stitch pattern. It is usually 5 to 7 skeins of standard worsted yarn. Dense stitches like moss stitch need more yarn. Open patterns like granny stitch need less. Always buy one extra skein to be safe.

### What size should a crochet throw blanket be?

A standard throw blanket is 50 x 60 inches. It fits well on a couch and covers one adult. If you want more coverage, go up to 55 x 65 inches. Measure your couch first. A throw that is too wide slides off constantly. Be sure to account for any border width in your final size.

### How do I calculate starting chains for a crochet blanket?

Multiply your stitches per inch (from a gauge swatch) by your target blanket width. Then add turning chains. For worsted weight at 4 stitches per inch and a 50-inch throw, that is 200 stitches plus 1 turning chain, which equals 201 chains. Always check your width after 4 to 5 rows. Chains stretch differently than worked fabric.

### Why does my crochet blanket keep getting wider or narrower?

Unplanned stitch increases or decreases cause blankets to change width. The most common cause is missing or adding stitches at row edges, especially when turning. Count stitches every few rows using a [Stitch Counter](/stitch-counter). Mark your first and last stitches with stitch markers to keep your edges straight.
