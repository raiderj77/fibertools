# Knitting Yarn Weight Chart: What Every Number and Label Actually Means

Yarn weight describes how thick a strand is. It controls how many stitches fit in an inch and how fast a project works up. The [Craft Yarn Council](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) standardized [seven weight categories (0 through 7) so labels mean the same thing](/blog/unlocking-yarn-weight-codes-understanding-the-standard-yarn-/) no matter which brand you buy. Check your gauge swatch before you start any pattern.

---

> **When I started:** I thought all yarn worked for all patterns. I did not check yarn weight, and it changed how everything looked and fit. The [yarn weight chart](/yarn-weight-chart) shows what each weight is good for.

## What Are the Seven Standard Yarn Weights?

The Craft Yarn Council system runs from 0 (Lace) to 7 (Jumbo). Each category has a number, a name, and a typical gauge range. Gauge is measured in stockinette over 4 inches. Here is the full chart:

| Weight Number | Name | Typical Gauge (sts per 4 in) | Common Needle Size (US) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Lace | 33-40+ | 000-1 |
| 1 | Super Fine | 27-32 | 1-3 |
| 2 | Fine | 23-26 | 3-5 |
| 3 | Light | 21-24 | 5-7 |
| 4 | Medium | 16-20 | 7-9 |
| 5 | Bulky | 12-15 | 9-11 |
| 6 | Super Bulky | 7-11 | 11-17 |
| 7 | Jumbo | 6 or fewer | 17 and up |

These ranges are starting points. Your actual gauge depends on your tension, your needle material, and the specific yarn. Always swatch.

## What Do Names Like "Worsted" and "DK" Mean?

These names are older terms. They were used before the numbered system existed. They map onto the numbers pretty cleanly.

Worsted is a Weight 4 yarn. It is the most common weight in North American yarn shops. DK (double knitting) is Weight 3. It is a little thinner than worsted and shows stitch detail a bit more clearly. Fingering is Weight 1. It is the top choice for socks. Aran often gets labeled Weight 4, but it tends to be on the denser end.

The confusion comes from the fact that these names were never formally standardized before the Craft Yarn Council system existed. You will still see "sport weight" (Weight 2) and "chunky" used interchangeably with "bulky" (Weight 5) by different brands. When you are not sure, look for the small skein symbol on the label. It has a number inside it. You can also check the recommended gauge printed on the ball band.

## How Do I Read a Yarn Label to Find the Weight?

Look for three things on the label. First, find the weight number or name. Second, find the recommended needle size. Third, find the gauge swatch information. A label might say "Weight 4 / Medium / Worsted." It might also just show the Craft Yarn Council symbol. The gauge on the label is what the yarn company got when they swatched it. It is not a guarantee of what you will get.

For example, a skein of Lion Brand Wool-Ease lists a gauge of 18 stitches and 24 rows over 4 inches on US 7 needles. That is a typical Weight 4 result. If your swatch comes out at 16 stitches over 4 inches on the same needles, your tension runs loose. Try going down a needle size before you cast on for anything fitted.

## Which Weight Should I Use for Common Projects?

Think about what the project needs first. Then pick your weight. Socks need fingering weight for durability and fit. Sweaters most often use DK or worsted. Blankets work up fastest in bulky. Baby knits work well in DK. DK gives nice detail and is not hard to wash and wear.

Here is what experienced knitters reach for most often:

**Socks:** Weight 1 (Fingering). Usually an 80/20 wool-nylon blend for durability. Expect 60 to 72 stitches for an average adult foot on US 1 to 2 needles.

**Baby items:** Weight 3 (DK) or Weight 4 (Worsted). DK gives you finer detail. Worsted works up faster. Both are easy to wash if you choose a superwash fiber.

**Sweaters:** Weight 4 (Worsted) is the most forgiving choice for a first garment. Weight 3 (DK) gives a drapier result. Weight 5 (Bulky) knits fast but adds visual mass. It suits outerwear better than fitted pullovers.

**Scarves and cowls:** Almost any weight works. Weight 4 and Weight 5 are popular because they move quickly and the stitch count stays manageable.

**Shawls:** Weight 0 to 2. Lace and fingering weights create the drape and delicacy that makes a shawl worth wearing.

**Blankets:** Weight 5 or 6 if you want to finish in a weekend. Weight 4 if you want more stitch detail and you are patient.

## Can I Substitute a Different Weight Than the Pattern Calls For?

You can, but it takes real math. Swapping one weight for another changes your gauge. That changes your stitch count. That changes your finished dimensions. A pattern written for Weight 4 at 18 stitches per 4 inches will not fit the same if you knit it in Weight 3 at 22 stitches per 4 inches without adjusting the stitch counts.

The cleanest approach is to [swatch in your substitute yarn, calculate the ratio between your gauge](/blog/demystifying-yarn-weights-for-crochet-using-the-yarn-weight-/) and the pattern's gauge, and multiply every stitch count in the pattern by that ratio. If the pattern says cast on 80 stitches at 18 stitches per 4 inches and your gauge is 22 stitches per 4 inches, you need roughly 98 stitches to get the same width. That math applies to every section of the pattern.

Going up a full weight category, like from worsted to bulky, is a big jump. The fabric structure changes. The drape changes. Seaming gets harder. A half-step substitution, like swapping worsted for a heavy DK, is much easier to manage.

## Why Does Yardage Matter as Much as Weight?

Two skeins can be the same weight but have very different yardage. [Yardage is what actually runs out mid-project](/blog/yarn-weight-chart-how-to-ensure-proper-fiber-weight-selectio/). A 100g skein of fingering weight typically holds around 400 yards. A 100g skein of worsted holds around 200 yards. A 100g skein of bulky might give you only 100 yards.

When a pattern lists yardage, use that number to calculate how much yarn to buy. Do not use the skein count. If a sweater pattern calls for 1,200 yards of worsted, check the yardage on your yarn's label and divide. Six skeins of a 200-yard worsted gets you there. Four skeins of a 280-yard worsted also gets you there, with some left over for seaming and repairs.

Buying one extra skein from the same dye lot is cheap insurance. Running out of yarn in a discontinued colorway is not a problem you want to solve.

## Where Can I Find Reliable Gauge Information for Any Yarn?

The [Craft Yarn Council's standards page](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) is the official reference for the numbered weight system. For specific yarns, [Ravelry's yarn database](https://www.ravelry.com/yarns) lets you filter by weight, fiber, and gauge. It also shows real project photos knit at different needle sizes. The ball band on your actual skein is your most reliable source for that specific yarn. Fiber content and construction affect gauge even within the same weight category.

No chart replaces a swatch. Knit at least a 6-inch square. Wash it the way you plan to wash the finished object. Let it dry flat. Then measure the center 4 inches. That number is your gauge. It is the only number that matters for your hands and your yarn.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is yarn weight and why does it matter for knitting?
Yarn weight refers to how thick a yarn strand is. It directly affects how your finished project looks and feels. Heavier yarns knit up faster and create chunkier, warmer fabric. Lighter weights produce delicate, drapey results. Choosing the correct weight helps your gauge match the pattern. That determines the final size and structure of your project. Using the wrong weight can lead to garments that are too large, too small, or structurally incorrect.

### What are the standard yarn weight categories from lightest to heaviest?
The standard yarn weight categories, from lightest to heaviest, are: Lace (0), Super Fine/Fingering (1), Fine/Sport (2), Light/DK (3), Medium/Worsted (4), Bulky (5), Super Bulky (6), and Jumbo (7). The Craft Yarn Council standardized these categories. They are used across knitting and crochet patterns. Each category matches a recommended needle size range and an approximate gauge. This makes it easier to substitute yarns and follow patterns accurately.

### How do I know what yarn weight a pattern requires?
Most knitting patterns list the required yarn weight in the materials section. They often include a recommended gauge and needle size too. Look for terms like "worsted weight" or "DK weight." You can also check the suggested needle size as a clue. If the pattern only lists gauge, use a yarn weight chart to match the stitches-per-inch measurement to the right weight category. Always knit a gauge swatch before starting. This confirms your chosen yarn behaves as expected.

### Can I substitute a different yarn weight than what a pattern calls for?
Yes, you can substitute a different yarn weight. But you will need to adjust your needle size, stitch count, and sometimes the pattern structure itself. Going up a weight category typically means fewer stitches and larger needles. Going down means more stitches and smaller needles. Substituting weights works best for simple projects like scarves or blankets. For fitted garments or structured items, big changes in weight can alter drape and fit in ways that are hard to fix without reworking the pattern.

### What needle size should I use for each yarn weight?
Each yarn weight matches a recommended needle size range. Lace uses 1.5 to 2.25mm. Fingering uses 2.25 to 3.25mm. Sport uses 3.25 to 3.75mm. DK uses 3.75 to 4.5mm. Worsted uses 4.5 to 5.5mm. Bulky uses 5.5 to 8mm. Super Bulky uses 8 to 12.75mm. Jumbo uses 12.75mm and above. These are guidelines, not strict rules. Your personal tension and the fabric density you want may lead you to size up or down. Always check your gauge swatch against the pattern's recommended gauge before you commit to a needle size.
