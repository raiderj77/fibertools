# UK vs US Crochet Terms: Complete Conversion Chart & Pattern Translator Guide

Have you ever followed a crochet pattern and ended up with fabric twice as tall as you expected? You probably ran into the UK vs US terminology problem. The same stitch name means totally different things depending on where the pattern was written.

A UK double crochet is the same as a US single crochet. A UK treble is the same as a US double crochet. Every stitch name shifts by one position. This has confused crocheters for over a century.

## Why Two Systems Exist and When It Matters

Both systems describe the exact same stitches. They just use different words. The split happened in the early 1900s. American publishers started renaming stitches on their own, apart from British ones. Neither system is right or wrong. But mixing them up will ruin your project.

You need to convert terms when:

- You are following a pattern from a UK designer or publisher
- You bought a vintage pattern book (British patterns from before 1990 almost always use UK terms)
- You are reading a pattern from Australia, New Zealand, or South Africa (they follow UK conventions)
- An online pattern does not say which system it uses
- You are translating a pattern for a friend who uses the other system

## Complete UK to US Crochet Stitch Conversion Table

This is the full conversion chart. Every stitch shifts up by one position when you convert from UK to US:

| UK Term | UK Abbreviation | US Term | US Abbreviation | Yarn Overs |
|---------|----------------|---------|-----------------|------------|
| Chain | ch | Chain | ch | 0 |
| Slip stitch | ss | Slip stitch | sl st | 0 |
| Double crochet | dc | Single crochet | sc | 0 |
| Half treble | htr | Half double crochet | hdc | 1 |
| Treble | tr | Double crochet | dc | 1 |
| Double treble | dtr | Treble crochet | tr | 2 |
| Triple treble | ttr | Double treble crochet | dtr | 3 |
| Quadruple treble | qtr | Triple treble crochet | ttr | 4 |

Notice the pattern: chain and slip stitch are the same in both systems. After that, every UK term maps to the US term that is one step shorter in name. The yarn overs column stays the same. Only the names shift.

### Additional Stitch Terms That Differ

Some pattern instructions also use different words beyond the basic stitches:

| UK Term | US Term |
|---------|---------|
| Tension | Gauge |
| Tension square | Gauge swatch |
| Miss (a stitch) | Skip (a stitch) |
| Yarn round hook (yrh) | Yarn over (yo) |
| Raise / relief | Front post / back post |
| Work straight | Work even |
| Cast off | Bind off / fasten off |
| Colour | Color |

These non-stitch terms trip people up just as often as the stitch names. A pattern that says "miss 2 stitches" means skip 2 stitches. It does not mean you made an error.

## How to Identify Which System a Pattern Uses

Before you start crocheting, figure out which system the pattern uses. Here are the clear signs:

**It is a UK pattern if:**
- It uses "tension" instead of "gauge"
- It uses "dc" but the gauge seems too short for double crochet (it is actually single crochet)
- It uses "treble" as the standard medium-height stitch
- The designer is based in the UK, Australia, New Zealand, or South Africa
- It uses "miss" instead of "skip"
- It uses "yrh" (yarn round hook) instead of "yo" (yarn over)
- Measurements are given in centimetres first

**It is a US pattern if:**
- It says "gauge" instead of "tension"
- It uses "sc" (single crochet). This stitch does not exist in UK terms.
- It uses "skip" instead of "miss"
- The designer is based in the US or Canada
- It uses "yo" for yarn over

**The easiest shortcut:** If a pattern mentions "single crochet" or uses "sc," it is definitely US terms. The UK system has no stitch called single crochet. That is the clearest sign.

## Vintage Pattern Considerations

Patterns from before the 1980s have extra challenges beyond the UK and US split:

**Pre-1960s British patterns** often use older words that do not match modern terms cleanly. "Afghan stitch" might mean Tunisian simple stitch. "Solomon's knot" appears under several different names.

**Hook sizes changed.** Old UK patterns use a numbered steel hook system. UK 14 equals a modern 2mm hook. UK 8 equals a modern 4mm hook. That system runs backward from today's metric system. Old US patterns used letter sizing, like H for 5mm and J for 6mm. Some of that letter sizing is still used today.

**Stitch heights were sometimes inconsistent.** Some patterns from the mid-1900s used "double crochet" to mean what we now call a half double crochet (hdc). If the gauge does not match and you have already confirmed the terminology system, the pattern might be using a non-standard stitch definition.

**Thread crochet** adds another layer of confusion. Steel hook numbers, thread sizes (10, 20, 30), and vintage lace terms all use conventions that differ from yarn crochet.

When you are unsure about a vintage pattern, crochet a gauge swatch using the stitch the pattern seems to call for. If the size does not match the stated gauge, try the next stitch up or down in height.

## Common Mistakes When Converting Terms

**Mistake 1: Converting only some stitches.** If a UK pattern uses both "dc" and "tr," you must convert both. The dc becomes sc and the tr becomes dc. Converting just one creates a pattern that does not work.

**Mistake 2: Converting a US pattern as if it were UK.** First, confirm the pattern actually needs converting. If you see "sc" anywhere, the pattern is already in US terms. Running it through a converter again would shift everything wrong.

**Mistake 3: Forgetting that turning chains follow the stitch.** In UK terms, a turning chain for double crochet (dc) is 1 chain. That same stitch in US terms (sc) also uses 1 chain. A UK treble (tr) uses 3 turning chains, and its US version (dc) also uses 3 turning chains. The turning chain count follows the stitch, not the name. So it converts correctly as long as you convert the stitch name.

**Mistake 4: Assuming Canadian patterns use US terms.** Most Canadian crochet publications do use US terms. But patterns from Quebec or bilingual Canadian publishers sometimes follow UK conventions. Use the "sc" test before you assume.

**Mistake 5: Mixing up increases.** "2dc in next st" in UK means "2sc in next st" in US. The increase method is the same. But misreading the stitch type means you work the wrong stitch height.

## Real Project Examples

### Example 1: Converting a UK Granny Square Pattern

**UK pattern reads:** Ch 4, join. Rnd 1: Ch 3 (counts as first tr), 2tr in ring, ch 2, \*3tr in ring, ch 2\* three times, join.

**US conversion:** Ch 4, join. Rnd 1: Ch 3 (counts as first dc), 2dc in ring, ch 2, \*3dc in ring, ch 2\* three times, join.

The UK "tr" becomes US "dc." The chains stay the same. The structure is identical. Only the stitch names change.

### Example 2: Converting a UK Amigurumi Pattern

**UK pattern reads:** Rnd 1: 6dc in magic ring. Rnd 2: 2dc in each st around (12). Rnd 3: \*1dc, 2dc in next st\* around (18).

**US conversion:** Rnd 1: 6sc in magic ring. Rnd 2: 2sc in each st around (12). Rnd 3: \*1sc, 2sc in next st\* around (18).

Amigurumi patterns use tight single crochet (US) or double crochet (UK) almost entirely. If you see a pattern that uses "dc" all the way through for a stuffed toy and it is from a UK designer, it is definitely single crochet.

### Example 3: Identifying a Mystery Vintage Pattern

You find a 1970s pattern card that reads: "Using a no. 9 hook, make 24 ch. Row 1: 1 dc into 2nd ch from hook, 1 dc into each ch, turn. 23 dc."

The "no. 9 hook" is UK sizing (3.5mm). The "dc" with a 1-chain turning chain is UK double crochet, which equals US single crochet. In modern US terms: Using a 3.5mm (US E/4) hook, chain 24. Row 1: Sc in 2nd ch from hook, sc in each ch across, turn. 23 sc.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why doesn't the UK have a single crochet stitch?

The UK system starts its named stitches at "double crochet" (dc). That is the shortest standard working stitch. It is the same as US single crochet. The UK treats the slip stitch as the simplest stitch and starts naming working stitches at "double." The US system added "single crochet" for that shortest stitch. That pushed all the other names up by one.

### Do Australian crochet patterns use UK or US terms?

Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa all follow UK crochet terms. If you are working from a pattern by an Australian designer, convert it the same way you would convert a British pattern. Look for "tension" instead of "gauge" and "dc" meaning single crochet. Check for those signs before you start.

### Can I use the conversion chart for Tunisian crochet too?

Basic Tunisian stitches use the same names in both UK and US systems. This includes Tunisian simple stitch, Tunisian knit stitch, and Tunisian purl stitch. The terminology confusion mainly applies to standard crochet stitches. However, some older UK patterns call Tunisian crochet "Afghan crochet" or "tricot crochet." That is worth knowing if you are working from vintage sources.

### What happens if I accidentally use the wrong terminology system?

Your fabric height will be wrong. If you read UK "dc" as US "dc" (double crochet), you will use a stitch that is about twice as tall as intended. Your project will be too tall. It will use more yarn than expected. It will also have a looser, more open texture than the designer planned. Always check the terminology system before you start row 1.
