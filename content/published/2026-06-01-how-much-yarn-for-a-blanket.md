# How Much Yarn Do You Need for a Blanket?

**The short answer:** most blankets need between 1,000 and 3,000 yards of yarn. The right amount depends on size, stitch, and yarn weight. A baby blanket in worsted weight needs roughly 1,000 to 1,500 yards. A throw for a couch needs closer to 1,800 to 2,500 yards. A full bed blanket can need 3,000 yards or more. These are starting points, not guarantees. Always check your gauge and buy extra.

---

## What size blanket are you actually making?

Size is the biggest factor in how much yarn you need. Figure this out before anything else. A baby blanket is about 30 by 36 inches. A throw is near 50 by 60 inches. A bed blanket can be 60 by 80 inches or larger. The bigger the blanket, the more yarn you need.

A baby blanket and a queen-size bed blanket are not even close to the same amount of yarn.

Here are the standard finished sizes most knitters and crocheters aim for:

- **Baby blanket:** 30 x 36 inches
- **Lap blanket:** 36 x 48 inches
- **Throw:** 50 x 60 inches
- **Full/Queen bed blanket:** 60 x 80 inches or larger

The size difference between a baby blanket and a throw is huge. Your yardage grows with the size. If someone says "a blanket takes about 2,000 yards" without asking what size, that number means almost nothing.

Here is a simple example. A basic stockinette throw in worsted weight at about 4.5 stitches per inch usually needs around 2,200 to 2,500 yards. The same throw in bulky weight at 3 stitches per inch might only need 1,200 to 1,500 yards. Each stitch is bigger, so it covers more area faster.

## Does yarn weight change how much yardage I need?

Yes. Heavier yarn covers less distance per yard. Thicker strands use up yardage faster. Two skeins can both weigh 100 grams but hold very different amounts of yarn. A super bulky skein might give you 106 yards. A fingering weight skein might give you 400 yards. Yarn weight is the second biggest factor after size.

This is the part that trips people up at the yarn store. Both skeins could weigh 100 grams, but the fingering yarn goes much further.

[Craft Yarn Council's standard yarn weight system](https://www.craftyarncouncil.com/standards/yarn-weight-system) sorts yarn into groups from lace (0) to jumbo (7). Here is a rough guide for a 50 x 60 inch throw:

- **Bulky (weight 5 to 6):** 1,000 to 1,500 yards
- **Worsted (weight 4):** 2,000 to 2,500 yards
- **DK (weight 3):** 2,500 to 3,200 yards
- **Fingering (weight 1):** 4,000 to 5,000+ yards

These numbers are for a fairly dense, solid stitch. Lacy or open patterns use less yarn. Dense stitches like seed stitch or moss stitch use more.

## Does the stitch pattern affect yardage?

Yes, and it surprises a lot of knitters. Cables and dense textures can use 20 to 30 percent more yarn than plain stockinette at the same size. Open lace or mesh patterns can use 15 to 20 percent less. Always swatch in your actual stitch before you estimate your final yardage.

Stitch pattern is often overlooked as a yardage factor.

Cables, bobbles, and dense stitches use more yarn than stockinette or a basic double crochet grid. A heavily cabled blanket can use 20 to 30 percent more yarn than the same size in plain stockinette. Open lacework or mesh patterns can use 15 to 20 percent less.

In crochet, stitch height matters too. Single crochet (sc) makes a dense, short fabric. It uses more yarn per square inch than double crochet (dc). Double crochet builds height faster with each stitch. A blanket worked in sc will need more yardage than the same size in dc.

If you are working from a pattern, use the pattern's yardage as your starting point. Then add a buffer. If you are designing your own or swapping stitch patterns, make a 6-inch square swatch. Weigh it or measure the yardage used. Then scale up from there.

## How do I calculate yardage without a pattern?

Swatch first, then do the math. Knit or crochet at least a 6 by 6 inch swatch in your planned stitch. Measure exactly how much yarn it used. Then figure out how many of those swatches fit into your finished blanket size. Multiply and add a buffer. It takes about 20 minutes and keeps you from running short.

Here is the method step by step:

1. Knit or crochet a swatch at least 6 x 6 inches in your planned stitch pattern.
2. Measure the exact yardage you used for that swatch. Wind it off and measure, or [weigh the swatch and calculate based on the yards-per-gram](/blog/creating-a-realistic-yarn-yardage-estimate-incorporating-fib/) of your yarn.
3. Calculate the square inches of your swatch, then [calculate the square inches of your finished blanket](/blog/accurately-calculating-your-yarn-yardage-with-a-customizable/).
4. Divide blanket square inches by swatch square inches. Then multiply by the yardage used in your swatch.
5. Add 10 to 15 percent as a buffer.

Example: your 6 x 6 inch swatch used 40 yards. Your target blanket is 50 x 60 inches, which is 3,000 square inches. Your swatch was 36 square inches. Divide 3,000 by 36 and you get 83.3. Multiply by 40 yards and you get 3,333 yards. Add a 15 percent buffer and you get about 3,833 yards total.

This math gets you much closer than any generic chart. It accounts for [your specific yarn, your tension, and your stitch pattern](/blog/optimizing-yarn-yardage-calculation-with-a-dynamic-gauge-and/) all at once.

## How much extra yarn should I buy?

Buy at least one skein beyond your calculated total. Make sure it shares the same dye lot as the rest. Dye lots can shift between production runs. Even the same colorway can look different when knit up side by side. If your original lot sells out before you finish, matching it becomes very hard.

Dye lots matter. [WEBS America's Yarn Store explains dye lots well](https://www.yarn.com/pages/understanding-dye-lots). Even the same colorway from the same brand can look visibly different between dye lots when knit up together. If you run out and your original dye lot is gone, you may see a visible stripe across your blanket where the new lot starts.

Most experienced knitters follow this rule: calculate what you need, round up to the next full skein, then buy one more skein on top of that. If you have yarn left over, it can become a swatch, a hat, or part of another project. If you run out, you have a problem.

For very large blankets or expensive yarn, it is worth calling the yarn shop. Ask them to hold extra skeins from the same dye lot while you work.

## Quick reference: yardage estimates by blanket size

These numbers are starting points for solid, medium-density stitch patterns. They are not guarantees. Cables, colorwork, or a very loose gauge will move you outside these ranges. Use them to get a rough skein count before you swatch. Then recalculate once you have real yardage data from your actual yarn and stitch pattern.

These are ballpark figures for solid, medium-density stitch patterns in each weight. Measure as you go and treat these as a starting point only.

| Blanket Size | Bulky | Worsted | DK |
|---|---|---|---|
| Baby (30x36") | 500 to 800 yds | 900 to 1,200 yds | 1,200 to 1,600 yds |
| Lap (36x48") | 800 to 1,100 yds | 1,400 to 1,800 yds | 1,800 to 2,400 yds |
| Throw (50x60") | 1,200 to 1,600 yds | 2,000 to 2,600 yds | 2,600 to 3,200 yds |
| Full/Queen (60x80") | 1,800 to 2,400 yds | 3,000 to 3,800 yds | 3,800 to 5,000 yds |

When in doubt, swatch it out. Spending 20 minutes swatching and doing the math is much better than frogging half a blanket or making an emergency yarn order.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much yarn do I need for a throw blanket?
A typical throw blanket (50 x 60 inches) needs roughly 1,000 to 2,000 yards of yarn. The exact amount depends on yarn weight and stitch pattern. Bulky or super bulky yarn uses fewer yards but is thicker per skein. Fingering or DK weight needs much more yardage. Your gauge, needle or hook size, and stitch choice all affect the total. Always add 10 to 15 percent extra to your estimate so you do not run short mid-project.

### Does yarn weight affect how much I need for a blanket?
Yes, yarn weight makes a big difference in yardage. Heavier yarns like bulky or jumbo weight cover more area per yard. That means you need fewer total yards. Sometimes as little as 500 to 800 yards works for a lap blanket in bulky weight. Lighter weights like sport or fingering yarn may need 3,000 yards or more for the same size. Always check the yardage per skein, not just the number of skeins, when planning your project.

### How do I calculate yarn for a blanket if I'm making my own pattern?
Start by knitting or crocheting a gauge swatch. Then calculate the total square inches of your blanket. Divide that by the square inches of your swatch and multiply by the yards used in the swatch. This gives you a reliable yardage estimate for any custom size or stitch pattern. Tools like the yarn calculator on FiberTools.app can do this math for you and adjust for different blanket sizes and yarn weights.

### How many skeins of yarn do I need for a baby blanket?
Most baby blankets need 3 to 6 skeins of medium (worsted) weight yarn. That is roughly 800 to 1,200 yards total for a standard 30 x 36 inch size. Bulkier yarn means fewer skeins. Finer yarn means more skeins. The stitch pattern matters too. Dense stitches like moss stitch use more yarn than open lace patterns. Check your pattern's recommended yardage first. Always buy one extra skein from the same dye lot just in case.

### What happens if I run out of yarn before finishing a blanket?
Running out of yarn mid-blanket is one of the most common problems in fiber crafts. If your yarn is discontinued or the dye lot is different, you may see a visible color shift in the finished piece. To avoid this, always overestimate your yardage by at least 10 to 15 percent. Buy all skeins from the same dye lot. Weigh your remaining yarn now and then as you work. If you do run short, consider adding a contrasting border using a yarn that works well with what you have.
