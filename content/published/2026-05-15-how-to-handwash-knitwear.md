# How to Handwash Knitwear Without Ruining It

Handwash knitwear in cool water (60 to 85°F / 15 to 30°C) with a wool-safe soap. Handle it gently. Never wring or twist it. Support the wet fabric at all times. Squeeze out the water, roll it in a towel, and [block it flat to dry](/blog/what-is-blocking-in-knitting-and-why-it-matters/). The whole process takes about 20 minutes of active time.

---

## Does wool actually need special soap?

Yes. Regular dish soap or laundry detergent can strip the natural lanolin from wool fibers. This can cause damage that cannot be fixed. Use a pH-neutral, wool-safe wash like Eucalan, Soak, or plain castile soap diluted well. A teaspoon of product in a full sink of water is usually plenty.

Standard detergents are risky for a reason. Wool fiber has a scaly outer layer called the cuticle. Alkaline detergents make those scales open up and lock together with nearby fibers. That is exactly how felting happens. [Wool fiber structure and felting mechanics are well-documented by the Wool Research Organisation of New Zealand (WRONZ)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0143720804001469). Once those scales lock together, there is no going back. A $12 bottle of Eucalan is cheap protection for a sweater that took you 40 hours to knit.

---

## What temperature should the water be?

Use cool to lukewarm water. Stay between 60°F and 85°F (15 to 30°C). Temperature shock means plunging wool from hot water into cold water, or the other way around. This makes the fiber scales react and felt. Keep every rinse at the same temperature as your wash water.

A good rule of thumb: if the water feels slightly warm on your inner wrist, it is about right for most wool. Superwash wool is more forgiving and can handle slightly warmer water. But there is no reason to go hotter, so stay cool. For plant fibers like cotton or linen, you have more flexibility. Still, cool water is gentler on the structure of your knit fabric.

---

## How do I actually wash it without stretching it out?

Fill a basin or clean sink with water first. Then add your soap. Then put the garment in. Press it gently under the water and let it soak for 10 to 15 minutes. Do not agitate, scrub, or rub the fabric against itself.

The soaking does most of the work. After 10 to 15 minutes, gently squeeze the soapy water through the fabric. Press it against the side of the basin. Never wring or twist it. For a dirty spot, like a cuff that picked up grime, you can add a tiny drop of soap and press it in with your fingers. Keep the motion gentle and straight, not circular.

Drain the soapy water. Refill with clean water at the same temperature. Press the garment through the rinse water the same way. Repeat until the water runs clear and you cannot smell soap. For most garments, this takes two rinses.

---

## How do I get the water out without damaging the fabric?

Lift the garment with both hands supporting its full weight. Never lift it by a corner or sleeve. Press it gently against the side of the basin to squeeze out as much water as you can. Then lay it flat on a clean dry towel. Roll the towel and garment together like a jelly roll. Press firmly along the roll.

[Wet wool can lose up to 30% of its tensile strength](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/257158907_Mechanical_properties_of_wool_fibres). That is why supporting the full fabric matters so much. A soaking wet sweater hanging from one sleeve will stretch that sleeve out in minutes. The towel-roll method removes a lot of water without stressing the fibers. Unroll the towel. If it is soaked through, swap it for a second dry towel and repeat.

---

## How do I dry it so it keeps its shape?

Lay it flat on a dry towel or a mesh blocking board. Reshape it to your measurements while it is still damp. Check the schematic from your pattern. Measure the width at the chest, the length from shoulder to hem, and the sleeve length. Pin if needed. Let it air dry completely before moving it.

Drying time varies a lot. A lightweight lace shawl might be dry in four hours. A bulky cabled sweater can take 24 to 48 hours, especially in a humid room. Flip the garment over halfway through drying. This helps the underside dry evenly and prevents a mildew smell. Do not dry knitwear in direct sunlight for a long time. UV light breaks down protein fibers over time.

---

## Can I ever machine wash handknits?

Some yarns labeled superwash or machine-washable can handle a machine on the wool or delicate cycle. Use a mesh laundry bag and cold water. [Always check your yarn label](/blog/how-to-read-yarn-labels-everything-you-need-to-know/) and [check your gauge swatch after washing it](/blog/analyzing-the-impact-of-yarn-weight-and-fiber-content-on-gau/) in the machine first.

Even superwash-treated yarns can grow a lot in the wash. This happens because of the way the scales are chemically removed or coated. [Superwash treatments change how wool absorbs water and responds to agitation](https://woolmark.com/industry/wool-processing/superwash/). Some superwash yarns will stretch out and not spring back. Wash your swatch in the machine before you wash a finished sweater. If the swatch grows or loses its shape, stick to handwashing the finished piece.

---

## How often should I wash knitwear?

Wool naturally resists odor. It does not need washing after every wear. Most knitters wash sweaters three to five times per season, or when a garment is visibly dirty or starts to smell. Between wears, air the garment out flat or draped over a chair for an hour before folding and storing it.

Washing too often breaks down fibers faster and shortens the life of your work. Spot-clean when you can. Save the full wash for when it is truly needed.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do you handwash knitwear without shrinking it?
Use cool or lukewarm water. Never use hot water. Use a gentle wool wash or mild detergent. Fill a basin, put the garment in, and gently squeeze the water through it. Do not rub or wring it. Rubbing and wringing cause friction and felting. Let it soak for about 10 minutes. Then rinse with water at the same temperature. Temperature shock and agitation are the two main causes of shrinkage. Keeping both to a minimum protects your knitting.

### What soap should I use to handwash knitted items?
Use a detergent made for wool and delicates. Good options include Eucalan, Soak, or Woolite. These products are pH-balanced to protect protein fibers like wool, alpaca, and mohair. Many no-rinse formulas are very convenient. You just squeeze out the water and skip the rinse step. Avoid regular dish soap or laundry detergent. Harsh surfactants and enzymes can strip natural lanolin and damage delicate yarn fibers over time.

### How do you dry handwashed knitwear so it keeps its shape?
Lay the garment flat on a clean dry towel to block and dry. After washing, gently press out extra water by rolling the knitwear inside the towel. Never wring or twist it. Unroll it, reshape the piece to its original measurements, and leave it flat on a drying rack or fresh towel. Keep it away from direct sunlight and heat. Hanging wet knitwear causes it to stretch out of shape under its own weight.

### Can you handwash knitwear made from acrylic yarn?
Yes. Acrylic knitwear is easy to handwash and is generally more forgiving than natural fibers. Acrylic does not felt or shrink the way wool does. Still, cool to lukewarm water and a gentle detergent are recommended. This helps keep the yarn soft and prevents pilling. Avoid hot water. Hot water can permanently distort acrylic fibers. Most acrylic blends can also handle a gentle machine cycle. But handwashing extends the life of your finished piece.

### How often should you wash handknitted or crocheted garments?
Wash knitwear only when it is visibly dirty or starts to smell. For garments worn against the skin, that is usually every three to five wears. Natural fibers like wool naturally resist odor, so washing too often breaks down the fiber structure without good reason. Between wears, air garments out flat or on a hanger to freshen them up. Spot-clean small stains right away to avoid needing a full wash.
