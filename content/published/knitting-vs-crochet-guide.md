# Knitting vs Crochet: How to Choose the Right Craft

"Should I learn to knit or crochet?" is the most common question in every fiber arts subreddit. It gets asked at least twice a week. The honest answer: both are worth learning. But one might suit you better. It depends on what you want to make, how you learn, and how patient you are.

Here is a direct comparison so you can pick the right starting point.

## What's the Actual Difference?

**Knitting** uses two needles, or circular needles. It keeps many live stitches on the needle at once. Each row moves stitches from one needle to the other by pulling loops through loops. The fabric comes out thin, drapey, and stretchy.

**Crochet** uses one hook and keeps only one live loop at a time. Each stitch is finished before you move to the next. You insert the hook, pull up a loop, and finish the stitch right there. The fabric comes out thicker, sturdier, and less stretchy.

Both crafts use the same yarn. Both make fabric stitch by stitch. The tools and hand movements are different. The fabric each craft makes is also different.

## Which Is Easier to Learn?

**Crochet is easier for most beginners.** You only track one live loop instead of dozens. If you drop a stitch in crochet, the fabric does not unravel. Mistakes are easier to spot and fix. You can learn the basic chain and single crochet in 30 minutes.

**Knitting has a steeper learning curve.** Managing many live stitches on needles takes coordination. A dropped stitch can run down several rows if you do not catch it. But once you get the rhythm, knitting feels peaceful. Many knitters find it more relaxing than crochet.

**Bottom line:** If you want to make something this weekend, start with crochet. If you are willing to practice for a few sessions before starting a project, knitting is just as doable.

## How Do the Fabrics Compare?

| Property | Knitting | Crochet |
|---|---|---|
| Thickness | Thin, single-layer | Thick, 1.5 to 2x knitting |
| Drape | Excellent | Moderate to stiff |
| Stretch | High (especially stockinette) | Low |
| Weight | Lighter per sq inch | Heavier per sq inch |
| Yarn usage | Less (about 25 to 35% less) | More |
| Reversibility | Often different front/back | Often reversible |
| Speed per stitch | Slower | Faster |

**Knit fabric** works great for garments, socks, lightweight shawls, and anything that needs stretch and drape. Stockinette is the thinnest and most drapey fabric in fiber arts.

**Crochet fabric** works great for blankets, structured bags, amigurumi (stuffed toys), hats, and anything that needs body and stiffness. The thicker fabric also makes warmer outerwear.

## Which Projects Work Best for Each Craft?

**Best for knitting:**
- Socks (stretchy fabric hugs the foot)
- Sweaters and cardigans (thin, drapey fabric wears well)
- Lace shawls (yarn overs create delicate openwork)
- Ribbed accessories (stretchy ribbing for hats and cuffs)
- Fair Isle colorwork (two-color stranded knitting)

**Best for crochet:**
- Amigurumi and stuffed toys (firm fabric holds stuffing)
- Blankets and afghans (works up faster in crochet)
- Bags and baskets (structured fabric holds its shape)
- Granny squares and modular projects (easy to join)
- 3D shapes and sculptures (precise stitch-by-stitch shaping)

**Both work equally well for:** Scarves, cowls, hats, baby blankets, dishcloths, pillows, headbands.

## How Do Speed and Yarn Usage Compare?

**Crochet is faster per stitch.** Each stitch is finished on its own. The hook motions are simpler than moving stitches between needles. A crochet row takes about 20 to 30% less time than a knit row with the same number of stitches.

**But crochet uses 25 to 35% more yarn** than knitting for the same finished size. The thicker fabric needs more yarn per square inch. A knit blanket needing 2,000 yards costs about $50 to $60 in worsted acrylic. The same size crochet blanket needs 2,500 to 2,700 yards and costs $65 to $80.

## What Tools Do You Need to Start?

**To start knitting:** One pair of US 8 (5.0mm) straight needles or a 32-inch circular needle ($5 to $12), one skein of worsted weight yarn ($4 to $8), scissors, and a tapestry needle. Total startup cost: $10 to $20.

**To start crochet:** One H/8 (5.0mm) crochet hook ($2 to $5), one skein of worsted weight yarn ($4 to $8), scissors, and a tapestry needle. Total startup cost: $8 to $15.

Crochet needs less equipment to start. One hook does everything. Knitting eventually needs different needle sizes, cable needles, stitch holders, and possibly interchangeable needle sets ($30 to $100).

## What Are Common Misconceptions?

**"Crochet is just for grandmothers and knitting is modern."** Both crafts have large, active communities with people of every age. Instagram and TikTok are full of modern crochet and knitting designs. Neither craft is outdated.

**"You can't make garments with crochet."** Crochet garments are everywhere. Crochet tops, cardigans, and dresses are trending. The fabric is different from knit garments. It is thicker and less drapey. But the results can be stunning.

**"Knitting is always better quality."** Quality depends on the maker, not the craft. A well-made crochet blanket outlasts a poorly made knit one. Both crafts can make heirloom-quality items when done with care and good materials.

**"You have to choose one."** Most fiber artists learn both eventually. The skills go well together. Many knitters add crochet borders to their knit projects. Many crocheters learn knitting to access sock and sweater patterns.

## What Do Real Beginners Experience?

**The crochet-first path.** One beginner started with crochet and made a dishcloth in 2 hours, then a scarf in 8 hours, then a baby blanket in 20 hours, all in the first month. She learned knitting 6 months later. Her yarn-handling skills from crochet made learning to knit easier. She now does both. She prefers crochet for blankets and knitting for garments.

**The knitting-first path.** One beginner chose knitting and spent 3 evenings learning to cast on, knit, and purl before starting a garter stitch scarf. The scarf took 12 hours. She found the rhythmic motion relaxing. She tried crochet a year later and picked it up in one afternoon. She already understood gauge, yarn weights, and pattern reading.

**The instant gratification learner.** One college student wanted to make a stuffed animal for a friend's birthday in 5 days. She chose crochet because the single-hook method was faster to learn. She finished an amigurumi cat, her first-ever fiber arts project, in 4 evenings. She is now a dedicated crocheter who has never tried knitting, and she is happy with that.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is crochet faster than knitting?

Crochet produces stitches about 20 to 30% faster than knitting. Each stitch finishes on its own with simpler hand motions. But crochet uses 25 to 35% more yarn for the same finished size, so you work more total stitches. For the same finished item, crochet finishes about 15 to 25% faster overall than knitting.

### Can I learn both knitting and crochet?

Yes, and most fiber artists eventually do. The skills go well together. Learning one first makes the second easier. You already understand yarn, gauge, patterns, and finishing. Start with whichever one appeals to you. Add the second craft when you feel comfortable with the first.

### Which craft is cheaper to start?

Crochet is slightly cheaper. A hook costs $2 to $5 versus $5 to $12 for needles. Both crafts use the same yarn. The ongoing cost difference is that crochet uses more yarn per project, about 25 to 35% more. So the yarn cost per project is higher for crochet, even though the tools cost less.

### Which craft is better for making clothes?

Knitting makes thinner, more drapey fabric. That is traditionally preferred for garments like sweaters, cardigans, and socks. Crochet makes thicker fabric that works well for structured garments like vests, jackets, and summer tops with open stitch patterns. Both crafts can make any type of garment with the right stitch choice and yarn weight.

## Try Both and See What Clicks

The best craft is the one you will actually do. If you like speed and structure, try crochet. If you like rhythm and drape, try knitting. Either way, grab a hook or needles and a ball of worsted weight yarn. Spend an evening finding out which one you like.
