# Lace Knitting and Crochet Guide

Lace turns yarn into something that looks impossible. Delicate holes, sweeping curves, and detailed patterns come from the same yarn and tools you would use for a dishcloth. The finished fabric is light enough to pull through a wedding ring. It is also strong enough to last a lifetime.

Lace is not as hard as it looks. Every lace pattern is built from basic stitches. Yarn overs make the holes. Decreases keep the stitch count the same. If you can knit or crochet, you can make lace.

## What Is Lace and When Should You Use It?

Lace is any openwork fabric made by placing holes, called eyelets, in a pattern on purpose. The holes are the design. The solid stitches around them are the frame.

**In knitting:** Holes are made with yarn overs (yo). Each yarn over adds a stitch. So a matching decrease, such as k2tog, ssk, or sl2-k1-p2sso, removes a stitch to keep the count even. The mix of yarn overs and decreases creates the pattern.

**In crochet:** Holes are made with chain spaces. Instead of working a stitch into every stitch of the row below, you chain 1 to 3 and skip 1 to 3 stitches. Then you work into the next stitch. Crochet lace includes filet crochet (a grid of open and solid squares), shell lace (fan-shaped clusters with gaps), and pineapple lace (layered fans that get smaller).

**Best projects for lace:** Shawls, wraps, doilies, table runners, curtain panels, wedding veils, christening gowns, lightweight summer tops, and edgings.

## How Do You Read a Lace Chart?

Lace patterns are almost always shown as charts instead of written out. That is because the chart looks like the finished lace.

**Chart symbols (knitting):**
- Empty square = knit on RS, purl on WS
- Circle (O) = yarn over
- Right-leaning slash (/) = k2tog (knit 2 together)
- Left-leaning slash (\) = ssk (slip, slip, knit)
- Inverted V = centered double decrease (sl2-k1-p2sso)

**Reading direction:** Read right to left on right-side rows. Read left to right on wrong-side rows. Odd rows are usually RS. Even rows are usually WS.

**Stitch count check:** Count your stitches after every charted row. The number should match your starting count. Every yarn over needs a matching decrease somewhere in the same row. This is true unless the pattern is meant to increase on purpose.

## How Do You Block Lace?

Blocking is what turns lace from a crumpled mess into something beautiful. Unblocked lace looks nothing like the finished piece. The yarn overs are scrunched shut. The pattern is hidden. The fabric looks lumpy. Blocking opens every hole, straightens every line, and shows the design.

**Wet blocking (recommended for lace):**
1. Soak the finished piece in lukewarm water with a drop of wool wash for 15 to 20 minutes.
2. Gently press out the water. Do not wring it. Roll the piece in a towel.
3. Pin the piece to a blocking mat. Stretch it to the finished size. Lace can stretch 20 to 40% beyond its off-needle size.
4. Pin every point and scallop along the edges. Use rustproof T-pins or blocking wires.
5. Let it dry completely. This takes 24 to 48 hours. Do not remove the pins until it is fully dry.

**Aggressive blocking** stretches lace to its biggest size. It opens all the eyelets fully. This is standard for lace shawls and doilies. Pin firmly and stretch generously.

**Gentle blocking** stretches less. It keeps some of the fabric's natural gather. This is better for lace garments where you do not want the fabric to be completely flat and open.

## What Yarn Works Best for Lace?

**Lace weight (CYC 0)** is the traditional choice. It has 800 to 1,200 yards per 100g skein and 30 or more WPI. It creates the most delicate, airy fabric. One skein can make a large shawl. The thin yarn makes mistakes harder to fix.

**Fingering weight (CYC 1)** is the most popular modern choice for lace. It has 350 to 450 yards per 100g and 14 to 20 WPI. It is thick enough to see your stitches clearly. It is also thin enough for open lace patterns. This is a great starting point for beginners.

**Sport and DK weight** make lace that is easier to see and a little chunkier. The holes are larger compared to the stitches around them. These weights work well for lace panel inserts on garments or lace-edged baby blankets.

**Best fibers:** Merino wool (blocks beautifully and holds its shape), silk (has a gorgeous sheen and crisp stitch definition), merino/silk blends (the top choice for lace shawls), alpaca/silk (has a luxurious drape).

**Avoid for lace:** Fuzzy yarns like mohair, because the fuzz fills in the holes. Cotton, because it does not stretch during blocking. Acrylic, because it does not block as crisply as wool or silk.

## What Are Common Tips and Mistakes?

**Use a lifeline every 10 to 20 rows.** Thread a piece of smooth scrap yarn through all the stitches on the needle. If you make a mistake, you can rip back to the lifeline without losing stitches. Lace is very hard to fix once you have frogged several rows.

**Use markers between pattern repeats.** Place a stitch marker after every pattern repeat. When you reach a marker, your stitch count should match the expected count for that repeat. This catches errors right away instead of 5 repeats later.

**Count after every patterned row.** Lace has zero room for stitch count errors. One extra or missing yarn over throws off the whole pattern. Count every RS row without exception.

**Use needles 2 to 3 sizes larger than the yarn label suggests.** Lace needs open fabric. If your fingering weight yarn recommends US 3 needles, try US 5 to 7 for lace. The looser gauge makes more visible holes and lighter fabric.

**Common mistakes:**
- Measuring gauge before blocking (lace stretches 20 to 40%)
- Forgetting to pair each yarn over with a decrease (stitch count drifts)
- Not using lifelines (one mistake can mean frogging all the way to the cast-on)
- Blocking too gently (the lace stays scrunched and the pattern does not open)
- Choosing fuzzy yarn (the fuzz fills the holes and hides the pattern)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is lace knitting hard for beginners?

The basic technique is simple. Yarn overs create holes and decreases keep the stitch count the same. The hard part is following the chart carefully and counting stitches on every row. Start with a simple eyelet pattern, such as yarn over and k2tog repeated, in fingering weight on larger needles. This teaches the core skills without complex charting.

### Does lace use more or less yarn than regular knitting?

Less. The holes in lace use zero yarn. So lace uses roughly 10 to 20% less yarn than stockinette at the same finished dimensions. A fingering weight stockinette shawl that needs 700 yards might only need 580 to 630 yards in a lace pattern. But lace needs blocking to reach its full size, so measure your finished dimensions after blocking.

### Can I make lace with crochet?

Yes. Crochet lace uses chain spaces and skipped stitches to create openwork. Filet crochet (a grid of open and closed squares) and pineapple lace are the most popular crochet lace techniques. Crochet lace tends to be sturdier than knit lace. It does not need to be blocked as hard, though blocking still makes the finished piece look better.

### Why does my lace look terrible before blocking?

Every piece of lace looks bad before blocking. The yarn overs are compressed, the pattern is hidden, and the fabric bunches up. This is normal. Trust the pattern, keep your stitch counts right, and block aggressively at the end. The change from ugly to finished lace is one of the most dramatic moments in fiber arts.
